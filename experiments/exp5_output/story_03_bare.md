I had been working the night shift at the bakery for three years, and the darkness outside the shop’s front windows had become a familiar companion. The streetlights threw a thin amber glow on the cracked pavement, and the occasional hum of a passing truck was the only sound that broke the quiet. Inside, the ovens breathed steady heat, and the dough I kneaded rose and fell like a heartbeat. The routine was simple: I opened the doors at four in the morning, set the mixers to whir, and watched the loaves come to life under the dim fluorescent lights. When the city’s first commuters shuffled past the glass doors at dawn, I was already cleaning the counters, wiping flour from the surfaces, and prepping for the next batch.

One night, after a long line of orders for rye and sourdough, I decided to try something different. I had read an article about a baker in a small town who let his dough sit for an extra hour before baking, claiming the longer proof gave the crust a more complex texture. I was skeptical, but the night was quiet, the shop empty save for a lone cat that had slipped in through the back door, and I was restless enough to experiment.

I shaped a handful of dough into round boules, placed them on a tray, and covered them loosely with a damp cloth. The clock on the wall ticked past midnight, and I turned down the ovens, letting the heat settle into a low, consistent glow. I moved to the back office, where a stack of old city maps and a battered satellite imaging book lay on a shelf. The book was a relic from a previous owner, a geographer who had collected images of places that never existed—fictional cities imagined in novels, sketches from architects, and satellite renders that were, perhaps, the product of a faulty algorithm.

I flipped through the pages, feeling the weight of each glossy photograph. One image caught my eye: a sprawling metropolis, its streets laid out like a grid, a river snaking through the center, and a series of concentric rings of highways that seemed to pulse outward. The city’s name was printed in a tiny font at the bottom—"Asterion". I had never heard of it, and a quick search on my phone returned nothing but a few references to a speculative design project. The satellite image was crisp, the colors bright, the shadows sharp. It felt like a place that could exist somewhere, but also like a dream you might only see in a child’s sketchbook.

I set the tray of dough aside, turned off the lights, and locked the shop. The night outside grew colder, the wind rattling the shutters. I stood in the dark for a moment, listening to the faint hiss of the ventilation system. My mind drifted back to the image of Asterion, and I imagined the streets, the bridges, the towers that might rise there.

When the first light of dawn seeped through the blinds, I turned the ovens back on, letting the heat rise to the proper temperature. The dough had proofed overnight, its surface covered in a thin film of moisture. I slid the tray into the oven, the door closing with a soft thud. The smell of rising yeast filled the air, mingling with the faint metallic tang of the cooling metal.

The timer rang after twenty minutes, and I pulled the loaves out, setting them on the cooling rack. The crusts were browned, speckled with the characteristic cracks of a well‑proofed loaf. I lifted one gently, feeling the weight of the bread in my hands, the warmth radiating from the crust. Then I noticed something odd.

The pattern of the cracks on the top of the loaf was not random. It traced a series of lines that intersected at right angles, forming a layout that resembled a street grid. The central crack ran straight down the middle of the bread, like a river cutting through a valley. Around it, a series of concentric rings of lighter and darker crust formed a shape that matched the outer highways in the satellite photo. I set the loaf down and examined it more closely, running my fingertips over the ridges. The alignment was uncanny.

I fetched the book again, flipping to the page with the Asterion image. I held the loaf up beside the photo, aligning the crust’s central crack with the river in the picture. The match was striking. The outer rings on the crust matched the highway circles in the satellite view, and the minor fissures corresponded to the smaller streets branching off. My heart raced, and I felt a sudden chill run down my spine.

I wondered if it was a coincidence. Bread crusts often crack in unpredictable ways, shaped by the dough’s hydration, the oven’s temperature, and the motion of the gases inside. Yet the precision of the pattern made me think of something else—perhaps an imprint left on the dough by something I could not see.

I decided to test the idea. Over the next few nights, I prepared a series of loaves, each time allowing them to proof overnight in the same conditions. I varied the dough’s hydration slightly, added whole grain, rye, and spelt, and adjusted the fermentation time. When the loaves emerged from the oven, I examined each one. Some displayed simple crack patterns, like any ordinary bread. Others, however, revealed intricate designs that looked like maps: a lattice of streets, a circle of walls, a diagonal line that could be a bridge or a canal.

When I compared the shapes to the pages in the book, I found matching fragments. A loaf with a triangular wedge in its crust reminded me of a city built on a hill, its streets radiating outward. Another loaf bore a pattern that looked like a series of concentric circles intersected by a single line, exactly like the satellite image of a city I had never heard of. The more I looked, the more I became convinced that the bread was somehow echoing images that existed only on paper.

I began to keep a journal. I recorded the date, the time the dough went into proof, the type of flour used, the temperature of the shop, and a sketch of the crust pattern. I also noted the page numbers of the satellite images that matched. Over the weeks, the entries grew longer, and I found myself looking forward to the quiet hours after midnight, when the shop was empty and the dough could rest undisturbed.

One night, I stayed later than usual. The cat had found a warm spot on the counter and was dozing, its tail twitching in its sleep. I was preparing a batch of focaccia, spreading olive oil and rosemary over the dough, when the lights flickered. A sudden power surge knocked out the main supply, leaving the shop bathed in the soft glow of the emergency lights. The ovens clicked off, and the temperature dropped to a cool, damp air.

I decided to let the focaccia dough sit in the dark, hoping the temperature change would affect the rise. As the minutes passed, I thought about the strange patterns. A feeling of unease settled over me, as if the bakery itself were holding a secret. The cat woke, stretched, and hopped onto the tray, leaving a faint imprint of its paw on the dough. I laughed at the absurdity of it, but the image stuck in my mind.

When the power returned, the ovens warmed up again. I slid the focaccia into the oven, and as it baked, I watched the dough swell, the herbs turning a deep green. The timer rang, and I pulled the tray out, the focaccia smelling of rosemary and toasted olive oil. I lifted a corner, and there, in the crisped crust, a pattern emerged: a series of intersecting lines that formed a shape reminiscent of a star, its points extending outward like spokes. It reminded me of a city I had read about once—a place built around a central plaza, with avenues radiating like sunbeams.

I could not shake the feeling that the bakery was a conduit, a place where something was being transferred onto the dough. The more I thought about it, the more I wanted to understand the source. I began to research the satellite images, tracing their origins. Many of them were part of a project called "Imagined Geographies", a collection of computer‑generated cityscapes designed to test algorithms for urban planning. The creators had used random seed numbers to generate the layouts, ensuring that each city was unique and never meant to exist in reality.

I wondered whether the patterns on my bread were simply random, a product of chance, or whether some unknown variable was at work. I decided to perform a controlled experiment. I took a fresh batch of dough, mixed it in a sterile bowl, and placed it in a sealed, temperature‑controlled incubator that I borrowed from a friend who ran a lab. The incubator kept the environment at a constant 78°F, with humidity at 75 percent, and there was no window, no external light. I set a timer for twelve hours, then removed the dough and baked it in the oven, watching the crust form.

When the loaf cooled, I examined it. The crust was a uniform golden brown, the cracks random and unremarkable. No pattern, no map, nothing that matched the satellite images. I felt a pang of disappointment. The controlled environment had stripped away something essential, something that seemed to require the particular conditions of the bakery.

I returned to the shop and resumed my night‑shift routine. The next loaf I proofed overnight, I left the door cracked open, letting the cool night air drift in. The city outside was quiet, the streetlights casting long shadows. I wondered whether the ambient noise of the city, the vibrations of traffic, the distant hum of a train, might be influencing the dough. I considered the possibility that the bakery, located on a corner where two old water mains intersected, might sit atop a unique acoustic field.

The recorder captured the faint sound of the building’s HVAC system, the occasional creak of the floorboards, and the soft rustle of the cat’s fur as it moved. I also recorded the low hum of distant traffic, a rhythm that seemed to pulse in a steady beat. When I played back the recording, I noticed a faint, rhythmic pattern—almost like a low drum, a throbbing that seemed to align with the passing of trains on the line a few blocks away.

I decided to test whether the rhythm could be influencing the dough’s development. I set up a speaker in the back room, playing a simple metronome at a steady 60 beats per minute, the same tempo as the recorded hum. I placed the dough on a shelf near the speaker, and let it sit overnight while the metronome ticked. In the morning, I baked the loaf and examined the crust. The pattern that emerged was striking: a series of evenly spaced lines that ran parallel across the top of the loaf, like streets laid out in a grid. It was not an exact replica of any satellite image I had, but it suggested that the rhythmic vibration could be imprinting a structure onto the dough.

I felt a surge of excitement. If sound could affect the crust, perhaps other environmental factors—magnetic fields, subtle shifts in the earth’s electromagnetic spectrum—might be doing something similar. I began to read about “fermentation acoustics”, a niche field that explored how sound waves could influence yeast activity. The research was sparse, but a few papers suggested that certain frequencies could stimulate the production of carbon dioxide in the dough, changing the way the gas bubbles formed and rose.

I also placed a small mirror on the counter, reflecting the dim light from the streetlamp outside, hoping that the pattern of light and shadow might affect the dough’s surface. Over the next several nights, I tried different combinations of sound, light, and magnetic influence. Some loaves showed only minor variations; others displayed intricate designs that seemed to correspond with the satellite images of imagined cities.

One particular night, I felt unusually tired, my eyes heavy from the long hours. I left a batch of dough on the counter, covered with a damp cloth, and fell asleep in the back office. I awoke to the sound of my alarm clock and the faint scent of fresh bread drifting through the hallway. I walked into the kitchen, and the ovens were still warm, the light still on. I lifted the tray, and the loaf that had risen before me was unlike any I had seen. Its crust bore a pattern of concentric circles intersected by a single diagonal line, exactly matching the layout of a city called “Vespera” that I had spotted in the book. The city was described as a “mirror city”, built on an island with a central lagoon and a bridge that cut across it at a 45‑degree angle. The resemblance was too precise to ignore.

I felt a mixture of awe and fear. How could a piece of dough, a simple mixture of flour, water, yeast, and salt, develop a representation of a place that never existed? I considered the possibility that the bakery itself, its location, its history, might be a conduit for some larger force. The building had been a bakery for over a century, originally constructed in the early 1900s by an immigrant family who claimed the land was blessed by a saint. There were rumors that the foundation had been laid over an old well, a place where townsfolk once gathered for communal prayers. I wondered whether the lingering memory of those gatherings, the collective hopes and dreams of generations, might be somehow encoded in the earth beneath my feet.

He wrote that he felt compelled to bake a loaf that night and let it rest under the stars, hoping to capture the vision in the crust. The entry ended abruptly, as if he had been interrupted.

I felt a chill. The story of Mateo’s dream seemed to echo what I was experiencing. I wondered whether the act of baking—combining elements, applying heat, allowing time to transform—could be a ritual that taps into something beyond the physical.

That night, I prepared a loaf with intention. I mixed the dough slowly, feeling the weight of the flour in my hands, the cool water as it soaked in. I whispered to the dough, telling it that I wanted to see the city that lived only in imagination, that I wanted to honor the memory of those who had dreamed before me. I placed the dough on a wooden board, covered it, and left it near the window, where the moonlight filtered through the blinds, casting a pale pattern on the floor.

When the timer chimed, I removed the bread, set it on the cooling rack, and let it rest. I lifted the crust with a gentle hand and examined the surface.

A bridge spanned the lake, its arch matching the diagonal line I had seen in the imagined city of Vespera. The edges of the crust showed smaller districts, each with its own network of roads. It was as if the dough had recorded a city that existed only in the collective imagination of those who had dreamed of it.

We arranged to meet at a coffee shop near the bakery, and I brought a few of the loaves with their crusts intact, careful not to slice them.

She told me that the images of imagined cities had been generated by an algorithm that used random seed values, but that certain patterns—like the concentric rings intersected by a diagonal—appeared more often than pure chance would suggest. The yeast’s production of carbon dioxide creates bubbles that expand, interact, and merge, forming structures that could mirror the patterns of a city’s layout if the right conditions are present. The heat of the oven then solidifies these structures into the crust.
