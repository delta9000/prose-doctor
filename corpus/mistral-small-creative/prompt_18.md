**Chapter VII: The Silent Star**

The observatory at the University of Padua was a cathedral of shadows, its high ceilings swallowed by the dark, its walls lined with the ghosts of men who had spent their lives chasing the heavens. Isabella Vianello adjusted the brass eyepiece of the telescope, her breath fogging the cold metal. Outside, the winter wind howled through the narrow streets of the city, but here, in this sanctum of glass and brass, the air was still—thick with the scent of oil, parchment, and the faint, acrid tang of gunpowder from the lanterns.

She had been at it for hours. Her back ached, her fingers were numb, but she did not dare stop. Not when Jupiter’s moons danced in perfect, predictable arcs. Not when the red spot—*her* red spot—pulsed like a living thing against the planet’s striated belly. She had first glimpsed it months ago, a fleeting smudge she had dismissed as a trick of the lens. But it had returned, stubborn, insistent. A storm, perhaps. Or something else entirely.

The door creaked open.

Isabella did not turn. She knew the rhythm of his steps—the confident, measured stride of a man who had never once doubted his place in the world.

“Still at it, I see,” Professor Alessandro Moretti said, his voice smooth as polished marble. He moved to stand beside her, his shoulder brushing hers just long enough to remind her of the space she was allowed to occupy. “You’ll ruin your eyes, Isabella. Or worse, your reputation.”

She exhaled through her nose, a sound too quiet to be called a scoff. “The stars do not care for reputations, Professor.”

“No,” he agreed, leaning in to peer through the eyepiece. His cologne—bergamot and something darker, like burnt sugar—filled the space between them. “But the *Accademia dei Lincei* does. And so does the Church.”

Isabella said nothing. She had heard this before. The warnings, the veiled threats, the way his fingers would sometimes linger on her wrist when he passed her a star chart, as if testing the weight of her obedience.

Moretti straightened, his dark eyes gleaming in the lantern light. “You’ve been observing Jupiter again.”

“Yes.”

“And?”

She hesitated. The discovery burned in her chest, a secret too bright to keep. But she had learned, over the years, that some truths were currency. And Moretti was a man who knew the value of a well-timed revelation.

“There’s a storm,” she said at last. “A persistent one. Unlike anything in the records.”

Moretti’s eyebrows lifted. “A storm.”

“Not just a storm.” She turned to him, her gloved fingers tightening around the edge of the table. “It’s *moving*. Not with the planet’s rotation—*against* it. As if… as if something beneath the surface is pulling it.”

For a moment, there was only silence. Then Moretti laughed, a rich, warm sound that made her skin prickle. “Isabella, you have a poet’s imagination. The heavens are a clockwork, not a living thing.”

“Then explain it.”

He studied her, his amusement fading into something sharper. “You’re certain?”

“I’ve tracked it for three nights. The calculations are there.” She gestured to the stack of parchment beside the telescope, her neat script covering page after page of observations, measurements, the careful dance of numbers that proved what her eyes had seen.

Moretti picked up the top sheet, his gaze scanning the columns. His expression darkened. “You’ve been working on this alone.”

“You’ve been in Venice.”

“And you didn’t think to send word?”

She met his stare. “I sent word. Three times.”

A muscle twitched in his jaw. He knew as well as she did that his secretary had “misplaced” her letters. That the courier he had hired to deliver his own correspondence had been instructed to ignore hers.

“This changes nothing,” he said at last, setting the parchment down. “The discovery will be published under my name. As always.”

Isabella’s chest tightened. “This isn’t like the others. This is—”

“*Exactly* like the others.” His voice was low, dangerous. “You think the *Lincei* will credit a woman with a discovery of this magnitude? You think the College of Cardinals will fund *your* research? You are a *tutor*, Isabella. A governess with a telescope. I am the professor. The *astronomer*.”

She wanted to scream. She wanted to seize the parchment and run, to burn it all before he could claim another piece of her work as his own. But she had spent years perfecting the art of stillness, of swallowing the fire in her throat until it became a quiet, smoldering coal in her gut.

“What do you propose?” she asked, her voice steady.

Moretti smiled, slow and satisfied. “We will present it together. At the next meeting of the *Lincei*. You will assist me in the demonstration, but you will not speak. Not unless spoken to.”

“And the credit?”

“Will be mine.” He reached out, his fingers brushing a loose strand of hair from her temple. “But I will ensure you are… *compensated*.”

She flinched. Not from the touch—she had grown accustomed to those—but from the implication. The unspoken debt that would bind her tighter than any contract.

“Very well,” she said.

Moretti’s smile widened. “Good. Now, pack your things. We leave for Rome at first light.”

---

The carriage ride was a blur of jostling wheels and Moretti’s ceaseless prattle about the *Lincei*, about the cardinals, about the *opportunity* this discovery presented. Isabella stared out the window, her gloved fingers tracing the edge of the leather satchel at her feet. Inside, her notes. Her *proof*. The one thing Moretti could not take from her, no matter how many times he signed his name to her work.

Rome was a city of gold and stone, of towering spires and whispered secrets. The *Accademia dei Lincei* met in a grand salon of the Palazzo Madama, its walls lined with portraits of men who had shaped the heavens with their words. Isabella followed Moretti inside, her skirts brushing the marble floor, her heart a drumbeat in her ears.

The room fell silent as they entered.

Moretti, ever the performer, bowed with flourish. “Gentlemen, I bring you a revelation.”

Isabella kept her gaze lowered, her hands clasped before her. She was a shadow. A ghost. The woman who had spent nights bleary-eyed at the telescope, who had calculated orbits until her fingers cramped, who had *seen* what no one else had.

And yet, when Moretti gestured for her to step forward, when he placed a hand on the small of her back and guided her toward the demonstration table, she did not resist.

The telescope had been set up before a large window, its lens trained on the night sky. Jupiter hung like a jewel in the velvet dark, its moons a string of pearls beside it. Moretti adjusted the focus, his movements practiced, confident.

“Gentlemen,” he said, stepping back, “behold.”

One by one, the scholars of the *Lincei* leaned in, their murmurs rising like a tide.

“A storm,” someone said.

“A most unusual one,” another agreed.

Isabella watched, her nails digging into her palms. *Unusual.* As if that were enough. As if the way it *moved*, the way it *defied*, were not a challenge to everything they thought they knew.

Moretti cleared his throat. “This is no mere storm. It is a *phenomenon*. One that suggests the very atmosphere of Jupiter is not as static as we have assumed. That there are forces at work beneath its surface—forces that may yet rewrite our understanding of the heavens.”

A ripple of excitement passed through the room. Isabella’s breath hitched. He was *stealing* her words. Her *thoughts*. And yet, when she opened her mouth to speak, to claim what was hers, Moretti’s hand closed around her wrist, his fingers digging in just enough to silence her.

“Professor Vianello has been instrumental in this discovery,” he said, his voice smooth as silk. “Her calculations have been… *invaluable*.”

The scholars nodded, their gazes flickering over her before dismissing her entirely. She was a tool. A quill. A pair of hands to hold the parchment steady.

Isabella’s vision blurred. She blinked rapidly, willing the tears back. She would not cry. Not here. Not in front of them.

Moretti released her wrist, his touch lingering just a second too long. “I propose we name this phenomenon the *Macula Moretti*,” he said, his voice ringing with pride. “In honor of the work done here today.”

The room erupted in applause.

Isabella turned away, her fingers trembling as she reached for her satchel. She had known this would happen. She had *known*. And yet, the betrayal still cut like a knife.

She did not wait for Moretti to finish his speech. She did not wait for the congratulations, the handshakes, the promises of patronage. She slipped out of the salon, her skirts whispering against the marble, her heart a leaden weight in her chest.

The night air was cold, biting. She drew her cloak tighter around her shoulders and walked, her boots clicking against the cobblestones. She did not know where she was going. Only that she could not stay.

A hand closed around her arm.

Isabella whirled, her free hand raised to strike—

“Easy,” a voice said. A woman’s voice.

Isabella blinked. The woman before her was cloaked in shadow, her face half-hidden beneath a wide-brimmed hat. But her eyes—sharp, knowing—gleamed in the lantern light.

“You’re the astronomer,” the woman said. It was not a question.

Isabella’s pulse pounded in her throat. “Who are you?”

The woman smiled, slow and secret. “Someone who knows what it is to be unseen.” She reached into her cloak and pressed a folded parchment into Isabella’s hand. “Read this. Then come to the *Caffè Greco* at dawn. Alone.”

Before Isabella could respond, the woman melted into the night, leaving only the scent of lavender and the weight of the parchment in her palm.

Isabella unfolded it with shaking fingers.

*The heavens are not theirs to claim,* it read. *But they are yours to uncover. Come. We have work to do.*

Beneath the words, a single initial: **E.**

Isabella’s breath caught. She knew that initial. Knew the name it stood for.

*Elena.*

Elena Carducci. The woman who had published under the name *E. Carducci*, whose treatises on the comets had been whispered about in the observatories of Italy like forbidden scripture. The woman who had vanished five years ago, her fate a mystery.

Isabella’s fingers tightened around the parchment. The night air no longer felt cold. It felt *alive*.

She looked back at the Palazzo Madama, at the golden light spilling from its windows. Moretti would be inside, basking in his stolen glory. The scholars would be toasting his name, his *discovery*.

But the stars did not belong to them.

And neither, she realized, did she.

Isabella turned her back on the palace and walked into the dark, the parchment clutched in her hand like a promise.

Dawn could not come soon enough.
