**Chapter Twelve: The Shape of Absence**

The plane touches down with a shudder, wheels screaming against tarmac, and Elias Veyra grips the armrests until his knuckles bleach white. Through the window, the world is too bright, too *clean*—the terminal’s glass walls reflecting a sky unmarred by smoke, the tarmac unbroken by craters. No one is running. No one is screaming. Just the quiet hum of engines cooling, the distant chatter of passengers unbuckling, the scent of disinfectant and stale coffee.

His hands shake as he fumbles with the seatbelt. Three years. Three years of dust and blood and the acrid tang of burning rubber, of nights spent pressed against cold stone while mortars lit up the horizon like fireworks. And now this: the sterile glow of fluorescent lights, the polite murmur of announcements in a language that isn’t his own anymore.

*You’re home.*

The words feel like a lie.

---

The airport is a maze of familiar signs and unfamiliar faces. He moves through it like a ghost, his duffel bag slung over one shoulder, the weight of it nothing compared to the lead in his chest. His boots—scuffed, caked with the grime of a dozen war zones—clack against the polished floor. He catches his reflection in a shop window: hollow cheeks, a beard too wild, eyes that don’t quite focus. He looks like a man who’s been dug up.

He should call her. He *should*. But his phone is dead, the battery long since given up, and the payphone’s keypad stares at him like an accusation. What would he even say? *I’m back. I’m sorry. I don’t know how to do this anymore.*

Instead, he walks.

---

The city hits him like a physical blow. The traffic, the honking horns, the way people move with purpose, with *direction*. No one flinches at the sound of a backfiring engine. No one ducks at the shadow of a drone. The air smells like rain and exhaust, not cordite and rotting meat. He passes a café where a woman laughs, her coffee untouched, her world unshaken. He passes a newsstand where the headlines are about elections and stock markets, not massacres. He passes a child dropping an ice cream cone, wailing as if the world has ended.

Elias stops. Bends at the waist. Presses his palms into his knees.

*Breathe.*

---

The apartment building is the same. Same chipped paint on the doorframe, same flickering light in the hallway, same scent of old wood and lemon cleaner. His key still fits the lock. The door swings open with a creak, and for a second, he’s back in that bombed-out safe house in Aleppo, heart hammering, waiting for the world to explode.

But the world doesn’t explode.

The apartment is warm. Alive. There’s a plant on the windowsill—something green and thriving, its leaves dusted with sunlight. A throw blanket is draped over the couch, the one she knitted during that winter when the power went out for three days and they huddled under it, sharing body heat. The kitchen counter is cluttered: a half-empty mug, a stack of mail, a single red apple in a bowl.

And then he sees the photos.

They’re pinned to the fridge with magnets—dozens of them. Some are old: the two of them at a beach, sunburned and grinning. Him in a tux, her in a dress the color of twilight, their wedding day. But the newer ones—*those* are the ones that gut him.

There she is, standing in front of a bakery, holding up a loaf of bread like a trophy. There she is, mid-laugh, her arms around a woman he doesn’t recognize, both of them flushed with wine. There she is, curled on the couch, a book in her lap, her hair longer than he’s ever seen it, her face softer, *happier*.

And then—

A man.

Not just any man. A man with his arm slung around her shoulders, his smile easy, his hand resting just a little too comfortably on her hip. They’re at a park, the one three blocks away, the one they used to go to on Sundays. He’s tall. Broad-shouldered. *Present.*

Elias’s stomach lurches.

---

He doesn’t remember sitting down, but suddenly he’s on the couch, the duffel bag at his feet, his hands clenched between his knees. The apartment hums around him, alive in ways it wasn’t when he left. There’s a new rug under the coffee table. A different lamp on the side table. A *life*, unfolding without him.

The front door clicks.

Elias freezes.

Footsteps. The rustle of keys being dropped into a bowl. The sigh of someone kicking off shoes. And then—

“Elias?”

Her voice.

He turns.

She’s standing in the doorway, a reusable grocery bag in one hand, her keys in the other. Her hair is longer, yes, but it’s not just that. It’s the way she *stands*—shoulders back, chin up, like she’s not waiting for the world to knock her down. She’s wearing a sweater he doesn’t recognize, soft gray, the sleeves pushed up to her elbows. Her nails are painted a pale pink.

For a second, neither of them moves.

Then her bag hits the floor.

“Oh my God.”

---

She crosses the room in three strides and then she’s there, her hands on his face, her thumbs brushing his cheekbones like she’s trying to memorize him. He can smell her—lavender and something sweet, like vanilla. Her breath is warm against his skin.

“You’re here,” she whispers.

He should say something. *I’m sorry. I missed you. I don’t know how to be here.* But his throat is a desert, his tongue a dead thing.

She pulls back just enough to look at him, her eyes searching his face. “You’re *really* here.”

He nods. Swallows. “Yeah.”

She exhales, a shaky laugh escaping her. “I didn’t think—I mean, I got your last letter, but it’s been months, and then the news said—” She cuts herself off, shakes her head. “Never mind. You’re here.”

She steps back, and the absence of her touch is like a wound.

“You look—” She gestures vaguely at him. “Different.”

He knows what she means. He’s not the man who left. Not the man who promised he’d be back before the year was out. Not the man who swore he’d keep her updated, who’d call when he could, who’d *come home*.

“I should’ve—” His voice cracks. He clears his throat. “I should’ve called.”

She crosses her arms, hugging herself. “You should’ve done a lot of things.”

The words land like a punch.

He looks at the floor. “I know.”

Silence stretches between them, thick and suffocating. He can hear the tick of the clock on the wall, the distant hum of the fridge. The apartment feels too small, too *full*.

“How long are you staying?” she asks.

The question is a knife twist.

“I don’t know,” he admits. “A few days. A week. I haven’t—” He stops. *I haven’t thought past getting here.*

She nods, like she expected that. Like she’s already braced for the answer.

“You’ve been busy,” he says, nodding toward the fridge, the photos.

She follows his gaze, then looks back at him, her expression unreadable. “Yeah. I have.”

Another silence. He wants to ask about the man. *Who is he? How long? Do you love him?* But the words stick in his throat.

Instead, he says, “You’ve got a plant.”

She blinks. “What?”

“The plant.” He gestures weakly toward the windowsill. “It’s alive.”

A flicker of something—amusement? Sadness?—crosses her face. “Yeah. I watered it.”

“I didn’t think you could keep anything alive.”

She almost smiles. Almost. “People change.”

The words hang between them, heavy with meaning.

---

She makes tea. He watches her move around the kitchen, the way her hips sway slightly as she reaches for the mugs, the way her fingers tap restlessly against the counter. She’s nervous. He’s making her nervous.

“You’re not drinking it,” she says, nodding at his untouched mug.

He wraps his hands around it, letting the heat seep into his skin. “I’m sorry.”

She doesn’t ask what for. She already knows.

“You look tired,” she says.

“I am.”

“When’s the last time you slept?”

He shrugs. “A few days ago. Maybe a week.”

She exhales through her nose, a sound that’s half laugh, half sigh. “You haven’t changed.”

“Some things have.”

She meets his eyes, and for the first time, he sees it—the anger, the hurt, the *years* of it, simmering just beneath the surface. “Not enough.”

---

She shows him the bathroom. The towels are new. The soap smells like her. He stands under the scalding water until his skin turns red, until the grime of three years washes down the drain in gray swirls. He scrubs until he bleeds, until the water runs clear.

When he steps out, the mirror is fogged. He wipes a hand across it, and the face that stares back is a stranger’s—gaunt, exhausted, eyes too bright, too *aware*. He presses his palm against the glass, as if he can push the reflection away.

---

She’s in the bedroom when he emerges, folding laundry. The bed is made, the sheets crisp and white. His old side is untouched, the pillow still dented from the last time he slept there.

“You kept my side,” he says.

She doesn’t look at him. “I didn’t know what else to do with it.”

He sits on the edge of the bed, the mattress dipping under his weight. “I should’ve written more.”

She folds a shirt, her movements precise, controlled. “You should’ve come home.”

“I couldn’t.”

“You *could*.” Her voice is sharp now, the anger finally breaking through. “You *chose* not to.”

He flinches. “It wasn’t that simple.”

“It *was*.” She turns to him, her eyes bright with unshed tears. “You had a choice, Elias. Every single day, you had a choice. And you *chose* to stay there. You *chose* to let me sit here, wondering if you were alive, if you were hurt, if you were ever coming back. You *chose* that.”

He wants to argue. He wants to tell her about the stories that needed telling, the people who needed voices, the *weight* of it all. But the words die in his throat because he knows she’s right.

“I’m sorry,” he says again, because it’s all he has.

She wipes at her eyes, her jaw set. “Sorry doesn’t fix it.”

“I know.”

She turns back to the laundry, her shoulders tense. “You can sleep here tonight. But tomorrow—” She swallows. “Tomorrow, you need to figure out what you’re doing. Because I can’t—” Her voice breaks. “I can’t do this again.”

---

He lies awake long after she’s fallen asleep, her back to him, her breathing slow and even. The room is too quiet. Too *safe*. He keeps waiting for the sound of gunfire, the distant wail of sirens, the shudder of an explosion. But there’s nothing. Just the hum of the city outside, the occasional car passing, the distant bark of a dog.

He reaches out, his fingers hovering just above her shoulder. He wants to touch her. He wants to pull her against him, to bury his face in her hair, to *breathe* her in. But he doesn’t. Because he doesn’t deserve to.

Instead, he stares at the ceiling and listens to the sound of her breathing, and he wonders how a man can be so close to home and still feel like he’s a million miles away.
