**Chapter Seven: The Weight of Unwritten Time**

The manuscript lay open on Elias Varga’s desk, its brittle pages yellowed like old teeth. The script—angular, precise, the letters carved into the vellum as if by a blade rather than a pen—had no name, no title. Only a date, scrawled in the margin of the first folio: *Anno Domini 1478*. But Elias knew better. The ink was wrong. The paper was wrong. The language itself was wrong, or rather, *too right*, a dialect of Old Church Slavonic so archaic it made his teeth ache just to parse it. No one had spoken it in five hundred years. No one had *written* it in five hundred years.

And yet, here it was.

He adjusted the lamp, the warm glow pooling over the desk, and leaned in. The words resisted him at first, as they always did. Dead languages were like that—stubborn, unyielding, their meanings locked behind centuries of silence. But Elias had spent his life prying them open. He traced the lines with his finger, murmuring the syllables under his breath, letting the rhythm of the syntax unfold in his mind.

*"And lo, the sky shall rend asunder, not with fire, but with a silence so vast it shall drown the cries of the faithful. The rivers shall run backward, and the stones of the earth shall remember their names."*

Elias exhaled slowly. Prophecy. Of course. The manuscript was riddled with it—apocalyptic visions, warnings of a world unraveling at the seams. He had seen variations of this before, in the margins of medieval chronicles, in the fevered scribblings of monks who saw the end times in every comet, every plague. But this was different. The language was too *clean*, too *intentional*. There were no smudges, no corrections, no signs of haste. Whoever had written this had known exactly what they were doing.

He turned the page.

*"The first shall be the city of bridges, where the water turns to glass and the dead walk among the living. The second shall be the tower of iron, where the voices of the unborn scream in the wires. The third shall be the garden of mirrors, where no reflection is true, and the hands that reach for you are not your own."*

Elias sat back, rubbing his temples. The descriptions were too specific. Too *modern*. A city of bridges—Venice? But the water turning to glass? That wasn’t Venice. That wasn’t *anywhere*. And the tower of iron, the voices in the wires—was that a radio tower? A cell network? The words were ancient, but the imagery was not.

He reached for his notebook, flipping to a fresh page. He had been working on this for weeks, translating in fits and starts, always pulling back when the text veered too far into the uncanny. But now, he forced himself to keep going.

*"And when the third sign comes, the keepers of the old words shall look upon their work and know fear, for the tongue they have spent their lives reviving shall be the tongue that names the end."*

Elias’s pen stilled. His breath hitched.

*Keepers of the old words.*

That was him. That was *exactly* him.

He set the pen down carefully, as if it might shatter. The room felt too small, the air too thick. He stood, pacing the length of his study, his bare feet silent on the worn rug. The manuscript had been sent to him anonymously, slipped into his mailbox at the university with no return address, no note. He had assumed it was a hoax, some graduate student’s elaborate prank. But the language was real. The script was real. And now, the words were speaking *to him*.

He stopped in front of the window, pressing his forehead against the cool glass. Outside, the city hummed with life—cars honking, voices drifting up from the street, the distant wail of a siren. None of it felt real anymore. Not after this.

He turned back to the desk, to the manuscript, to the words that had just called him by name.

---

The café was loud, the kind of place where the clatter of dishes and the murmur of conversation drowned out thought. Elias sat across from Dr. Lina Kovacs, his colleague in the department of dead languages, her dark eyes sharp behind her wire-rimmed glasses. She had been his mentor once, back when he was still a student, back when he believed that languages were just puzzles to be solved, not warnings to be heeded.

"You’re telling me," Lina said, stirring her coffee with deliberate slowness, "that this manuscript is predicting the future."

Elias didn’t flinch. "I’m telling you that the descriptions match things that haven’t happened yet. The language is fifteenth century, but the *content* is—"

"Convenient?" Lina cut in. "Elias, you’re a scholar. You know better than to let a text dictate your reality. If this were a first-year student’s paper, you’d fail them for this kind of thinking."

Elias clenched his jaw. "I’m not saying it’s *literally* predicting the future. I’m saying the parallels are too exact to ignore. The city of bridges—what if it’s Venice? The water turning to glass—what if it’s a reference to the 2003 heatwave, when the canals dried up and the fish died? The tower of iron—what if it’s the Eiffel Tower, or the Empire State Building? The voices in the wires—what if it’s the internet?"

Lina set her spoon down with a clink. "Or what if it’s a coincidence? What if some medieval monk had a vivid imagination and you’re reading too much into it?"

Elias leaned forward. "The third sign. The garden of mirrors. Where no reflection is true. Lina, what if it’s a reference to social media? To deepfakes? To the way we can’t trust what we see anymore?"

Lina sighed, pinching the bridge of her nose. "Elias, you’re exhausted. You’ve been locked in that office for weeks, staring at a dead language until it starts talking back to you. That’s not scholarship. That’s obsession."

Elias opened his mouth to argue, but the words died on his tongue. Maybe she was right. Maybe he *was* exhausted. Maybe the manuscript was just a curiosity, a fascinating artifact of a bygone era, and he was the fool who had let it crawl under his skin.

But then he remembered the last line he had translated before leaving his office.

*"And the keeper of the old words shall see the signs and know that the end is not a day, but a slow unraveling, and that he has been chosen to bear witness."*

He had been chosen.

The thought settled over him like a shroud.

---

That night, Elias dreamed of the manuscript.

In the dream, he was not in his study, but in a vast, empty library, the shelves stretching endlessly into the dark. The manuscript lay open on a lectern before him, its pages glowing faintly, as if lit from within. He reached out to turn the page, but his fingers passed through it, as insubstantial as smoke.

A voice spoke behind him.

*"You have been reading the wrong way."*

Elias turned. The library was empty. The voice had come from nowhere and everywhere at once.

*"The words are not a warning,"* the voice said. *"They are an invitation."*

Elias woke with a gasp, his sheets tangled around his legs, his heart hammering. The dream clung to him, vivid and unshakable. He swung his legs over the side of the bed, his bare feet hitting the cold floor. The clock on his nightstand read 3:17 AM.

He stood, his body moving before his mind could catch up. He needed to see it again. He needed to *understand*.

The manuscript was still on his desk, exactly where he had left it. He turned on the lamp, the sudden brightness making him squint. He sat down, his fingers trembling as he turned to the last page he had translated.

And then he saw it.

The script was different.

Not just the words—the *shape* of them. The letters were no longer the precise, angular marks of Old Church Slavonic. They had softened, rounded, as if written by a different hand. And the language—

Elias’s breath caught.

It wasn’t Slavonic anymore.

It was *English*.

His English.

His handwriting.

The page read:

*"Elias, you have been chosen to bear witness. The signs are already here. The city of bridges is drowning. The tower of iron is screaming. The garden of mirrors is waiting. Turn the page."*

His hands shook as he reached for the next folio. The paper was new, fresh, the ink still damp. It hadn’t been there before. He *knew* it hadn’t been there.

He turned the page.

And the world fell away.

---

The words on the page were no longer in any language he recognized. They were symbols, shifting and writhing like living things, rearranging themselves before his eyes. He tried to look away, but he couldn’t. His vision tunneled, the edges of the room dissolving into blackness until all that remained was the manuscript, the symbols, the *truth* of them.

And then he *saw*.

He saw the city of bridges—Venice, yes, but not as it was now. The canals were frozen, the water solid and clear as glass, the fish trapped inside, their mouths open in silent screams. The people walked above them, their faces blank, their eyes empty. And then the ground trembled, and the bridges collapsed, not into the water, but *through* it, as if the earth itself had become porous, swallowing them whole.

He saw the tower of iron—a skyscraper, its windows shattered, its steel beams groaning as something *inside* it clawed its way out. The air hummed with voices, not human, but something else, something that had never been human, screaming in a language that made his bones ache.

He saw the garden of mirrors—a vast, endless hall of reflections, but none of them were true. He saw himself in one, but it wasn’t *him*. It was a version of him, older, hollow-eyed, his mouth sewn shut. And then the reflection reached out, its fingers pressing against the glass, and the glass *melted*, and the thing that was not him stepped through.

And then he saw *himself*, sitting at his desk, the manuscript open before him, his face illuminated by the lamp, his eyes wide with terror.

The vision snapped shut.

Elias gasped, his chest heaving as if he had been holding his breath for hours. The manuscript was still there, the symbols still shifting, but now they were settling, reforming into words he could read.

*"You have seen. Now you must choose. Will you bear witness, or will you turn away?"*

Elias’s hands hovered over the page. He could close the manuscript. He could burn it. He could walk away and never look back.

But he knew, with a certainty that settled into his bones, that it was already too late.

He had been chosen.

And the end was coming.
