**Chapter Seven: The Loaf That Remembers**

The oven hums like a sleeping beast, its breath warm and yeast-scented, curling through the bakery in slow, golden tendrils. Elias kneads the dough with the rhythm of a man who has done this a thousand times—press, fold, turn—his fingers sinking into the yielding flesh of the flour and water, coaxing it into submission. The shop is quiet this early, the way he likes it. Outside, the street is still half-asleep, the cobblestones damp with morning mist, the air thick with the promise of rain. Inside, the counters gleam under the dim glow of the brass lamps, their light catching the dust motes that drift like slow-falling stars.

Elias works alone. He always has.

The dough rises in its proofing basket, a living thing, swelling with secrets. He knows better than to rush it. Bread, like memory, cannot be forced. It must come in its own time, or not at all.

By six, the first customers trickle in—old Mrs. Hargrove with her cane tapping against the floorboards, the miller’s boy with his cap pulled low, the widow from the end of the lane who always buys two loaves and never says why. Elias hands them their bread with a nod, his hands flour-dusted, his apron streaked with the ghosts of yesterday’s batches. They take their loaves and leave, and he watches them go, wondering, as he always does, what the bread will show them.

Because that is the thing about Elias’s bread.

It remembers.

Not the way a man remembers—the way a loaf *remembers*. The way dough holds the imprint of a baker’s hands long after the shaping is done. The way a crust carries the echo of the oven’s heat, the way the crumb remembers the steam that made it light. Elias’s bread does not lie. It does not soften the edges or blur the lines. It pulls the truth from the eater like a thread from a sweater, unraveling them until they are left staring at their own past, raw and steaming on their tongue.

He does not know why it happens. He only knows that it does.

---

The bell above the door chimes, and Elias looks up.

A woman stands in the doorway, her coat damp at the shoulders, her dark hair tucked behind her ears. She is familiar in the way a half-remembered dream is familiar—there, but not quite there. Her eyes meet his, and for a moment, the world tilts.

He knows her.

But he shouldn’t.

“Good morning,” she says, her voice low, like the murmur of a river over stones. “Do you still make the sourdough?”

Elias’s hands still in the dough. The shop suddenly feels too small, the air too thick. “Yes,” he says, because it is the only word he can manage.

She steps inside, and the door swings shut behind her. The scent of rain follows her, sharp and clean, cutting through the warm, buttery smell of the bakery. She moves to the counter, her fingers tracing the edge of the display case, leaving no prints in the glass.

“One loaf,” she says. “Please.”

Elias reaches for the sourdough, his movements slow, deliberate. The crust is dark, almost black in places, cracked like the surface of a dried riverbed. He wraps it in paper, his fingers brushing against hers as he hands it to her. A spark. A memory.

*Her hands in his. A field of wildflowers. Laughter.*

He jerks back as if burned.

She does not react. She only smiles, a sad, knowing thing, and presses a coin into his palm. “Thank you, Elias.”

His name on her lips is a key turning in a lock.

He watches her leave, the bell chiming softly in her wake. The shop is empty again, but the air hums with something new, something electric. His hands tremble.

---

He should not remember her.

He *does* not remember her.

And yet.

---

The rest of the morning passes in a blur. Customers come and go, their voices a distant murmur, their faces a smear of colors and shapes. Elias moves through the motions—kneading, shaping, baking—but his mind is elsewhere, tangled in the threads of a past that should not exist.

*Her name. What was her name?*

He cannot recall.

But the bread remembers.

---

By noon, the shop is empty. Elias leans against the counter, his arms crossed, his gaze fixed on the door. He should close early. He should go home, lock the door, pretend this morning never happened. But he does not move.

The bell chimes.

She is back.

Her coat is dry now, her hair slightly tousled, as if she has been walking in the wind. She holds the loaf in her hands, unwrapped, the crust glistening with a fine sheen of oil. She does not speak. She only looks at him, her eyes dark and endless, like the sky before a storm.

“You ate it,” Elias says. It is not a question.

She nods.

A beat of silence. The oven ticks as it cools.

“What did it show you?” he asks, though he already knows.

She sets the loaf on the counter between them. It is half-eaten, the crumb exposed, soft and pale. She reaches out, her fingers hovering over the bread, as if afraid to touch it.

“A house by the sea,” she says. “White walls. Blue shutters. A garden full of lavender.”

Elias’s breath catches.

“You were there,” she continues. “We were both there. You were younger. So was I.” Her voice is steady, but her hands are not. They tremble, just slightly, like leaves in a breeze. “We were happy.”

Elias swallows. His throat is dry. “That’s impossible.”

“Is it?” She looks up at him, her eyes bright with something like hope, something like fear. “Elias, I remember you. I remember *us*. But you don’t remember me at all, do you?”

He wants to lie. He wants to tell her she is mistaken, that she has the wrong man, the wrong bakery, the wrong life. But the words die on his tongue.

Because he *does* remember.

Not in the way she does—not in the way the bread remembers. But in fragments. In flashes. A hand in his. A laugh like wind chimes. The salt taste of the sea on his lips.

And then—nothing.

As if someone had taken a knife to the fabric of his memory and cut her out.

“Who are you?” he whispers.

She reaches into her coat and pulls out a small, worn photograph. She slides it across the counter to him.

Elias picks it up.

It is a picture of two people standing on a cliff, the sea stretching out behind them, endless and blue. The man is him—younger, yes, but unmistakably him. The woman is her, her hair longer, her smile brighter, her arm linked through his.

On the back, in faded ink, are the words: *Elias and Mara, Summer 1998.*

Mara.

Her name is Mara.

And he *knows* her.

---

The photograph trembles in his grip. “This isn’t possible,” he says, but his voice is weak, uncertain. “I would remember. I *should* remember.”

Mara’s fingers curl around the edge of the counter. “Something happened,” she says. “Something took you from me. From *us*.” She gestures to the loaf between them. “The bread showed me. It showed me everything.”

Elias’s stomach twists. He does not want to know. He does not want to see.

But he already has.

Because the bread has shown *him* things, too. Not in the way it shows others—not in the way it pulls memories from the eater like a fisherman hauling in a net. No, for Elias, the bread is different. It is a whisper. A hint. A shadow at the edge of his vision.

He has seen flashes of her before. In the steam rising from a fresh batch. In the way the dough sometimes resists him, as if it knows something he does not. In the dreams that haunt him, where he wakes with the taste of salt on his tongue and the echo of a woman’s laugh in his ears.

He has always told himself it was nothing. A trick of the mind. The loneliness of a man who bakes alone.

But it was never nothing.

---

Mara reaches for the loaf. She breaks off a piece, the crust snapping like a twig. She holds it out to him.

“Eat,” she says.

Elias hesitates. He has never eaten his own bread. He has never dared.

“Please,” she whispers.

He takes the piece from her. It is warm, almost hot, the crumb soft and yielding. He brings it to his lips.

The moment it touches his tongue, the world dissolves.

---

He is standing on the cliff again, the wind whipping at his hair, the salt spray stinging his face. Mara is beside him, her hand in his, her laughter bright and wild. The sea stretches out before them, endless and blue, the sun dipping low on the horizon, painting the sky in hues of gold and pink.

*You’ll never leave me, will you?* she says, her voice teasing, her eyes shining.

*Never,* he promises.

And then—

A shadow.

A figure standing at the edge of the garden, watching them. Tall. Pale. Its eyes like black holes, its mouth a thin, cruel line.

Elias does not recognize it. But he *feels* it.

A wrongness. A hunger.

Mara does not see it. She is still laughing, still holding his hand, still looking out at the sea.

The figure steps forward.

And then—

---

Elias gasps, the vision shattering like glass. He is back in the bakery, his hands gripping the counter, his breath coming in ragged bursts. Mara is watching him, her face pale, her eyes wide.

“You saw it,” she says. It is not a question.

Elias nods, his throat tight. “What was that?”

Mara’s voice is barely a whisper. “The thing that took you from me.”

A silence falls between them, heavy and suffocating. The oven ticks. The bread cools.

Elias looks at the photograph again. At the two people standing on the cliff, unaware of the shadow creeping up behind them.

“What do we do?” he asks.

Mara reaches across the counter and takes his hand. Her fingers are cold, but her grip is strong.

“We remember,” she says. “And then we fight.”
