**Chapter Seven: The Weight of What Remains**

---

The rain had started just after dawn, a fine, relentless mist that clung to the collar of Elias Varga’s black overcoat as he stood beneath the awning of the funeral home. He had arrived early—not out of reverence, but because he couldn’t bear the thought of being late, of walking in while the room was already full, of eyes turning to mark his presence like a stain. The awning dripped steadily, each drop hitting the pavement with a sound like a metronome counting down to something inevitable. He lit a cigarette, the flare of the lighter brief and useless against the gray morning. The smoke curled into the damp air, dissipating before it could rise.

Inside, the funeral home smelled of lilies and lemon-scented cleaner, a cloying sweetness that made his stomach clench. The casket was closed—*thank God*—its polished mahogany surface reflecting the dim overhead lights. A framed photograph of his father sat atop it, taken years ago, when Daniel Varga’s hair had still been more black than silver, when his smile hadn’t yet been worn thin by disappointment. Elias exhaled through his nose, the smoke stinging his eyes. He hadn’t seen his father in person in nearly a decade. The last time had been in a hospital room, the air thick with the antiseptic tang of illness, his father’s voice a rasp as he’d said, *“You always did have a talent for showing up just in time to leave.”* Elias had left that night. He hadn’t come back.

A hand settled on his shoulder.

He stiffened, the cigarette burning too close to his fingers. He didn’t turn. He didn’t need to.

“You’re here,” his sister said.

Her voice was different. Deeper, rougher, as if she’d spent years shouting into the wind. Elias dropped the cigarette, grinding it beneath his heel. “So are you.”

Mira Varga stepped around him, her heels clicking against the tile. She was taller than he remembered, her dark hair pulled into a severe knot at the nape of her neck, her black dress severe and expensive-looking, the kind of thing that didn’t wrinkle, didn’t smudge, didn’t *breathe*. Her eyes—same shape as his, same unnerving green—flicked over him, taking in the scuffed boots, the ill-fitting suit jacket he’d bought off a rack at a thrift store in Portland, the stubble he hadn’t bothered to shave. Her mouth thinned.

“You look like hell,” she said.

“And you look like you’re about to inherit a fortune,” he shot back.

A muscle twitched in her jaw. For a second, he thought she might slap him. Instead, she turned away, her gaze landing on the casket. “He’s not in there, you know. Not really. Just… what’s left.”

Elias said nothing. He knew. He’d seen enough death to understand that the body was just a husk, a discarded skin. But that didn’t make this easier.

---

Mira had rehearsed this moment a hundred times in her head. She’d imagined what she would say to him, how she would hold herself—cool, composed, untouchable. She’d imagined the words like weapons, sharp and precise, designed to wound just enough to make him flinch. But now, standing beside him, the air thick with the scent of flowers and old wood, she found her throat tight, her hands clenched into fists at her sides.

She hadn’t seen Elias in eight years. Not since the night he’d packed a duffel bag and walked out of their father’s house without a backward glance. She’d been twenty-two, fresh out of college, already working at the firm, already climbing. He’d been twenty-four, restless, angry, a storm waiting to break. She’d begged him to stay. He’d laughed in her face.

*“You really think this is a life, Mira?”* he’d said, gesturing at the house, the neighborhood, the carefully curated existence their father had built. *“You think any of this is real?”*

She’d slapped him then. The crack of her palm against his cheek had echoed through the hallway. He’d touched his face, his eyes dark with something she hadn’t recognized as grief until much later. Then he’d walked out the door.

And now here he was, smelling of cigarette smoke and cheap cologne, his hands shoved into his pockets like a teenager caught skipping class. She wanted to hate him. She *did* hate him. But the sight of him, so familiar and yet so foreign, made her chest ache.

She turned away, focusing on the casket. The photograph. Their father, smiling. She remembered that day—the picnic in the park, the way the light had slanted through the trees, the way her father had laughed when Elias had tripped over a root and sent a plate of sandwiches flying. She’d been twelve. Elias had been fourteen. They’d still been a family then, or something close to it.

“Do you remember the picnic?” she asked, her voice quieter than she intended.

Elias was silent for a long moment. Then, gruffly: “Yeah.”

She didn’t look at him. “He kept that photo on his desk for years.”

“He kept a lot of things.”

The bitterness in his voice was a live wire, sparking between them. Mira exhaled sharply. “You could’ve come back, you know. At any time. He would’ve—”

“He would’ve what, Mira?” Elias’s voice was low, dangerous. “Forgiven me? Taken me back like nothing happened? You really think that’s how it works?”

She whirled on him. “I think he was your *father*, and he loved you, and you *left*.”

Elias’s face darkened. “You don’t know anything about it.”

“I know you walked away and never looked back.”

“And you stayed,” he snapped. “You stayed and played the perfect daughter, the golden child, the one who never disappointed him. Congratulations, Mira. You won.”

The words hit her like a physical blow. She staggered back a step, her breath coming fast. For a second, she was sixteen again, standing in the kitchen while her father’s voice boomed through the house, *“Where is your brother? Where the hell is Elias?”* and she was lying, *“I don’t know,”* because she *did* know—he was out back, smoking behind the garage, or down by the river, or God knew where, and she was the one who had to cover for him, who had to smooth things over, who had to be *good* because someone had to be.

She swallowed hard. “You don’t get to do that,” she said, her voice trembling. “You don’t get to show up here and act like you’re the only one who suffered.”

Elias’s expression flickered, something raw and vulnerable flashing across his face before he shut it down. “I never said I was.”

The silence between them was a chasm, wide and deep and impossible to cross. Mira turned away, her fingers digging into her palms. She could feel the weight of the room pressing in on her—the murmured conversations of distant relatives, the soft sobs of her father’s old colleagues, the way the light slanted through the windows, turning everything to gold and shadow.

She had spent years perfecting the art of control. She had built walls around herself, brick by brick, until nothing could touch her. But standing here, with her brother’s presence like a ghost at her side, she felt those walls trembling.

---

Elias watched as Mira’s shoulders tensed, her back rigid as she stared at the casket. He wanted to say something—*anything*—to bridge the gap between them, but the words stuck in his throat like shards of glass. He had spent so long running, so long telling himself he didn’t care, that he didn’t need them, that the sight of her now, so close and yet so far, made his chest ache.

He remembered the last time he’d seen her before today. It had been at their mother’s funeral. Mira had been nineteen, her face pale and drawn, her hands clutching a crumpled tissue. She’d looked at him then with something like hope, like she’d believed, even then, that they could still be a family. He’d walked away from her that day, too.

He lit another cigarette, his hands shaking. The funeral director, a gaunt man with a somber expression, approached them. “Ms. Varga, Mr. Varga,” he said, his voice a low murmur. “We’re about to begin. If you’d like to take your seats…”

Mira nodded, her movements stiff. Elias followed her into the chapel, the weight of the moment pressing down on him. The room was filled with people—faces he recognized, faces he didn’t. His father’s colleagues, his old friends, distant relatives he hadn’t seen in years. They all turned to look as he and Mira walked down the aisle, their gazes heavy with curiosity, with judgment, with pity.

Elias kept his eyes forward, his jaw clenched. He didn’t want their sympathy. He didn’t want their whispers. He just wanted this to be over.

---

Mira slid into the front pew, her fingers gripping the edge of the wooden seat. She could feel Elias beside her, his presence like a live wire, crackling with tension. She didn’t look at him. She couldn’t.

The minister began to speak, his voice a low, soothing drone. *“We are gathered here today to celebrate the life of Daniel Varga…”*

Mira barely heard him. Her mind was a whirlwind of memories—her father’s laughter, his rare moments of tenderness, the way he’d ruffled her hair when she’d brought home her first report card, all A’s. *“That’s my girl,”* he’d said, his pride palpable. She’d basked in it, in the warmth of his approval, in the knowledge that she was *good*, that she was *enough*.

But then there had been Elias, always Elias, the storm that had threatened to tear their family apart. She remembered the fights, the slamming doors, the way her father’s face had darkened whenever Elias’s name was mentioned. *“That boy has no discipline,”* he’d say, his voice tight with frustration. *“No respect.”* And Mira had nodded, because she’d wanted to be the one who *did* have discipline, who *did* have respect. She’d wanted to be the one who stayed.

The minister’s voice faded into the background as Mira’s thoughts spiraled. She had spent her life trying to live up to her father’s expectations, trying to be the daughter he deserved. But now, sitting here in this cold, sterile chapel, she wondered if she’d ever really known him at all.

She glanced at Elias, his profile sharp against the dim light. He was staring straight ahead, his expression unreadable. She wanted to reach out, to touch his arm, to say something—*anything*—to bridge the gap between them. But the words wouldn’t come.

---

Elias listened to the minister’s words, but they washed over him like water, meaningless and fleeting. He didn’t need some stranger to tell him who his father had been. He knew. He knew the man who had worked himself to the bone, who had expected perfection, who had loved his children in the only way he’d known how—through discipline, through expectation, through the unspoken demand that they be *better*.

He remembered the last conversation he’d had with his father, the one that had driven him away for good. It had been in this very house, in the study, the air thick with the scent of leather and old books. His father had been sitting behind his desk, his hands steepled, his expression stern.

*“You’re wasting your life,”* he’d said, his voice calm, measured. *“You have potential, Elias. But you’re throwing it away.”*

Elias had laughed, a sharp, bitter sound. *“Potential for what? To be just like you? To spend my life chasing money and respect while my family falls apart around me?”*

His father’s eyes had darkened. *“You don’t know what you’re talking about.”*

*“Don’t I?”* Elias had stepped forward, his hands clenched into fists. *“You think I don’t see it? You think I don’t know what this family is really like? You’re so busy being the perfect father, the perfect provider, that you don’t even *see* us.”*

His father had stood then, his chair scraping against the floor. *“Get out.”*

Elias had left. And he hadn’t come back.

Now, sitting in this chapel, he felt the weight of that moment pressing down on him. He had spent years running, years telling himself he didn’t need them, that he didn’t need *this*. But the truth was, he had missed them. He had missed his father’s rare smiles, his sister’s laughter, the way their family had once been, before everything had fallen apart.

He glanced at Mira, her profile sharp in the dim light. She was staring straight ahead, her expression unreadable. He wanted to say something to her, to tell her that he was sorry, that he missed her, that he didn’t know how to fix this. But the words stuck in his throat.

---

The service ended with a hymn, the notes swelling through the chapel like a sigh. Mira stood, her legs unsteady, as the mourners began to file out. She could feel Elias beside her, his presence like a shadow she couldn’t shake. She wanted to say something to him, to break the silence that had settled between them like a wall. But the words wouldn’t come.

Outside, the rain had stopped, leaving the air damp and heavy. The mourners gathered in small clusters, their voices low, their expressions somber. Mira stood beneath the awning, her arms wrapped around herself, her gaze fixed on the distant horizon.

Elias stepped beside her, his hands shoved into his pockets. “You going to the cemetery?” he asked, his voice gruff.

Mira hesitated. She had planned to go. She had planned to stand beside the grave, to watch as her father’s casket was lowered into the ground, to say her goodbyes. But now, with Elias standing beside her, his presence a reminder of everything that had been broken between them, she wasn’t sure she could do it.

“I don’t know,” she said quietly.

Elias was silent for a long moment. Then, softly: “I’m going.”

Mira looked at him, really looked at him, for the first time in years. His eyes were the same green as hers, but darker, shadowed with something she couldn’t name. He looked tired. He looked lost.

She swallowed hard. “Okay,” she said. “I’ll come.”

---

The cemetery was quiet, the only sound the crunch of gravel beneath their feet as they walked toward the open grave. The mourners had already gathered, their black umbrellas a sea of dark blooms against the gray sky. Mira stood beside Elias, her hands clenched at her sides, her breath coming in short, sharp gasps.

The minister began to speak, his voice a low murmur against the wind. *“Ashes to ashes, dust to dust…”*

Mira felt her chest tighten, her vision blurring. She had spent so long being strong, so long holding everything together. But now, standing here beside her brother, watching as her father’s casket was lowered into the ground, she felt her control slipping.

She reached out, her fingers brushing against Elias’s. He stiffened, but he didn’t pull away. Instead, he turned his hand, his fingers intertwining with hers, his grip tight, desperate.

For a second, the world narrowed to that single point of contact—the warmth of his skin, the steady pressure of his hand. It wasn’t enough to fix everything. It wasn’t enough to bridge the years of silence, of anger, of grief. But it was something. It was a start.

Mira squeezed his hand, her throat tight. Elias squeezed back.

And for the first time in years, they stood together.
