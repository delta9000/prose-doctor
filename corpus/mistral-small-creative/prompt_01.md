**Chapter Seven: The Weight of Paper**

The house smelled of dust and lavender, the same scent that had clung to her mother’s skin in the last years, when the cancer had hollowed her out but left her hands still capable of pressing sachets between the folds of sweaters, tucking them into drawers like small, fragrant secrets. Eleanor stood in the foyer, her fingers brushing the peeling wallpaper—tiny blue forget-me-nots on a cream background, the same pattern that had been there when she was a child, though now the edges curled like old photographs. The floorboards groaned under her weight, a sound so familiar it might have been her own voice.

She had not expected to come back. Not like this.

The lawyer’s call had been brief, efficient. *"Your mother’s estate is settled, but there are personal effects. You’ll need to decide what to do with the house."* Eleanor had flown in from Portland with a single suitcase, her life reduced to the essentials: a toothbrush, a notebook, the clothes she’d worn for the funeral. She had not packed for grief, only for the practicalities of closure.

Now, standing in the hallway, she realized how little she had prepared for the way the house would pull at her, not with nostalgia, but with the insistent, almost physical drag of memory. The banister, worn smooth by decades of small hands—hers, her brother’s—still bore the faint grooves where she had carved her initials at twelve, a rebellion that had earned her a week of silence from her mother. The living room stretched before her, the same overstuffed sofa where she had curled as a girl, reading under the lamp while her father dozed in his armchair, the newspaper slipping from his fingers. The armchair was still there, its upholstery faded to the color of weak tea, the stuffing spilling from a tear in the seam. She reached out, her thumb pressing into the fabric, and for a moment, she could almost feel the ghost of his presence, the way he had always smelled of pipe tobacco and the faint metallic tang of the factory where he’d worked.

Eleanor exhaled and turned toward the stairs.

The second floor was worse. The hallway stretched like a throat, the doors to the bedrooms standing slightly ajar, as if the house itself had been holding its breath. Her old room was at the end, the door closed. She hesitated, then pushed it open.

The air inside was stale, thick with the scent of old wood and the faintest trace of the perfume she’d worn in high school—something sweet and cheap, bought with babysitting money. The bed was made, the quilt her grandmother had stitched pulled taut over the mattress. On the dresser, a brush still held a few strands of her hair, pale and brittle. She picked it up, running her fingers over the bristles, and for a second, she was seventeen again, standing in this same spot, her mother in the doorway, saying, *"You’ll be late for your shift if you don’t leave now."*

She set the brush down.

The closet door was slightly ajar. She pulled it open, half-expecting to find her old clothes still hanging there, the dresses she’d outgrown, the jeans with the frayed hems. But the rod was bare except for a single wire hanger, swaying slightly as if disturbed by a draft. On the shelf above, a shoebox sat, its lid askew. She reached for it, her pulse quickening.

Inside were letters.

Not the kind she had expected—no birthday cards, no holiday notes. These were yellowed with age, the envelopes brittle, the handwriting unfamiliar. She pulled one out, her fingers trembling as she unfolded the paper.

*"My dearest Margaret,"* it began.

Eleanor’s breath caught.

Her mother’s name had been Margaret, but no one had ever called her that. To Eleanor, she had always been *Mom*, or, in the rare moments of frustration, *Mother*. The handwriting was slanted, precise, the ink faded but still legible. She read the first line again, then the second, her eyes scanning the words as if they might rearrange themselves into something less devastating.

*"I miss you more than I can say. The days here are long, and the nights longer still. I close my eyes and see you standing in the kitchen, your hair caught in the sunlight, and I wonder how I ever let you go."*

Eleanor’s stomach twisted. She set the letter down, her hands shaking, and reached for another.

*"They say time heals all wounds, but I don’t believe it. Some wounds are too deep, too deliberate. I think of you every morning when I wake, and every night when I try to sleep. Do you ever think of me?"*

She didn’t recognize the name signed at the bottom—*Daniel*—but the date at the top of the letter was from 1978, the year before she was born.

Eleanor sank onto the edge of the bed, the mattress dipping under her weight. The letters were stacked neatly, tied with a faded ribbon. She counted them—twelve in all. Twelve letters, written over the course of a year, each one more intimate than the last. The words blurred as she read, her vision swimming.

*"I dreamt of you last night. We were back in the orchard, the way we used to be. You were laughing, and the sound of it was like music. I woke up reaching for you, and the bed was empty."*

Her mother had never mentioned an orchard. She had never mentioned *Daniel*.

Eleanor’s hands clenched around the paper. She thought of her father, of the way he had always been there—steady, quiet, his presence a constant in the house. He had worked long hours at the factory, coming home with grease under his nails, his shoulders slumped with exhaustion. He had never been a man of many words, but he had been *hers*, or so she had thought. The letters suggested otherwise.

She stood abruptly, the shoebox tumbling from her lap. The letters scattered across the floor, their edges curling like fallen leaves. She knelt, gathering them, her fingers brushing against the worn wood of the floorboards. Beneath the bed, something caught her eye—a small, leather-bound journal, its spine cracked with age.

She pulled it out.

The pages were filled with her mother’s handwriting, the same careful script she remembered from grocery lists and birthday cards. But these were not lists. These were entries, dated, meticulous. The first one was from 1977.

*"Met Daniel today. He came into the library, asked for a book on botany. I showed him where it was, and he smiled at me. I don’t know why, but I couldn’t stop thinking about him after he left."*

Eleanor’s throat tightened.

She flipped through the pages, her eyes skimming the words. *"He took me to the orchard yesterday. The apples were just starting to ripen. He said he wanted to show me something beautiful."* *"I told him about the baby. He held my hand and said we would figure it out."* *"Henry knows. I don’t know how, but he does. He hasn’t spoken to me in three days."*

Henry. Her father.

Eleanor’s breath came in short, sharp gasps. She pressed a hand to her mouth, as if she could physically hold back the truth. The journal fell from her fingers, landing open on the floor. The last entry was dated June 1979—three months before she was born.

*"Henry says we’re moving. He says we need a fresh start. I don’t know if I can do this. I don’t know if I can leave Daniel behind. But I don’t have a choice. Not anymore."*

The room tilted. Eleanor reached for the bedpost, her knuckles white. The house creaked around her, the walls pressing in, the air thick with the weight of secrets. She thought of her father’s silence, the way he had never spoken of the past, the way he had worked himself to the bone as if he could outrun his own history. She thought of her mother, the way she had sometimes stared out the window, her fingers twisting the wedding ring on her left hand, her expression distant, as if she were somewhere else entirely.

Eleanor picked up the journal again, her fingers tracing the words. *"I don’t have a choice. Not anymore."*

She thought of her own life, the way she had built it carefully, deliberately, as if she could control the shape of her own story. But stories were never just one person’s to tell. They were made of fragments, of half-truths, of the things left unsaid.

She stood, the journal clutched in her hands, and walked to the window. The backyard stretched below her, the old apple tree still standing in the corner, its branches heavy with fruit. She had forgotten about that tree. Or perhaps she had never known it was there at all.

Eleanor pressed her forehead against the glass. The letters, the journal, the tree—each was a piece of a puzzle she had never known she was trying to solve. The house groaned around her, the floorboards shifting under her feet, as if the very structure of the place was rearranging itself, making room for the truth.

She turned away from the window and walked back to the bed. She gathered the letters, the journal, and placed them carefully in the shoebox. Then she carried it downstairs, her steps slow, deliberate.

In the kitchen, she set the box on the table. The room was just as she remembered it—the same chipped Formica countertops, the same faded curtains at the window. She pulled out a chair and sat, her fingers tracing the grain of the wood. The table had been her mother’s domain, the place where she had rolled out dough for pies, where she had scolded Eleanor for tracking mud across the floor, where she had sat in silence with her father, the two of them orbiting each other like distant stars.

Eleanor opened the journal to the first page and began to read again, this time more slowly, as if the words might reveal something new if she only paid attention. The kitchen filled with the sound of her breathing, the tick of the old clock on the wall, the distant hum of the refrigerator. Outside, the wind rustled through the leaves of the apple tree, a sound like whispers.

She read until the light faded, until the words blurred together, until the house around her felt less like a place of ghosts and more like a place of answers. And when she finally closed the journal, her hands were steady.
