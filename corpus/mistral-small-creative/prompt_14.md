**Chapter Seven: The Locked Room**

The house smelled of damp rot and old sin when Eleanor Cross stepped inside for the first time. The air clung to her skin like a second layer of grief, thick and suffocating. She had expected the weight of inheritance—her aunt’s death had been sudden, a stroke in the middle of the night, the kind of thing that left no time for goodbyes—but she hadn’t expected the house itself to feel like a living thing, breathing slow and shallow through its sagging floorboards.

The lawyer had called it a *gift*. The townsfolk of Blackthorn Hollow called it a *mistake*.

Eleanor ran her fingers along the peeling wallpaper in the foyer, the floral pattern faded to ghostly smudges. The house had been in her family for generations, though no one in her immediate line had lived in it since her great-uncle Harlan died in the late sixties. The will had been clear: *To my niece, Eleanor, I leave the house on Magnolia Lane, along with all its contents, and the burden of its memory.* Burden. That word had stuck in her throat like a fishbone.

She dropped her suitcase in the center of the hallway and listened. The house groaned around her, settling into her presence like an old dog adjusting to a new master. Somewhere deep in its belly, a floorboard creaked. Not under her foot. Not from the wind.

From the locked room.

---

The realtor had warned her about the room. *"You’ll want to have that door replaced,"* he’d said, his voice too bright, his smile too wide. *"Old lock’s rusted shut. No key, far as I know."* He hadn’t met her eyes when he said it. No one in Blackthorn Hollow did, not for long. They looked at her like she was already half-haunted, like the house had marked her the moment she crossed the county line.

Eleanor had pressed him for details—*Why was it locked? What was inside?*—but he’d only shaken his head, his Adam’s apple bobbing like a cork in rough water. *"Some things are best left alone, ma’am. That’s all I’ll say."*

She found the room on the second floor, at the end of a hallway that smelled of mildew and something sharper, like old pennies. The door was solid oak, dark with age, the brass handle tarnished green. She turned it anyway. The lock held fast. She pressed her ear against the wood.

Silence.

Then—a whisper.

Not a voice, not exactly. More like the sound of fabric dragging across a floor, slow and deliberate. A breath, maybe. A sigh. The hair on her arms stood on end. She stepped back, her pulse hammering in her throat.

*"You hearin’ things already?"*

Eleanor whirled. A woman stood at the top of the stairs, her silver hair pulled into a tight bun, her hands clasped around a basket of what looked like fresh-baked bread. She was old—seventy, maybe eighty—but her eyes were sharp as flint.

*"I—I didn’t hear you come in,"* Eleanor stammered.

The woman—*Mrs. Hargrove*, the realtor had called her, the closest thing Blackthorn Hollow had to a welcoming committee—smirked. *"Door was open. Figured you’d need some proper food. Can’t live on grief and dust."* She held out the basket. *"Cornbread. My mama’s recipe."*

Eleanor took it, the warmth seeping through the cloth. *"Thank you."*

Mrs. Hargrove’s gaze flicked to the locked door. *"You ain’t been in there yet."*

*"No."*

*"Good."* She turned to leave, then paused. *"Your aunt used to say that room was where the house kept its secrets. Said if you listened close enough, you could hear ‘em whisperin’ back."*

Eleanor’s fingers tightened around the basket. *"What kind of secrets?"*

Mrs. Hargrove’s smile didn’t reach her eyes. *"The kind that’ll keep you up at night."* She descended the stairs, her footsteps light for a woman her age. *"You’ll want to board that door up, child. For your own good."*

---

The house was worse by morning.

Eleanor woke to the sound of water dripping. She followed it to the kitchen, where a slow, steady *plink-plink-plink* echoed from the sink. The faucet was dry. The noise came from the cabinet beneath it. She knelt, pulled the door open.

A rat, bloated and dead, lay curled in the corner, its fur matted with something dark and glistening. The stench hit her like a fist—rot and copper, the sickly sweet reek of decay. She gagged, stumbling back, her hand flying to her mouth.

*"You find ‘im, then."*

Eleanor spun. A man stood in the doorway, his overalls stained with what looked like motor oil, his face lined like cracked leather. *Mr. Calloway*, the neighbor from down the road. He’d been the one to find her aunt’s body.

*"Find who?"* she managed.

He jerked his chin toward the cabinet. *"Old Tom. Been dead near a week, I reckon. Your aunt, she fed ‘im. Said he kept the other varmints out."* His eyes flicked to the ceiling, toward the locked room. *"Ain’t nothin’ keeps *them* out, though."*

Eleanor’s skin prickled. *"What do you mean, *them*?"*

Mr. Calloway spat into the sink. *"Ain’t my place to say. But you mark my words—this house ain’t right. Never has been."* He turned to leave, then paused. *"Your aunt, she used to talk to that door. Late at night, when she thought no one was listenin’. Said she was *negotiatin’*."* He shook his head. *"Ain’t no negotiatin’ with the dead, girl. You’d do well to remember that."*

---

She boarded up the door that afternoon.

Not because she believed in ghosts or whispers or whatever dark history festered in the walls of this house. But because the way the townsfolk looked at her—like she was already half-lost—made her skin crawl. She hammered the plywood into place, the nails biting into the wood with a sound like teeth grinding.

That night, she dreamed of the room.

In the dream, the door was open. The space beyond was vast, a yawning blackness that stretched farther than the house possibly could. And in that darkness, something moved. Not a person. Not an animal. Something *else*, its shape shifting like smoke, its voice a chorus of whispers that slithered into her ears and coiled around her bones.

*"You let us out,"* it hissed. *"You let us out, and we’ll let you keep breathin’."*

She woke gasping, her nightgown soaked with sweat. The house was silent. Too silent. No creaking floorboards. No settling beams. Just the heavy, watchful stillness of a predator holding its breath.

Then—

A knock.

Not at the door. Not at a window.

At the *boarded-up door*.

Three slow, deliberate raps. Like knuckles on wood. Like a hand testing the barrier between them.

Eleanor’s blood turned to ice. She didn’t move. Didn’t breathe.

The knocking came again. Louder. Insistent.

*"Eleanor."*

Her name, whispered through the wood. A woman’s voice. Her aunt’s voice.

*"Let us out."*

She scrambled out of bed, her hands shaking as she fumbled for the lamp. The bulb flickered, then died. The room plunged into darkness.

The knocking stopped.

For a heartbeat, there was only silence.

Then—the sound of something *scraping* against the plywood. Slow. Methodical. Like fingernails dragging down the grain.

Eleanor backed away until her spine hit the wall. Her breath came in ragged gasps. The scraping stopped.

A long, shuddering sigh seeped through the cracks in the wood.

*"Soon,"* the voice murmured. *"Soon, you’ll understand."*

---

Dawn found her curled in the armchair in the parlor, her knees drawn to her chest, her aunt’s old quilt wrapped around her shoulders. She hadn’t slept. Hadn’t dared.

The house felt different in the daylight. Less like a living thing, more like a corpse left to rot in the sun. The air was stale, the light thin and watery through the grimy windows. She told herself it had been a dream. A nightmare. The product of grief and exhaustion.

But when she pressed her ear to the boarded-up door, she heard it again—the whisper of fabric, the drag of something heavy across the floor. And beneath it, fainter now, the sound of *breathing*.

She stepped back, her hands clenched into fists.

*"What do you want?"* she demanded, her voice raw.

Silence.

Then—a laugh. Low. Wet. Like something drowning in its own throat.

*"What we’ve always wanted,"* the voice crooned. *"A way out."*

---

The sheriff’s office was a squat brick building at the end of Main Street, its paint peeling like sunburnt skin. Eleanor pushed through the door, the bell above it jingling like a warning.

The deputy behind the desk looked up, his expression shifting from boredom to wariness the moment he saw her. *"Help you, ma’am?"*

*"I need to talk to someone about my aunt’s house,"* she said. *"The one on Magnolia Lane."*

The deputy’s fingers stilled over his keyboard. *"Ain’t much to say about that place."*

*"There’s a room,"* she pressed. *"Locked. Boarded up now. People in this town act like it’s cursed. I want to know why."*

The deputy exhaled through his nose, his jaw tightening. *"You ain’t from around here, are you?"*

*"No."*

*"Then let me give you some advice. Some doors ain’t meant to be opened. Some rooms ain’t meant to be entered. Your aunt knew that. Her daddy knew that. Hell, half the folks in this town’s granddaddies knew that."* He leaned forward, his voice dropping. *"That house ain’t just old, ma’am. It’s *hungry*. And it don’t take kindly to folks pokin’ around in its business."*

Eleanor’s pulse pounded in her ears. *"What’s in that room?"*

The deputy’s eyes darkened. *"Ain’t nothin’ in there. Not no more. But that don’t mean it’s empty."* He sat back, his chair creaking. *"You want my advice? Sell the place. Burn it down. But don’t you *dare* open that door."*

---

She didn’t sell the house.

She didn’t burn it down.

That night, she stood in the hallway outside the boarded-up door, a crowbar in her hands, her breath coming in short, sharp bursts. The house groaned around her, the walls pressing in like the ribs of a dying beast.

*"You sure about this?"* she whispered to herself.

The whisper came back.

*"Yes."*

She didn’t recognize the voice. It wasn’t hers. It wasn’t her aunt’s. It was something older. Something that had been waiting.

She wedged the crowbar beneath the plywood and pulled.

The nails screamed as they tore free. The wood splintered. The door behind it—dark, warped, the lock rusted shut—stood revealed.

Eleanor dropped the crowbar. It clattered to the floor.

The door *creaked*.

Not from the wind. Not from the house settling.

From the *other side*.

She reached out, her fingers trembling. The brass handle was ice-cold beneath her touch. She turned it.

The lock clicked.

The door swung open.

Darkness yawned beyond it, deep and absolute. The air that rushed out was stale, thick with the scent of old blood and something sweeter, like rotting flowers. Eleanor’s stomach lurched.

Then—

A hand shot out of the blackness and seized her wrist.

Cold. Bone-dry. *Strong*.

She screamed.

The hand yanked her forward, into the dark. The door slammed shut behind her.

And then—

Silence.

The house held its breath.

Waiting.
