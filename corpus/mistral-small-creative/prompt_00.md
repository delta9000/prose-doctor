**Chapter One: The Edge of the Known**

The air here is different.

Elias Veyra knows this before he even steps out of the tent. It’s not just the cold—though that bites at his cheeks, sharp as a blade left in the snow—or the way the wind carries the scent of pine and something older, something damp and buried. No, it’s the *weight* of it. The air presses down on him, thick as wet wool, as if the sky itself is holding its breath.

He exhales, watches his breath curl into the dawn like a ghost. The thermometer outside his tent reads -12°C, but the wind makes it feel like -20. His fingers, already stiff inside his gloves, fumble with the zipper of his parka. The fabric sticks, resistant, as if the cold has sewn it shut. He yanks harder. The zipper gives with a sharp *riiiip*, and he steps into the world.

The landscape stretches before him, vast and indifferent. To the east, the jagged teeth of the Transantarctic Mountains claw at the horizon, their peaks still shrouded in the blue-gray haze of early morning. To the west, the ice shelf yawns—endless, cracked, a frozen sea that doesn’t end but *begins*, a place where the earth forgets its own rules. And straight ahead, where no map has ever dared to go, the white gives way to something else. Something that doesn’t belong.

Elias squints. His goggles fog with the heat of his breath. He wipes them with the back of his glove, but the condensation clings, stubborn. He pulls them off entirely, lets the cold air sting his eyes. There. A smudge of color. Not the blue-white of ice, not the gray of rock, but something *green*. A smear of it, like a watercolor left too long in the rain.

His pulse quickens. *Impossible.*

He reaches for his binoculars, the leather case stiff with frost. His fingers slip on the strap. The binoculars clatter to the ice. He curses under his breath, crouches, brushes away the snow. The lenses are fogged. He exhales onto them, rubs them against his sleeve. Lifts them again.

The green is still there.

Not a trick of the light. Not a mirage. A strip of land—no, not land, something *alive*—cutting through the ice like a scar. A valley. A *gorge*. And beyond it, if his eyes aren’t deceiving him, the suggestion of trees. Not the stunted, wind-bent things that cling to the edges of the continent, but *trees*. Tall. Dense. A forest.

His hands tremble. He lowers the binoculars, presses his palms to his thighs. *Breathe.* The rational part of his brain—the part that has spent the last decade mapping the unmapped, the part that knows the weight of a compass, the precision of a sextant, the cold mathematics of latitude and longitude—whispers that this is a mistake. A trick of the light. Exhaustion. Hallucination. He hasn’t slept more than four hours a night since they landed, and the isolation does things to a man’s mind.

But the other part—the part that has always believed in the *possibility* of the unknown, the part that chose this life because the world was too small, too *mapped*—that part *leaps*.

“Veyra.”

The voice cuts through his thoughts like a blade. Elias turns.

Dr. Lien Zhao stands a few meters away, her parka unzipped despite the cold, her dark hair escaping the confines of her hood in wild strands. She holds a steaming mug in her gloved hands, the scent of instant coffee cutting through the crisp air. Her eyes—sharp, assessing—flick from his face to the binoculars still clutched in his hands.

“You’re up early,” she says. Not a question. A statement. An observation.

Elias swallows. “Couldn’t sleep.”

Lien takes a sip of her coffee, winces at the heat. “Nightmares again?”

He doesn’t answer. She knows. They all do.

She steps closer, her boots crunching in the snow. “You look like you’ve seen a ghost.”

Elias hesitates. Then, slowly, he lifts the binoculars and points. “Look.”

Lien follows his gaze. Her breath catches.

For a long moment, neither of them speaks. The wind howls between them, carrying the distant groan of shifting ice.

“That’s not possible,” Lien says finally. Her voice is quiet. Almost reverent.

“No,” Elias agrees. “It’s not.”

She lowers the binoculars, turns to him. “You’re sure it’s not—”

“A trick of the light? Exhaustion? No.” He shakes his head. “It’s there.”

Lien exhales, a cloud of white between them. “We need to tell the others.”

Elias doesn’t move. “Not yet.”

She raises an eyebrow. “Why not?”

Because he needs to see it first. Because if he tells them now, they’ll want to rush. To *document*. To *claim*. And he needs—just for a moment—to stand at the edge of this impossible thing and let it be *his*. Just for a little while.

But he can’t say that. Not to Lien.

“Because we don’t know what it is,” he says instead. “It could be nothing. A shadow. A—”

“A forest,” Lien finishes. “In Antarctica.”

Elias says nothing.

Lien studies him for a long moment. Then she nods, once, sharp. “Fine. But we go together. And we take the drone.”

Elias almost argues. Almost tells her he wants to go alone. But he knows better. Lien isn’t just the team’s geologist; she’s the only one who’s ever come close to understanding the way his mind works. The way he *needs* to see things for himself before he can believe them.

“Fine,” he says.

Lien turns back toward the camp, her boots leaving deep prints in the snow. “Meet me at the equipment tent in twenty. And Veyra?”

“Yeah?”

“If this is real…” She glances back at him, her dark eyes unreadable. “It changes everything.”

---

The equipment tent is a chaos of gear—ice axes, crampons, GPS units, solar panels, the sleek black body of the drone folded like a sleeping bat. The air inside is thick with the scent of oil and metal and the faint, acrid tang of battery acid. Elias ducks under the flap, his breath fogging in the dim light.

Lien is already there, kneeling beside the drone, her fingers deft as she checks the propellers, the camera, the battery pack. She doesn’t look up as he enters.

“Took you long enough,” she mutters.

Elias ignores her, crouching beside the stack of maps. His maps. The ones he’s spent the last three years compiling, the ones that show every crevasse, every ridge, every hidden lake in this godforsaken place. He pulls the most recent one free, unfolds it carefully. The paper is thin, almost translucent, the ink sharp and precise.

His finger traces the edge of the known. The line where the maps end and the white space begins. The place where, according to every satellite image, every survey, every scientific consensus, there should be nothing but ice. Endless, unbroken ice.

But the map doesn’t show the green.

Of course it doesn’t.

He folds it again, tucks it into his pocket.

“You ready?” Lien asks.

Elias looks up. She’s holding the drone, her gloved hands steady. The camera lens glints in the low light, a single unblinking eye.

“Yeah,” he says. “Let’s go.”

---

The snowmobile’s engine growls beneath them, a deep, guttural sound that vibrates through Elias’s bones. He grips the handlebars, his knuckles white inside his gloves. Lien sits behind him, her arms wrapped around his waist, her body tense. The drone is strapped to her back, its weight pressing against him with every bump, every shift in the terrain.

The wind whips at them, stinging their faces, stealing their breath. The ice stretches endlessly ahead, a blank canvas of white and blue and the occasional jagged ridge of black rock. But Elias doesn’t look at the ice. He looks beyond it. To the green.

It’s closer now. Closer than it should be.

The snowmobile lurches over a ridge, and for a moment, they’re airborne. Elias’s stomach drops. Then the skis hit the ice again, and they’re moving, faster, faster—

“Slow down!” Lien shouts over the wind.

Elias eases off the throttle. The snowmobile slows, the engine settling into a steady rumble.

“There,” Lien says, pointing.

Elias follows her gaze.

The green is no longer a smudge. It’s a *line*. A sharp, defined edge where the ice ends and something else begins. Something *alive*.

He kills the engine.

Silence.

The wind stills, as if holding its breath. The only sound is the distant groan of the ice shelf, the faint *tick-tick-tick* of the snowmobile’s cooling engine.

Elias swings his leg over the seat, steps onto the ice. His boots sink slightly, the crust giving way beneath his weight. He takes a step forward. Then another.

Lien follows, her breath coming in short, sharp bursts. “This isn’t right,” she says. “The satellite images—”

“Lied,” Elias finishes.

He stops at the edge. The transition is abrupt, almost violent. One moment, there’s ice beneath his boots, cold and unyielding. The next, there’s *earth*. Dark, rich soil. Moss. Lichen. The scent of damp earth, of decay, of *life*.

And beyond that, the trees.

They rise like sentinels, their trunks thick and gnarled, their branches heavy with needles that shimmer in the pale Antarctic light. Not pine. Not anything Elias recognizes. The bark is dark, almost black, streaked with veins of green and gold. The needles are long, slender, a deep emerald that seems to drink in the light.

Elias reaches out. His fingers hover just above the nearest trunk. The air here is warmer. Not by much—just a few degrees—but enough to make the difference between life and death.

He touches the bark.

It’s warm.

Not the cold, damp warmth of a living thing in winter, but something else. Something *alive* in a way that defies explanation. The bark is rough beneath his glove, but not unyielding. It *gives*, just slightly, as if breathing.

“Elias.”

Lien’s voice is a whisper. A warning.

He pulls his hand back. Turns.

She’s standing a few meters away, her face pale. In her hands, she holds the drone, its camera lens pointed at the trees. The screen on the controller flickers, the feed grainy but clear.

“Look,” she says.

Elias steps closer. The screen shows the forest, the trees rising like a wall. But there’s something else. Something moving between the trunks.

A shape.

Tall. Slender. Almost *human*.

Elias’s breath catches.

The shape steps forward.

The camera feed glitches. The image distorts, pixels scattering like ash in the wind. Then it’s gone.

Lien curses, slaps the side of the controller. The screen flickers back to life, but the feed is static. White noise.

“What the hell was that?” she demands.

Elias doesn’t answer. He’s already moving, his boots sinking into the soft earth as he steps beneath the first tree. The air is thicker here, heavier. The scent of pine is stronger, mixed with something else—something sweet, almost floral.

“Elias, wait—”

He ignores her. His pulse roars in his ears. The trees press in around him, their branches weaving a canopy overhead, blocking out the sky. The light is different here. Softer. Almost *golden*.

And then he sees it.

A path.

Not a trail. Not a game path worn by animals. A *path*. Worn smooth by footsteps. By *time*.

His breath comes faster. His fingers tremble as he reaches for his camera, fumbles with the lens cap. He lifts it, frames the shot.

The flash goes off.

For a single, suspended moment, the world is white.

Then the image resolves.

The path stretches ahead of him, winding between the trees. And at the end of it, just visible in the dim light, is a structure.

Not a rock. Not a formation. A *building*.

Stone. Old. Crumbling. But unmistakably *made*.

Elias’s hands shake. The camera slips from his fingers, hits the ground with a soft *thud*. He doesn’t pick it up.

Behind him, Lien’s voice is distant, muffled, as if she’s speaking from the end of a long tunnel.

“Elias. *Elias.*”

He doesn’t turn.

The building looms ahead of him, its walls covered in carvings. Symbols. Patterns. A language he doesn’t recognize.

But one symbol stands out.

A circle. A dot in the center.

A *map*.

His breath comes in ragged gasps. His vision swims.

This changes everything.

And then, from the darkness of the building’s doorway, something *moves*.
