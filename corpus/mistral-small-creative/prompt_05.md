**Chapter Seven: The Weight of Paper**

The air in the processing center smelled like wet concrete and old sweat. Liora pressed her palms against the cool metal of the bench, her fingers tracing the grooves where countless others had sat before her, waiting. The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting a sickly yellow glow over the rows of identical chairs, the scuffed linoleum, the flickering screens mounted on the walls. Outside, the city pulsed—neon signs bleeding into the perpetual twilight of smog-choked skies, the distant thrum of maglev trains cutting through the canyon of skyscrapers. But in here, time moved differently. It stretched, thick and sluggish, like the river of bureaucracy she’d been drowning in for the past three weeks.

She adjusted the strap of her duffel bag, the weight of it digging into her shoulder. Inside were the last remnants of her life: a change of clothes, a half-empty bottle of antibiotics, a photograph of her mother, its edges frayed from handling. The rest had been left behind in the flood zones of what used to be Bangladesh, now just another statistic in the global displacement reports. *Climate refugee. Economic migrant. Unverified status.*

The line inched forward. Ahead of her, a family of four huddled together, the father clutching a sheaf of papers like a lifeline. His hands trembled. Liora looked away, her gaze snagging on the posters plastered to the walls—*WORK PERMITS: NOW AVAILABLE FOR APPROVED CANDIDATES!* in bold, cheerful font, as if the word *approved* didn’t carry the weight of a guillotine. Below it, smaller text: *Priority given to skilled laborers. Agricultural experience preferred.*

She didn’t have either.

A screen above the counter flickered to life, displaying a number: **47-B**. The family in front of her stiffened. The father straightened his shoulders, as if bracing for impact, and stepped up to the window. Liora watched as the clerk—a woman with a face like a shut door—scanned the family’s documents without looking up. The father spoke in halting English, his voice tight. The clerk shook her head. No. Not enough. Try again.

The family shuffled back to their bench, their faces hollow. The mother pressed a hand to her mouth, her shoulders shaking. Liora’s stomach twisted. She knew that look. She’d worn it herself, back when the water had first risen over the rice paddies, back when the officials had come with their clipboards and their cold eyes, back when the word *relocation* had still sounded like hope.

Her number flashed on the screen: **48-C**.

She stood, her legs unsteady, and approached the window. The clerk didn’t look up. “ID. Processing fee. Proof of employment or transferable skills.”

Liora slid her papers across the counter. The clerk’s fingers moved with mechanical precision, flipping through the pages—her birth certificate, her old work visa (revoked), a letter from the Red Cross (stamped *INSUFFICIENT*). The clerk’s nails were short, bitten to the quick. A habit, Liora thought. A tell.

“You have no verified work history in the new economy,” the clerk said, finally meeting her eyes. They were a dull, exhausted brown. “No digital footprint. No corporate sponsorship.”

“I can learn,” Liora said. “I worked in agriculture. I can—”

“Agriculture is automated.” The clerk tapped a key on her terminal. A red stamp appeared on Liora’s file. *DECLINED: LOW PRIORITY.* “You’ll be notified if your status is reconsidered. Next.”

Liora’s hands curled into fists. “Where am I supposed to go?”

The clerk’s expression didn’t change. “You have seventy-two hours to appeal or find alternative housing. After that, you’ll be processed for temporary shelter in the outer zones.”

*The outer zones.* The words hit like a punch to the gut. Everyone knew what that meant—crowded, half-collapsed tenements on the fringes of the city, where the air was thicker with pollution and the gangs ruled like warlords. Where people disappeared.

She opened her mouth to argue, but the clerk was already waving the next person forward. Liora stepped back, her vision blurring. The duffel bag slipped from her shoulder, hitting the floor with a dull thud. She didn’t bend to pick it up.

---

The streets outside were a maze of neon and shadow. The city had been built in layers, each one stacked on top of the last like a geologic record of human desperation. The upper tiers—gleaming spires of glass and steel—housed the corporate elite, their lives sealed behind climate-controlled walls, their needs met by drones and automated systems. Below them, the middle tiers teemed with the precariat: the gig workers, the service bots’ human overseers, the white-collar drones who kept the machine running. And at the bottom, the outer zones, where the city’s refuse—human and otherwise—was dumped and forgotten.

Liora walked without direction, her shoes scuffing against the pavement. The sidewalks were crowded with vendors hawking knockoff tech, black-market meds, synthetic food paste in bright, unappetizing colors. The air smelled like ozone and fried oil. A holographic advertisement flickered above her, a smiling woman in a pristine lab coat holding up a vial of *Purified Air™*—*Breathe Easy, Live Free!* The tagline made Liora’s throat tighten. She hadn’t breathed easy in years.

She ducked into a narrow alley to escape the press of bodies. The walls were covered in graffiti—*THE SKY IS A LIE*, *WE ARE THE DROWNING*—the words scrawled in jagged, desperate strokes. A rat scuttled past her feet, its fur matted with grime. She leaned against the wall, pressing her forehead to the cool brick. Seventy-two hours. Three days to find a way in, or be cast out.

A sound made her lift her head. A child’s cry.

She followed it to the end of the alley, where a small figure crouched beside a dumpster. A girl, no older than eight, her dark hair tangled, her clothes too thin for the chill of the evening. She clutched a half-eaten protein bar in one hand, her other arm wrapped around a scrawny dog. The dog whined, its ribs visible beneath patchy fur.

“Hey,” Liora said softly.

The girl flinched, pressing herself against the dumpster. Her eyes were wide, feral. “Go away.”

Liora crouched down, keeping her distance. “You okay?”

The girl didn’t answer. She tore off a piece of the protein bar and fed it to the dog, her movements quick, protective.

Liora reached into her duffel bag and pulled out the last of her own rations—a nutrient pack, sealed in foil. She held it out. “Here. It’s better than that.”

The girl stared at the pack, then at Liora. Suspicion etched into every line of her face. “Why?”

Liora almost laughed. *Why.* As if kindness required a reason. “Because you look hungry.”

The girl hesitated, then snatched the pack. She tore it open with her teeth, shoving half into her mouth before offering the rest to the dog. The animal lapped at the powder greedily.

Liora watched them, her chest aching. “What’s your name?”

“Mira.”

“You live around here, Mira?”

The girl shook her head. “Nowhere. Everywhere.”

A refugee, then. Like her. Liora exhaled. “You got family?”

Mira’s fingers tightened around the dog’s scruff. “No.”

Liora knew that tone. The finality of it. She also knew the lie behind it—the way grief could carve a person hollow, leave them floating in the world like a ghost. She’d been that girl once. Still was, in some ways.

She reached into her bag again and pulled out the photograph of her mother. The edges were soft from years of handling, the colors faded. She held it out. “This is my mom. She’s gone too.”

Mira didn’t take the photo, but she looked at it. Her lower lip trembled. “Mine got sick. The air made her cough. Then she didn’t wake up.”

Liora’s throat burned. She knew that story too. The slow poison of the city, the way it seeped into your lungs, your bones, until one day you just… stopped. “I’m sorry.”

Mira wiped her nose with the back of her hand. “You got a place to stay?”

Liora almost lied. Almost said *yes*, just to spare the girl the weight of her own truth. But the words stuck in her throat. “No. Not yet.”

Mira studied her for a long moment. Then, slowly, she stood. “Come on.”

“Where?”

“Somewhere warm.”

---

The somewhere warm turned out to be an abandoned subway tunnel, its entrance hidden behind a rusted service door. Mira led her down a series of crumbling steps, the dog trotting beside them, its nails clicking against the concrete. The air grew thicker, heavier, the scent of damp earth and something metallic filling Liora’s nose. Then, light.

Not the harsh glow of fluorescents, but the soft flicker of candles. Dozens of them, lined up along the walls, their flames casting long shadows over the faces of the people huddled in the tunnel. A makeshift community. A family of strangers.

Mira didn’t stop to introduce her. She just walked to the far end of the tunnel, where a woman sat on a pile of blankets, her back against the wall. The woman looked up as they approached, her dark eyes sharp, assessing. She was older—forties, maybe—her face lined with the kind of wear that came from too many years of fighting just to stay alive.

“Found her outside,” Mira said, nodding at Liora. “She gave me food.”

The woman’s gaze flicked to Liora, then back to Mira. “You trust her?”

Mira hesitated. Then, quietly: “Yeah.”

The woman exhaled through her nose, then patted the blanket beside her. “Sit.”

Liora sat.

The woman’s name was Nadira. She’d been in the city for five years, ever since the floods had swallowed her village in Indonesia. She ran this tunnel—this *home*—with a mix of iron will and quiet kindness. “We don’t turn people away,” she said, pouring Liora a cup of tea from a dented thermos. “But we don’t suffer fools either.”

Liora wrapped her hands around the cup, the heat seeping into her skin. “I’m not a fool.”

Nadira smirked. “Good. Because fools don’t last long out here.”

Around them, the tunnel hummed with life. A group of children played a game with pebbles and a piece of string. An old man tuned a radio, the static hissing like a living thing. A woman braided another’s hair, her fingers moving with practiced ease. It was a world unto itself, carved out of the city’s underbelly.

Liora took a sip of the tea. It was bitter, herbal, laced with something sharp—maybe painkillers, maybe just desperation. “How do you survive down here?”

Nadira’s smile was thin. “We take what the city gives us. Scrap metal. Discarded tech. Sometimes, if we’re lucky, a corporate drop-off.” She nodded toward a stack of crates in the corner, their sides stamped with a familiar logo—*Nexus Agri-Corp*. “They ‘donate’ expired rations to the outer zones. We intercept them before the gangs do.”

Liora’s stomach twisted. She’d seen those crates before, back in the processing center. *Corporate sponsorship.* Another word for charity, another way to keep the desperate from rioting.

Nadira must have seen the look on her face. “Don’t mistake survival for gratitude,” she said. “We take what we need. No more, no less.”

Liora set the cup down. “I need a job. Something that’ll get me a work permit.”

Nadira studied her for a long moment. Then she reached into her pocket and pulled out a crumpled flyer. She handed it to Liora.

The paper was cheap, the ink smudged. *NOW HIRING: Urban Farm Laborers. No experience necessary. Housing provided.* At the bottom, a logo—*GreenHaven Co-Op*—and an address in the lower tiers.

Liora’s fingers trembled. “This is real?”

Nadira shrugged. “Real enough. They’re one of the few co-ops still hiring humans. Most of the farms are automated now, but they’ve got a few plots left for manual labor. Pay’s shit, but it’s a start.”

Liora clutched the flyer to her chest. A start. That was all she needed.

Nadira’s voice softened. “You’ll need to move fast. They fill spots quick.”

Liora nodded. “I will.”

Mira, who had been listening silently, suddenly spoke up. “I’ll go with you.”

Liora blinked. “What?”

“To the farm,” Mira said, her chin lifted. “I can work too.”

Nadira sighed. “Mira—”

“I can!” Mira insisted. “I’m strong. And I know plants. My mom grew things before…” She trailed off, her throat working.

Liora looked at Nadira, who met her gaze with a weary resignation. Then, slowly, Nadira nodded. “Fine. But you listen to Liora. And you come back if it’s not safe.”

Mira grinned, her earlier ferocity replaced by something brighter, something like hope. “I will.”

Liora exhaled, the weight in her chest easing just a fraction. She had a lead. A plan. A girl who reminded her of herself, and a woman who had offered her a blanket and a cup of bitter tea.

It wasn’t much. But it was enough.

For now.
