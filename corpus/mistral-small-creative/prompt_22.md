**Chapter 12: The Unseen Thread**

The extraction chamber hums like a hive of sleeping bees, its sterile glow casting long shadows across the tiled floor. Lira adjusts the neural lace with practiced fingers, the filaments whispering against her gloves as she aligns them with the client’s temples. The man—*Subject 47-B*—lies motionless on the gurney, his breath shallow, his pupils dilated to black pools under the dimmed lights. The sedative has done its work. He won’t remember this. He won’t remember *anything* by the time she’s done.

She taps the console, and the chamber’s AI chirps in confirmation: *Neural sync established. Memory core accessible.*

Lira exhales, slow and controlled. This is the part that always feels like theft.

The screen flickers to life, a cascade of fragmented images spilling across the display. Not a single memory, but a storm of them—sensory echoes, emotional imprints, the raw data of a life lived. She sifts through them with the precision of a surgeon, her fingers dancing over the holographic interface. The regime’s algorithms have already flagged the target: *Incident 789-K. Public disturbance. Civil unrest suppression.*

She finds it quickly—a memory marked in crimson, pulsing like an infected wound. Lira hesitates. She’s seen these before. The regime calls them *disruptions*, moments where citizens step out of line, where the carefully constructed illusion of order fractures. Her job is to smooth those fractures away, to ensure the collective memory remains unblemished, compliant.

She reaches for the memory, her fingers hovering over the extraction command.

Then she sees it.

Not the disturbance itself—a protest, a man shouting, the flash of riot batons—but *what comes after*. A secondary memory, buried beneath the first, untagged by the regime’s scanners. A glitch. A mistake.

Lira’s breath catches.

The man—*Subject 47-B*—is on his knees in an alley, his hands cuffed behind his back. A black-clad enforcer stands over him, but it’s not the enforcer who speaks. It’s the man’s own voice, raw and trembling, pleading: *"I didn’t know. I swear, I didn’t know about the children."*

The enforcer doesn’t answer. Instead, he presses a device to the man’s temple—a memory editor’s tool, but crude, brutal. The man’s body jerks. His eyes roll back.

And then—

Lira’s stomach twists.

The memory shifts. She’s no longer in the alley. She’s in a facility, sterile and white, rows of cryo-pods lining the walls. Children. Dozens of them, their small bodies suspended in gel, tubes snaking into their arms. A scientist in a lab coat leans over one pod, adjusting a dial. A monitor flickers with vital signs. A name flashes on the screen: *Subject 004-A. Viable. Proceeding with Phase Two.*

Lira’s hands tremble. She’s seen classified files before—memories of dissidents, of failed uprisings—but this is different. This isn’t suppression. This is *creation*.

The memory cuts off abruptly, the feed dissolving into static. Lira’s pulse roars in her ears. She should log this. She should flag it for review. That’s protocol. That’s *survival*.

But the regime doesn’t tolerate questions.

She swallows hard and forces her fingers to move, pulling up the extraction sequence. The memory of the protest, the disturbance—it’s still there, waiting to be erased. She could do it. She could make this man forget, just like all the others. She could walk out of here, go home, pretend she never saw the children in the pods.

Her thumb hovers over the command.

*"You’re not supposed to see this."*

The voice is a whisper, so faint she almost thinks she imagined it. But it’s not in her head. It’s in the memory. The man—*Subject 47-B*—is speaking, his lips moving in the frozen frame of the extraction feed. His eyes are wide, locked onto hers as if he can *see* her through the veil of sedation.

*"They’ll kill you if you know."*

Lira’s breath comes fast. The chamber feels smaller, the air thicker. She’s broken protocol before—small things, harmless things. A memory left intact here, a distress call ignored there. But this? This is a death sentence.

She should abort. She should call for an enforcer, report the anomaly, play the loyal servant.

Instead, she does something reckless.

She *saves* the memory.

Not the protest. Not the disturbance. The *other* one. The one with the children. The one that will get her killed if anyone finds it. She isolates the fragment, compresses it, and buries it in the deepest recess of her personal archive—a hidden drive, encrypted, untraceable. Or so she hopes.

Her hands shake as she finalizes the extraction. The protest memory dissolves into nothing, wiped clean from the man’s mind. When he wakes, he’ll remember only the regime’s approved narrative: *A minor altercation. Resolved peacefully. No further action required.*

Lira steps back from the console, her legs unsteady. The chamber’s AI chirps again: *Procedure complete. Subject stable.*

She should feel relief. She doesn’t.

The door hisses open behind her. Lira turns, her heart in her throat.

It’s just the nurse, a young woman with hollow cheeks and tired eyes. "Editor Veyra," she says, her voice flat. "The next subject is prepped."

Lira nods, forcing her expression into something neutral. "I’ll be right there."

The nurse hesitates, her gaze flickering to the console. "Everything alright?"

"Fine," Lira lies. "Just… a glitch in the system."

The nurse doesn’t press. No one ever does.

When she’s gone, Lira exhales, long and slow. She should delete the memory. She should burn it from her drive, pretend she never saw it. But she can’t.

Because now she *knows*.

And knowing is the most dangerous thing of all.

---

The next subject is already on the gurney when she enters the prep room, his wrists restrained, his eyes glassy with sedatives. Lira moves through the motions—neural lace, sync check, memory scan—but her mind is elsewhere. The children in the pods. The scientist’s cold voice. *Phase Two.*

She’s about to begin the extraction when her wrist comm buzzes. A priority alert.

*Editor Veyra, report to Sector 9 immediately. Directive from Central Command.*

Her blood turns to ice.

Sector 9 is where they take the problematic ones. The ones who ask too many questions. The ones who see too much.

She swallows hard and forces her voice steady. "Nurse, I need to step out. Hold the procedure."

The nurse blinks. "But—"

"Now," Lira snaps.

She doesn’t wait for a response. She’s already moving, her boots clicking against the tile as she strides toward the lift. Her fingers twitch at her side, itching to pull up the hidden memory, to confirm it’s still there. But she doesn’t dare. Not here. Not where the cameras can see.

The lift doors slide shut behind her. She’s alone.

For now.

---

The walk to Sector 9 is a blur. The corridors are too bright, the air too thin. Every enforcer she passes feels like a threat, every flicker of a monitor like an accusation. She tells herself it’s just a routine check. A glitch in the system. Nothing to do with the memory.

But when the doors to Sector 9 hiss open, she knows she’s lying to herself.

The room is small, windowless, the walls lined with blackened screens. A single figure sits at the desk, his back to her. He doesn’t turn as she enters.

"Editor Veyra," he says, his voice smooth, practiced. "Thank you for coming so quickly."

She recognizes him immediately. *Director Kael.* Head of Memory Compliance. The man who signs the orders for purges.

He gestures to the chair across from him. "Please. Sit."

Lira obeys, her spine rigid. "What’s this about, Director?"

Kael finally turns, his pale eyes locking onto hers. There’s no warmth in them. No humanity. Just calculation. "We’ve detected an anomaly in your recent extraction logs."

Her stomach drops. "Anomaly?"

"Subject 47-B," he says. "The memory you extracted was… incomplete."

Lira’s fingers dig into her thighs. "That’s not possible. The system flagged it for full removal."

Kael leans forward slightly. "And yet, when we reviewed the feed, we noticed a discrepancy. A secondary memory fragment was accessed but not logged."

Her pulse hammers in her throat. *They know.*

She forces a frown. "That’s impossible. The system would’ve alerted me."

Kael studies her for a long moment. Then, slowly, he smiles. It’s not a kind expression. "Would it?"

The air in the room thickens. Lira’s mind races. She could deny it. She could claim a malfunction, a glitch in the neural lace. But Kael isn’t stupid. He wouldn’t have called her here if he didn’t already have his answer.

She swallows. "Director, if there was an error, it was unintentional. I’ll submit a full report—"

Kael cuts her off with a raised hand. "That won’t be necessary."

A beat of silence. Then, quietly: "You saw something you weren’t supposed to see, didn’t you?"

Lira’s breath stutters. She could lie. She should lie.

But the weight of the hidden memory burns in her mind like a brand.

"Yes," she whispers.

Kael exhales, almost amused. "And what did you do with it?"

She meets his gaze. "I saved it."

The words hang between them, heavy with implication. Kael doesn’t move. Doesn’t react. Then, slowly, he reaches into his desk and pulls out a small device—a memory editor’s tool, but older, more brutal than the ones she uses.

"Editor Veyra," he says, his voice low, "you understand the consequences of what you’ve done."

She does.

But she also understands something else.

If she lets him erase her memory of the children, they’ll never be found. They’ll never be saved.

And she can’t live with that.

Lira stands abruptly, her chair scraping against the floor. "No."

Kael’s eyebrows lift. "No?"

She shakes her head, her voice steady despite the fear clawing at her chest. "I won’t let you take it."

For a moment, Kael just stares at her. Then, slowly, he sets the device down. "You realize this is treason."

"I realize," she says, "that you’re hiding something. And I want to know what it is."

Kael studies her for a long moment. Then, to her shock, he laughs—a dry, humorless sound. "You have no idea what you’re asking for."

"Then tell me," she demands.

He leans back in his chair, his expression unreadable. "Alright, Editor. Since you’re so eager to know…" He taps a command into his console. The screens behind him flicker to life, displaying rows of data—names, ages, vital signs. The same children from the memory.

Lira’s breath catches.

"Project Renewal," Kael says. "A classified initiative to ensure the future of the regime."

Her stomach twists. "You’re stealing children."

"We’re *preserving* them," Kael corrects. "The outside world is collapsing, Editor. Disease, war, famine. The regime is the only thing holding civilization together. And to survive, we need *continuity*."

Lira’s hands clench into fists. "You’re turning them into weapons."

Kael’s smile is cold. "We’re turning them into *leaders*."

She thinks of the pods, the tubes, the scientist’s detached voice. *Phase Two.* "And what happens to the ones who don’t… comply?"

Kael doesn’t answer. He doesn’t have to.

Lira’s mind races. She could run. She could try to expose this, to warn someone. But who would believe her? The regime controls everything—the memories, the truth, the people.

Unless…

Unless she gives them a memory they can’t ignore.

She meets Kael’s gaze, her voice low and steady. "You’re going to let me walk out of here."

Kael’s eyebrows lift. "Am I?"

"Yes," she says. "Because if you don’t, I’ll make sure every editor in this facility sees what I saw. And then you’ll have a rebellion on your hands."

Kael studies her for a long moment. Then, slowly, he smiles. "You’re bluffing."

Lira doesn’t flinch. "Try me."

Silence.

Then, Kael exhales, almost impressed. "You’re either very brave or very stupid, Editor."

"Maybe both," she says.

He leans forward, his voice a whisper. "If you walk out that door, you’ll be a fugitive. The enforcers will hunt you. The editors will turn on you. And when they find you—and they *will* find you—you’ll wish you’d let me erase your memory."

Lira holds his gaze. "Then I’ll make sure they don’t find me."

Kael studies her for another beat. Then, with a sigh, he waves a hand toward the door. "Go. Before I change my mind."

She doesn’t wait. She turns and strides out of the room, her heart pounding, her mind racing.

She has one chance.

And she’s going to take it.
