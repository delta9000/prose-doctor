**Chapter Twelve: The Man from the Black River**

The dam hummed before dawn, a low, steady vibration that traveled through the earth and into the bones of those who lived close enough to feel it. Elias Veyra woke to the sound, as he always did, his body attuned to the rhythm of the turbines even in sleep. He lay still for a moment, listening to the water rush through the penstocks, the distant clank of metal as the night crew adjusted the gates. The air smelled of damp stone and pine, the scent of the river thick in his throat.

Outside, the world was still dark, but not silent. The generator shed coughed to life, its diesel engine sputtering before settling into a rough purr. Somewhere downriver, a dog barked—once, twice—then fell quiet. Elias swung his legs over the edge of the cot and sat up, rubbing the sleep from his eyes. His fingers brushed the scar along his jawline, a habit he’d never broken. Twenty years, and the skin there still pulled tight when he yawned.

He dressed quickly in the dim light of a single oil lamp, the wick trimmed low to conserve fuel. His boots were worn but sturdy, the soles patched with scrap leather. He pulled on his jacket—a heavy thing, lined with the fur of a rabbit he’d trapped last winter—and stepped out into the predawn chill.

The village of Blackthorn was already stirring. Smoke curled from the chimneys of the longhouses, where families huddled around banked coals, stirring pots of porridge or heating water for tea. The dam loomed above it all, a monolith of concrete and steel, its face slick with mist. The river roared beneath it, a constant reminder of what they had rebuilt.

Elias made his way down the wooden walkway that connected the dam’s control house to the village proper. The planks were slick with dew, and he moved carefully, his breath fogging in the air. Below him, the reservoir stretched out like a dark mirror, its surface broken only by the occasional ripple of a fish or the wake of a night heron taking flight.

At the foot of the dam, the village square was already alive with activity. A group of children—no older than ten—knelt in the dirt, their small hands busy with the morning’s task: sorting through a pile of scavenged parts. Nuts, bolts, lengths of wire, all cleaned and organized for the day’s repairs. One of the boys, a wiry kid named Jace, looked up as Elias passed.

“Morning, Veyra,” he said, grinning. His teeth were crooked, but his eyes were sharp. “Heard the turbines were acting up last night.”

Elias nodded. “Gate three’s sticking again. We’ll need to bleed the system before the shift change.”

Jace’s grin widened. “I’ll tell Pa. He’s got a new grease blend he’s been dying to try.”

Elias clapped the boy on the shoulder and moved on. The village was a patchwork of salvaged materials—corrugated metal roofs, walls built from the skeletal remains of old cars, windows patched with plastic sheeting. But it was home. And it was theirs.

---

The dam had been a skeleton when they found it. The EMP had fried the control systems, left the turbines silent and the gates frozen. But the concrete had held. The river had kept flowing. And so had they.

Elias had been seventeen when the lights went out. He remembered the panic, the way the world had stuttered and then stopped. The cars dead in the streets, the phones useless, the hospitals dark. His father had been a civil engineer, one of the few who understood how the dam worked. He’d dragged Elias and his mother into the mountains, away from the cities, away from the chaos. They’d found Blackthorn by accident—a cluster of cabins, a few determined families, and the dam, half-buried in the wilderness.

It had taken years to bring it back. Years of scavenging, of trial and error, of learning how to coax power from the river without the grid to guide them. But they’d done it. And now, the dam was the heart of Blackthorn. It powered the lights in the longhouses, the pumps that drew water from the river, the forge where they melted down scrap metal into tools. It was their lifeline.

And it was why the stranger had come.

---

Elias found Councilor Mara at the edge of the square, her arms crossed as she watched the morning’s trade begin. A woman in a patched coat bartered a basket of eggs for a length of woven cloth. A man with a scarred face traded a handful of bullets for a jar of honey. Mara’s dark eyes missed nothing.

“You’re late,” she said without looking at him.

Elias shrugged. “Turbines needed checking.”

Mara finally turned, her braided hair catching the first light of dawn. She was in her fifties, her face lined with years of hard decisions. “The stranger’s still at the gate. Says he won’t come in until we agree to hear him out.”

Elias exhaled. “You believe him?”

“That he’s from another settlement?” Mara’s lips thinned. “Maybe. That he’s alone? Not a chance.”

Blackthorn had seen outsiders before. Most were desperate—hungry, sick, fleeing something worse. Some were dangerous. A few had stayed. But none had come claiming to represent a larger group. Not like this.

“What do you want to do?” Elias asked.

Mara’s gaze flickered to the dam, then back to him. “We hear him. But not here. Not in the square. Too many ears.”

Elias nodded. “The old mill?”

“Good. Quiet. And if he’s lying, we can toss him in the river before anyone’s the wiser.”

---

The stranger was lean, his face weathered by wind and sun. He wore a long coat of dark wool, the collar turned up against the cold. His boots were scuffed but well-made, the soles thick with layers of patched leather. He carried a rifle slung over his shoulder, but his hands were empty, held away from his body in a gesture of peace.

Elias and Mara met him at the edge of the village, where the road narrowed into a dirt path leading to the old mill. The building was half-collapsed, its roof sagging under the weight of years, but the stone walls still stood. It was a good place for secrets.

The stranger’s eyes flicked between them, assessing. “You’re the ones in charge?”

Mara didn’t answer. “You said you had a message.”

The man nodded. “Name’s Kael. I come from a settlement downriver—Black River. Heard you’ve got power here. Real power.”

Elias crossed his arms. “And?”

Kael’s gaze lingered on the dam, visible through the trees. “We’ve got people. Skilled people. Farmers, blacksmiths, doctors. But no power. Not like this.” He gestured toward the village. “We can trade. Food. Medicine. Tools. Whatever you need.”

Mara’s expression didn’t change. “And what do *you* need?”

“Access to the grid,” Kael said. “We’ve got a generator, but it’s old. Needs parts. Fuel. You’ve got the dam. You’ve got the power. We can help each other.”

Elias studied the man. His hands were steady. His voice was even. But there was something in his eyes—a hunger, maybe, or desperation. Or both.

“You’re not the first to ask,” Mara said. “We’ve managed fine on our own.”

Kael’s jaw tightened. “For how long? You think you’re the only ones who’ve rebuilt? There are others out there. Bigger groups. Stronger. They’ll come for what you’ve got. And when they do, you’ll wish you’d had allies.”

A silence settled between them, broken only by the distant rush of the river.

“You’re threatening us,” Elias said quietly.

Kael shook his head. “I’m warning you. Black River isn’t the only settlement looking for power. And not all of them will ask nicely.”

Mara exhaled sharply. “You want us to trust you. To let you in. To share what we’ve built. And you expect us to believe you’re the good guys?”

Kael met her gaze. “I expect you to be smart.”

---

The debate raged through the morning.

The village square was packed by midday, the air thick with the scent of woodsmoke and unwashed bodies. Voices clashed—some for caution, others for opportunity. Elias stood at the edge of the crowd, listening.

“We don’t know anything about them!” a woman shouted. “For all we know, they’ll take what they want and leave us with nothing!”

“Or they’ll bring raiders down on us!” a man added. “You think we’re the only ones who’ve heard about the dam? If they know, others do too!”

Mara stood on the steps of the longhouse, her voice cutting through the noise. “We don’t have to decide today. We can send a scouting party. See this Black River for ourselves.”

“And if it’s a trap?” someone called.

“Then we’ll know,” Mara said. “And we’ll be ready.”

Elias pushed through the crowd, making his way to the dam. The argument followed him, a low hum of fear and hope. He climbed the metal stairs to the control house, the familiar creak of the steps grounding him.

Inside, the air was cooler, the hum of the turbines louder. The control panel was a relic—salvaged wires, jury-rigged switches, a single dial that flickered with the dam’s output. Elias ran his fingers over the worn metal, feeling the vibration of the river’s power.

He thought of the stranger’s words. *They’ll come for what you’ve got.*

Blackthorn had survived this long by being careful. By being self-sufficient. But Kael was right about one thing—they weren’t the only ones left. And sooner or later, someone would come.

The question was whether they’d come as friends or as enemies.

---

That evening, Elias found Kael sitting by the river, his back against a fallen log. The stranger didn’t look up as Elias approached, but his hand rested on the rifle beside him.

“You’re still here,” Elias said.

Kael shrugged. “Didn’t figure you’d let me leave yet.”

Elias sat a few feet away, close enough to talk, far enough to react if he needed to. The river rushed past, its surface catching the last light of the setting sun. “You really think we’re in danger?”

Kael picked up a stone and skipped it across the water. “I think the world’s a lot smaller than it used to be. And power’s power. People will kill for it.”

Elias exhaled. “We’ve got rules here. No stealing. No violence. We work together, or we don’t survive.”

“Sounds like a good system,” Kael said. “But systems break.”

“Not if you don’t let them.”

Kael turned to him, his expression unreadable. “You really believe that?”

Elias didn’t answer. He thought of the years they’d spent rebuilding. The losses. The sacrifices. The way the dam had become more than just a source of power—it was a symbol. A promise.

And now, that promise was being tested.

---

The council met at dusk, the longhouse lit by the flickering glow of oil lamps. The air was thick with the scent of pine and sweat, the murmur of voices rising and falling like the river outside.

Mara stood at the head of the table, her hands pressed flat against the wood. “We have two choices. We send a scouting party to Black River, or we turn this man away and hope he’s telling the truth about the others.”

“And if he’s lying?” a voice called from the back.

“Then we’ve lost nothing,” Mara said. “But if he’s not…”

The room fell silent. Elias looked around at the faces—some fearful, some hopeful, all uncertain. He thought of the dam, of the power it held. Of the world beyond the river, a world they’d tried to forget.

“We send the scouting party,” he said.

Heads turned. Mara raised an eyebrow. “You’re sure?”

Elias nodded. “We can’t hide forever. And if there are others out there… we need to know what we’re facing.”

Mara studied him for a long moment, then nodded. “Then it’s decided. We send a party. Small. Quiet. And we see what’s downriver.”

The room erupted into murmurs, some in agreement, others in protest. But the decision was made.

As the meeting broke up, Elias stepped outside, the cool night air a relief after the stuffy longhouse. The dam loomed above him, its silhouette dark against the star-strewn sky. The river roared, a constant reminder of what they’d built.

And what they might lose.

Kael was waiting for him at the bottom of the steps, his rifle slung over his shoulder. “So?” he asked. “What’s the verdict?”

Elias met his gaze. “We’re sending a party. To Black River.”

Kael’s expression didn’t change, but something flickered in his eyes—relief, maybe, or satisfaction. “Good. You won’t regret it.”

Elias hoped he was right.

But as he turned back toward the dam, the weight of the decision settled over him like the coming night. The world had changed. And Blackthorn would have to change with it.

Whether they were ready or not.
