**Chapter Seven: The Ghost in the Alley**

The rain had been falling for three days straight when I got the call. Not the kind of rain that cleans the city, but the kind that turns sidewalks into slick black mirrors and makes the neon signs bleed. I was nursing a whiskey at the back of O’Malley’s, the kind of dive where the bartender doesn’t ask questions and the jukebox only plays Sinatra because the owner thinks it makes the place classy. The phone buzzed against the scarred wood of the bar, vibrating like a dying insect. I let it ring twice before I picked up.

“Marlowe,” I said, because that’s what the sign on my door says, even if it’s not my real name.

“Mr. Marlowe, my name is Eleanor Voss.” The voice was smooth, cultured, the kind of voice that belonged in a high-rise office with a view of the bay, not in my ear at 10:17 on a Tuesday night. “I need your help finding someone.”

I swirled the whiskey in my glass, watching the amber liquid cling to the sides. “Who’s missing?”

“My husband. Daniel Voss.”

The name didn’t ring any bells, but then, most of the names that crossed my desk didn’t. “How long?”

“Five days.”

I took a sip. The whiskey burned, but not enough. “You call the cops?”

A pause. “Yes. They’re… not optimistic.”

That meant they’d already written him off. Another rich guy who’d probably skipped town with his secretary or his mistress or whatever flavor of the week he was chasing. But the wife was paying, so I wasn’t in a position to turn down work.

“Where do you want to meet?”

---

Eleanor Voss lived in one of those glass-and-steel towers downtown, the kind of place where the doorman wears a suit that costs more than my monthly rent. She let me up without a word, and when the elevator doors opened, she was standing there in a black dress that probably had a designer name I couldn’t pronounce. Her hair was pulled back tight, her face carefully made up, but her eyes were red-rimmed, like she’d been crying or hadn’t slept in a while. Maybe both.

“Mr. Marlowe,” she said, stepping aside to let me in. “Thank you for coming.”

The apartment was all white and chrome, the kind of place that looked like it belonged in a magazine. No personal touches, no photos, no clutter. Just cold, expensive perfection. It made my skin itch.

She led me to a sitting area with a view of the city, all twinkling lights and distant sirens. “Can I get you a drink?”

“No, thanks.” I sat down, pulling out my notebook. “Tell me about Daniel.”

She perched on the edge of the sofa, her hands folded in her lap. “He’s a financial analyst. Works for Sterling & Lowe. He left for work on Monday morning, like always, but he never came home. His phone’s been off since Tuesday.”

“Any enemies? Debts? Bad habits?”

She hesitated. “Daniel was… ambitious. He made a lot of money for his clients, but he also made a lot of enemies. There were people who didn’t like the way he did business.”

“Like who?”

“Other firms. Competitors. A few clients who felt… misled.”

I scribbled in my notebook. “What about personal life? Any affairs? Gambling? Drugs?”

Her lips thinned. “Daniel wasn’t perfect, but he wasn’t a criminal. He worked hard. Too hard, sometimes.”

I didn’t press. Not yet. “When was the last time you saw him?”

“Monday morning. He kissed me goodbye, said he’d be home by seven. He always said that.”

“And when did you realize he was missing?”

“Tuesday night. I called his office, his friends, his colleagues. No one had seen him.”

“Did the cops find anything?”

She shook her head. “They said he probably left on his own. That he’d turn up eventually.”

I closed my notebook. “Mrs. Voss, I need to be honest with you. If your husband doesn’t want to be found, I might not be able to find him. And if I do, he might not want to come back.”

She looked at me, her eyes sharp. “I don’t care what he wants. I want to know where he is.”

I stood up. “I’ll need access to his financials, his phone records, his work emails. And I’ll need to talk to his colleagues.”

She nodded, reaching for a manila envelope on the coffee table. “Everything’s in here. His bank statements, his calendar, his contacts. His assistant at work, Lisa Chen, she’s expecting your call.”

I took the envelope. “I’ll be in touch.”

---

Daniel Voss’s office was on the 47th floor of a building that looked like it had been designed by someone who hated humanity. The receptionist, a young woman with a nameplate that read *Lisa Chen*, gave me a tight smile when I walked in.

“Mr. Marlowe,” she said. “Mrs. Voss called ahead.”

I nodded. “I need to ask you some questions about Daniel.”

She hesitated, then led me to a small conference room with a view of the bay. “What do you want to know?”

“When was the last time you saw him?”

“Monday morning. He came in early, like always. He had a meeting at nine, but he canceled it last minute. Said he had to take care of something personal.”

“Did he say what?”

She shook her head. “No. But he seemed… distracted. Like he was worried about something.”

“Did he have any enemies here? Anyone who didn’t like him?”

She bit her lip. “Daniel was good at his job. Too good, sometimes. He made people a lot of money, but he also made them nervous. There were rumors he was taking risks, betting big on volatile stocks. Some of the partners weren’t happy about it.”

“Any names?”

“Richard Kelleher. He’s one of the senior partners. He and Daniel had a… heated discussion last week.”

I wrote it down. “What about personal life? Any affairs? Gambling?”

She hesitated. “I don’t know about affairs. But Daniel liked to gamble. Not seriously, but enough that it was a problem. He owed some people money.”

“Who?”

“I don’t know. He never talked about it.”

I leaned back in my chair. “Did he have a safe deposit box? A second phone? Anywhere he might’ve stashed something he didn’t want found?”

She thought for a moment. “He had a storage unit. Down by the docks. He kept some old files there, things from before he worked here.”

I made a note. “Anything else?”

She shook her head. “No. But…” She hesitated. “Daniel wasn’t a bad man. He just made some bad choices.”

I stood up. “Thanks, Lisa.”

---

The storage unit was in a run-down complex near the docks, the kind of place where the rats outnumbered the humans and the air smelled like salt and rust. The manager, a grizzled old man with a cigarette dangling from his lips, barely glanced at me when I showed him Daniel’s key.

“Unit 127,” he grunted. “End of the row.”

The unit was small, crammed with boxes labeled in neat, precise handwriting. *Tax Records 2018-2020. Personal Files. Old Electronics.* I started with the personal files, flipping through bank statements, old letters, a few photos of Daniel with a woman who wasn’t Eleanor. I pocketed one of the photos—just in case.

Then I found the ledger.

It was tucked inside a manila envelope, hidden beneath a stack of old tax forms. The pages were filled with names, dates, and numbers—big numbers. Some were circled in red. Others had question marks next to them. At the back, a single name stood out: *Victor Kane.*

I knew that name. Everyone in the city did. Victor Kane was a loan shark, the kind of guy who didn’t just break legs—he broke entire lives. And Daniel Voss owed him money. A lot of it.

I flipped through the ledger again, my stomach tightening. The numbers added up to more than Daniel could’ve earned in a year, even with his salary. He’d been betting big, and he’d lost. And now he was gone.

---

Victor Kane’s office was above a strip club in the worst part of town. The kind of place where the neon sign flickered like a dying heartbeat and the bouncer at the door had a face like a bulldog who’d lost too many fights. He gave me a once-over, then jerked his chin toward the stairs.

“Kane’s expecting you.”

The office was small, cluttered, the air thick with cigarette smoke and the scent of cheap cologne. Victor Kane sat behind a desk that looked like it had been salvaged from a 1970s motel. He was a big man, broad-shouldered, with a face that had seen too many fists. His eyes were cold, calculating.

“Marlowe,” he said, not looking up from the newspaper he was reading. “Heard you’ve been asking about Daniel Voss.”

I sat down without being invited. “He owes you money.”

Kane folded the newspaper carefully, setting it aside. “He does.”

“How much?”

“Enough.”

I leaned forward. “Where is he?”

Kane smiled, but it wasn’t a friendly smile. “Why do you care?”

“His wife hired me to find him.”

Kane chuckled. “Eleanor Voss. Now there’s a woman who knows how to hold a grudge.” He leaned back in his chair, steepling his fingers. “Daniel came to me a few months ago. Needed cash. Said he had a sure thing, some stock that was gonna skyrocket. I lent him the money. He lost it all.”

“And now he’s missing.”

Kane shrugged. “People disappear all the time.”

“Did you have him disappeared?”

Kane’s smile faded. “I don’t kill people, Marlowe. I just make sure they understand the consequences of their actions.”

“So where is he?”

Kane reached into his desk drawer and pulled out a business card. He slid it across the desk. “Try this place. Ask for Maggie.”

I picked up the card. *The Silver Lining. A Place to Rest.* The address was in the industrial district, near the old train yards.

“What is this?”

“A motel,” Kane said. “For people who need to lie low.”

I stood up. “Thanks.”

Kane’s voice stopped me at the door. “Marlowe.”

I turned.

“If you find him, tell him I want my money. With interest.”

---

The Silver Lining was a two-story motel with peeling paint and a neon sign that buzzed like a swarm of angry bees. The parking lot was half-empty, the kind of place where people came to disappear. I knocked on the office door, and a woman in her sixties with dyed red hair and a cigarette dangling from her lips answered.

“Maggie?”

She squinted at me. “Who’s asking?”

“Name’s Marlowe. I’m looking for Daniel Voss.”

She exhaled a stream of smoke, her eyes narrowing. “Never heard of him.”

I pulled out the photo I’d taken from the storage unit—the one of Daniel with the other woman. “This is him. He’s been here.”

Maggie’s gaze flickered to the photo, then back to me. “You a cop?”

“Private investigator. His wife hired me.”

She studied me for a long moment, then stepped aside. “Room 207. But he ain’t there.”

I followed her inside. The office smelled like stale coffee and cigarette smoke. She rummaged through a drawer and pulled out a key.

“He checked in three days ago. Paid cash. Said he needed a place to think.”

“Did he say anything else?”

She shook her head. “Just that he didn’t want to be found.”

I took the key. “Thanks.”

Room 207 was at the end of the second-floor walkway. The door was slightly ajar, the room dark. I pushed it open, my hand hovering near the gun at my hip.

The room was empty.

The bed was unmade, the sheets rumpled. A half-empty bottle of whiskey sat on the nightstand, next to a glass with a lipstick stain. The closet was open, a few clothes hanging inside—men’s clothes, Daniel’s size. A suitcase lay open on the floor, half-packed.

I checked the bathroom. The mirror was fogged, the shower still damp. He’d been here recently.

Then I saw the note.

It was tucked under the whiskey bottle, a single sheet of paper with neat, precise handwriting. *Eleanor, I’m sorry. I can’t do this anymore. Don’t look for me.*

I sat down on the edge of the bed, the note in my hand. Daniel Voss didn’t want to be found. He’d run, and he’d left his wife behind.

I thought about Eleanor Voss, sitting in her cold, perfect apartment, waiting for answers. I thought about the ledger, the debts, the lies. I thought about the code I’d sworn to live by—the one that said I didn’t lie to clients, didn’t take their money for nothing.

But I also thought about the look in Maggie’s eyes when she’d handed me the key. The way she’d said, *He didn’t want to be found.*

I folded the note and slipped it into my pocket.

Then I walked out of the room and locked the door behind me.
