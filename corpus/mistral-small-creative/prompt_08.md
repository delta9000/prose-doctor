**Chapter Twelve: The Ink of Lies**

The quill trembled in Elara Vey’s fingers.

She had been copying the same passage for the third time that morning, her hand moving with the mechanical precision of a woman who had spent sixteen years transcribing the annals of the Kingdom of Veldros. The scriptorium’s high windows let in slanted bars of autumn light, gilding the parchment and the inkwell before her, turning the dust motes in the air to flecks of gold. The scent of beeswax and aged vellum filled her lungs, familiar as breath.

And yet.

Her eyes snagged on the words again.

*"By the decree of King Aldric the Second, in the Year of the Shattered Moon, the northern provinces were granted autonomy in matters of trade, so long as tribute was paid in full and fealty sworn anew at the solstice. This was done in the presence of the High Arbiter and the Lords of the Council, and the terms were sealed with blood and silver."*

Elara’s brow furrowed. She had copied this exact passage—word for word, stroke for stroke—from the original record just last week. But the original record, the one kept under lock and key in the Archival Vault, had read differently.

She was certain of it.

Her memory was not infallible, but it was *good*. She had spent her life memorizing dates, names, the precise phrasing of treaties and edicts. And she remembered, with the clarity of a blade drawn across skin, that the original text had not mentioned the High Arbiter. Nor had it spoken of blood and silver.

She set the quill down.

The scriptorium was quiet, the only sounds the scratch of other scribes’ pens and the distant murmur of the city beyond the thick stone walls. Master Harkin, the head archivist, was at his desk across the room, his back to her as he pored over a ledger. No one was watching. No one ever watched.

Elara reached for the stack of parchment beside her—the original records she had been given to copy. She flipped through them, her pulse quickening. There. The decree from Aldric the Second. She read it again, her lips moving silently.

*"By the decree of King Aldric the Second, in the Year of the Shattered Moon, the northern provinces were granted autonomy in matters of trade, so long as tribute was paid in full and fealty sworn anew at the solstice. This was done in the presence of the Lords of the Council, and the terms were sealed with ink and parchment."*

No High Arbiter. No blood. No silver.

Her stomach twisted.

She had made a mistake. She *had* to have. Perhaps she had misread the original, or perhaps the copy she held now was the one that had been altered. But no—she had been the one to fetch the original from the vault just days ago. She had handled it herself. She had *seen* it.

Unless someone had changed it *after* she had copied it.

The thought sent a chill down her spine.

Elara glanced around the scriptorium again, her fingers tightening around the edge of the parchment. The other scribes were absorbed in their work, their heads bent low. Master Harkin was still at his desk, his quill scratching steadily across the page.

She had to be sure.

Carefully, she slid the original decree from the stack and tucked it into the sleeve of her robe, the parchment cool against her wrist. She would compare it to the copy in the vault. If they matched, then she had been mistaken. If they didn’t—

She didn’t let herself finish the thought.

---

The Archival Vault was a cavern of knowledge, its shelves stretching into the dimness like the ribs of some great, slumbering beast. The air was thick with the scent of old paper and leather, the silence so profound it hummed in her ears. Elara moved swiftly, her slippers making no sound on the stone floor.

The decree from Aldric the Second was kept in the northern wing, in a section reserved for royal edicts. She found the correct shelf, her fingers tracing the spines of the bound volumes until she located the one she sought. She pulled it free, her breath shallow.

The book was heavy, its cover tooled with the royal crest. She opened it carefully, turning the pages until she found the decree. Her eyes scanned the text, her heart pounding.

*"By the decree of King Aldric the Second, in the Year of the Shattered Moon, the northern provinces were granted autonomy in matters of trade, so long as tribute was paid in full and fealty sworn anew at the solstice. This was done in the presence of the High Arbiter and the Lords of the Council, and the terms were sealed with blood and silver."*

Her blood turned to ice.

The original record—the one she had copied from just days ago—had been altered. Someone had added the High Arbiter. Someone had changed *ink and parchment* to *blood and silver*.

And that someone had done it *after* she had already transcribed it.

Elara’s hands shook as she closed the book. She had to think. She had to understand what this meant.

The High Arbiter was a figure of legend, a myth from the old days when the kingdom had been fractured and warring. The idea that Aldric the Second—who had ruled a century ago, in an era of relative peace—had involved the Arbiter in a trade decree was absurd. It was *impossible*.

Unless it wasn’t.

Unless the records had been altered for a reason.

Elara’s mind raced. The northern provinces had been restless of late, their lords whispering of independence, of breaking free from the crown. If the king’s historians were changing the past, twisting it to suit the present—

She swallowed hard.

This was treason. This was *dangerous*.

She had to tell someone. But who? Master Harkin? No—he was the one who assigned the records to be copied. He was the one who oversaw the vault. If the alterations were being made under his authority, then he was complicit.

The king’s spymaster, then? Lord Dain? But Dain was a serpent, a man who played games with lives as easily as others played at dice. If she went to him, she would be admitting she knew something she shouldn’t. And men like Dain did not suffer loose ends.

Elara’s fingers curled into fists.

She had to be careful. She had to *think*.

First, she needed to see if this was an isolated incident. She needed to check other records, other decrees. She needed to see if the past had been rewritten in more places than this.

She replaced the book on the shelf, her movements precise, controlled. She would come back later, when the vault was empty. She would search. She would find the truth.

And then she would decide what to do with it.

---

That night, Elara did not sleep.

She lay in her narrow cot in the scribes’ dormitory, staring at the ceiling, her mind a whirlwind of possibilities. The other women in the room breathed deeply, their sleep untroubled. Elara envied them.

She waited until the moon was high, until the city beyond the scriptorium walls was silent and still. Then she rose, dressing quickly in the dark. She moved like a shadow, her bare feet silent on the stone floor.

The vault was locked, but she had a key. All the scribes did—it was necessary for their work. She turned it in the heavy iron lock, the mechanism groaning softly as it gave way.

Inside, the vault was darker than the dormitory, the only light the pale glow of the moon through the high, narrow windows. Elara lit a single candle, its flame casting long, wavering shadows.

She worked quickly, pulling volumes from the shelves, flipping through the pages. She checked decrees, treaties, accounts of battles and coronations. She looked for discrepancies, for changes, for the telltale signs of a hand that had altered the past.

And she found them.

A battle that had been lost was now a victory. A treaty that had been broken was suddenly honored. A king who had been weak was now strong, his reign marked by wisdom instead of folly.

Her hands trembled as she closed the last book.

The past had not just been altered. It had been *rewritten*.

And she was the only one who knew.

---

Elara did not return to the dormitory that night.

Instead, she went to the one place in the scriptorium where she could think, where she could be alone. The old reading chamber, a forgotten corner of the building where the air was thick with dust and the scent of aged parchment. It was a place the other scribes avoided, a place where the past felt closer, more tangible.

She sat at the long, scarred table, her candle flickering before her. The books she had taken from the vault were spread out in front of her, their pages open to the altered passages.

She had to decide what to do.

If she stayed silent, if she pretended she had seen nothing, she would be complicit. She would be part of the lie. But if she spoke, if she revealed what she knew—

She would be a threat.

The king’s historians did not take kindly to those who questioned their work. And the king himself? Aldric the Third was a man who valued control above all else. He would not tolerate a scribe—*a woman*, no less—challenging the official record.

Elara’s fingers traced the edge of one of the books, her touch light, reverent.

She thought of the northern lords, of their whispers of rebellion. She thought of the High Arbiter, a figure pulled from myth to justify the king’s grip on power. She thought of the blood and silver, of the way the past had been twisted to serve the present.

And she thought of the danger.

If she was caught, if she was accused of treason—

She would disappear. Just like the others.

Elara had heard the rumors, the whispers in the dark. Scribes who had asked too many questions. Historians who had challenged the official record. They vanished, their names struck from the rolls, their memories erased as thoroughly as the ink on the page.

She could not let that happen to her.

But she could not stay silent, either.

There had to be another way.

Elara leaned back in her chair, her eyes burning with exhaustion. She had spent her life serving the kingdom, serving the truth. And now she was being asked to serve a lie.

She would not do it.

But she would be smart. She would be careful.

She would find a way to expose the truth without exposing herself.

And she would start with the one person she trusted above all others.

Her brother.

---

Liran Vey was a merchant, a man who moved through the world with the easy confidence of someone who had never known the weight of a quill in his hand. He was everything Elara was not—bold, brash, unafraid of the dangers that lurked in the shadows.

She found him in the market square, his stall piled high with bolts of silk and spices from the southern lands. He was laughing with a customer, his dark eyes crinkling at the corners, his teeth white against his sun-browned skin.

Elara waited until the customer had left before stepping forward.

"Liran," she said, her voice low.

He turned, his smile fading as he saw her face. "Elara? What’s wrong?"

She glanced around, her heart pounding. The market was busy, the air filled with the cries of vendors and the chatter of buyers. No one was paying them any mind.

"I need to talk to you," she said. "Somewhere private."

Liran’s expression darkened. He nodded, gesturing for his apprentice to mind the stall. Then he led her through the winding streets, his stride long and purposeful.

They ended up in a quiet alley, the walls slick with moisture, the air thick with the scent of damp stone. Liran turned to her, his arms crossed.

"Tell me," he said.

Elara took a deep breath. And then she told him everything.

She told him about the altered records, about the High Arbiter, about the blood and silver. She told him about the danger, about the risk of speaking out. And she told him about her fear, the cold, gnawing terror that had taken root in her gut.

When she was done, Liran was silent for a long moment. His jaw was tight, his eyes hard.

"You’re sure about this?" he asked finally.

Elara nodded. "I’ve seen it with my own eyes."

Liran exhaled sharply, running a hand through his dark hair. "This is bad, Elara. This is *very* bad."

"I know," she whispered.

He looked at her, his gaze searching. "What do you want to do?"

Elara swallowed. "I don’t know. But I can’t just—let it happen. I can’t let them rewrite the past like this."

Liran was quiet for another moment. Then he nodded, his expression grim. "Then we’ll find a way to stop them."

Elara felt a flicker of hope, fragile and bright. "We?"

Liran’s mouth quirked, just slightly. "You didn’t think I’d let you face this alone, did you?"

Elara’s throat tightened. She reached out, her fingers brushing his sleeve. "Thank you."

Liran covered her hand with his, his grip warm and steady. "We’ll figure this out, Elara. Together."

And for the first time since she had discovered the truth, Elara allowed herself to believe that maybe—just maybe—it would be all right.
