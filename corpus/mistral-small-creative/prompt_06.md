**Chapter Seven: The Weight of Whispers**

The air in the apothecary was thick with the scent of crushed comfrey and the sharp tang of vinegar. Elspeth wiped her brow with the back of her wrist, leaving a streak of dried yarrow across her forehead. The mortar and pestle lay abandoned on the scarred wooden table, half a dozen poultices still to be mixed before dusk. Outside, the city of Veldros groaned under the weight of the sickness—another wave of the Black Cough, they called it, though Elspeth knew better. It was no single malady, but a tangle of fevers, rot, and despair, fed by the filth in the streets and the rats that thrived in the shadows.

She reached for the jar of elderflowers, her fingers brushing the worn leather of her mother’s old recipe book. The pages were soft with age, the ink faded in places, but the words still held their power. *For the lungs, steeped with honey and thyme. For the fever, willow bark and mint.* Simple things. True things. And yet, with every passing day, the whispers grew louder.

*"Witchcraft."*
*"Herbs won’t save them."*
*"The priest says it’s God’s judgment."*

Elspeth’s jaw tightened. She had heard it all before.

A knock at the door—hesitant, then insistent. She set down the mortar and crossed the dim room, her boots scuffing against the rush-strewn floor. When she pulled the door open, a woman stood there, her face gaunt, her eyes red-rimmed. Behind her, the narrow street was slick with rain, the cobblestones glistening like broken teeth.

“Please,” the woman rasped. “My boy—he can’t breathe.”

Elspeth didn’t hesitate. “Bring him inside.”

---

The child was no older than eight, his small body wracked with coughs that sounded like wet cloth tearing. His mother hovered nearby, wringing her hands, her breath coming in shallow gasps of her own. Elspeth knelt beside the pallet she had laid out near the hearth, pressing her palm to the boy’s forehead. His skin burned.

“How long?” she asked.

“Three days,” the mother whispered. “The priest came yesterday. He said… he said it was sin that brought this on us. That we must pray, not—” She gestured weakly at the bundles of herbs hanging from the rafters. “Not *this*.”

Elspeth’s fingers twitched. She had heard that, too. Father Aldric had been preaching from the steps of the cathedral for weeks now, his voice booming over the sick and the dying. *Repentance, not potions. Faith, not folly.* As if God had ever answered a single prayer for the poor in the slums.

She turned back to the boy. “His lungs are full. I’ll need to draw the phlegm out.” She reached for a clay pot of mustard seeds and a length of linen. “Hold him steady.”

The mother nodded, her grip tightening on her son’s shoulders as Elspeth mixed the paste. The boy whimpered, his lips cracked and dry. “It’ll sting,” she murmured, “but it’ll help you breathe.”

She spread the paste thickly over his chest, then wrapped the linen around him, securing it with a knot. The boy gasped, his back arching, but after a moment, the worst of the coughing eased. His mother let out a shuddering breath.

“Thank you,” she whispered.

Elspeth didn’t answer. She was already measuring out a tincture of horehound and coltsfoot, her mind racing. The boy would need more—broth, rest, clean air—but the slums offered none of those. And if the cough turned to blood, as it so often did…

She handed the vial to the mother. “Three drops in warm water, every few hours. Keep him warm, but not too close to the fire.”

The woman clutched the vial like a talisman. “Will he live?”

Elspeth met her eyes. “I don’t know.”

The truth was a blade, but it was all she had.

---

The bell for Vespers had just begun to toll when the priest arrived.

Elspeth was in the back room, crushing dried chamomile, when the door slammed open. She didn’t need to turn to know who it was—the scent of incense and self-righteousness preceded him.

“Elspeth of Veldros,” Father Aldric intoned, his voice dripping with the kind of piety that made her teeth ache. “I had hoped you would see reason by now.”

She set the pestle down slowly and turned. The priest stood in the doorway, his robes immaculate, his face a mask of disapproval. Behind him, two of the cathedral’s guards loomed, their hands resting on the hilts of their swords.

“Reason?” Elspeth wiped her hands on her apron. “You mean the kind that lets children die while you preach about sin?”

Aldric’s lips thinned. “You dare speak to me of sin? You, who peddles your pagan remedies like some hedge-witch? The people are suffering, and you offer them *herbs* instead of salvation.”

“Salvation won’t stop the coughing,” she said. “Nor will it fill their bellies or clean the filth from their streets. But you know that, don’t you? It’s easier to blame the sick than to fix what ails them.”

The priest’s face darkened. “You test the limits of my patience, woman. The bishop has already warned you. One more accusation of witchcraft, and you’ll answer to the Inquisitor.”

Elspeth felt the weight of his words like a stone in her gut. She had heard the stories—whispers of pyres in the countryside, of men and women dragged from their homes for daring to heal. But Veldros was a city of merchants and scholars, not some backwater village. Surely, they wouldn’t—

“You think yourself above the law?” Aldric pressed. “Above *God*?”

She met his gaze, unflinching. “I think I’m above *you*.”

For a moment, the priest said nothing. Then, with a slow, deliberate motion, he reached into his robe and withdrew a small wooden cross. He held it out to her, the symbol of his authority.

“Take it,” he said. “Swear on it that you will cease this heresy. Swear that you will close this den of sorcery and seek forgiveness.”

Elspeth didn’t move.

Aldric’s eyes narrowed. “Very well.” He turned to the guards. “Seize her.”

---

The cell was small, the air thick with the stench of damp stone and old fear. Elspeth sat on the thin pallet, her back against the wall, her knees drawn to her chest. The guards had taken her herbs, her tools, even the small knife she used for cutting roots. All that remained was the weight of her own doubt, gnawing at her like a rat in the dark.

*What if he’s right?*

The thought slithered into her mind, unwelcome. She had spent her life learning the old ways, the true ways—the ones that didn’t rely on miracles or divine intervention, but on the earth itself. Her mother had taught her, and her mother before her. They had never been witches. They had been *healers*.

But what if the people were right? What if her knowledge was just another kind of pride? What if God *did* demand suffering, and she was standing in His way?

She pressed her palms to her eyes, as if she could squeeze the thoughts out. No. No, she had seen the truth with her own hands. She had watched patients recover when they should have died, had seen the way a simple poultice could draw out poison, the way willow bark could ease a fever. Those were not lies. Those were *facts*.

And yet…

Aldric’s voice echoed in her memory. *Witchcraft. Heresy. The Inquisitor.*

She had heard the screams from the pyres before. Not in Veldros—never here—but in the villages beyond the city walls. Men and women burned for knowing too much, for daring to think beyond the church’s teachings.

Her hands trembled.

What if she was next?

---

The door creaked open.

Elspeth lifted her head, expecting a guard, a priest, some new torment. But it was only the old jailer, his face lined with exhaustion. He carried a wooden tray—a bowl of thin gruel, a chunk of black bread, a cup of water.

“Eat,” he muttered, setting it down before her. “You’ll need your strength.”

She didn’t touch it. “For what?”

The jailer hesitated. Then, in a voice so low she almost missed it, he said, “The Inquisitor arrives at dawn.”

The words hit her like a blow. She had known it was coming, but hearing it aloud made it real. The Inquisitor. The man who decided who lived and who burned.

The jailer shifted uncomfortably. “I—I shouldn’t have told you. But my daughter… she’s sick. The cough. You helped her last winter, when the fever took her. I remember.”

Elspeth looked up at him, really looked at him for the first time. His eyes were red-rimmed, his hands rough with callouses. A father. A man who had watched his child suffer and been powerless to stop it.

“What’s her name?” Elspeth asked softly.

“Mira.”

She nodded. “Bring her to me. Before dawn.”

The jailer’s breath hitched. “They’ll kill me.”

“Then don’t let them find out.”

He stared at her for a long moment. Then, with a jerky nod, he turned and left, the door clanging shut behind him.

Elspeth exhaled slowly. She reached for the bowl of gruel, her fingers steady now. She would eat. She would rest. And when the girl came, she would do what she had always done.

She would heal.

Even if it damned her.
