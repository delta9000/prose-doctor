**Chapter Seven: The Roots Remember**

The first frost had come early that year, a silver breath on the pumpkins before the sun had fully risen. Elias knelt in the damp earth, his fingers working the soil around the base of the last gourd, pressing the loam firm against its swollen belly. The vines had run wild this season, twisting through the rows of kale and beet greens like serpents, as if the plants themselves had conspired to slow his hands. He didn’t mind. The garden was the only thing that still asked nothing of him.

He wiped his brow with the back of his wrist, leaving a streak of dirt across his temple. The forest watched. It always watched.

Beyond the split-rail fence, the trees stood unnaturally still, their branches heavy with the weight of unfallen leaves. Oak and ash and birch, their bark grooved like the palms of old men. Elias had learned not to trust their stillness. The forest moved when he wasn’t looking—an inch here, a foot there—creeping closer while his back was turned. He had measured it once, years ago, driving a stake into the earth at the tree line and marking the distance with a length of twine. When he checked again three months later, the stake had vanished, swallowed by the undergrowth. The twine remained, stretched taut between the fence and the first row of trunks, as if the forest had decided to keep it as a trophy.

He reached for the pruning shears and snipped a dead runner from the squash vine. The blade was sharp, honed daily on the whetstone he kept in his pocket. A habit from another life. The garden required precision, but not the kind that left men bleeding in the dirt. Here, the violence was quiet: the severing of a stem, the uprooting of a weed, the careful thinning of carrots so the remaining might grow fat and sweet. It was a different kind of war, one he could win.

A rustle in the underbrush made him pause. Not the wind—too deliberate, too *aware*. He didn’t turn. If it was a deer, it would bolt. If it was something else, it would wait.

“You’re late,” he murmured.

The rustling stopped. Then, a voice—dry as dead leaves:

“You’re observant.”

Elias exhaled through his nose and turned. Old Man Harkin stood at the fence, his gnarled hands resting on the top rail. His coat was patched at the elbows, the wool frayed at the cuffs. He had the look of a man who had long since given up on mending things.

“Thought you might’ve gone back to the city,” Elias said.

Harkin snorted. “City’s no place for a man who remembers how to breathe.” He jerked his chin toward the forest. “You notice it’s getting bolder?”

Elias didn’t answer. He didn’t need to.

Harkin spat into the dirt. “My granddaddy used to say the woods here were hungry. Said they’d take a man if he wasn’t careful.” He squinted at Elias. “You ever think about that? About what it wants?”

Elias set the shears down and wiped his hands on his thighs. “It wants what everything wants. Space.”

“Space ain’t all it is.” Harkin’s voice dropped. “My boy went in there once. Twenty years back. Said he heard singing.” His fingers tightened on the fence. “Came out three days later, didn’t say a word. Just… changed. Like something had taken root in him.”

Elias picked up a trowel and turned it over in his hands. The wood was smooth from years of use, the blade still bright. “People change.”

“Not like that.” Harkin’s eyes were sharp, almost feverish. “You ever been in there?”

“No.”

“Liar.”

Elias didn’t flinch. The trowel bit into the earth beside the pumpkin, turning the soil. “I’ve been in worse places.”

Harkin studied him for a long moment. Then, with a grunt, he reached into his coat and pulled out a mason jar, half-full of something amber and cloudy. “Brought you this. Dandelion wine. Last of the batch.”

Elias took it, the glass cool against his palm. “Appreciate it.”

Harkin nodded, then turned to go. “You ever decide to leave, you let me know. I’ll help you burn the place down.”

Elias watched him shuffle back toward the road, his boots kicking up little puffs of dust. The forest seemed to lean in, listening.

---

The sun was low when Elias finally stood, his knees popping like dry kindling. He stretched, rolling his shoulders to ease the ache in his back. The garden was quiet now, the last of the daylight pooling in the hollows between the rows. He had worked the soil until his hands forgot how to make fists.

He gathered his tools—the trowel, the shears, the pruning knife—and carried them to the shed. The door hung crooked on its hinges, the wood swollen from years of rain. Inside, the air smelled of oil and rust. He hung the tools on their hooks, each in its place. Order was a kind of armor.

As he turned to leave, his boot caught on something. He looked down.

A root.

Thick as his wrist, it coiled across the threshold, pale and glistening. He hadn’t noticed it before. He knelt, running his fingers along its length. Smooth. Almost *soft*. Not like the gnarled roots of the oaks or the brittle tendrils of the brambles. This was something else.

He followed it with his eyes. It vanished beneath the shed, burrowing into the earth like a worm.

Elias stood slowly. The forest had never come this close before.

He stepped outside and circled the shed. The root emerged on the far side, slithering toward the fence. He crouched, pressing his palm to the soil. The earth was warm. *Breathing*.

A memory surfaced, unbidden: the jungle, the way the ground had trembled before the ambush. The way the trees had *moved*.

His hand twitched toward the knife at his belt. Old habits.

He forced himself to exhale. This wasn’t the jungle. This was his garden. His land.

He stood and walked to the fence. The root thickened as it neared the tree line, merging with the tangle of roots that knotted the forest floor. He could see where it had pushed through the soil, the earth split like a wound.

Elias reached into his pocket and pulled out the whetstone. He ran his thumb along its edge, feeling the familiar bite. Then he knelt and pressed the stone to the root.

It didn’t bleed. It *screamed*.

The sound was high and thin, like a child’s wail, and it cut through the evening air like a blade. Elias jerked back, the stone clattering to the ground. The root twitched, then stilled. The forest held its breath.

Silence.

Then, from the trees, a whisper:

*“You remember.”*

Elias didn’t move. The voice was neither male nor female, neither young nor old. It was the sound of wind through dead leaves, of water over stone.

He picked up the whetstone and stood. His hands were steady.

“No,” he said. “I don’t.”

The forest sighed. The trees swayed, though there was no wind.

Elias turned his back on them and walked to the house. The screen door groaned as he pushed it open. Inside, the air was thick with the scent of dried herbs and old woodsmoke. He washed his hands at the sink, scrubbing the dirt from beneath his nails. The water ran brown, then clear.

He dried his hands on a towel and poured himself a glass of Harkin’s dandelion wine. It was sweet, with a bitter aftertaste. Like memory.

Outside, the forest waited.

Elias sat at the kitchen table and sharpened his knife. The blade caught the last of the light, flashing like a signal. He didn’t look up.

The garden would need tending in the morning.
