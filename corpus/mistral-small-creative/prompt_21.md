**Chapter Seven: The Tide’s Old Secrets**

The harbor at dusk was a place of slow, creaking rhythms—wooden piers groaning under the weight of lobster traps, the slap of water against hulls, the distant clatter of a winch hauling in the day’s last catch. Elias Carter stood at the edge of the dock, his sneakers scuffed with salt and the faint, metallic tang of fish guts clinging to the air. He’d come here every evening for the past week, not to help his dad with the boat like he used to, but to stare at the water as if it might give up its secrets.

His grandfather’s boat, the *Marigold*, was still tied up at the far end of the dock, its peeling blue paint faded to the color of old bruises. No one had touched it since the funeral. Elias’s father had muttered something about selling it, but the words had dissolved into the kind of silence that meant the subject was closed. The *Marigold* was just another relic now, like the yellowed maps in the house or the old man’s pipe, still sitting in the ashtray by the armchair.

Elias jumped down onto the deck, the wood shifting slightly under his weight. The cabin door was unlocked—his dad had never bothered to check—and inside, the air smelled of damp wool and something sharper, like turpentine. He ran his fingers along the edge of the navigation table, where a half-empty bottle of whiskey rolled slightly with the motion of the waves. His grandfather had always kept a bottle there, "for the cold," he’d say with a wink. Elias had never asked what kind of cold he meant.

He knelt and pulled open the storage compartment beneath the bench. Inside, tangled in a nest of frayed rope, was a waterlogged ledger, its pages swollen with seawater. He’d found it three days ago, tucked beneath a loose floorboard in the house, and had been too afraid to open it then. Now, his fingers trembled as he flipped through the pages. The handwriting was his grandfather’s—loopy, confident—but the entries made no sense. Not at first.

*June 12th, 1987 – 200 lbs, northern run. Met J at the usual. Paid in cash, no questions.*
*July 5th – Delay at customs. Had to offload near Bar Harbor. J says it’s getting risky.*

Elias’s stomach twisted. He didn’t know what "200 lbs" referred to, but he knew *J*. Everyone in town knew *J*—James O’Donnell, the old fisherman who ran the bait shop and had a reputation for knowing things he shouldn’t. Elias had seen him at the funeral, standing at the back of the church with his hands clasped, his face unreadable.

He closed the ledger and pressed it to his chest. The *Marigold* wasn’t just a fishing boat. It never had been.

---

The bait shop smelled of brine and decaying kelp. James O’Donnell was behind the counter, his thick fingers sorting through a bucket of squirming herring. He looked up when the bell jingled, his sharp eyes landing on Elias.

"Well, if it isn’t young Carter," James said, wiping his hands on his apron. "You here for worms, or you just missin’ the old man?"

Elias swallowed. "I need to ask you something."

James leaned against the counter, his expression unreadable. "Alright. But I ain’t promisin’ answers."

Elias pulled the ledger from his jacket and slid it across the counter. James didn’t touch it. He just stared at the swollen pages, his jaw tightening.

"You know what this is," Elias said.

James exhaled through his nose. "Where’d you find it?"

"Does it matter?"

"Yeah, kid. It does."

Elias hesitated. "Under the floorboards. In the house."

James nodded slowly, as if that explained something. "Your granddad was a careful man. If he left that where you could find it, he wanted you to."

Elias’s chest tightened. "Wanted me to what? Know he was a smuggler?"

James didn’t flinch. "Smuggler’s a harsh word. He moved things. Things people needed."

"Like what?"

James glanced toward the door, then lowered his voice. "Medicine, mostly. Back in the eighties, before the border got tight. Folks up north—Canada—couldn’t get what they needed. Your granddad helped."

Elias stared at him. "That’s illegal."

James shrugged. "So’s starvin’."

Elias thought of his grandfather—the way he’d ruffle his hair, the stories he’d tell about the sea, the way he’d always slip Elias an extra dollar when his dad wasn’t looking. None of it fit with this. None of it fit with the man who’d taught him to tie knots and check the weather and respect the water.

"Why didn’t he tell me?" Elias asked, his voice cracking.

James’s expression softened. "Some things ain’t for kids to know. Not till they’re ready."

Elias clenched his fists. "I’m not a kid."

James studied him for a long moment. "No. Reckon you ain’t." He reached under the counter and pulled out a small, rusted key. "Your granddad left this with me. Said if anything ever happened to him, I was to give it to you when you were old enough to understand."

Elias took the key, turning it over in his fingers. It was cold, heavy. "What’s it for?"

James jerked his chin toward the harbor. "The *Marigold*. There’s a compartment under the stern. Not the one you’re thinkin’ of—the real one. The one he didn’t want your dad to find."

Elias’s pulse quickened. "What’s in it?"

James shook his head. "That’s for you to see. But Elias?" He leaned forward, his voice low. "Your granddad did what he thought was right. And he loved you. Don’t you forget that."

---

The compartment was hidden beneath a false panel, its edges nearly seamless. Elias had to pry it open with a screwdriver, his hands slick with sweat. Inside, wrapped in oilcloth, was a wooden box. He lifted it out, his breath shallow.

The box wasn’t locked. The key fit the hasp, but the lid lifted easily, as if it had been waiting for him. Inside, nestled in straw, was a stack of yellowed envelopes, their edges frayed. Elias pulled one out at random.

*June 3, 1988*
*Dear Mr. Carter,*
*I don’t know how to thank you. The medicine you brought—it saved my daughter. She’s breathing easy now, and the doctor says she’ll be fine. I don’t have much, but if there’s ever anything I can do…*
*—M. L.*

Elias’s throat burned. He pulled out another.

*October 12, 1990*
*You’re a good man, Carter. Not many would risk what you do. If the law ever comes knockin’, you tell ‘em James O’Donnell sent ‘em. They’ll think twice.*
*—J.*

His hands shook as he flipped through the rest. Letters from strangers, from people his grandfather had never mentioned. Gratitude. Fear. Love, even. And then, at the bottom of the box, a single photograph. His grandfather, younger, standing on the deck of the *Marigold*, his arm around a woman Elias didn’t recognize. She was smiling, her hand resting on her swollen belly. On the back, in his grandfather’s handwriting: *Lena and me, 1979.*

Elias’s breath hitched. His grandmother had died before he was born. He’d never seen a picture of her pregnant.

He sat back on his heels, the weight of the box heavy in his lap. His grandfather hadn’t just been a smuggler. He’d been a man who carried secrets—secrets that had shaped lives, that had saved people. And he’d done it all in silence, in the dead of night, while the rest of the town slept.

Elias thought of his father, who had spent his life following the rules, who had never once questioned the *Marigold*’s late-night trips or the extra cash that always seemed to appear in the cookie jar. His father, who had buried his own father with a eulogy about honesty and hard work, never knowing the truth.

Loyalty was a complicated thing. Elias had always thought it meant blind obedience, unquestioning trust. But now he wondered if it was something else—something quieter, something that lived in the spaces between what was said and what was done.

He traced his fingers over the letters, over the photograph. His grandfather had loved him. That much was clear. But love wasn’t just about the stories you told. It was about the ones you kept.

Elias closed the box and locked it. He would tell his father. Eventually. But not yet. Some secrets, like the tide, needed time to reveal themselves.

Outside, the harbor lights flickered to life, casting long shadows over the water. The *Marigold* creaked softly, as if whispering to him. Elias took a deep breath, the salt air filling his lungs.

He had a lot to think about. And for the first time, he understood that some answers weren’t meant to be found all at once. Some were meant to be uncovered, slowly, like the slow reveal of the moon over the water.
