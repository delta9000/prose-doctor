**Chapter Seven: The Pattern in the Dark**

The fluorescent lights of Mercy General’s ICU hummed like a swarm of distant bees, casting a sickly yellow glow over the rows of beds. The air smelled of antiseptic and something older—damp concrete and the metallic tang of blood. Clara Voss adjusted the elastic band of her surgical cap, her fingers brushing against the dampness at her hairline. The night shift had only just begun, but the weight of it already pressed down on her shoulders.

She moved between the beds with practiced efficiency, checking monitors, adjusting IV drips, murmuring reassurances to the half-asleep patients. Most of them wouldn’t remember her in the morning. That was the way of night nursing—you were a ghost, a fleeting presence in the dark hours when the body fought its hardest to heal or surrender.

Clara paused at Bed 7. Mr. Harold Finch, 78, post-op cardiac bypass. His vitals were stable, his breathing steady. She adjusted the oxygen tubing beneath his nose, her fingers lingering for a moment on his wrist. His pulse was strong, but his skin was cool, almost clammy. A shiver ran down her spine. She’d felt this before.

She pulled up his chart on the tablet, scrolling through the notes. Admitted three days prior. Surgery went well. Recovery on track. No red flags.

But Clara’s stomach twisted.

She moved to the next bed, then the next, her eyes flicking between the patients and the tablets mounted at each station. Something gnawed at her, a persistent itch at the back of her mind. She pulled up the patient logs for the past month, filtering for cardiac patients, post-op recoveries. Her breath hitched.

Five. Five cardiac patients in the last four weeks. All post-op. All stable. All dead within 72 hours of being moved from ICU to the step-down unit.

Clara’s fingers trembled as she cross-referenced the names. Mr. Finch wasn’t the first. He was the sixth.

She exhaled sharply, her pulse hammering in her throat. Coincidence. It had to be. Cardiac patients were high-risk. Complications happened. But six? In four weeks?

She pulled up the death certificates. *Cardiac arrest. Respiratory failure. Sudden cardiac event.*

No autopsies. No further investigation.

Clara’s hands clenched into fists. She needed to talk to someone.

---

Dr. Elias Carter was hunched over a desk in the back office, his tie loosened, his salt-and-pepper hair sticking up in tufts. He didn’t look up when Clara knocked.

“Dr. Carter? Do you have a minute?”

He sighed, rubbing his temples. “Voss, right? Night shift?”

“Yes, sir.”

He finally glanced up, his bloodshot eyes flicking over her. “What’s the issue?”

Clara swallowed. “I’ve noticed a pattern in the cardiac post-op patients. Six in the last month, all moved to step-down, all dead within 72 hours.”

Dr. Carter leaned back in his chair, folding his arms. “And?”

“And it’s unusual. The survival rates for post-op cardiac patients are much higher than that. Something doesn’t add up.”

He exhaled through his nose, a sound that was half-laugh, half-scoff. “Voss, you’re a nurse. You know how this works. Cardiac patients are fragile. Complications happen. We don’t have the resources to investigate every unexpected death.”

Clara’s jaw tightened. “But six in a month? That’s not just complications. That’s a pattern.”

Dr. Carter’s expression darkened. “Are you suggesting malpractice? Negligence?”

“I’m suggesting we look into it.”

He stood abruptly, his chair scraping against the linoleum. “You’re suggesting we waste time and money chasing ghosts. These patients were stable. They were moved because they were improving. If something went wrong, it was an act of God, not the hospital.”

Clara didn’t flinch. “Then let’s prove it. Request autopsies. Review the charts more closely.”

Dr. Carter’s voice dropped, low and dangerous. “You’re overstepping, Nurse Voss. Your job is to care for the living, not dig up the dead. If you’ve got a problem with how this hospital operates, take it up with HR. Otherwise, I suggest you focus on your patients.”

Clara’s hands curled into fists at her sides. “Yes, sir.”

She turned on her heel and left, her boots clicking sharply against the floor. The door swung shut behind her with a quiet *click*, but the weight of his dismissal pressed down on her like a physical force.

---

Clara didn’t go back to the nurses’ station. Instead, she slipped into the supply closet, her fingers flying over the tablet as she pulled up the patient logs again. She cross-referenced the names with the nursing schedules, her breath coming faster.

Same nurses. Same shift.

Her stomach lurched.

She pulled up the medication logs. Morphine. Fentanyl. Midazolam.

All six patients had received higher-than-average doses in the hours before their deaths.

Clara’s hands shook. She zoomed in on the signatures. The same nurse had administered the drugs to all six.

*L. Mercer.*

Clara’s blood ran cold.

She knew Mercer. Late 50s, quiet, always volunteering for the night shift. Never caused trouble. Never stood out.

Clara’s mind raced. She needed proof. She needed to see the charts in person.

She slipped out of the supply closet and made her way to the records room, her pulse roaring in her ears. The door was locked, but she had the code. She punched it in, her fingers fumbling once before the light turned green.

The room was dim, the air stale. She moved to the filing cabinets, pulling out the charts for the six patients. Her fingers flew through the pages, her eyes scanning the notes.

*Patient stable. Vital signs normal. Administered 2mg morphine IV per protocol. Patient became agitated. Administered additional 1mg morphine. Patient’s respirations decreased. Called code blue.*

Clara’s breath hitched. The notes were identical. Almost word-for-word.

She flipped to the next chart. Same thing.

And the next.

Her hands trembled as she set the charts down. This wasn’t a coincidence. This was deliberate.

She pulled out her phone, snapping photos of the relevant pages. She needed to get this to someone. Someone who would listen.

But who?

She thought of Dr. Carter’s dismissive sneer. Of the way he’d shut her down without a second thought.

She thought of the way Mercer had always been there, quiet and unassuming, in the background.

Clara’s stomach twisted. She needed to act. Now.

---

She found Mercer in the break room, hunched over a cup of coffee, her dark hair pulled into a tight bun. She looked up as Clara entered, her expression unreadable.

“Clara. You’re working late.”

Clara forced a smile. “Couldn’t sleep. Thought I’d grab a coffee.”

Mercer nodded, her gaze flicking back to her cup. “Long night.”

Clara poured herself a cup, her hands steady despite the adrenaline coursing through her veins. She leaned against the counter, her voice casual. “Heard about Mr. Finch. Tough break.”

Mercer’s fingers tightened around her mug. “Yeah. He was doing well, though. Stable.”

Clara’s pulse pounded in her throat. “Funny. That’s what they all were.”

Mercer’s head snapped up, her dark eyes locking onto Clara’s. “What?”

Clara set her cup down, her voice low. “The other five. All stable. All dead within 72 hours of being moved to step-down.”

Mercer’s face paled. “What are you implying?”

Clara didn’t blink. “I’m not implying anything. I’m stating a fact. Six cardiac patients. All post-op. All dead under your care.”

Mercer stood abruptly, her chair scraping against the floor. “You don’t know what you’re talking about.”

Clara stepped forward, her voice a whisper. “I know exactly what I’m talking about. I’ve seen the charts. I’ve seen the logs. You administered the drugs. You signed the orders.”

Mercer’s hands clenched into fists. “You’re accusing me of murder.”

Clara’s heart hammered against her ribs. “I’m saying something’s not right. And I’m going to find out what it is.”

Mercer’s expression twisted, something dark and desperate flashing in her eyes. “You don’t understand. You don’t know what it’s like.”

Clara’s breath caught. “Then tell me.”

Mercer’s voice cracked. “My daughter. She’s sick. Leukemia. The treatments—” She swallowed hard. “The insurance won’t cover it all. I can’t afford it. I can’t—” Her voice broke. “I can’t watch her die.”

Clara’s stomach lurched. “So you’re killing other people’s parents? Their spouses? Their children’s fathers?”

Mercer’s eyes filled with tears. “I didn’t mean for it to go this far. I just—I needed the money. The life insurance payouts. It was supposed to be a few extra thousand. Enough to cover the treatments. But then—” She shook her head. “It spiraled. I didn’t know how to stop.”

Clara’s hands trembled. “You have to stop. Now.”

Mercer’s voice was a whisper. “It’s too late.”

Clara’s blood ran cold. “What do you mean?”

Mercer’s gaze flicked to the door. “He knows.”

Clara’s breath hitched. “Who?”

Before Mercer could answer, the break room door swung open. Dr. Carter stood in the doorway, his expression unreadable.

“Nurse Voss. Nurse Mercer.” His voice was calm, almost pleasant. “I think it’s time we had a chat.”

Clara’s pulse roared in her ears. She took a step back, her mind racing. *He knows.*

Dr. Carter’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. “Clara, I think you’ve been working too hard. Maybe it’s time you took a break.”

Clara’s hands curled into fists. “I know what’s happening. I know about the patients. I know about Mercer.”

Dr. Carter’s smile faded. “Do you now?”

Clara’s voice was steady. “Yes. And I’m not going to let it continue.”

Dr. Carter sighed, shaking his head. “That’s a shame.”

He reached into his pocket and pulled out a syringe. Clara’s blood turned to ice.

Mercer let out a choked sob. “Elias, please—”

Dr. Carter’s voice was cold. “It’s too late for that, Laura.”

Clara’s mind raced. She needed to get out. She needed to scream. She needed to—

The door swung shut behind Dr. Carter with a final, ominous *click*.
