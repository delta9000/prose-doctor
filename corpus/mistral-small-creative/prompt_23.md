**Chapter Twelve: The Weight of Stillness**

The ceasefire came at dawn, a fragile thing, announced by the sudden absence of gunfire rather than any formal declaration. Elias Varga had been stitching a gash in Corporal Harkin’s thigh when the world went quiet. His hands didn’t stop moving—needle in, pull, knot—but his ears strained for the next explosion, the next scream. Nothing came. Just the wet, ragged breathing of the wounded and the distant cry of a crow circling the battlefield.

He tied off the suture, pressed a wad of gauze against the wound, and barked at the nearest corpsman, “Hold pressure. If he bleeds through, pack it.” Then he was up, stripping off his bloodied gloves, already scanning the field.

The medevac birds weren’t coming. Not yet. The ceasefire was too new, too uncertain, and the brass wouldn’t risk losing choppers to a sudden change of heart. That left Elias and the other medics to sort the living from the dying in the mud and the mist.

He grabbed his ruck, slung his rifle, and stepped out of the aid station’s flimsy cover. The battlefield stretched before him, a graveyard of craters and broken men. Smoke curled from smoldering vehicles, and the air smelled of cordite and copper. Somewhere, a man was sobbing. Another was praying in a language Elias didn’t recognize.

*Triage first. Always triage.*

He moved fast, his boots sinking into the churned earth. The first casualty was one of theirs—a private, barely more than a kid, his abdomen torn open by shrapnel. Elias knelt, pressed two fingers to the boy’s neck. No pulse. His skin was already cooling. *Dead on arrival.* He marked the body with a black tag and moved on.

The next was an enemy soldier, his uniform a mottled green, his face twisted in pain. He clutched at his thigh, where a bullet had shattered the femur. Blood pulsed between his fingers. Elias hesitated. The man’s eyes locked onto his, wide and desperate.

“Help me,” the soldier rasped in broken English. “Please.”

Elias exhaled through his nose. *Protocol says prioritize your own.* But the man was bleeding out. If he didn’t act now, the soldier would die in minutes.

“Fuck,” Elias muttered. He dropped his ruck, ripped open a trauma kit. “Hold still.”

The soldier’s breath hitched as Elias cut away the fabric of his trousers. The wound was bad—bone fragments, torn muscle, a geyser of dark blood. Elias packed the wound with gauze, then wrapped a tourniquet above the knee. The soldier gasped, his fingers digging into the mud.

“You’re going to be okay,” Elias lied. “Just keep pressure on it.”

The soldier nodded, his face pale. “Thank you.”

Elias didn’t answer. He moved on.

---

The next hour was a blur of blood and decisions. A sergeant with a sucking chest wound—*his own, priority one.* A kid with a shattered pelvis—*stable for now, but he’d need evac fast.* An enemy sniper, his throat torn open by a ricochet—*already gone, black tag.*

Elias worked on autopilot, his mind a ledger of life and death. *Who lives? Who dies? Who do I save today?*

He found another enemy soldier, this one younger, his arm nearly severed at the elbow. The boy was conscious, his teeth chattering, his good hand pressed against the stump. Elias knelt beside him.

“You speak English?” Elias asked.

The boy nodded. “A little.”

“What’s your name?”

“K-Karim.”

“Karim, I’m going to help you, okay? But I need you to stay still.”

The boy’s eyes filled with tears. “It hurts.”

“I know.” Elias pulled out a morphine syrette, tapped it against his palm. “This’ll help.” He injected it into the boy’s thigh. Karim’s breath shuddered out, his body relaxing slightly.

Elias worked quickly, wrapping the stump in sterile gauze, then securing a pressure bandage. The bleeding slowed. The boy’s eyelids fluttered.

“You’re going to be okay,” Elias said. “Just hold on.”

Karim’s voice was weak. “Why… why help me?”

Elias didn’t look up. “Because I can.”

---

The ceasefire held, but the tension didn’t ease. Every few minutes, Elias glanced toward the tree line, half-expecting the crack of a rifle. The enemy medics were out there too, moving among their own wounded. Once, their eyes met across the field. A silent acknowledgment. *We’re all just trying to keep them alive.*

Elias found another of his own—a lieutenant, his leg pinned under the wreckage of a Humvee. The man was conscious, his face slick with sweat.

“Doc,” the lieutenant gasped. “Get me out.”

Elias assessed the situation. The Humvee was heavy, the leg trapped at an unnatural angle. *Compound fracture, possible arterial damage.* He needed help.

“Corpsman!” Elias shouted. No answer. He turned to Karim, who was propped against a crate, his face ashen. “Hey. You. Can you walk?”

Karim blinked. “I… I think so.”

“Good. Come here.”

Karim stumbled over, his good arm braced against his side. Elias pointed to the Humvee. “Help me lift this.”

Karim’s eyes widened. “But—”

“Now.”

Together, they heaved. The Humvee groaned, shifting just enough for Elias to drag the lieutenant free. The man screamed as his leg came loose, the bone jutting through torn flesh. Elias didn’t have time to splint it. He packed the wound, applied a tourniquet, then turned to Karim.

“Go find your people. Tell them we’ve got wounded who need evac. Both sides.”

Karim hesitated. “They won’t listen.”

“Try.”

Karim nodded, then limped away, his bandaged stump held close to his body.

---

Elias worked until his hands cramped and his back ached. The field was a sea of groans and whimpers, the air thick with the metallic tang of blood. He lost count of how many he tagged—red for urgent, yellow for delayed, black for the dead. The black tags were the worst. Each one was a failure, a life he couldn’t save.

He found another enemy soldier, this one older, his face lined with exhaustion. The man clutched at his side, where a bullet had punched through his ribs. He was breathing in shallow gasps, his lips blue.

Elias knelt. “You hit in the lung?”

The soldier nodded. “Can’t… can’t breathe.”

Elias checked his pulse. Weak, thready. *Pneumothorax.* He needed to decompress the chest, but the man was already fading.

“Hold on,” Elias said. He grabbed a 14-gauge needle from his kit, ripped open the man’s uniform. The soldier’s skin was clammy, his breath rattling. Elias found the second intercostal space, just above the rib, and plunged the needle in. Air hissed out. The soldier gasped, his chest expanding.

For a moment, Elias thought he might make it. Then the man’s eyes rolled back, his body going limp. Elias checked for a pulse. Nothing.

*Damn it.*

He marked the body with a black tag and moved on.

---

The sun climbed higher, the mist burning off. The ceasefire held, but the tension didn’t ease. Elias’s water was gone, his throat raw. He drank from a canteen he found on a dead enemy soldier, the water warm and metallic.

He found another of his own—a private, her face pale, her abdomen distended. *Internal bleeding.* Elias pressed his fingers to her wrist. Her pulse was weak, her skin cool.

“Hey,” he said, shaking her gently. “Stay with me.”

Her eyes fluttered open. “Doc?”

“Yeah. You’re gonna be okay.” He grabbed his stethoscope, listened to her belly. *Distant bowel sounds. Tense.* He needed to get her to surgery, fast.

But the evac birds still weren’t coming.

Elias looked around. The field was a mess of wounded, both sides mixed together. Some of the enemy medics were working on their own, but others were helping the Americans. A strange, uneasy truce.

He turned back to the private. “I’m gonna get you out of here. Just hold on.”

She nodded weakly.

Elias stood, scanning the horizon. He needed a stretcher. He needed help.

Then he saw Karim, limping toward him, his face set in determination.

“Doc,” Karim said. “Your commander says evac is coming. Ten minutes.”

Elias exhaled. “Good. Help me get her to the LZ.”

Karim nodded. Together, they lifted the private onto a stretcher, then carried her toward the landing zone. The weight was brutal, the ground uneven. Elias’s arms burned, his back screaming.

Halfway there, the private’s breathing grew shallow. Elias stopped, checked her pulse. *Weak. Too weak.*

“Come on,” he muttered. “Just a little farther.”

They reached the LZ just as the first Black Hawk descended, its rotors kicking up dust. Elias and Karim lowered the stretcher, and the crew rushed forward, loading her aboard.

Elias watched the chopper lift off, then turned to Karim. The boy was swaying on his feet, his face gray.

“You need to sit down,” Elias said.

Karim shook his head. “I have to help.”

Elias grabbed his arm. “You’re no good to anyone if you collapse. Sit. Now.”

Karim sank to the ground, his breath coming in ragged gasps. Elias knelt beside him, checked his bandage. The bleeding had stopped, but the boy was in shock.

“You did good,” Elias said.

Karim looked up at him, his eyes hollow. “Why?”

Elias didn’t pretend to misunderstand. “Because it’s the right thing to do.”

Karim didn’t answer. He just stared at the horizon, where the next chopper was already descending.

---

The evac lasted another hour. Elias worked until his vision blurred, until his hands moved on their own. He saved who he could. He lost who he couldn’t.

When it was over, the field was quiet again. The wounded were gone, the dead tagged and lined up. The ceasefire held, but the war wasn’t over.

Elias sat on the edge of the LZ, his ruck beside him, his rifle across his lap. His hands were steady, but his mind was a storm.

*How many did I save? How many did I lose?*

He thought of the lieutenant, the private, the enemy soldier with the pneumothorax. He thought of Karim, limping away, his arm gone, his face haunted.

*Was it enough?*

He didn’t know.

But the choppers were coming back. And the fighting would start again.

Elias stood, slung his rifle, and walked back toward the aid station.

There was still work to do.
