The dust never truly settled. It hung in the midday air, a fine, ochre shroud clinging to Sergeant Elias Thorne’s sweat-soaked fatigues. The ceasefire had been called three hours ago, a fragile, shaky thing brokered in a tent miles back, yet the silence was heavier, more dangerous, than the sustained artillery barrage that had preceded it.

Elias knelt beside the first patient, a boy no older than seventeen, wearing the faded olive drab of the Northern Coalition. The boy’s eyes were wide, fixed on a point just beyond Elias’s left shoulder. His left leg was a ruin, the femur likely shattered, the blast having vaporized the lower half of his boot and pulped the surrounding flesh.

*Triage.* The word was a cold, sharp instrument in Elias’s mind, cutting through the exhaustion and the metallic tang of blood in the air.

He pinched the boy’s eyelids, checked his pupils. They reacted sluggishly. Pulse rapid, thready. Breathing shallow.

“Hey, son,” Elias murmured, his voice raspy. He spoke the local dialect fluently, a necessity born of three years in this forgotten valley. “My name is Elias. I’m going to help you.”

The boy let out a low, gurgling sound that might have been assent.

Elias pulled the IFAK—Individual First Aid Kit—from his belt. Immediate concern: hemorrhage control. He didn’t waste trauma dressing on the obvious surface wounds clinging to the boy’s torso; those could wait. He grabbed a CAT tourniquet, the orange plastic lever stark against the dirt. He wrapped it high on the thigh, just above the worst of the fragmentation damage, and twisted until the boy’s muffled cry turned into a strangled gasp. Then, a second one, just below the knee, hoping to reduce the immediate pressure gradient.

*Status: Red.* Critical, but salvageable with immediate, invasive care. The field hospital was an hour’s drive if the road held.

Elias marked the boy’s forehead with a smear of red grease pencil from his kit: 1. He moved on.

The second casualty was South. A man in a dark grey uniform, helmet gone, a massive exit wound blooming on his back where a rifle round had punched through his lung. Elias rolled him carefully, biting down a curse at the slick, warm sensation against his gloves. The man was not breathing adequately. His lips were blue.

He lifted the man’s chin, listening for breath that wasn't there. Pneumothorax, likely bilateral given the position of impact. Sucking chest wound.

Elias ripped open a sterile chest seal and slapped it over the wound. The seal immediately began foaming slightly as air struggled to escape from within the chest cavity. He needed a needle decompression, and soon.

He grabbed the longest needle he carried, a two-inch catheter meant for administering IV fluids under duress. He found the second intercostal space, just beneath the clavicle, above the fifth rib. He braced himself, inhaled sharply, and drove the needle in, aiming posterior and superior.

The man convulsed, an involuntary spasm that shot pain up Elias’s arm. There was a satisfying, loud hiss as the trapped air vented. The man coughed, a wet, rattling sound, and his chest rose slightly, even with the seal in place.

*Status: Red.* Salvageable, but precarious. He marked the grey uniform with a quick, practiced slash of black grease pencil: 1.

The work was systematic, brutal in its detachment. He moved between friend and enemy, friend and enemy, applying pressure, applying tourniquets, administering morphine when the screams became too loud, or when the vital signs dipped too low. The ceasefire meant they were stopping the killing, not the aftermath.

He paused only when he heard the approach. Bootfalls, slow, careful. He looked up, his heart rate spiking despite himself.

Two men, armed with AKs, moving tentatively across the blasted earth. They wore the same grey uniforms as the second patient. They stopped ten meters away, weapons lowered but not holstered. They were Medics, too; they carried no heavy ordnance, only white armbands and battered packs.

“We are here to assist,” the taller one said, his voice rough with smoke inhalation. “We saw your marker flares. We are not engaging. Ceasefire holds, Medic.”

Elias stood slowly, wiping his hands on his trousers—a useless gesture. “I see that. I have my own casualties and yours. We work together, or we lose them all in the next hour waiting for extraction.”

The taller medic nodded once, taking in the scene: the makeshift collection point in the lee of a shattered concrete barrier, the rows of suffering. “My name is Jovan. This is Kirill.”

“Elias,” he said, gesturing toward the Northern boy he had first treated. “He needs a medevac bracketed for femoral arterial bleed the moment the air corridor opens. He’s running dangerously fast.”

Jovan moved to the boy, his movements precise. He checked the tourniquets, nodded approval, then quickly began assessing the torso wounds, bypassing the superficial cuts Elias had ignored.

“Good work on the lung,” Jovan said to Elias, his eyes flicking toward the grey-clad soldier. “He was fading fast.”

“He’s got a chance now,” Elias replied, turning his attention to a new arrival—one of his own platoon, Private Chen, who had taken shrapnel to the abdomen.

The next hour was a strange, silent communion. They were enemies bound by the same grim oath: *Primum non nocere.* First, do no harm. They shared iodine, they shared suture kits, they used the same field splints on men who, hours ago, had been trying to kill one another.

Elias finished Chen, applying a drain and applying heavy pressure dressing, knowing the boy’s appendix was likely nicked, but the internal bleeding seemed slow. He marked Chen black: 2. High priority, but likely stable enough to survive transport to the rear aid station.

He approached the Southern soldier whose lung he had taped. The man was conscious now, tracking Elias’s movements with dull, dark eyes.

“Water,” the soldier croaked, his voice barely there.

Elias shook his head. “No oral intake, not yet. You’ve got trauma to the chest wall. You’ll aspirate.”

“I’m not dying,” the soldier stated, a flat declaration of fact, not bravado.

“Not today, maybe,” Elias countered, checking the dressing. It was holding. “What’s your name?”

“Milos.”

Elias paused in reapplying fresh gauze. “Milos. I’m Elias.”

“I know who you are,” Milos whispered, his gaze drifting toward the fallen Northmen. “Every medic has a reputation. Yours precedes you, Thorne. You patch up everyone.”

Elias suppressed a shudder of resentment. Being known for impartiality here was both a shield and a curse. If he played favorites, he’d be shot by his own lines. If he ignored the wounded enemy, he’d be judged by the ghost of medicine.

“We are under a ceasefire, Milos. That means we pause the killing. It does not mean we stop helping the injured.”

“Does it weigh on you?” Milos asked, his brow furrowing slightly with effort. “Deciding which one gets the last vial of plasma before the transport comes?”

The question was too direct, too honest for the brutal theatre they occupied. Elias felt the internal calculus flare up, hot and sharp, even as he maintained his exterior calm.

He looked from Milos, whose life he had pulled back from the brink of respiratory failure, to the Northern boy, whose leg was little more than exposed bone pulsing with every beat of his heart. The Northern boy was young, a conscript, likely terrified. Milos was older, his face weathered, probably a career soldier.

The internal calculus was never about politics, never about ideology, and rarely about rank, though that played a statistical part. It was triage applied at the soul level: *Who has the highest chance of full recovery given the limited resources?*

The Northern boy needed immediate surgery to save the limb, and likely the leg, requiring a major transfusion and an OR within the hour. His chances of long-term mobility were low even with immediate intervention.

Milos needed stabilization, monitoring, and a clean chest cavity. He had already passed the critical point of immediate collapse Elias had pulled him through. His needs were less time-sensitive *right now*, though delayed care would be fatal.

“It weighs on me every second I’m here,” Elias admitted, kneeling so their eyes were level. “But the decision isn't about who deserves to live, Milos. It’s about who *can* live.”

He pointed to the dressing on Milos’s back. “You’re stable. You’re breathing on your own now. That buys you time until the transports run their first circuit.” He then gestured toward the two men Jovan was working on—one of whom was the Northern boy. “They are actively bleeding out or failing airway management. They take the first slot.”

Milos nodded slowly, assimilating the cold logic. “So, you save the ones you can save *now* to give them a shot, and you let the stable ones wait.”

“Precisely,” Elias confirmed. “It’s cruel arithmetic, but it keeps the head count low on the morgue list.”

Jovan called out from beside the Northern boy. “Elias, the artery is weeping again under the tourniquet pressure. We need to pack the wound deeper or risk losing him before the chopper lands. I don’t have enough hemostatic gauze.”

Elias immediately moved. He pulled out his last two rolls of Combat Gauze, meant for his own team’s emergency use. They were nearly exhausted.

“Use this,” Elias said, handing them over. “Pack deep, tight as you can get it. I need you to push until you feel resistance.”

Jovan worked with focused intensity, his breathing matching Elias’s own hurried rhythm. Kirill, the second South medic, was busy stabilizing the vitals of the other Northern casualties, moving them slightly onto makeshift stretchers—tarps pulled from a wrecked vehicle.

The air suddenly vibrated—not with the heavy thud of artillery, but the distinctive, rhythmic *whop-whop-whop* of rotor blades approaching down the valley.

“Air support incoming,” Elias yelled, standing to assess the chaotic scene. “Jovan, they’ll take the most critical first. We have three Red-1s and two Red-2s.”

The classification was unspoken but understood: Red-1 meant catastrophic, life-threatening injuries requiring immediate OR access. Red-2 meant critical but stable enough for a short hold, or injuries that could become Red-1 if delayed past two hours.

The Northern boy was definitely a Red-1. Milos, despite being conscious now, was borderline Red-2 leaning toward Red-1 if his chest cavity re-pressurized.

The first medevac bird, a bulky, grey machine marked with the neutral emblem of the UN monitoring force, settled softly fifty yards away, kicking up a renewed storm of dust.

The team leader, a stoic woman in a heavy flight suit, approached, clipboard in hand. “Status report, Medic Thorne.”

Elias walked to her, projecting authority he didn't feel. “Three critical, two stable. The critical are: Northern Coalition male, severe arterial hemorrhage, left leg; Northern Coalition male, tension pneumothorax, re-breathing now but fragile; and my own soldier, Private Chen, abdominal trauma, stable for transport but needs surgery.”

He deliberately listed his own man last. The neutral observer would see the distribution of nationalities first.

The flight medic’s eyes scanned his notes, then swept over the wounded. “We take the two most critical non-combatants first. Who is failing fastest?”

Elias pointed to the Northern boy, whose color was starting to grey again despite Jovan's packing. “The leg. He loses consciousness soon.”

Jovan quickly confirmed, “His pulse is dropping again, Doctor. He’s spiking hypotension.”

“Understood.” The flight medic nodded briskly. “We take the leg wound and the lung patient. Both North. We can’t take three Reds in one run.”

Elias felt a cold knot tighten in his stomach. That meant Milos and Chen had to wait for the second run—at least an hour, maybe more, depending on fuel and the safety of the route.

He hurried back to Milos. “You’re stable enough for the wait, Milos. They’re taking the two worst off first. You’re on the second run.”

Milos managed a weak smile. “Good. You saved me from the first round.”

Elias didn't return the smile. He turned to Jovan. “You and Kirill need to keep monitoring the breathing on the lung patient. If he decompresses again, give him the chest tube—don’t wait for the chopper.”

Jovan nodded. “We will.”

The litter bearers loaded the Northern boy and the wounded Northern soldier onto the first stretcher. As they were carried toward the hovering bird, Elias watched the boy’s face—a mask of pale shock, utterly devoid of the fire that had fueled his fighting yesterday. He was simply biology, broken and desperately needing repair.

The helicopter lifted, the sound receding until the world returned to the strained quiet of the ceasefire.

Elias looked at the remaining wounded. Private Chen, conscious but pale, offering him a weak thumbs-up. And Milos, watching the sky where the chopper had disappeared.

“Why do you do this, Elias?” Milos asked again, his voice gaining the barest strength now that the immediate danger had passed. “Why risk the same bullet for the man you fought yesterday?”

Elias sat back on his heels, pulling a canteen off his belt. He uncapped it, took a long, slow drink, letting the cool water cut through the dry heat coating his throat.

“Because I spent six months in the rear echelon, sorting paperwork, before they shipped me to the front,” Elias said, meeting Milos’s gaze again. “And I realized something in that fluorescent lighting. The men on the other side—they read the same magazines. They miss the same kind of terrible food. They have mothers who wait up for calls that never come.”

He gestured around the makeshift clearing. “When the shooting stops, and it’s just air and dirt and blood, we are all just bodies that failed. If I stop caring about the uniform, I can start caring about the person. And without that, Milos, I’m just a butcher wearing a white cross.”

He paused, leaning forward to check Chen’s dressings one last time. “And frankly, I’m too tired to be a butcher.”

He took out his final morphine syrette. He didn’t give it to Milos yet; Milos was lucid. He gave it to a low-priority casualty, a young Northern conscript with a fractured arm and deep shock, marking him Green. The boy whimpered, then his eyes drifted shut in blessed relief.

Elias looked at the approaching plume of dust on the horizon—the second medevac, carrying the heavy threat of return to the fight.

“It’s a temporary break, Milos,” Elias said, standing up, readying his supplies for the next rush. “And while we have it, we clean up the mess. Then we go back to hating each other, if we must.”
