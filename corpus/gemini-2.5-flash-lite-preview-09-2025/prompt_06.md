The air in Oakhaven tasted of ash and something cloyingly sweet, the scent of dying lilies mixed with the metallic tang of fever. Elara moved through the narrow, cobbled streets like a phantom, her grey woolen cloak drawn tight around her thin frame, a woven basket bumping against her hip. The wicker creaked a familiar, mournful rhythm.

It had been three weeks since the sweating sickness had first struck the Tanner’s District, a week since it had leaped the river and begun its methodical march through the merchant quarter. Now, the silence was the loudest sound in the city—a heavy, unnatural quiet where the blacksmith’s clang and the fishmonger’s cry should have been.

Elara adjusted the kerchief covering her hair. She avoided the main squares; the guards were too quick to eye strangers, and the zealots had begun patrolling, their voices raw with fear and accusation. Her destination was the cramped tenement housing near the city wall, a place the official City Physicians shunned as ‘too far gone.’

She reached the doorway of Number Twelve, a splintered archway slick with rainwater run-off. A woman, whose face was etched with terror, stood leaning against the frame, clutching a soiled shawl.

“Mistress Elara,” the woman whispered, her voice brittle. “He is worse. The black spots—”

“I know, Mara,” Elara said calmly, though her heart hammered against her ribs. She hated the spots. They meant the sickness had already reached the core.

She squeezed past Mara, entering the oppressive gloom of the single room. The air inside was thick, humid with the breath of the sick and the close proximity of too many bodies. A pallet of straw lay in the center of the room. On it, Thomas, Mara’s husband, writhed, his skin the color of spoiled milk, slick with sweat.

Elara set her basket down. Inside, carefully wrapped in muslin, were dried meadowsweet flowers, willow bark strips, and a small vial of tincture made from valerian root, all gathered under the waning moons of the previous spring.

“Stay back from him, Mara,” Elara instructed, pulling on the thin, oiled leather gloves she reserved for the worst cases. “Open the window wide, even if the air is cold. We need the exchange.”

Mara obeyed instantly, fumbling with the rusted iron latch until the small window shuddered open a crack, admitting a thin draught of the putrid city air.

Thomas groaned, his eyes rolling back to show only the whites. “Fire,” he rasped, clawing at his throat.

“It is the sickness, Thomas. It burns from within,” Elara murmured, her voice low and steady, a counter-spell to the panic.

She did not touch him yet. She never touched the skin first. Instead, she took the willow bark, chewing a piece until it softened into a bitter pulp, then mixing it with a spoonful of honey she carried in a silver locket.

“Mara, stir three spoonfuls of this into water. Bring it to a quick boil, just for a moment, then let it cool until you can bear the heat on your wrist. It will ease the pain in his head.”

As Mara hurried to a soot-stained hearth in the corner, Elara turned her attention to the dark, bruise-like lesions spreading across Thomas’s abdomen. These were the devil’s marks, the whispers claimed. The City Physicians, with their bloodletting and purging purgatives, had done nothing but hasten the end for most.

Elara did not dispute the spiritual aspect of suffering, but she trusted the earth more than she trusted the learned men who demanded silver for leeches.

She reached into her basket and pulled out the meadowsweet. These were for the fever, to draw the heat down without shocking the system. She crushed the dry blossoms between her palms, releasing a scent of crushed almond and honey, surprisingly sweet in the fetid room.

“When the water is ready, give him the drink slowly. A sip every half-hour,” Elara said. Then, turning to Mara, she added, “Now, watch me.”

She took a clean linen scrap, dipped it in warm goat’s milk, and gently dabbed the valerian tincture onto the deepest of the black spots. The tincture smelled sharply of earth and sleep. It would not cleanse the poison, but it might soothe the resulting nerve pain and prevent the gangrene from spreading too quickly.

“What is that liquid?” Mara asked, her eyes wide with fearful curiosity as she watched the dark stain of the tincture absorb into her husband’s ravaged skin.

“It is nightshade and valerian, Mara. It tells the body to rest, to stop spending its strength fighting the fire.”

“Nightshade,” Mara repeated, the word tasting dangerous on her tongue. The common folk knew nightshade was poison, a tool for execution or, worse, for summoning shadows.

“It is only poison in the wrong measure,” Elara countered gently, wiping her gloves clean on a rag. “Everything is poison or cure, depending on the dose and the hand that measures it.”

She spent the next hour adjusting Thomas’s position, forcing small amounts of the willow-bark tea past his lips, and sponging the sweat from his brow with cool, clean river water.

As the fever seemed to dip by a fraction, Thomas managed to whisper a single, clear word: “Thank you.”

Elara offered Mara a small, tight smile. “Keep the room aired. Do not let him sleep deeply, but let him be still. I will return on the morrow, if the Watch allows.”

She gathered her basket. On the threshold, Mara stopped her.

“Mistress Elara,” Mara said, her voice trembling. “The priest at St. Jude’s says that only confessions and prayer can lift this curse. He says any remedy drawn from the root and the leaf is… unnatural.”

Elara looked back at the damp, dark room, at the man whose life she had momentarily stabilized with things the earth provided freely.

“Tell the priest,” Elara said, her voice losing its gentle edge, hardening like clay under the sun, “that if prayer is enough, why do the wealthy die just as fast as the paupers? Tell him nature follows its own laws, and I follow hers.”

She turned and walked out, pulling her hood low against the accusatory eyes of the street.

***

Elara sought refuge in the only place she felt safe: the narrow, walled garden behind the abandoned apothecary shop on Weaver’s Lane. The shop had burned down two years prior, but the small patch of soil remained, tended secretly against the encroaching blight.

She sat on an overturned barrel, inhaling the sharp, clean scent of rosemary. She needed to process the willow; the supply was running low, and the next harvest was months away. The City Council had forbidden foraging outside the walls, fearing travelers brought the sickness in, leaving the healers dependent on dwindling, expensive stores.

A shadow fell over the garden gate.

It was Father Malachi, his robes dark and heavy, his face pale and severe beneath the shadow of his cowl. He carried a thick oak staff, not for support, but for menace.

“Elara of the Weald,” Malachi boomed, his voice echoing off the brick walls.

Elara did not rise. “Father.”

“I see you still busy yourself with your unholy work, even as the city suffers the righteous judgment of God.”

“I am busy keeping souls in their bodies,” Elara corrected him, leaning forward slightly. “The soul is easier to save when the body is not consumed by rot.”

Malachi stepped into the garden, his boots crushing the fragile sprigs of thyme. “Your remedies are poison, woman. We have buried thirty souls this week who were under your care. They died clutching your bitter teas, their livers blackened by your strange brews.”

“And how many died under the care of the three *official* physicians?” Elara challenged him, allowing a sliver of her own fear to sharpen her tone. “They drain the blood until the patient is too weak to fight, and then they charge five silver farthings for the privilege of expiring.”

“They work within the tenets of the Holy Church! They call upon the Almighty to guide their hands. You call upon… upon what? The bog and the shadow?” Malachi gestured contemptuously toward the handful of drying herbs spread on a flat stone. “That lavender you use—it dulls the senses. It makes the dying docile. Is that not the work of the Tempter, to make men accept their damnation sweetly?”

“It dulls the pain of the high fever, Father. It gives the body a rest it needs to fight the infection,” Elara said, standing now, drawing herself to her full, modest height. “I use what grows. I see what works. I do not pretend to speak to God; I only listen to the earth.”

Malachi slammed the butt of his staff onto the earth. “There is only one authority! And the plague is the visible manifestation of spiritual corruption. To treat it with earthly charms is to mock divine will. People speak of you, Elara. They say you whisper to the fever itself. They say you can stop the heart with a touch, and restart it with a potion brewed under a hanged man’s gibbet.”

Elara felt the familiar chill of public hysteria creeping in, cold and inescapable. She knew this path. It led only one way.

“I heal, Father. I have always healed. My mother taught me, and her mother before her. We use what alleviates suffering.”

“Suffering is the cleanser of sin! You rob penitents of their necessary ordeal!” Malachi’s breath hitched with outrage. “I have seen the faces of the distraught wives, the terrified children. They look to your charms instead of the Cross. They call you the ‘Fever Witch.’”

“And what would you have me do, Father?” Elara asked, her voice dangerously soft. “Shall I stand by and watch Thomas choke on his own humors while I pray until he turns to dust?”

“You shall cease your blasphemous trade. You shall surrender your vile powders to the flames, and you shall confine yourself to the chapel to knit shrouds, as is proper for a woman unguided by the priesthood.”

Elara looked past Malachi, past the high, unforgiving walls of the city, toward the distant, hazy grey of the uninfected fields. She had lived in Oakhaven for twenty-five years. She had never left.

“I cannot do that,” she said. “I still have jars of feverfew left. And the moon favors the harvest of nettle root next week, which will help with the bleeding. People need those things.”

Malachi’s face hardened into a mask of absolute religious certainty. “Then you leave me no choice. I have warned you. I will speak with the Bailiff tomorrow. I will tell him that you are willfully obstructing the purification of this city. I will tell him your herbs are instruments of the Devil, designed to preserve the unrepentant.”

He turned, his dark robes swirling dramatically, and strode from the garden, crushing the remaining thyme underfoot.

Elara stood alone in the small, walled space. She did not flinch at the threat. She had expected it. Fear bred accusation, and accusation bred the pyre.

***

Night fell quickly, bringing with it a dense fog that seemed to leach the sound and color from the world. Elara returned to her own lodging—a small, cold room above a silent bakery near the river gate.

She banked the fire in her small hearth, the wood crackling weakly, struggling against the damp. She ate a small piece of stale bread dipped in weak broth. Then, she went to the pantry.

Her stores were meager. The panic had cleared the markets of anything remotely useful, and the fear had kept her from venturing too far afield in daylight.

She pulled down the small, locked wooden chest where she kept her truly powerful reserves—the things the common folk never saw, and the priests most feared.

Inside, nestled on velvet, were dried mandrake root—carefully prepared, its shape vaguely humanoid—and a small quantity of powdered belladonna, the ‘deadly nightshade’ in its pure, lethal form. These were not for the common sickness. They were for seizures, for deep, paralyzing pain, or, if necessary, for a swift, quiet end.

Elara carefully picked up the mandrake. It was pale, wrinkled, and looked terribly fragile, yet it held the potential to alter the course of a life, for good or ill.

She ran a thumb over the root’s surface, feeling the slight, cool ridges.

*If you are a witch, what magic do you have to stop Malachi from condemning you?*

She placed the mandrake back down. Her doubt was not in her knowledge, but in her effectiveness against the tide of human panic. She could mend a broken bone, soothe a raging fever, clean a festering wound, but she could not mend the fissures in men’s souls that led them to seek scapegoats.

She remembered Thomas, his sweat-soaked brow, the look of profound relief when the pain eased, even momentarily. She remembered the gratitude in Mara’s eyes, quickly masked by fear of the priest’s judgment.

Was it more virtuous to preach faith into the face of certain death, or to offer a few more hours of respite, bought with the dangerous currency of suspicion?

Elara moved to the window, pushing aside the heavy curtain. The fog outside was so thick it seemed to press against the glass. The city below was a landscape of shadows and muffled sounds—the distant, irregular tolling of a death bell, the scurry of rats in the alleyway.

She picked up the vial of willow bark tincture, holding it to the weak light of the hearth fire. It was a simple thing, derived from a common tree, yet tonight it felt weighted with impossible responsibility.

“I trust the earth,” she whispered to the glass. But tonight, the earth felt distant, and the city felt too small, too hot, and too ready to consume itself in fear.

She set the vial down, the glass clicking softly against the wooden sill. She was tiring. The constant vigilance, the lack of sleep, the fear of discovery—it was leeching her strength faster than any fever.

Elara closed her eyes, picturing the garden—the rosemary, the thyme, the nettles. Those were honest things. They demanded only knowledge and patience. Malachi demanded submission. The dying demanded miracles.

She knew she would go out again tomorrow. She would brew the meadowsweet and grind the willow. She had to. If she stopped, the silence in Oakhaven would become permanent, and the only healing left would be the final, cold stillness of the grave.

Elara pulled her thin blanket tighter around her shoulders, choosing tomorrow’s ingredients in her mind: the willow for the fever, the honey to make it palatable, and the rosemary, just a sprig, to clear her head before she faced the Watch. She drifted toward sleep, guarded only by the knowledge that if the worst came, she had a few sharp tools hidden beneath the floorboards, tools that offered an alternative to the pyre. But she prayed, in her own silent, earthbound way, that she would not need them. Not yet.
