The air in the tenement room hung heavy, a physical weight that tasted of sour milk, unwashed linen, and the copper tang of old blood. Elara pulled the rough woolen blanket higher over the boy’s shivering shoulders. Little Thomas, no more than six, lay still as a stone, his skin waxy and cool to the touch save for the fever that burned beneath the surface. The dark blotches of the Black Sickness were already spreading from his neck, purple blooms that would turn black in days, then burst.

Elara ignored the stench, focusing instead on the mortar and pestle in her lap. Dried willow bark was soaked in vinegar and crushed to a fine powder; she added a sprinkle of crushed poppy heads for sleep, a pinch of yarrow to staunch any internal bleeding, and honey to make the mix palatable. It was a mixture of science and survival. In the city of Oakhaven, the city had been closed for three weeks, sealed by stone walls to keep the dead from the living, but the sickness had already seeped through the cracks of the walls and the fear of the people.

She dipped a rag into the concoction and wiped the boy's lips. He groaned, a sound like a tear in dry fabric, and his eyes fluttered open. They were clouded, the pupils too wide, reacting to light with a sluggish, drunken gait.

"Mama?" he whispered, though his mother had died in the alley three streets over.

Elara forced a smile, though it felt brittle on her face. "No, little one. It is Elara. Drink." She held the cup to his mouth. He swallowed, a weak rattle of breath escaping his throat.

"Does it hurt?" she asked softly.

"The cold," he muttered. "It is inside my bones."

"It is passing through," she lied, and then corrected herself. "It is leaving."

She sat back on her heels, watching the boy drift back toward unconsciousness. This was the rhythm of her life now: the grinding of the mortar, the watching of the breath, the waiting for the end. Sometimes, the end was delayed by a day or two, and those were the days she felt like a queen. Other times, the medicine was a lie, and the medicine was nothing.

She had just finished packing the herbs into a leather pouch when the heavy oak door groaned. It wasn't the tentative knock of a neighbor bringing bread or a priest coming to hear a deathbed confession. It was the firm, impatient rap of authority.

Elara stood, wiping her hands on her apron. Her heart hammered against her ribs, a frantic bird trapped in a cage. She knew that footfall. Father Thomas.

The door opened, and the chill of the hallway cut into the room. The priest stepped inside, his black cassock swallowing the light, the silver cross at his throat glinting like a knife. Behind him stood two guardsmen in leather jerkin, their hands resting on the wooden clubs at their belts. They didn't enter the room fully, remaining in the threshold as if guarding the boundary between the holy and the profane.

"Elara of the Red Ward," Father Thomas said. His voice was low, carrying a weight that silenced the room. He did not look at the boy, but at the mortar, then at her hands. They were stained green and brown.

"Father," Elara said, bowing her head slightly. She did not curtsy; she knew better. "I was tending to a sick child."

"The sickness is the work of the Lord's wrath," Thomas said, stepping further into the room. He inhaled sharply, recoiling slightly at the scent of the herbs. "And yet you are here, mixing your dusts and roots while the church prays for mercy. Do you not think that God provides more than... willow bark?"

Elara straightened, her spine locking into place. "God provides nature, Father. He provides the willow to cut, the honey to heal, the knowledge to know the difference between a fever and a curse. This is not magic. It is medicine."

"It smells of sorcery," Thomas countered, his eye narrowing. He walked to the table where the jars of dried roots sat in rows: valerian, elderflower, horehound. To him, it looked like an apothecary of hell. To Elara, it was the library of the living. "I have heard whispers from the market. They say people who take your brew do not die as quickly. That the sickness leaves them like a guest at a party. That is unnatural."

"Natural things are often quick," Elara said, her voice steady. "The sickness kills fast because it attacks the blood. I slow the pain. I cool the head. I make them comfortable."

"Comfort is a lie," Thomas said. He reached out and picked up the jar of willow bark. He held it up to the light. "You claim to fight the will of God by delaying His verdict. If the man must die, let him die. If the boy must survive, let him survive by prayer, not by powder."

Elara stepped forward, placing herself between the priest and the jars. "If I let him pray instead of cooling his fever, he will die in delirium. He will suffer. Is your God so cruel that He demands the child burn in the dark while you stand in the hall?"

The priest's face twisted, not with anger, but with a cold, calculated judgment. "You speak of cruelty, yet you stand here with your potions. We have seen the effects of your work. There are cures that come, and then there are cures that demand payment. Who do you serve, Elara? Who do you draw from when you mix this?"

"I draw from the earth," she said. "From the earth God made."

"Then prove it," Thomas said. "Leave the jars. Leave the boy to the Lord. If his soul is saved, he will survive. If not, you cannot save a body that has been judged."

The guards stepped in, their hands tightening on their clubs. Elara looked at the boy again. He was still in the throes of a heavy sleep, his chest rising and falling in a shallow rhythm. If she left the jars, she would leave the boy to the fever. If she kept the jars, she risked the fire that burned the accused in the square.

"You cannot command God, Father," Elara said, her voice dropping. "He hears you. He hears me. But He does not stop the sweat or the black spots. Only I can do that."

Thomas stared at her for a long moment, the silence stretching until it felt tight enough to snap. "This is your warning, healer. If I find one more person on your path, one more jar that smells of dark alchemy... I will not send guards to escort you. I will send the magistrate. And the pyre."

He turned on his heel and strode out, the guards trailing like shadows. The door slammed, the sound echoing in the small room.

Elara stood alone with the boy and the smell of the herbs. She reached down and covered the jars with a thick canvas cloth, her movements precise, as if she were packing away a bomb. She went to the bed and took the boy's hand. It was hotter now.

"Drink the other half," she whispered, tilting the cup again. He swallowed automatically, a reflex deep in the throat. She wiped his mouth and checked his forehead. It was damp. The fever had not broken, but it had not risen.

She took the leather satchel from the table and tucked the jars inside. She had to leave. The sight of their house was not enough to satisfy a man in a hurry to make a point. If Thomas returned to the magistrate before sunrise, they would burn this door down and her with it.

She walked out into the hallway. The stone stairs were cold, damp with the moisture of the plague season. The city outside was a silence that hummed. The people were indoors, behind locked doors, afraid to breathe the same air as a neighbor.

She descended into the narrow alley. The street was empty, save for the occasional figure wrapped in a cloak, moving like a ghost. The smell of the city was worse here, a mix of sewage and burning wood. She kept her head down, her satchel clutched against her chest.

She walked to the edge of the river, where the city’s walls were weakest. Here, the water was black, choked with refuse and the slow drift of dead wood. She needed to wash her hands. She needed the water that didn't smell like death.

She crouched by the embankment, the river lapping against the rotting wood of the bridge supports. She submerged her hands in the water, scrubbing until the skin was raw. The willow scent was still there, stubborn as a stain.

Elara sank to the ground, letting her back rest against a cold stone pillar. She had survived the accusation, but the shadow of it remained. It sat heavy on her shoulders.

She had always believed that her role was clear. She was a woman of the city, using the tools given to her by nature. But Thomas’s words had cut deeper than she realized. *Who do you draw from?*

It wasn't a question of magic. She knew she had no magic. But the city feared what it could not understand. In the shadow of the cathedral, where the stained glass painted the light in colors of gold and blue, her brown jars looked like tools of the devil. She had seen the faith in the faces of the mothers she treated. They clutched their rosaries and looked at her with hope mixed with dread.

If she was right, if the herbs worked, then her power was greater than the prayers.
If she was wrong, if the herbs were poison, then she was killing them with every dose.

She pulled her knees to her chest, resting her chin on them. The river rushed past, indifferent to the plague or the priest or the healer.

She thought of the boy, Thomas. He had called for his mother. She had answered. Was that a sin? Was it pride to think she could intervene where God had drawn a line? The city believed this sickness was a punishment for sin. If she healed them, she was shielding the wicked. She was letting them continue in their wickedness, denying God the chance to purge the city.

But she had seen the sin in the eyes of the rich and the poor alike. The rich hoarded grain; the poor stole water. The disease did not care. It killed the sinner and the saint with equal indifference.

Her doubt gnawed at her. If she stopped, would the city be safer? Would the priest stop his persecution if she vanished?

She looked at her hands again. They were shaking. Not from fear, but from exhaustion. The tremor ran through her bones. She had not slept in two days. She had not eaten a proper meal in a week. The work was endless.

A raven circled overhead, crying out. It landed on a nearby chimney, watching her.

"Is it the devil?" she asked the empty air. Her voice was raspy.

The raven cawed once and took flight.

She stood up. The river water was cold against her legs, but it woke her. The doubt was a fire, but it was not a light. It was just heat.

She wiped her face and looked toward the city. The bells of the cathedral were ringing. Not the soft toll of prayer, but the harsh clang of the alarm. The guards were moving. Something had happened. A fire? Another arrest?

Elara tightened the strap of her satchel. She would not go home to the Red Ward. It was too close to the church. She would walk to the docks, to the warehouses, where the sick had nowhere else to go.

She had no proof of innocence. She had no protection. But she had the knowledge of the willow. She had the knowledge of the bone and the blood.

She walked into the shadows of the alley. The city was afraid. The city needed help. And the priest could not cure a fever.

She stopped at the door of a darkened house, one where a coughing sound had been heard earlier. She knocked, once, twice, hard.

"Elara?" a voice whispered from behind the door. It was an old woman, the mother of the baker's boy.

"Open," Elara said. "I have the salve."

The door creaked open a crack. The woman's face was pale, her eyes wide. "They say you are a witch. They say Father Thomas will burn you."

Elara reached into her bag and pulled out the cloth. "Take it. It will not burn me. It will make him sleep. It will make the pain stop."

The woman hesitated, then the door creaked shut and re-opened wide.

Elara stepped inside, into the dark, smelling of the fear and the sickness. She closed the door on the city. She closed the world. She opened her satchel. The jars sat there, brown glass and green herbs, the only magic in a world of prayers.

She felt the weight of the doubt one last time. Then she set it down. She went to work.
