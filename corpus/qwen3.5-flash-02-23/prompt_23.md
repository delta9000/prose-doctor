The white flag hung limp on the line strung between two jeeps, a tattered sheet of canvas that offered no warning of the silence. Elias Vane stood in the shadow of the main aid tent, listening to the distant, rhythmic thunder of artillery that had gone silent only an hour ago. Now, the ceasefire was a fragile thing, held together by a truce of convenience and the mutual exhaustion of the men on both sides.

Elias wiped his hands on a rag that had long since lost its original white color. The air inside the tent smelled of antiseptic, copper, and unwashed bodies. There were twenty-four men stretched on tarps on the dirt floor, and the light of a single kerosene lamp flickered against the canvas walls, casting dancing shadows that looked like reaching hands.

He looked down at the triage tags hanging around their necks. Green meant they could walk and wait. Yellow meant they could not walk but would survive the night. Red meant immediate attention. And Black meant they were not worth saving.

Elias was a Red. He had to keep them all Red.

The first patient was Private First Class Miller, nineteen years old, American. He lay on the central cot, his left leg a ruin of torn denim and twisted bone. The tourniquet Elias had applied was soaked through with fresh crimson. Miller was awake, eyes darting frantically, breathing in short, shallow gasps.

"Vitals?" Elias asked the corpsman hovering nearby.

"Slow, sir," the corpsman whispered. "BP dropping. Pulse thready."

"Shock," Elias muttered. It was a clinical diagnosis, but it sounded heavy in his mouth. "Bag of O-neg?"

"Empty," the corpsman said, holding up a plastic bag with a red cross printed on the side. "We had two more. One went to the surgeon for the shrapnel in the thorax. The other... well."

Elias followed the corpsman's gaze to a pile of black cloth in the corner. They hadn't even buried the dead yet. The ceasefire meant they could move freely, but they couldn't leave.

He stepped back from Miller and looked across the tent. There, lying on the ground, was the enemy soldier. The uniform was a mottled grey pattern, distinct from the olive drab of the American forces. Jovan was the name Elias had scrawled on the tag; it had been written on the man's dog tags in a moment of frantic efficiency before the shooting stopped.

Jovan was also Red.

He was a young man, face pale as ash, skin slick with sweat. A wound in the abdomen had ripped through the fabric of his shirt. The injury was severe, but the blood was dark, venous. It wasn't arterial spurting; it was a slow leak of the interior. But without a laparotomy and a surgeon to stitch the intestines, he would be dead before the dawn.

Elias knelt beside Jovan. The enemy's eyes opened. For a second, there was nothing but confusion, then the recognition of pain, then the recognition of the man kneeling above him.

Elias pressed a gloved hand to Jovan's stomach. The muscles were rigid, guarding a wound that would eventually kill him.

"Water," Jovan murmured. The voice was barely audible, cracked with dust.

"Shh," Elias said softly. He reached for a canteen on the rack beside him but stopped. There was only one.

He looked back at Miller. Miller needed the blood. Miller was bleeding out internally. Miller was twenty-two, a father of two, Elias knew because he had seen the photo tucked into Miller's webbing. Miller belonged here. Miller belonged to the side that Elias was sworn to protect.

Jovan belonged to the side that had been trying to kill him six hours ago.

The internal calculus was a cold, automated script running in the back of Elias's mind. It weighed probability against probability. Miller had a 40% chance of survival if he got the blood now. Without it, he had a 5% chance. Jovan's wound was clean enough to hold, but the infection risk was high. If Elias operated now, without a surgeon, Jovan had a 20% chance. If he left him alone, it was 100% mortality.

Resources were finite. The blood, the morphine, the attention.

"Vane," a voice called from the tent flap. A Lieutenant, holding a radio. "Ceasefire holding? Or are we about to hear the drums again?"

Elias looked up, his face a mask of indifference. "Ceasefire is holding. I'm working the triage."

"Keep them quiet," the Lieutenant said. "I don't want to see any casualties moving unless they're going to the back line."

The Lieutenant retreated, the canvas flap snapping shut. The silence returned.

Elias looked down at Jovan again. The young man was shivering. The grey uniform was stiff with dried blood. His eyes were dark, not fearful, just tired.

"The blood," Elias said, his voice rough. "You need blood."

Jovan shook his head slightly. "No. My brother. He has the blood."

Elias paused. "You don't have blood."

"Brother," Jovan whispered. "He died. He is... red." He gestured vaguely to the pile in the corner. "I wanted to save him."

Elias felt a chill that had nothing to do with the desert night. "Why did you save him?"

"Because he was my brother," Jovan said. "So I did not run."

Elias looked at Miller. Miller was whimpering now, his head rolling on the pillow. He was in agony. The pain was a tangible thing in the tent, vibrating in the air. Elias reached for the morphine syringe on the table. The box was labeled *Morphine Sulfate*. There was only one left in the drawer.

He had to make a choice. Miller needed the painkiller to reduce the stress on his heart. Jovan needed the painkiller to calm his breathing while they waited for a surgeon who wasn't there.

If he gave it to Miller, the pain would stop. His heart would stabilize. The blood bag would be filled by a transfusion from the back, but the wait was three hours. If he gave it to Jovan, Jovan would sleep. Miller would scream. The stress would accelerate Miller's decline.

It was a medical decision. It was a moral decision. It was a calculation of who deserved to live, and Elias hated the weight of it.

He looked at the two tags. PFC Miller. Private J.

"Will it hurt?" Elias asked.

"No," Jovan said. He watched Elias with an unnerving calmness. "When you put the needle in. It tastes like metal."

Elias hesitated. His hand hovered over the morphine syringe. He looked at Miller's face, pale and terrified. He looked at Jovan's face, grey and resigned.

"Tell me about your brother," Elias said.

Jovan closed his eyes. "He liked to cook. We grew up in the hills. No one comes there. Just mountains." He opened his eyes again. "You have a dog tag. You are a medic."

"I am," Elias said. "I am the only one right now."

"You know how to stop the bleeding?"

"I try," Elias said.

"Then you kill us both," Jovan said. It wasn't an accusation. It was a statement of fact. "You do not have enough hands. You do not have enough time."

Elias didn't answer. He looked at the morphine. He thought about the contract he had signed when he enlisted. He thought about the oath to the wounded, regardless of flag. The Geneva Convention was not just words on a piece of paper; it was a shield. It was the line that kept men from becoming monsters. But the math was still there, lurking in the shadows.

"The morphine is for pain," Elias said. "Not for life."

"My brother said," Jovan whispered, "the sky in the hills... it is blue. Always."

Elias swallowed hard. He looked at Miller. Miller was losing it. The veins in his neck were distending. The pressure was too high. If Elias didn't give the morphine, Miller's heart might stop from the pain-induced shock. And if Miller died, Elias would have to carry that. But if Elias gave the morphine to Jovan, Jovan would be comfortable as he died, and Miller would suffer for the rest of the few minutes he had left.

There was no winning. There was only the weight.

"I will give you the morphine," Elias said. "But you have to promise me something."

Jovan's eyes narrowed. "What?"

"Don't die on me," Elias said. "I need you to survive so I can show you the blue sky."

Jovan smiled. It was a small, broken thing. "If I die... tell my brother I am sorry."

"I know where you live," Elias said. "Hills."

"Go there," Jovan said. "Put the flag down."

"I can't do that," Elias said.

"Then you are a soldier," Jovan said. "And not a man."

Elias stood up. He walked to the table. His hand moved without thinking. He picked up the morphine. He looked at the syringe. He knew what he was doing was technically wrong. But he also knew that if he didn't do it, there would be more blood on his hands than if he did.

But wait. If he gave it to Jovan, Jovan wouldn't die from pain. He would die from the wound. The morphine wouldn't save him.

But Miller... Miller might survive.

Elias looked at the blood bag. It was empty. He had to go to the back line. He had to leave the tent. If he left, Miller would die. If he stayed, Jovan would die.

The internal calculus shifted. It wasn't about the morphine. It was about the blood. He had to go get the blood. It would take twenty minutes. Twenty minutes. Miller might not last that long. Jovan might bleed out in that time.

"Wait," Elias said. He looked at the tourniquet. He could tighten it. It would stop the blood flow to Miller's leg. It would save Miller's life for now. But it would guarantee permanent loss of the limb.

He knelt back down.

"Miller," Elias said, grabbing the boy's good arm. "Listen to me. I have to go get more blood. I'll be back. If you feel faint, squeeze my hand."

"I can't," Miller whispered. "I can't move."

"I'll hold you," Elias said. He looked at Jovan. "Get up."

Jovan's eyes widened. "What?"

"Get up," Elias said. "Help me carry him."

"That is impossible. You cannot move a casualty like that. You need the stretcher."

"I don't have time," Elias said. "There is a wounded man in the back. He needs blood. If we move Miller now, we can get him to the Jeep. The Jeep is right outside. If the ceasefire holds for ten minutes, the medevac is coming."

Jovan struggled to sit up. He clutched his stomach. "You want me... to help?"

"I need two strong hands," Elias said. "You can lift him?"

Jovan looked at Miller. He looked at the American blood flowing into the dirt. "He is... like you."

"I know," Elias said.

They moved together. Elias and Jovan. The medic and the enemy. It was a grotesque dance, lifting the weight of a broken man. They got Miller on the stretcher. The wheels squeaked on the dirt.

Elias pushed the cart. Jovan followed, clutching his side, his uniform tearing where the wound was. They moved outside the tent.

The night air was cool. The white flag snapped in the wind.

"Go," Elias said to Jovan. "Go back. Stay here. If the shooting starts, stay low."

Jovan nodded. "You should come too."

Elias stopped. He looked at the Jeep. The back was open. He could drive the blood back. He could come back for Jovan.

"I have to drive," Elias said. "Miller needs that blood. I have to go to the supply line."

"Miller is American," Jovan said. "I am... what?"

"Elias Vane," Elias said. "Don't move."

"Why?"

"Because if the shooting starts," Elias said, looking at the line of jeeps, "they will shoot the wounded. They won't wait for the ceasefire."

Jovan looked at him. "I know. I know that."

"Then stay," Elias said.

Elias got into the Jeep. He started the engine. The roar of the motor shook the ground. He looked in the rearview mirror. Jovan was standing in the dust, the moonlight catching his face. He didn't move. He didn't run. He just watched.

Elias drove. He knew the supply line was ten miles away. Ten miles of darkness. He knew the risk. If the ceasefire was broken, he would be driving into a gauntlet. If he broke the protocol, he would be court-martialed.

But he drove.

He was an enemy to both sides. A traitor to the rules of war, or perhaps, the only one following it.

When he reached the back line, the medevac was already loading. He handed over the empty blood bag, demanded a new one. He didn't ask why they had extra. He just took the bag.

He drove back. The roads were dangerous. He didn't see the enemy fire. He didn't see the friendly fire. He drove until he saw the Jeeps. He saw the tent.

He stopped. He got out.

Jovan was still there. The American, Miller, was gone. He was being moved to the Jeep. Elias saw him being lifted onto the stretcher by two other medics.

Elias ran to the Jeep. "Miller," he yelled.

The medics looked at him. "The boy is stabilized," one of them said. "He's getting the blood. You need to come back for the other one."

Elias froze. "The other one?"

"Jovan. He is... he is gone."

Elias looked at the spot where Jovan had been standing. There was no body. There was no blood. Just the dust.

"The ceasefire ended," the medic said. "Artillery started ten minutes ago."

Elias looked up. The sky was still dark, but the flashes were lighting up the horizon. The white flag was gone.

He walked to the spot where Jovan had stood. There was a small, grey dog tag in the dirt. The chain was broken.

Elias picked it up. It was warm. He wiped the dust off with the back of his thumb.

He turned back to the Jeep. Miller was already gone, taken to the back. The war had moved on.

Elias put the tag in his pocket. He walked back to the tent. It was empty. The wounded had been evacuated. The dead were gone. The silence was loud.

He knelt by the cot where Jovan had lain. There was a stain on the dirt. Dark. Wet.

Elias stayed there for a long time. He didn't clean it. He didn't mourn. He just remembered the math.

Miller was alive. Jovan was dead.

And Elias carried the weight of it, a heavy, invisible burden in his chest, heavier than the tourniquet, heavier than the blood, heavier than the war. He stood up. He looked at the white flag hanging limp on the line.

He didn't move it. He didn't take it down.

He just looked at the stars, wondering if the sky in the hills was blue.
