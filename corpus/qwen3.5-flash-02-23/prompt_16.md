The engine cuts. The silence that rushes in is heavier than the engine noise. It presses against the sides of the car, a physical weight. Elias sits for a moment with his hands on the steering wheel, knuckles white. The keys are in his hand. They feel too light. He knows what this silence means. It is the silence of safety. It is the silence of a house where the windows are intact and the locks work from the inside.

Here, twenty minutes ago, the silence had been different. The silence of a night without mortar fire is a lie. The air is thick with dust and the possibility of a second strike. Here, the air smells of exhaust and pine needles. He gets out. The gravel crunches beneath his boots, a sound that is too loud, too immediate. He checks the rearview mirror. No one is there. The horizon is flat, green hills rolling into darkening clouds.

He unlocks the front door. The latch clicks, a sharp mechanical sound. Inside, the air is conditioned. It smells of lemon cleaner and the faint, lingering scent of toast. It is a smell that feels like a violation. It triggers a reflex in his throat, a dryness that has nothing to do with water and everything to do with the sudden intrusion of normalcy. He kicks off his boots by the mat, the sound muted by the fabric. They look out of place, caked in a grey dust that doesn't belong to this neighborhood.

He leaves them there. He cannot bring them into the living room. The floorboards are polished. He walks over them. They creak. He stops at the end of the hallway. The house is dim. A lamp in the kitchen casts a yellow pool of light onto the granite countertop.

Maya is sitting at the table. She is not reading. She is staring at a stack of mail. Her hair is tied back in a loose twist, strands escaping to frame her face. She looks tired, but it is a different kind of tired. It is the tired of folding sheets and balancing a household budget, not the tired of a body wound tight with adrenaline. She looks up. Her eyes do not widen in shock. They do not fill with tears. She simply blinks, once, and sets a piece of paper down.

"You're back," she says. Her voice is calm. It lacks the inflection of a greeting. It lacks the inflection of an accusation.

"I am," Elias says. His own voice sounds rusty to him. He clears his throat. The air in his mouth tastes like metal.

He walks into the kitchen. He is aware of the space between them. It is a physical distance, the three feet of open floor separating the dining table from the counter. In this space, he sees the details of the life he missed. A calendar on the fridge. The days are circled in blue ink. Birthdays, anniversaries, dentist appointments. Dates where he should have been present. He sees a stack of envelopes addressed to him at the same address, the postmarks stamped with the weeks of his deployment. They have been opened. The return stamps are on them. She has been dealing with him on his behalf while he was gone.

"Did you sleep?" she asks. She begins to gather the mail, stacking it by color.

"Not yet."

"Sit," she says. She points to the stool opposite her. It is a wooden stool with a soft cushion. It looks uncomfortable to his bruised hips, comfortable to anyone who has never run from a building that is collapsing.

He sits. The cushion squeaks. Maya pours a glass of water from a pitcher and slides it toward him. The condensation on the glass drips onto the granite counter. A single drop. Then another.

"I need to eat," he says. He doesn't want to eat. He wants to strip off his clothes, the ones that carry the sweat of the desert, the ones that smell of burnt rubber, and throw them on the floor. He wants to scrub his skin until it turns raw. But he stands up.

"I made pasta," she says. "It's on the stove."

"I should take a shower first."

"You can do that after. You look like you haven't drunk water in six hours."

Elias looks at her. He wants to tell her that he is not the same man who left. He wants to tell her that the version of himself that packed these bags is gone. But the words sit in his throat, heavy and useless. If he says it, it forces her to acknowledge the change. If he doesn't, he remains a ghost in the room, a man who doesn't fully inhabit his own skin.

He goes to the stove. He ladles sauce onto a plate. He sits back down. He eats. The taste is bland. The texture is soft, too soft. It reminds him of the rations he ate in the bunker. But there is no fear there. There is no fear here. That is the problem. The fear was a constant companion. It was the thing that kept him alive. It was the thing that sharpened his senses. Without it, he feels like he is floating.

Maya watches him eat. She wipes a spot on the counter with a cloth. She does not ask about the front page of the papers he left the house to work on. She does not ask what he saw. She does not ask if people died.

"What happened with the car?" he asks. He tries to fill the silence. He tries to bridge the gap with mundane logistics.

"It stalled on Tuesday," she says. "I had to tow it. The shop said it needs a new timing belt."

"Okay," he says. He doesn't know what else to say. A timing belt is not the same as a mortar strike. A timing belt is something that fixes itself with a mechanic and a wrench. A mortar strike cannot be fixed.

He puts the fork down. He pushes the plate away. "I'm going to get into bed."

"It's ten o'clock," Maya says.

"I haven't slept in three days."

"You just said you had a shower to take."

"Later."

He stands up. He walks out of the kitchen. He walks down the hallway. He stops at his side of the bed. The sheets are white. They are crisp. They smell like laundry detergent, the kind with lavender. He lies down. The mattress is firm. It does not creak.

For a moment, he is back in the field. He is under the bunk bed, waiting for the sound of the jet fighter to fade. He holds his breath. He listens to the wind. But here, he hears the refrigerator hum. He hears the house settling. He hears the car, parked in the driveway, sitting in the dark.

He is safe. The safety is a cage.

He closes his eyes. He tries to find the edge of the room. He tries to orient himself to the geography of the bedroom. The window is to the left. The closet is to the right. He knows this. But his mind has mapped the world differently. He knows the distance from the trench to the safe line. He knows the coordinates of the hospital. He knows the time it takes for a body to cool.

He opens his eyes. The ceiling is white. There is a stain near the corner, a spiderweb. It is a small thing. A house stain. A thing that should not matter.

He turns to the other side of the bed. He expects Maya to be there. She is not. He hears the shower running in the bathroom door down the hall. The water runs against the tiles. It sounds like rain. It sounds like the shelling, but softer.

He sits up. He looks at his hands. They are shaking slightly. He clenches them into fists. He tries to stop the tremor. It is not the shaking of trauma. It is the shaking of the body adjusting to a lack of adrenaline. It is the body saying, *It is over. The threat is gone.* It is a betrayal.

He gets out of bed. He walks to the bathroom. The door is open. The steam is thick. He steps inside. The tiles are cold under his feet. He turns on the shower. He strips off the clothes. The fabric falls to the floor. It makes a dull sound.

He steps into the water. It is hot. It stings his skin. He turns his face up and lets the water wash over him. He scrubs the dust from his arms. It comes in grey streaks. He scrubs until the skin is pink and raw. He tries to wash the smell of the desert from his pores. He tries to wash the smell of the dead from his clothes.

When he turns the water off, the silence rushes back in, even louder. He wraps a towel around his waist. He walks out.

Maya is standing at the sink. She is brushing her teeth. She looks in the mirror. She sees him reflected behind her. She spits. She rinses. She turns off the tap.

"You should brush your teeth," she says.

"I have my own brush."

"Here." She hands him a toothbrush. It is new. The bristles are pristine. He looks at it. He looks at her.

"Did you sleep?" she asks again.

"I need to think."

"About what?"

"About the timing belt."

She pauses. She looks at him, really looks at him, through the lens of the mirror. She sees the darkness under his eyes. She sees the way his muscles are held tight, even when he is standing still. She sees the distance between him and the bed, the distance between him and the man he was five years ago.

"I think about how we used to argue about the timing belt," she says. "Because you were driving it too hard. Because you never put it on the list of things to do."

"It wasn't a list."

"It is now," she says. She reaches past him to the cabinet to get the toothpaste. Her hand brushes his shoulder. It is a warm touch. The skin is unscarred. The muscle is soft. It feels like a country he is not allowed to inhabit anymore. "I need to put it on the list, Elias. I can't keep doing it myself."

The tension in the room tightens. It is not a fight. It is not a shouting match. It is the quiet realization that the list is the only thing that matters now. The things that were important before the departure are now the only things that exist.

"I can't fix the list," he says.

"I know." She puts the toothpaste cap on the counter. She turns off the light. The bathroom is dark, save for the light from the bedroom down the hall. "You don't have to fix the list. Just be here to sign it."

He nods. He doesn't say anything else. He walks back into the bedroom. He sits on the bed. The sheets are cool. He lies down on his back. He looks at the ceiling stain again. He traces the shape of the spiderweb with his eyes.

He thinks about the war. He thinks about the way the ground absorbs the sound. He thinks about the way the sky changes color when the fire burns the trees. He thinks about the way people run. He thinks about the way people stop running.

He thinks about the woman who is sleeping in the other room. He thinks about the life she built while he was gone. It is a life made of small things. Groceries. Bills. Planting flowers in the garden. The timing belt. It is a life that requires no maps. It requires no permission. It is a life that is entirely hers.

He turns onto his side. He faces her side of the bed. The pillow is indented. She has been there ten minutes ago.

He closes his eyes. He tries to imagine the future. He tries to imagine the next day. He imagines the timing belt being replaced. He imagines the car running. He imagines the calendar on the wall with a new page turned. It feels impossible. It feels like a dream he will wake up from, but the alarm clock is not on the nightstand. The alarm clock is a phone that rings in a city that doesn't exist.

He lies still. He listens to the sound of her breathing in the dark. It is rhythmic. It is steady. It is a heartbeat that he does not control.

The world outside is quiet. The cars on the road are far away. The wind moves through the trees. He feels the dust in the carpet, the invisible grit of the war that he brought in with him. He cannot shake it. He cannot wash it out. It is part of the landscape now. It is part of the house.

He knows that tomorrow, the sun will rise. He knows that she will wake up. She will make coffee. She will make a list. She will ask him if he saw the news.

He will say yes. Or he will say no. It does not matter. The list does not care about the news. The list cares about the timing belt. It cares about the grocery budget. It cares about the house.

He realizes with a sudden, sharp clarity that he is not the main character anymore. He is supporting cast. He is the one who walks through the house. He is the one who sleeps in the bed. He is the one who comes home from a place no one wants to talk about.

He closes his eyes. He lets the darkness take him. It is the same darkness as the war. But without the sirens. Without the screams. Just the dark. Just the quiet. Just the waiting for the morning.

He does not sleep immediately. He waits. He listens to the house breathe. He listens to the car parked outside. He listens to the silence that is not silence. It is the hum of a life continuing. It is the hum of a life that does not need him.

It is a sound he knows well. It is the sound of survival.

He waits for the sun. He waits for the morning. He waits for the list to be filled. He waits for the gap to close, or to become a river that he can no longer cross. He waits for the water to rise. He waits for the timing belt to turn.

He is here. He is here. He is here. The repetition is a mantra. A shield. A promise.

He finally sleeps. The darkness is deep. It is not the dark of the bunker. It is the dark of a bedroom in a house that is safe. It is the dark of a life that continues without him.

He is safe. And he is lost. And that is the only truth that remains.

The sun rises at 6:03 AM. It hits the window. It hits the dust motes dancing in the light. The house warms. The silence breaks with the sound of a bird. It tweets once. Then again.

Elias opens his eyes. He is not startled. He does not jump. He looks at the ceiling. The spiderweb is still there.

He gets up. He puts on his boots. They are by the door. He puts them on. He opens the front door. The air is cool. He steps out. He walks to the car. He checks the timing belt. He checks the oil. He checks the tires.

He does not go anywhere. He just sits in the car. He turns the engine on. It runs smoothly.

He waits for her to come out. He waits for her to join him. He waits for the list to begin.

He waits. He breathes. He lets the dust fall off his hands. He lets the dust settle.

He is home. He is home. He is home.

The words do not ring true. They are just sounds. He drives on. The road is empty. The road is wide. He drives until the car is full of gas. He drives until the sun is high. He drives until the silence is louder than the engine.

He sits in the garage. He turns the car off. He sits in the dark. He waits for the engine to cool. He waits for the world to stop spinning.

He is home. He is home. He is home.

He knows that he will never leave again. He knows that he will never be able to leave. He knows that the war is over, but he is still at the front. He is still in the bunker. He is still in the sand. He is still in the silence.

He gets out of the car. He walks to the door. He enters the house. The light is warm. The smell of lemon is still there.

He sits at the table. He opens the list. He takes a pen. He writes his name. He signs the paper.

He stops. He looks at the pen. He looks at the paper. He looks at the door.

He is home. He is home. He is home.

The house is silent. The silence is heavy. He waits for someone to speak. He waits for someone to move. He waits for someone to come in.

He is alone.

He closes his eyes. He waits for the sun to set. He waits for the night to fall. He waits for the war to start again.

It does not start. It never does.

He sits in the dark. He waits. He waits. He waits.
