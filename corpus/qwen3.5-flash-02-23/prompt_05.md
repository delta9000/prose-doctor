The heat inside the Spire District was unnatural, a cycled, pressurized warmth that tasted of recycled water and lavender. Elara stood on the pedestrian bridge of Sector 7, watching the mist roll off the lower levels where the filters had failed. Above, the windows of the corporate towers gleamed with a hard, synthetic light, cutting through the humidity. She adjusted the mask on her face, the silicone seal biting into her cheekbones. It was a Level 3 respirator, standard for a refugee with a Temporary Transit Permit. Her lungs burned slightly, a reminder that the air down here was still being scrubbed by machines that prioritized the citizens above.

She looked down at her wrist. The biometric implant glowed a faint, pulsing amber. It counted her steps, her heart rate, and her remaining credits. She had three hundred and forty-two credits left. Enough for food. Not enough for a full upgrade to the filtration system. She needed to get to the Residency Authority. If she wanted to stay in Oakhaven, she needed to pass the intake. The city above had been built to keep the sea out, but the sea had taken the lower districts first, and now the sea was trying to take the people.

Elara pushed through the crowd. They moved like a single organism, a river of grey coats and polished boots, all wearing the masks of their class. Those on the right side of the walkway had higher-grade seals, white plastic gleaming in the neon glare. Those on the left, like her, wore the matte black of the transit class. The hierarchy was written in the filtration levels. To breathe cleaner air, you paid more taxes. To pay more taxes, you had rights. To have rights, you had to be born under the dome.

She turned into the shadow of the Authority building. It was a monolith of grey concrete and smart-glass, designed to make anyone entering feel small. The entrance was guarded by drones, hovering silently at eye level, their cameras blinking red. Elara swiped her pass. The drones didn't move, but the lights shifted to yellow. She walked through the turnstile. The air inside was cooler, smelling of ozone and old paper.

The lobby was vast, echoing with the sound of footsteps. There were no chairs. The design was deliberate; comfort was a reward for citizenship, not a right for the newly arrived. Elara joined the line. It stretched back into a cavernous waiting area where holographic screens displayed the rules of the new world. *Compliance is Survival. Dissent is Displacement.* The text cycled in a soothing, hypnotic font.

She shifted her weight. Her boots scuffed the floor, the sound loud in the quiet. A man in a suit walked past her, carrying a tablet. He looked at her implant, at the amber light, and then at his own blue one. He didn't stop. He didn't slow down. He just walked through her personal space, the scent of his cologne cutting through the smell of stale air. He was a Citizen, First Tier. He probably paid for his air by the hour.

Elara kept her eyes on the floor. She had learned that eye contact with the upper tiers was a sign of aggression. In Oakhaven, looking someone in the eye without a clearance badge was an invitation to be stopped. She focused on the scuffed tiles, counting the cracks. One, two, three. She was already inside. She just had to get through the door.

When her turn finally came, the booth was a small glass box high up on a platform. She climbed the stairs, her breath coming faster due to the exertion. The desk inside was pristine white. Behind it sat an officer, a woman with silver hair pulled back tight. She wore a standard mask, white plastic, no personalization.

"Pass your credentials," the officer said. Her voice was processed through a synthesizer, stripping all warmth from the tone.

Elara tapped her wrist against the reader. The screen on the desk blinked. "Elara Vance. Sector 9. Arrival Code: 440. Current Status: Transit Permit. Expiration: 30 days."

The officer tapped her stylus against the glass. "You are applying for Residency?"

"Yes. I have the funds. I have the medical clearance. I can work." Elara leaned forward. The air in the booth was stale. "I just need time. The drought in Sector 6 took my home."

The officer stopped tapping. She looked at the screen, then up at Elara. Her eyes were dry, the mask hiding the lower half of her face. "The drought is a historical event, Ms. Vance. We are past the era of emotional narratives. The quotas for Sector 7 are full. There is a waiting list."

"I know the list. I know the wait is ten years."

"Then you will need to leave. Your permit is expiring." The officer slid a card across the desk. It was a rejection slip. A digital token that told the drones on the street to arrest her if she stayed beyond the grace period.

"I can't leave. I have nowhere to go." Elara gripped the edge of the desk.

"Then you must seek temporary housing. The shelters are in the lower sectors. They are overcrowded, but they are open."

Elara looked at the card. It was a death sentence, or at least a slow one. If she was caught sleeping in a building not designated for her status, she would be deported to the Wastes. The Wastes were still flooding.

"Can I... appeal?" Elara asked. Her voice was quiet.

"No appeals for Transit classes. Submit your departure voucher and exit the building."

The officer turned away to a tablet, signaling the end of the conversation. Elara stood there for a moment, the rejection hanging in the air between them. It wasn't cruel, not exactly. It was just the system operating as intended. The city was a ship, and it had a limited capacity. She was a weight that had to be jettisoned to keep the ship afloat.

She took the card. It was cool, smooth plastic. She turned and walked back down the stairs. The line was shorter now. The drones outside hadn't moved. She pushed through the turnstile, and the air outside hit her again. The humidity was oppressive, heavy with the smell of rain that didn't come.

She walked to the edge of the Spire District, where the lights began to fail. The transition was marked by a difference in the pavement, from the polished synthetic tiles to the rough, dark asphalt. Here, the air scrubbers hummed less. The sound of the city was louder. Sirens wailed in the distance. People in worn clothes huddled against the walls, breathing through dirty masks.

Elara found a spot under the overhang of a maintenance shed. She pulled the card out of her pocket and looked at it. She could go to the shelter. The food would be lukewarm, the space a few meters of cot, and the guards would be rude. She could live there for a month before being moved again.

She sat on the edge of the concrete. She took off her mask for a second, just to let her skin feel the damp air. It felt like a crime. She put it back on.

"Rough day?"

Elara jumped. She hadn't heard anyone approach. A woman sat on the adjacent bench, her legs stretched out. She was ageless, her features blurred by a heavy scarf wrapped around her face. She wore a coat that had seen better decades, patched with silver tape. She held a small, wooden cane.

Elara nodded, unsure if she should speak. "Just... the intake."

The woman nodded slowly. "They don't like the refugees, then?"

"Not really. The quotas are tight."

The woman hummed. She reached into her pocket and pulled out a small object. It was a battery pack, old and scratched. She held it out to Elara.

"You take the mask off too much?" the woman asked.

Elara hesitated. "I need to breathe."

"The filters are clogging," the woman said. She tapped her own throat. "You smell like ozone. You're burning out your cells. Your body knows you're outside of it."

Elara looked at the battery pack. It wasn't a power cell for the implant. It was a standard medical battery.

"I don't have credits to pay for that," Elara said.

"I don't want credits," the woman said. "I want to see you breathe." She pressed the battery into Elara's hand. "It has a full charge. It can power a small purifier. A real one. Not the cheap ones the Authority sells."

Elara stared at the battery. It was heavy, dense. It felt like a lifeline. "I can't take this."

"Take it," the woman said, her voice firm. "The city takes from you every day. You keep. Just for tonight."

Elara looked at the woman's eyes. They were old, lined with the same fatigue she felt in her own bones. There was no pity in them, only a recognition of shared struggle.

"Why?" Elara asked.

"Because I remember when the air tasted like metal," the woman said. "My father worked in the filtration plants. He died when the valves froze. I know the cold." She leaned back on the bench. "Just keep it charged. When the power goes, you can recharge it. There are outlets in the alley."

Elara clutched the battery. It was warm from the woman's hand. It felt heavier than the rejection slip. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me," the woman said, turning her head away from the lights. "Just pass it on."

Elara sat there for a long time. The woman didn't speak again. She just sat, listening to the rain start. It was a light drizzle, but it felt like a storm to Elara. She put the battery in her pocket, where her heart beat against it. She stood up and looked toward the lower sectors. She still had no place to go. She still had a permit expiring. But now she had a purifier.

She walked away from the bench, the battery burning against her hip. The city was still cold, and the hierarchy was still rigid. The drones were still watching. But for the first time since she had crossed the sea line to get here, she didn't feel like a ghost. She felt like a person who had been given a tool to fight back. She adjusted her mask, tighter this time, and stepped out into the rain. The water hit the lens of her glasses, blurring the neon lights, but she didn't care. She walked toward the lower sectors, where the lights were dim, following the path the woman had pointed out in the map of the shadows.
