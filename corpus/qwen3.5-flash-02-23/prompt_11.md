The air inside St. Jude’s Church was thick with the scent of waxy lilies and old wool, a perfume of decay that hung suspended in the cold, drafty light that filtered through the high windows. It smelled of wet moss and the stagnant water of the baptismal font. Elias sat in the second pew from the altar, his hands clasped tightly over his cane, which leaned against his knee like a silent, skeletal guest waiting its turn to speak. He did not fidget. He did not shift in his hard, wooden seat with its fabric worn thin at the edges. He simply endured, his posture rigid against the softness of the room, his spine a straight line of resistance. He had known this would be the day he would have to sit opposite ghosts, or at least the living echoes of the man who had made them ghosts in their own way. The church was cold, the draft creeping in through the stained glass where the lead had cracked decades ago, allowing beams of gray light to pierce the gloom and illuminate the dust motes dancing in the shafts. They danced in the silence, weightless and indifferent to the grief below them. He watched them, counting the moments, letting the seconds pile up like coins in a jar. They were all waiting for him to move, or waiting for the man in the box to move, but mostly they were waiting for Elias to decide how much of his father’s legacy he would accept today, how much of the burden he could bear without his knees buckling. Arthur hadn’t changed in death. He still demanded order, even in his silence. The coffin was a polished mahogany box, too heavy for the men carrying it a week ago, sitting at the altar where the cross hung slightly askew, a crooked judgment on the ceiling. Elias felt the familiar tightness in his chest, the same tightness that had kept him here for twenty years while the younger one took flight, leaving him to tend the house like a jailer. He looked at the empty chair next to him, the black velvet cushion pristine and mocking, untouched by the weight of a body. It was meant for the sister, but she was not here. Yet. The silence stretched, thick and suffocating, like a blanket pulled over a sleeping child who couldn’t breathe, the air growing heavier with every passing minute as the congregation shuffled in their seats.

Outside, the rain fell in sheets, turning the gravel of the churchyard into a muddy slurry that threatened to swallow tires whole, the water pooling in the potholes that had formed over the last winter storm. Mara stood under the awning of the rectory, watching the water run off the slate roof in a curtain of silver, blurring the world into a watercolor painting of gray and black. She held the collar of her coat tight against her throat, though it offered little protection from the chill that started in her bones and radiated outward to her fingertips, turning her skin the color of a bruised plum. She had arrived ten minutes late. Ten minutes was enough time for the neighbors to whisper behind veiled hands, enough time for the gossip to spread that she had only come back for the house, that she had only come back for the inheritance that waited in the lawyer’s office. She hated herself for arriving on time, for the way her car had sputtered to a halt in the driveway, her tires kicking up mud that she would have to scrub from her shoes later, from the leather of her coat, from her soul, which felt stained with the same dirt. She looked at the church doors. They were oak, heavy and varnished, the brass handles tarnished by years of touch, years of prayer or fear, years of the dead and the living alike seeking comfort. She thought of the letter on her dashboard, the one he had never sent, the one she had never opened until three days ago. It was an invitation to come home. It felt like an order, a command from a king whose kingdom had crumbled into dust. She pushed the door open, the wood groaning under the force of her entry, a sound of protest against the intrusion into the quiet. The sudden silence inside was heavier than the rain outside, a physical weight that pressed on her eardrums, muffling the sound of her own breath. She stepped into the pews, her heels clicking on the stone floor, a rhythm that cut through the murmur of the mourners like a knife, a sharp noise in a room designed for whispers. She saw him in the second pew. She had expected to see the father’s eyes in him, but Elias had his mother’s stubborn mouth, a mouth that had never learned to smile, a mouth that had only ever spoken in commands.

Elias saw her before she saw him. He felt the shift in the room, the way the air grew thinner as she moved down the aisle, the displacement of the atmosphere, the way the other mourners stiffened. He did not look up immediately. He let her pass, let her navigate the gap between the pews, let her feel the weight of the stare from the congregation, from the people who had known her name before hers was forgotten. It was a deliberate punishment, one he had rehearsed in the car on the drive over, the anger building up like steam in a pressure cooker until it shook his hands. He watched her hands, the way she gripped the back of the pew to steady herself as if she were unsure of the ground beneath her, her knuckles white against the dark wood. She looked older than in the photograph on the mantle, which was ironic considering he was the one who had stayed behind and aged, while she had gone out and lived, while he had grown old in the dust. She wore black, but it was a modern black, a sleek coat that cost more than his first car, expensive and practical and cold to the touch. He felt a flare of resentment, hot and sudden, rising in his throat, an acid burn that made him taste bile. Why did she look so put together while he was in these worn trousers and this stained shirt, the one with the coffee ring on the cuff? It wasn't justice, he knew. It was just life, uneven and unfair, a scale that tipped without reason. She took the seat in the fourth pew, far enough away to create a buffer zone, a moat of empty space, but close enough to be a presence, a reminder of what could have been, of the sister he had lost before she was even fully gone. The minister began to speak, his voice echoing off the high vaulted ceiling, booming out of the speaker system with artificial resonance that made the words sound hollow. The words were familiar, comfortingly generic. *We are gathered here in the hope of the resurrection.* Elias didn’t believe in resurrection. He believed in the cold earth and the weight of the dirt. He watched Mara’s profile in his peripheral vision. She stared at the altar, not the coffin. She was thinking of where she would live. She was thinking of the inheritance, or perhaps the house that sat on the hill, the house he had kept running on a dying budget.

The service ended. The congregation stood, a mass of wool and black suits rising from the pews, the sound of fabric rubbing together like dry leaves in the wind, a rustling of bodies leaving a room they had not wanted to enter. Elias stood slowly, the joints in his knees popping in time with the church bell, a rhythm of decay, a sound of bones complaining of the movement. He waited for Mara. She stood, smoothing the fabric of her coat, her movements precise and controlled, as if she were trying to erase the last ten minutes of her arrival. They did not walk out together. Elias moved toward the exit, his cane tapping a steady beat against the floorboards, a countdown, a metronome ticking down the moments. Mara lingered, waiting for the crowd to thin, for the minister to shake a few more hands, for the room to empty of people who wanted to say something they didn't mean. When she finally stepped into the sanctuary, Elias was at the door, waiting. He didn’t turn. He simply opened the door and let the cold air rush in, a gust of damp wind that smelled of wet earth and pine and the rot of fallen leaves. They walked in silence until they were under the shelter of the porch, the rain drumming on the metal roof above them, the sound relentless and loud enough to drown out their thoughts. The minister was shaking hands with the older women, his voice warm, oblivious to the tension crackling between the two siblings like static electricity, the air charged with unspoken history. The gravel crunched under Elias’s boots as he turned to face her. There was no hug. There was no embrace. Just the space between them, filled with the rain and the smell of wet earth. *You look tired,* Elias said. His voice was rough, unused to softness, the words worn down by years of silence and the gravel in the throat. Mara stared at the mud at his feet, the boots scuffed and worn, the laces frayed. *You look the same,* she replied. The lie was heavy on her tongue, thick and difficult to swallow, heavy as a stone. He looked ten years older. He had the gray that had taken the black years ago, the skin the color of parchment.

Elias reached into his pocket and pulled something out. It was an old, leather-bound book. The journal of the estate. Arthur’s. It was supposed to be in the lawyer’s office, in a locked drawer. Mara looked at it. It was the thing she wanted to avoid. She thought he was going to offer it to her, or throw it, or demand she take it. He held it out, the leather worn, the pages yellowed with age, the edges soft and frayed. *He wrote notes,* Elias said. *Every day. About the soil. The crops. The rain.* Mara reached out. Her fingers brushed his. The contact was brief, electric, a spark in the damp air, a flash of warmth in the cold day. *He wrote about you,* Elias said. *The day you left.* Mara’s breath hitched. She took the book. It was heavy, the weight of the past settling in her arms, the spine cracking slightly. *He didn't want you to go,* Elias said. He didn't mean it. It was a story, or maybe it was the truth, or maybe it was just what he needed to say to make the silence easier. It didn't matter. It was the bridge. Mara nodded, a small movement, her eyes stinging with cold. She tucked the book under her arm. *Thank you,* she said. It was the first time they had spoken in years without the sound of a door slamming. She walked to the grave site. Elias followed. The ground was churned, the grass ripped from the earth, the hole a dark mouth waiting to close, the dirt piled high on the side like a barrier. The minister lowered the box into the hole. The sound of the wood hitting the dirt was final, a heavy thud that echoed in the hollow chest of the grave. Elias stood beside her. He didn't speak. He just stood there, a wall of gray wool and rain, his shoulders hunched against the weather. Mara looked at the dirt. It was the same dirt that covered the garden bed where they used to plant the tomatoes, the same black earth that fed the weeds and the roses. He had hated the tomatoes, she thought. He said they made his back ache. She looked at him. He wasn't looking at the dirt. He was looking at the horizon. The sun was trying to break through the clouds, just a sliver of gold, a promise of warmth. *The tomatoes were a mistake,* Elias said. *But they were sweet.* Mara smiled. It was a small thing, a crack in the ice, a small shift in the pressure. *Yeah,* she said. *They were.* They stood there until the earth was piled high, a mound of brown against the gray sky, filling the hole where the wood had been. Then they walked back to the car. Elias opened the front door of his truck. Mara got in the passenger seat. It wasn't a truce. It was just a beginning, a quiet acknowledgment that they were both still alive, both still here, both still bound to the house and the man and the rain.
