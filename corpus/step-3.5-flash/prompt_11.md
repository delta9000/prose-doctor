The rain began as a mist when the hearse turned onto the cemetery road, a soft grey veil blurring the newly turned earth and the bowed heads of the twenty or so mourners. It was a pathetic fallacy so cliché it felt like a personal insult. Daniel adjusted his black tie, the silk already feeling too tight against his throat, and watched the pallbearers—his father’s old golfing buddies, men with faces like worn leather—struggle with the weight of the walnut casket. He had chosen the model himself, a streamlined, unadorned box that cost more than his first car. A final, silent act of control from a man who exercised it to the very end.

From across the gravel drive, Eleanor saw him. Daniel. Her brother. He was standing ramrod straight, hands clasped in front of him, a study in composed grief. She wanted to laugh, or maybe scream. His suit was a perfect, expensive drape of charcoal wool. Hers, from a rental shop that smelled of mothballs and regret, was a size too big, the jacket gaping at the shoulders. She shoved her hands into her pockets, fingers curling around a crumpled tissue and the smooth, cool metal of her father’s old Zippo lighter, which she’d found in his desk drawer last week. A secret she hadn’t told anyone.

The priest’s voice, amplified and tinny through a portable speaker, was swallowed by the drizzle. *“Ashes to ashes, dust to dust…”* The words were a litany, empty syllables. Daniel’s mind was on the eulogy he hadn’t given. He’d stood at the podium in the funeral home, mouth dry, the script his mother had written for him burning a hole in his pocket. He’d looked out at the faces—aunts who smelled of lavender and disapproval, cousins who exchanged curious glances—and seen only the void where his father’s approval should have been. He’d nodded once to the officiant and sat down, the silence roaring in his ears. He could still feel his mother’s disappointed pressure on his arm.

Eleanor’s mind was on the argument. The last one. Three years ago, in this same sterile garden, at her mother’s graveside. Her father, his face a granite mask, had told her she was throwing her life away, that her art was “a hobby for people with trust funds.” She’d thrown a clump of sod at his polished shoe. “You never saw anything I made!” she’d shouted. “You only saw what it wasn’t!” He’d turned and walked away, his back rigid. She hadn’t spoken to him since. Now, his body was in that box, and she couldn’t remember the sound of his voice saying her name with anything but coldness.

The service ended. The first few shovelfuls of wet dirt were thrown onto the casket with a sickening, final thud. People began to shuffle forward, a receiving line of condolences. Daniel took his place beside his mother, who was a fragile bird of a woman in a black dress two sizes too large for her shrinking frame. He put his arm around her, feeling the fine, relentless tremor in her shoulders.

Eleanor hung back, letting the line flow past her. She watched Daniel perform his role. *So sorry for your loss, Mrs. Harrison. He was a great man.* He said it with a smooth, professional sorrow that made her teeth ache. A cousin, Margaret, clasped his hand, her eyes glistening. “He was so proud of you, Daniel. The firm, the house…” Daniel gave a tight, minimal smile. *Proud.* The word was a ghost. Proud of the son who took over the business, not the one who painted.

Then Margaret saw Eleanor. Her expression shifted to one of polite, pained obligation. “Eleanor. You made it.” She enveloped Eleanor in a cloud of cheap funeral flowers and damp wool. “Your father… he mentioned you, in the last weeks. He hoped you’d come.”

*He mentioned me.* Not *he missed you*. Not *he loved you*. Just a factual report, like noting the weather. Eleanor nodded, the lie of “Thank you” sticking in her throat. She moved past, her eyes fixed on the slick, muddy ground, avoiding Daniel’s gaze. But she felt it on her, a physical weight. She risked a glance. He was looking at her, his face an unreadable mask. His eyes, the same pale blue as her own, were chips of ice. She gave a single, stiff nod and moved toward the collection of umbrellas at the edge of the lawn, putting space between them.

The wake was held at her mother’s insistence in the Harrison house, the big Tudor Daniel had helped his father fix up. Eleanor hovered near the bay window in the living room, nursing a lukewarm soda. The room was a museum of a life she didn’t recognize. Framed certificates for Daniel’s business awards. A silver picture frame with a photo of her father and Daniel, both in crisp white shirts, arms slung over each other’s shoulders at Daniel’s college graduation. No pictures of her. She’d long ago removed the ones from her own room.

Daniel worked the room like a politician. He knew everyone’s names, their children’s names, their ailments. He refilled drinks, offered canapés from a tray with a steady hand. He was the son, the heir, the one who carried the torch. Every now and then, his eyes would flick to the window where Eleanor stood, a dark, silent blot against the rainy afternoon light. He saw her looking at the wall of photos, her shoulders hunched. He felt a familiar, hot surge of frustration. *What did you expect?* he wanted to shout. *A shrine to watercolor landscapes?* But the words died. She was his sister. The one who’d taught him how to catch a frog in the creek behind this very house. The memory was a sharp, painful thing.

His father’s brother, Uncle Frank, a man with a booming voice and a liver-spotted nose, clapped Daniel on the back. “The spitting image, Danny. Honest to God, I look at you and see my brother at your age.” He lowered his voice, beer breath hot in Daniel’s ear. “Tough about the old man and Eleanor. Never could understand it. She always was a difficult girl.”

Daniel’s smile didn’t waver, but inside, something cracked. *Difficult.* Because she’d refused to be polished, because she’d asked questions, because she’d painted the ugly, passionate things instead of building safe, profitable things. “She’s going through a lot,” Daniel heard himself say, the words tasting strange and generous. He glanced at Eleanor again. She was now talking to an elderly aunt, her smile brittle, performing her own role of the bereaved, wayward daughter.

Eleanor was listening to Aunt Myrtle reminisce about her father’s famous Christmas fruitcake, a story she’d heard a hundred times. She nodded mechanically, her eyes drifting to a shelf where her father’s prized hunting trophies were displayed: a majestic, glass-eyed buck, a snarling bobcat. And there, tucked behind a leather-bound set of encyclopedias, was something else. A small, ceramic figurine of a dancing mouse, its paint chipped, one ear missing. Her breath caught.

She’d made that in third grade. A clumsy, lopsided thing. She’d brought it home, mortified, but her father had put it right there on his desk, next to his fountain pens and financial reports. “Best dancer in the house,” he’d said, his voice not cold, but warm with a rare, unguarded delight. She’d forgotten. Completely. The years of silence, the last bitter fight, had sanded that memory away. Seeing it now, hidden but not discarded, was a physical blow. Her composure, the thin veneer of detached sorrow she’d maintained all day, shattered. A hot, treacherous tear escaped, tracing a path down her cheek.

Daniel saw it happen. The slight tremor of her chin. The swift, angry swipe of her hand across her face. He saw the object of her gaze—the stupid, ugly little mouse. A wave of understanding, so profound it left him dizzy, washed over him. It wasn’t just about pride, or careers, or his father’s terrible, limited capacity for love. It was about a thousand small, buried moments like that one. Moments he’d been too busy, too ambitious, too intent on being the *good son* to see. Moments his father had kept, hidden in plain sight.

The room was full of noise, of clinking glasses and murmuring voices. But in that instant, for Daniel, it was silent. He saw his sister not as the source of familial strife, not as the embarrassment, but as a little girl with clay-smeared hands, presenting a ridiculous gift to a man who, for once, had seen her completely.

He left his post by the punch bowl. He moved through the crowd, a strange calm settling over him. He stopped a few feet from her. She was looking down, trying to regain her composure, her back to him.

“Eleanor,” he said. His voice was quiet, not the public orator’s voice, but the one he used when he was fourteen and had broken her favorite model spaceship.

She turned. Her eyes were red-rimmed, but clear. She waited, guarded.

“The mouse,” he said, nodding toward the shelf. “I’d forgotten about that.”

She followed his glance, then looked back at him. A muscle twitched in her jaw. “I thought you’d thrown it out.”

“He kept it,” Daniel said. The statement was simple, enormous. *He kept it. For you.*

A long silence stretched between them, filled with the ghost of that little ceramic mouse and the deafening roar of all the words never said. The unspoken tension—the years of blame, of chosen sides, of wounded pride—was still there, a thick, palpable wall. But in the space where that wall met the ground, a single, hairline crack had appeared, lit by the memory of a shared, hidden truth.

“He was impossible,” Eleanor whispered, the words tearing out of her.

Daniel let out a short, sharp breath that wasn’t quite a laugh. “The most.”

Another silence. This one different. Carrying the weight of the admission, but also a strange, fragile relief. The rain outside had softened to a drizzle, pattering gently on the leaded glass.

“I should go,” Eleanor muttered. “Help Mom with the… the leftovers.” It was a feeble excuse, and they both knew it.

“Yeah,” Daniel said. He didn’t move to stop her. He watched her navigate the crowd, her steps a little less stiff, and slip out of the room.

The connection was over as quickly as it had formed. No hug, no promise to talk, no reconciliation. Just a single, silent moment of recognition in a room full of ghosts. But it was enough. It was a crack. Daniel turned back to the crowd, to Uncle Frank’s next story, but his eyes kept drifting to the shelf, to the chipped, dancing mouse. He felt the tightness in his chest ease, just a fraction. The wall was still there. But for the first time, he could see a way, maybe not through it, but around it. He picked up a fresh glass of punch, his hand steady, and raised it in a silent, solitary toast to a terrible father and a lost sister, and to the small, stubborn, enduring facts that no one could ever take away.
