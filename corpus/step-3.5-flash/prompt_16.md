The plane’s tires kiss the tarmac with a sound that feels obscenely gentle. No thud, no shudder, just a long, soft sigh of rubber on concrete. Alexander watches through the small window as the familiar sweep of the airport terminal approaches, all glass and steel and advertised destinations. His hands are clean. That’s the first thing he notices. For six months, his hands have been a map of the conflict: ingrained dust that no amount of scrubbing could shift, old nicks and scars that reopened in the dry heat, the permanent faint stain of ink from his pen. Now, they are pale and empty, resting on the arms of the chair like foreign objects.

He moves through the disembarkation in a daze, a ghost in the machine of homecoming. The smells change first. The recycled, chemical scent of the aircraft cabin gives way to the airport’s amalgam of coffee, disinfectant, and luggage wax. It’s a clean smell. A sanitized smell. His lungs, accustomed to the taste of smoke and decay and dust, feel cheated.

Customs is a formality. The agent stamps his passport with a decisive *thump* and waves him through. “Welcome home,” the agent says, a phrase so generic it’s almost meaningless. Alexander gives a tight nod, the muscles in his face feeling stiff. Home. The word is a stone in his shoe.

He sees her then, just outside the arrivals gate. Elara. She’s not holding a sign. She’s standing perfectly still, wearing a simple, expensive-looking coat the colour of wet slate. Her hair is different—shorter, tucked neatly behind one ear. She’s looking at a phone, her thumb moving in a slow, deliberate scroll. The sight of her, so composed, so *untouched*, knocks the air from his lungs. He’d imagined her there, breathless, scanning the crowd, her eyes finding his and lighting up. Instead, she is a statue in a sea of movement.

He stops three feet away. “Elara?”

She looks up. Her eyes are the same, a startling grey-green like sea glass. But the expression in them is not the one he carried in his memory over bombed-out streets and through refugee columns. It’s not warmth, not exactly. It’s a kind of careful, polite assessment. “Alexander,” she says. Her voice is the same, but it’s being used differently. It’s the voice she probably uses for difficult clients or to calm an anxious neighbour.

“You’re here,” he says, stating the obvious. He hefts his single, battered duffel bag. It feels obscenely heavy.

“I said I would be.” She gives a small, tight smile that doesn’t reach her eyes. “Come on. The car’s this way.”

The drive is a silent film with a soundtrack of traffic. She navigates with calm precision, one hand on the wheel, the other resting lightly on her knee. The car is new to him—a silent, sleek electric vehicle that glides over the road. No radio plays. The silence is not companionable; it’s a thicket they are both carefully avoiding.

He stares out the window. The city is the same, and yet utterly alien. The buildings have been repainted. New cafes with minimalist signage have squeezed between old bookshops. The park where they used to walk their dog, now a ghost he hasn’t allowed himself to fully mourn, has new landscaping. Everything is polished, vibrant, *finished*. It’s a world that has continued its inexorable march forward without him, and he is a relic from a different geological era.

“How was the flight?” Elara asks, her eyes on the road.

“Fine. Long.” He is a master of the understatement. What does he say? That the woman in the seat next to him wept quietly for the entire last hour, overcome by simple exhaustion? That the clouds over the ocean looked like god’s own cotton wool, pristine and meaningless?

“Good.” A pause. “You look tired.”

“I am.” He is bone-deep tired, but not in a way sleep can fix. It’s a weariness of the soul, a constant, low-grade vibration of remembered screams and collapsing structures. Here, the loudest sound is the hum of the tires on the asphalt.

They pull into a driveway. His stomach lurches. It’s not their old flat. It’s a house. A small, terraced house with a little patch of herb garden in the front and a bright blue door. “We moved,” Elara says, as if reading his mind. “Eighteen months ago. You would have gotten the forms.”

He had. Filed them in a box with other bureaucratic horrors. He’d signed them with a shaking hand in a plywood office, the generator humming outside. *Your possessions will be held in storage. Your tenancy has been terminated.* He’d thought of her, alone in their old life, packing his books, his clothes, the stupid ceramic mug he’d made her in a pottery class. He’d imagined her weeping over a cardboard box. The reality is this clean blue door.

Inside, the house is a masterpiece of serene order. Light floods through large windows. The air smells of lemon polish and something floral—maybe the bunch of white lilies on the kitchen counter. There are no piles of mail on the side table, no coats thrown over chairs. It is a space that has not been contested by chaos or absence.

“This is nice,” Alexander manages. It’s a lie that tastes like ash.

“It’s got a good garden,” Elara says, toeing off her shoes. They are soft, leather ballet flats. He remembers her in worn-out Converse, boots caked in mud from weekend hikes. “And a proper shower. No more wrestling with the old boiler.”

He stands in the hallway, a bulky intrusion in his combat trousers and worn fleece. His duffel bag feels like a sack of surrendered flags. “Can I… put this down?”

“Of course.” She gestures vaguely towards a door. “That’s your old study. It’s the guest room now.”

*Guest room.* The phrase lands like a physical blow. The room he designed with her, choosing the paint colour—a soft grey—the desk by the window for the light. He pushes the door open.

It is, as she said, a guest room. A neutral duvet cover in beige. A small,.empty bookshelf. His large oak desk is gone, replaced by a simple Ikea table holding a lamp and a few books on mindfulness and sustainable architecture. His life, his work, his chaos—reduced to a storage unit somewhere, while this room has been sanitized of him. There is no trace.

He dumps his bag on the floor and turns to find Elara in the doorway, holding a glass of water. “I thought you might want this.”

“Thanks.” He takes it. The glass is cold and smooth. It feels like a prop in a play he doesn’t know the lines to.

“The bathroom’s at the end of the hall. I’ll… I’ll let you get settled.” She retreats, her footsteps silent on the wooden floor.

He showers until the water runs cold. He scrubs at his skin, at the memory of grit under his nails, but the clean, floral-scented soap only seems to highlight the chasm. Dressed in clean but unfamiliar sweatpants and a t-shirt from a Samaritan aid organization—a relic from his last trip—he emerges. The house is quiet. He finds her in the living room, sitting on the floor with a yoga mat, a laptop open beside her. She’s in leggings and a cropped top, her body lithe and strong. She’s watching a tutorial, her body moving into a complex pose with an ease that shocks him. His Elara loved a good hike, but she was clumsy, uncoordinated, always tripping over roots.

“You do yoga?” he asks from the doorway.

She holds the pose for a beat longer, then flows into a seated position, stretching her neck. “Yeah. For my back. And my head.” She closes the laptop. “It helps.”

He sits on the edge of the sofa, a vast continent of fabric between them. “I didn’t know you had back problems.”

“It’s just… tension. From sitting at a desk all day.” She says it like it’s the most obvious thing in the world. And it is. Her world now involves a desk. His world involves the ground, the hard-packed earth, the inside of armoured vehicles.

“What do you do?” he asks. The question feels huge, unanswerable.

“I’m a project manager for a green energy co-op.” She says it simply. “We’re retrofitting social housing. It’s good work. Busy.”

*Green energy. Social housing.* Words from a different language. His vocabulary is of cordons and checkpoint delays, of IED blasts and medical triage, of the terrible, beautiful, broken poetry of people who have lost everything. Her vocabulary is of budgets, permits, and sustainable materials.

“That’s… great,” he says. It’s all he can offer.

Dinner is a salad she made, with roasted vegetables and quinoa. It’s delicious. It is also the most alien meal he has ever eaten. There is no communal pot, no tearing of bread with dirty fingers, no shared plate in a cave of a kitchen with the stench of unwashed bodies and diesel. Here, they use separate bowls. The only sound is the clink of cutlery on ceramic.

“How’s your mum?” she asks, a safe, pre-approved topic.

“She’s good. Sends her love.” He talks about his mother’s garden, a predictable, safe narrative. He leaves out the part where he had to call her from a satellite phone, his voice cracking as he told her he was okay after a mortar landed fifty metres from his position. He’d screamed into the receiver after hanging up, the sound swallowed by the desert wind.

“That’s nice,” Elara says. “You should see her more often.”

The accusation, mild as it is, hangs in the air. *You are not here.*

“I will,” he promises, a promise he knows is already a lie. How can he explain that the very idea of routine, of scheduled visits, feels like a cage? His life is measured in embeds, in deadlines, in the next dangerous trip. Hers is measured in quarterly reports and yoga classes.

After dinner, she doesn’t suggest they watch a film. She says, “I should probably let you sleep. Jet lag, right?”

Right. The great, convenient excuse. “Yeah.”

“The pulls in the hall are fresh. There’s a spare toothbrush in the cabinet.” She gives him that small, closed smile again. “Sleep well, Alexander.”

He lies in the bed in the beige guest room, staring at the ceiling. The sheets are cool and smooth. The room is silent, so profoundly silent it rings in his ears. He is used to the night sounds: the distant, nervous pop of a gun, the murmur of generators, the ever-present hum of anxiety. Here, there is only the quiet hum of the refrigerator downstairs and the sigh of the central heating coming on. A siren wails in the distance, a police or ambulance, and his body goes rigid before he remembers—this is not a city under bombardment. This is a city with functioning emergency services.

He thinks of her on the yoga mat, her body strong and controlled. She built this. She built this life of order and health and forward momentum. She didn’t just wait. She *constructed*. She took the rubble of his absence and laid new bricks. She found a new language, a new rhythm.

And he is the ghost who has wandered into the finished building, a draft from a broken window. He is the echo of a man she once knew, a man who is buried under layers of sand and smoke and trauma she has no map for. She looks at him with a kind, sad bewilderment. Who is this tired, jumpy, deeply un-yoga man in her clean hallway? He doesn’t know the answer himself.

He gets up, pads to the window. The street is dark, peaceful. A neighbour’s cat slips between the garden fences. A life continuing. He has come home to a person who is, in every way that matters, already gone. The war he left is a physical place of shattered concrete and screaming. The war he has returned to is this silent, beautiful house, this woman he loves who lives in it, and the terrifying, unmappable gap between the man he was and the ghost he has become. The most dangerous front line, he realizes, shivering under the cool duvet, is right here, in this quiet, lavender-scented room. And he has no strategy, no flak jacket, no press pass for this.
