The city of Veridian was a corpse breathing. Its breath was the thin, sour mist that clung to the cobblestones each dawn, carrying the scent of damp stone, overflowing cesspits, and the underlying, cloying sweetness of decay. Elara moved through the narrowing arteries of the artisan quarter, her woven basket heavy with dried herbs, her boots whispering over the flagstones. The usual din was gone, replaced by a hollow, watchful silence. Shutters were bolted, doors stood ajar only for the desperate or the dying.

Her own door, marked by a simple sprig of dried rosemary tied to the lintel—a common enough charm against ill humors—was always open. She entered the cool, shadowed space of her cottage, the air within thick with the competing perfumes of her craft: the sharp pine of juniper berries, the earthy musk of valerian root, the clean bitterness of wormwood. Shelves bowed under the weight of clay pots, linen bundles, and glass jars filled with tinctures that held the sunset in amber or the twilight in deep violet.

The first knock came as the bells of the Cathedral of the Illuminated Heart tolled the third hour after noon. It was a weak, scraping sound. Elara opened it to find Tomas, the weaver’s apprentice, clinging to the doorframe. He was a skeleton draped in parchment skin, his eyes wide with a fever that burnished them. He clutched a small, grimy pouch.

“Mistress Elara,” he rasped, the words tearing from a throat raw with ulcers. “The cough… it’s tearing me apart from the inside. Master Cren said… he said your smoke would help.”

She ushered him in, her hands gentle on his bone-thin arm. “Sit. Let me see.” His chest was a battlefield of ragged sounds. She listened with her ear to his back, her fingers pressing gently along his spine. The rhythmic, wet crackle was unmistakable.

“You’ve been breathing the bad air in the dye-works,” she murmured, more to herself than him. She moved to her hearth, where a small pot of water simmered. From her stores, she took a generous handful of dried horehound, a spoonful of sticky marshmallow root, and a few flakes of dried coltsfoot. These she added to the pot. “This will loosen the phlegm. You must drink it hot, three times a day.” She fetched a clean cloth, dipped it in a basin of water steeped with lavender and a touch of vinegar, and pressed it to his forehead. The coolness made him sigh.

From his pouch, he produced a few tarnished coppers, his payment. She accepted them, her expression unreadable. “The smoke,” he whispered, looking at the small, cold hearth. “They say you make a smoke that carries the sickness out.”

Elara hesitated. The fumigation was her riskiest remedy. A blend of juniper, rosemary, and cloves, burned in a sealed chamber. It could scour the lungs, but in the wrong lungs, it could inflame them. To the city’s fearful eye, a cloud of pungent smoke rising from a healer’s window looked less like medicine and more like a conduit for demons. “I will not burn it in your master’s house,” she said firmly. “Come here at night. I will treat you then. The smoke is for my own… purification.” It was a half-truth, a shield.

He nodded, desperate, and she sent him on his way with a small bundle of the horehound mixture. As the door closed behind him, the weight of the city’s gaze seemed to settle on her shoulders. She was a necessary evil, a last resort when the priests’ prayers and the physicians’ bleedings failed. But necessity had a short fuse.

The confrontation came two days later, as she was gathering chamomile from her small, walled garden—a defiant patch of green. Father Caelum’s shadow fell across her plants, tall and stark against the afternoon sun. He was not a large man, but his presence filled the alley, backed by the unspoken authority of the Cathedral and the fear of the townsfolk peering from behind their curtains.

“Elara,” he said, his voice a dry rustle of old parchment. It held no warmth. “I have received… concerns.”

She did not stand, merely plucked a flower head, its petals golden. “Concerns about the dying, Father? Or about my methods?”

“Your methods are the concern.” He stepped closer, his robes brushing the soil. “Tomas, the weaver’s boy. He was seen entering your house at night. Under cover of darkness. What fumigation do you practice? What spirits do you summon with your smoke?”

“I summon clean air, Father,” she said, meeting his eyes. “I use juniper and rosemary. Smoke to carry the miasma away. It is an old trick of the marsh-dwellers.”

“An old *pagan* trick,” he spat the word. “You traffic in the humors, in secret humours. You play at physician while the true healers, the servants of the One God, pray for souls. You offer a false hope, a fleshly cure for a divine scourge. This plague is a test of faith, a purging of the corrupt! Your herbs… your *smoke*… it tempts people from the path of repentance.”

Elara’s hands, usually so steady, trembled slightly. She closed them around the basket’s handle. “Tomas has a mother who will bury no more children. If my herbs give him a chance to breathe until God decides his time, what is the sin in that?”

“The sin is in your arrogance!” Caelum’s voice rose, drawing the attention of the hidden watchers. “You place your trust in dirt and roots, in material things, and so you lead others to do the same. You are a distraction from the truth. The plague takes whom it will. Your ‘remedies’ are but theatre, and dangerous theatre at that. I have heard whispers… of powders that cause sleep, of salves that burn like hellfire. This is witchcraft, Elara. The Book of Radiance is clear.”

He was quoting the city’s legal and religious code. *Witchcraft. The word hung in the air, heavier than the plague mist.* It carried the weight of the pyre, of the iron, of being cut off from the community and from God’s mercy.

“My powders are poppy for pain, and my salves are honey and mustard to draw out corruption,” she said, her voice low and fierce. “I have never taken a life. I have only tried to save them from the ones the fever takes.”

“And for what?” Caelum demanded, his face flushed with conviction. “For a few extra breaths? For a moment’s comfort? You meddle in the sacred order of sickness and death. You are a weed in the Lord’s vineyard, and you will be rooted out.” He looked at her garden with utter disdain. “Your works of flesh will be exposed for the devil’s workings they are. Repent. Burn your books of secrets. Kneel with the rest of us and pray for mercy.”

He turned and left, his black robes a slash against the grey stone. Elara remained frozen, the chamomile sprig in her hand now a fragile, useless thing. The watchers behind their curtains saw her stillness and took it for guilt, or for a stubborn, unrepentant soul. The word *witchcraft* was already out of the priest’s mouth and into the whispering wind.

That night, the doubt came. It was a cold, slithering thing, far worse than the priest’s anger. She sat in the dark, save for a single guttering candle, surrounded by her jars and bundles. The city’s sounds were a distant, feverish murmur. She had saved Kael, the tanner’s son, from a septic wound with a poultice of comfrey and garlic. She had eased old Maera’s final hours with a draught of valerian and passionflower, so she did not die fighting the terror in her own mind. Was that witchcraft? Was it arrogance?

Her mother had taught her these things. A woman of the marshes, who knew which moss stopped bleeding and which berry soothed a belly. “The world is full of medicine, child,” she’d say, holding up a common weed. “You have only to learn its language.” That language was not in the holy texts Father Caelum thumped. It was in the whispered lore of midwives, in the careful observation of what made a fever break and what made it burn brighter.

But what if she was wrong? What if the fever broke on its own in Kael? What if Maera’s peace was just the mercy of exhaustion? The priests said all healing came from God. If she healed, and it was not from God… whose power was she borrowing? The doubt whispered that her confidence was a mask for ignorance. Every potion she brewed, every poultice she applied, was a gamble. And now, the stakes were her life. Her work was no longer a quiet service; it was an accusation. Every patient who improved was a testament to her “witchcraft.” Every one who died, as most would, was proof of her failure and her guilt.

She thought of the smoke. The fumigation. A desperate, risky measure. She’d used it on a family in the rookeries, the father and two children drowning in their own phlegm. She’d sealed them in their single room, burned her blend on a brazier, and forced them to inhale the cleansing, stinging fumes. The children had worsened for a day, terrible to hear. Then, the mucus had shifted. They had coughed, and coughed, and brought up the thick, coloured ropes. They lived. Was that God’s will, working through her knowledge? Or was it her, playing with fire—literal and metaphorical—and getting lucky?

She stood and went to a high shelf, pulling down a heavy, leather-bound book. It was not a book of spells, but a compilation of notes, sketches of plants, recipes passed down from her mother and the few other “cunning women” she’d known. It was her truth. She opened it to a pressed flower, its colour faded to a ghost. *Artemisia absinthium. Bitter, but drives out the worm. Use with caution in the weak.*

This was her arsenal. Not dark power, but a fragile, hard-won science. A language the world was forgetting, or choosing to forget. Father Caelum’s world had no room for the nuance of a herb’s strength, only the stark binary of God’s will and the Devil’s work. In his eyes, her doubt was a sin; her certainty, a greater one.

She closed the book, the decision hardening in her chest like cooling wax. She would not burn her books. She would not kneel and confess to a power she did not wield. But she would be more careful. The fumigation would stop. The treatments would be quieter, less dramatic. She would become a ghost in the city’s machine of fear, slipping in and out, a whisper of relief rather than a beacon of defiance.

But as she blew out the candle, plunging the room into near-darkness, a final, sharper doubt pierced her resolve. What if the caution was the cowardice? What if the courage was to stand in the market square, herbs in hand, and tell them all that this—this tangible, smelling, *working* knowledge—was the only God she knew? The thought was treasonous, terrifying. It would be a death sentence, swift and sure.

She lay on her narrow pallet, listening to the city cough itself to sleep. She was a healer. She believed that. But in the silent, judging dark, the word *witchcraft* echoed, and for the first time, she could not say where the healing ended and the sin began. The line was as thin and invisible as the miasma itself, and she was lost in the fog.
