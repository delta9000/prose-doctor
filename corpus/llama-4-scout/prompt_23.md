Sergeant Jameson crouched beside the makeshift stretcher, her eyes scanning the wounded soldier before her. The ceasefire had held for a few hours, long enough for her team to set up a basic aid station in the ruined building. The walls were cracked, the floor littered with debris, but it was better than nothing.

The soldier, a young man with a scar above his left eyebrow, gazed up at her with a mixture of fear and desperation. Jameson quickly assessed his condition – multiple shrapnel wounds, likely from a recent skirmish. She noted the severity of the injuries, the amount of blood soaking through his uniform, and the way he struggled to breathe.

"Triage category?" she called out to her team.

"Alpha," her assistant, Corporal Patel, replied, "but we need to move him to the Charlie category. His vitals are slipping."

Jameson nodded, making a quick decision. "Get him to the Charlie tent. We'll need to prioritize his treatment."

As her team worked to move the soldier, Jameson's gaze fell on the next patient in line – an enemy soldier, dressed in worn fatigues, his arm twisted at an unnatural angle. He looked up at her with a mix of anger and resignation.

"Hey," Jameson said softly, "I'm here to help you."

The soldier spat at her feet. "You won't help me. You'll kill me."

Jameson sighed, recognizing the fear and hatred that drove his words. She'd seen it before, in the eyes of countless soldiers on both sides. "My job is to treat your wounds, not to take sides," she said firmly. "Can you tell me your name?"

The soldier hesitated, then muttered, "Ali."

Jameson nodded. "Okay, Ali. I'm Sergeant Jameson. Can you tell me what happened?"

Ali's expression twisted in a grimace. "We were ambushed. I fell, and...and my arm."

Jameson examined the arm, wincing at the severity of the break. "We'll need to set it, but first, I need to check for other injuries."

As she worked, Ali asked, "Why are you helping me? You're not supposed to."

Jameson hesitated, unsure how to respond. She'd been trained to treat all wounded soldiers, regardless of allegiance, but it wasn't always that simple. In the chaos of war, lines blurred, and emotions ran high.

"I'm helping you because you're hurt," she said finally. "And it's my duty to do so."

Ali snorted. "Duty? You're a liar. You don't have to help me. No one does."

Jameson met his gaze, searching for a glimmer of understanding. "Maybe I don't have to, but I do. Because if I don't, what's the point of being a medic?"

Ali's expression softened, and for a moment, Jameson saw a glimmer of humanity beyond the uniform.

As she finished treating Ali, Jameson's thoughts turned to the calculus of triage. Who would she save? Who would she have to let go? The ceasefire was fragile, and she knew it wouldn't last. More wounded would come, and she'd have to make impossible decisions.

Her team called out, "Sergeant, we have a Bravo patient coming in!"

Jameson rushed to attend to the new arrival, a young woman with severe burns and possible internal injuries. As she worked to stabilize the soldier, Jameson's mind whirled with the numbers, weighing the likelihood of survival against the resources available.

She thought back to Ali, the enemy soldier she'd treated. He'd been a low-risk patient, but his injuries had been severe enough to keep him in the Charlie category for now. The young woman, code-named "Bravo," was a higher priority, but Jameson wasn't sure if she could save her.

The decisions swirled in her mind like a vortex, pulling her under with every passing moment. She worked mechanically, her training taking over, but her thoughts lingered on the impossible choices ahead.

In the chaos of war, Jameson had learned to compartmentalize, to shut off her emotions and focus on the task at hand. But sometimes, like now, the weight of those choices bore down on her, threatening to crush her.

As she finished treating the young soldier, Jameson took a deep breath, steeling herself for what was to come. She glanced at Ali, who watched her with a mixture of curiosity and wariness.

For a moment, their eyes locked, and Jameson saw a glimmer of recognition – that they were both human beings, caught in the midst of something much larger than themselves.

The ceasefire would eventually break, and the wounded would keep coming. Jameson steeled herself for the decisions ahead, knowing that in the midst of war, the only constant was the need to choose who would live, and who would die.
