Istanbul, 1928


Erol dipped the calligraphy reed into the inkwell, his mind churning with thoughts – some as dark as the pot into which he submerged the quill. He exhaled slowly, then, in one fluid motion, drew the reed upward. A shimmering black droplet clung to its tip for an instant before succumbing to gravity and falling back into the waiting pool of ink. Erol wiped the nib on a rag beside him, his gaze never wavering from the blank sheet of paper that lay before him like a challenge. He paused only to breathe and focus, gathering his thoughts as he did before commencing his art. Then, with the same careful deliberation he always employed, Erol began to write, his hand moving in sweeping strokes that spoke of years of practice and dedication. His fingers trembled from the effort, from the care he put into each stroke and curve of the Arabic letters he penned, but his hand was steady, trained by decades of habit. This would be, he knew, one of his last works of art.


The calligrapher’s studio was dimly lit by the fading sunlight that filtered through the grimy panes of the lone window overlooking Istanbul’s cobbled streets. Shadows stretched across the wooden floorboards, creeping ever forward as the late afternoon light waned. Erol worked by the window, his desk an oasis of calm in the midst of a city being transformed by a relentless modernization drive. Outside, the sounds of construction and the shouts of vendors mingled with the cacophony of automobile horns and the clip-clopping of horses’ hooves. Inside, it was quiet save for the scratch of Erol’s reed pen on paper. Here, in this small space, time seemed to stand still. His studio, filled with the scent of ink and teak wood, was an island of tranquility in a sea of chaos. But even islands, Erol knew, could be eroded by the tides of change.


As he worked, his mind wandered. It had been over a year since the government had decreed that the Arabic script, which had been used to write Turkish for centuries, was to be replaced by the Latin alphabet. The reform was part of a broader series of changes aimed at secularizing and Westernizing the country in the wake of the Ottoman Empire’s collapse. At first, Erol had dismissed it as a fad, a fleeting political maneuver. He had continued to practice his craft, confident in the knowledge that tradition and beauty would ultimately triumph over fleeting political trends. But as months passed, he began to realize that this was not merely a fad. It was a revolution, and it threatened to make his life’s work obsolete.


Erol’s hand moved across the page and a verse from the Quran began to take shape, the elegant Arabic script flowing from his pen as if by magic. Each letter connected seamlessly to the next in a dance that was both beautiful and precise. The calligrapher paused to admire his work. The ink glistened in the fading light, a testament to centuries of Islamic artistry and scholarship. Erol felt a surge of pride. This was his legacy, his gift to the world. He had spent years perfecting his craft, Porcelain skin marred only by a few wisps of hair that strayed into his vision and the slight tremble of his hand as he wrote. Age, he knew, was catching up to him, just as surely as the Latin alphabet was supplanting the Arabic script.


But Erol’s heart was heavy. His art, the art of Islamic calligraphy, was dying. He had seen it coming for years, but he had always believed that beauty and tradition would endure. Now, he wasn’t so sure. The government’s reforms, ushered in by the charismatic and iron-willed Mustafa Kemal Atatürk, were sweeping away centuries of tradition in a bid to modernize and secularize the country. Erol understood the reasons behind the reforms – the desire to break free from the legacy of the Ottoman Empire and forge a new, modern Turkish identity. But he could not help but feel that something precious was being lost in the process.


A knock on the door startled Erol from his reverie. He looked up, his eyes narrowing as he recognized the familiar figure of his friend and former student, Ahmet. The younger man stood in the doorway, his face etched with concern. Erol waved him in, setting down his pen and rising to greet his guest.


“Erol, my old friend,” Ahmet said, clasping the calligrapher’s hands in his own. “I came as soon as I heard. I know you’ve been struggling with the new reforms, and I wanted to see how you were faring.”


Erol nodded, gesturing for Ahmet to take a seat. “I’m managing,” he said, his voice heavy with emotion. “But it’s not easy. Every day, I feel as though I’m losing a part of myself.”


Ahmet leaned forward, his eyes earnest. “I understand, Erol. But you must try to see the bigger picture. These reforms, they’re necessary for our country’s progress. We can’t cling to the past forever.”


Erol bristled at his friend’s words. “Progress?” he asked, his voice rising. “Is it progress to throw away centuries of tradition and beauty? To discard an art form that has been a part of our culture for generations?”


Ahmet sighed, running a hand through his hair. “It’s not about discarding our traditions, Erol. It’s about adapting to a changing world. The Latin alphabet will make it easier for our people to learn to read and write. It will open up new opportunities for them, connect them with the wider world.”


Erol shook his head, his eyes blazing with passion. “And what about the beauty of the Arabic script? The artistry and skill that goes into each stroke? Are we to throw all of that away for the sake of convenience?”


Ahmet met his gaze, his voice soft but firm. “We have to move forward, Erol. We can’t let ourselves be trapped by the past.”


The two men stared at each other for a long moment, the air thick with tension. Finally, Erol looked away, his shoulders slumping in defeat. “I know you believe in what you’re saying, Ahmet,” he said, his voice weary. “But I can’t help but feel that we’re losing something precious – something that makes us who we are.”


Ahmet reached out, placing a hand on Erol’s shoulder. “I know it’s hard, my friend. But change is inevitable. We must adapt and find new ways to honor our traditions, even as the world around us transforms.”


Erol nodded, his eyes glistening with unshed tears. “I suppose you’re right,” he said, his voice barely above a whisper. “But it doesn’t make it any easier to bear.”


The two men sat in silence for a moment, the weight of their conversation hanging heavy in the air. Finally, Ahmet rose to his feet. “I should go,” he said, his voice gentle. “But I wanted you to know that I’m here for you, Erol. Whatever happens, know that your art, your legacy, will always be a part of who we are as a people.”


Erol stood as well, embracing his friend tightly. “Thank you, Ahmet,” he said, his voice thick with emotion. “That means more to me than you can know.”


Ahmet smiled, clasping Erol’s hand one last time before turning to leave. “Take care of yourself, my old friend,” he said, his voice warm with affection. “And keep creating your beautiful art, even in the face of change.”


As the door closed behind Ahmet, Erol sank back into his chair, his heart heavy with grief and uncertainty. He looked down at the half-finished calligraphy piece before him, the elegant Arabic script a stark reminder of all that he stood to lose. Tears welled in his eyes, and for a moment, he allowed himself to surrender to his sorrow, his body shaking with silent sobs.


But even as he wept, Erol knew that he could not give up. His art, his very identity, was rooted in the Arabic script, in the centuries of tradition and skill that had been passed down to him through generations of calligraphers. He could not simply abandon it, no matter how much the world around him changed.


With a shuddering breath, Erol picked up his pen once more, his fingers trembling slightly as he dipped the reed into the inkwell. He paused for a moment, gathering his thoughts and his strength, before beginning to write again. The Arabic letters flowed from his pen like water, each stroke a testament to his skill and his dedication to his craft. As he wrote, Erol felt a sense of calm wash over him, a feeling of connection to the long line of calligraphers who had come before him, and to the rich cultural heritage that he had dedicated his life to preserving.


But even as he lost himself in the rhythm of his work, Erol could not entirely shake the sense of grief that lingered in his heart. He thought of his late wife, Fatma, who had always been his greatest supporter and his fiercest champion. She had believed in his art, in the power of beauty to transcend the limitations of language and culture. And now, with her gone, Erol felt more alone than ever, adrift in a world that seemed to be changing too fast for him to keep up.


As the sun set over Istanbul, casting long shadows across the city’s winding streets and crowded bazaars, Erol continued to work, his pen moving across the page with practiced precision. The calligrapher’s studio was quiet now, the only sound the scratch of his reed on paper and the distant echo of the city’s bustling streets. Erol felt a sense of peace wash over him, a feeling of connection to something greater than himself, to the long line of artists and scholars who had come before him, and to the enduring power of art to soothe the soul and elevate the spirit.


But even as he worked, Erol knew that his art, like the Arabic script itself, was fading into history. The world was changing, and there was nothing he could do to stop it. All he could do was cling to the beauty and the tradition that had shaped his life, and hope that somehow, in the face of all the changes and challenges that lay ahead, the essence of what he had dedicated his life to would endure.
