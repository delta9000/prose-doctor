The ceasefire had lasted three days when the first casualties began trickling in. They came in ones and twos, stumbling out of the jungle with crude bandages wrapped around wounds, supporting each other, some crawling on their hands and knees. There were a dozen of them now gathered outside the medical tent – nine enemy soldiers, three of their own. PFC Billy Johnson, the medic, did his best to ignore the politics. He didn't care who had started this war or who had the stronger claim to the strip of jungle they were fighting over. He was here to save lives. If the ceasefire meant a trickle of wounded instead of a flood, he was grateful for that much.

He turned his attention to the oldest of the group, a grizzled veteran with a thatch of gray hair who leaned heavily on a makeshift crutch. His foot was missing below the ankle, the bone whittling down to a ragged stump. From the amount of dirt packed into the wound, Johnson guessed he had been moving through the jungle for at least a day. The bleeding had mostly stopped, but it would start again soon; the crude tourniquet tied around his leg was too tight, cutting off circulation. The man needed a doctor to remove what was left of his foot before he could be sent to the rear for treatment.

Next to the old soldier, a young kid missing an eye and most of his ear on one side of his face waited patiently. He might have been handsome before the war, but his face would be permanently scarred by the time Johnson was finished with him. A jagged piece of shrapnel was embedded in his cheek, just below the empty socket. Johnson would leave that for the surgeons; his job was to stop the bleeding and prevent infection. The kid was young, and he would live, but he would never be the same.

Johnson moved among the wounded, stopping to talk to each man, offering a word of comfort or a sip of water where he could. They were a sorry lot, and he wished he could do more for them. But there were too many wounded and not enough supplies. He did what he could with what he had, fashioning bandages from strips of cloth torn from his shirt and using his dwindling supply of sulfa powder to stave off infection.

As he worked, he tried to ignore the sound of the man dying behind him. The soldier had been hit in the chest by shrapnel, and his lungs were filling with blood. He would be dead before the day was out, and there was nothing Johnson could do for him. He had seen too much death in his time as a medic to dwell on the ones he couldn't save. He had to focus on the ones he could.

He moved on to the next patient, a young soldier with a shattered arm. The bone was broken in several places, and the arm hung limp at his side. Johnson examined the wound, feeling for pieces of bone beneath the skin. The soldier winced in pain, but he didn't cry out. Johnson admired his bravery. He had seen grown men weep like children when they were in pain, but this kid was tough. He would need surgery to set the bone, and even then, he would never have full use of his arm again.

As he worked, Johnson thought about the conversation he had had with an enemy soldier the day before. The soldier had been young, barely eighteen, and he had been crying when Johnson found him. He had lost his best friend in the battle, and he didn't want to live anymore. Johnson had comforted him as best he could, assuring him that his life was not over just because his friend was gone. He had talked to the soldier for hours, listening to him pour out his heart, and in the end, the soldier had decided to live. Johnson had sent him to the rear with the promise that he would be safe there. He wondered if the soldier would keep his promise, or if he would try to find a way to die in the jungle.

As the day wore on, the number of wounded grew. Johnson was soon surrounded by a crowd of men, all clamoring for his attention. He did his best to help them, but he knew he would never be able to treat them all. He had to prioritize, and that meant making tough choices about who would live and who would die.

He started with the young ones first. They had the best chance of surviving their wounds, and they had the most to lose if they died. He treated a young soldier with a bullet wound in his leg, and another with a piece of shrapnel embedded in his shoulder. He bandaged their wounds and gave them painkillers, hoping to make them as comfortable as possible.

Next, he turned his attention to the older soldiers. They were tougher, and they could withstand more pain, but they were also more likely to die of infection. He treated a soldier with a severe burn on his arm, and another with a deep gash in his side. He knew they might not survive, but he had to try.

Throughout the day, Johnson thought about the medic he had met during the ceasefire. The medic had been treating wounded from both sides, just like Johnson was doing. They had talked for hours, sharing stories and comparing notes on the best ways to treat different wounds. The medic had been impressed by Johnson's skills, and Johnson had been impressed by the medic's dedication to his patients. They had traded supplies, and Johnson had given the medic a bottle of painkillers in exchange for a few bandages.

As the sun began to set, Johnson knew he was running out of time. He had treated as many of the wounded as he could, but there were still dozens more waiting for his help. He made one last round of the camp, checking on the men he had already treated and offering words of comfort to those he couldn't help. He knew he had done his best, but he also knew that it wasn't enough. There would always be more wounded, more dying, more death. It was the nature of war, and there was nothing he could do to change that.

As he walked back to his tent, he thought about the conversation he had had with the enemy soldier. The soldier had been crying, and Johnson had comforted him as best he could. He had told the soldier that his life was not over just because his friend was gone, and the soldier had seemed to believe him. Johnson hoped that the soldier would find a way to live with his loss, and he hoped that he would be able to do the same.

He reached his tent and sat down on his cot, exhausted from the long day. He knew he would have to be up early the next morning to treat more wounded, but for now, he allowed himself a moment of rest. He closed his eyes and tried to forget the horrors he had seen that day, but he knew they would be with him forever. He was a medic, and his job was to save lives, but he knew that he would never be able to save them all. It was a heavy burden to bear, but he knew he had to keep going. He had to keep fighting, even when the fighting seemed hopeless. He had to keep saving lives, even when it seemed like there were too many to save. It was his duty, and he would not rest until the war was over and the wounded were healed.
