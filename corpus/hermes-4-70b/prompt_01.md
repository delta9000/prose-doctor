The cab dropped her off in front of the house, a place she hadn't set foot in for nearly fifteen years. She hesitated before climbing out, her fingers gripping the door handle until her knuckles turned white. Her mother’s funeral had been a blur of hushed voices and sympathetic pats on the back, a surreal experience that still felt more like a bad dream than reality.

"I'll send someone to pick up your bags later," the driver said, pulling her back to the present.

"Thanks," she replied, stepping out onto the sidewalk. 

The house loomed before her, imposing and unfamiliar. It was a large Victorian with a wraparound porch, the paint peeling and the gutters overflowing with leaves. She remembered her mother complaining about the upkeep, how she’d wanted to move somewhere smaller and easier to manage. But her father had refused, insisting on staying in the only home their daughter had ever known.

Taking a deep breath, she walked up the front steps, the wood creaking beneath her feet. She reached for the doorknob, hesitating for just a moment before pushing it open.

Inside, the house was exactly as she remembered. The same floral wallpaper lined the walls, the same faded armchairs sat in the living room, the same chandelier hung from the ceiling. It was like stepping back in time, into a place that had been preserved in amber.

She made her way through the foyer, her footsteps echoing on the hardwood floors. She ran her fingers along the banister as she climbed the stairs, memories flooding back with each step. She could still hear her father's voice, booming through the halls as he called her down for dinner.

At the top of the stairs, she paused outside her parents' bedroom door. She hadn't been inside since she was a child, when she would crawl into bed with them on lazy Sunday mornings. Slowly, she turned the knob and pushed the door open.

The room was untouched, exactly as it had been when her parents were alive. The bed was meticulously made, the curtains drawn. On the dresser, a framed photo of her parents on their wedding day smiled back at her.

She walked over to the closet, pulling open the doors to reveal her mother's wardrobe. Dresses and blouses hung neatly on hangers, the scent of her perfume still lingering in the air. She ran her fingers over the fabrics, memories washing over her in waves.

As she turned to leave, something caught her eye. Tucked behind a stack of old shoeboxes was a small wooden box, its surface worn and faded. Curiosity piqued, she pulled it out and lifted the lid.

Inside, nestled among yellowed tissue paper, were letters. Dozens of them, tied together with a faded ribbon. She picked one up, turning it over in her hands. The postmark was dated nearly thirty years ago, the year she was born.

Her heart raced as she untied the ribbon and unfolded the first letter. It was written in her mother's handwriting, the ink smudged in places as if it had been written in a hurry.

"My dearest Peter," it began. "I hope this letter finds you well. I miss you more with each passing day. The house feels so empty without you here."

She furrowed her brow, confused. Her father's name was John, not Peter. Who was her mother writing to?

She kept reading, her hands shaking as the words blurred before her eyes. The letter went on to describe her mother's loneliness, her fear that her husband no longer loved her. It spoke of arguments and simmering tensions, of a marriage on the brink of collapse.

But her parents had always seemed so happy, their love for each other a constant presence in their lives. She couldn't reconcile the woman in the letters with the mother she knew, the one who had always been so strong and steadfast.

She sank down onto the bed, the letters scattered around her like fallen leaves. She read them one by one, each word a hammer blow to her heart. They told a story of a love affair, a decades-long relationship between her mother and a man named Peter. 

Tears streamed down her face as she realized the truth. Her mother had been living a double life, sneaking away to be with her lover while her father worked late at the office. She had born a child out of wedlock, a child her husband had raised as his own.

The weight of this knowledge settled over her like a heavy cloak. She thought back to her childhood, to the whispers and glances exchanged between her parents. She had always assumed they were talking about her, worrying over her wellbeing. But now she wondered if they had been discussing something else entirely, something she could never have imagined.

She stood up, the letters clutched to her chest, and walked over to the window. Outside, the sun was setting, casting long shadows across the lawn. She thought of her father, of the sacrifices he must have made to keep their family together. He had loved her enough to raise her as his own, despite knowing the truth about her parentage.

A wave of gratitude washed over her, followed by a deep, aching sorrow. Her parents had both kept this secret, carrying it with them to their graves. They had loved her in their own way, even if that love had been built on a foundation of lies.

She looked around the room, at the relics of her mother's life. The dresses in the closet, the perfume on the dresser, the chandelier that had sparkled overhead as she fell asleep at night. These objects were imbued with meaning, with the weight of a history she had never known.

She realized then that she would never truly know her mother, not the way she thought she had. The woman in the letters was a stranger to her, a ghost that haunted the corners of her childhood home.

But even as this knowledge settled over her, she felt a strange sense of peace. She understood now that her parents had been human, flawed and fallible, just like everyone else. Their love for her had been real, even if it had been born out of pain and betrayal.

She tucked the letters back into their box, closing the lid with a soft click. She would keep this secret, just as her parents had. She owed them that much, at least.

As she stepped out of the room and made her way back downstairs, she felt a new resolve settling over her. She would honor her parents' memory, cherishing the love they had given her while acknowledging the truth they had hidden. She would carry this knowledge with her, a secret tucked away in the corners of her heart.
