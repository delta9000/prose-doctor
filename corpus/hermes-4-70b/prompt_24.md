I hung up the phone with another dead end, the cheap plastic handset clattering against the cradle. My desk was littered with scribbled notes, grainy photographs, and half-empty coffee cups. This case was turning into a real headache.

Jessica Barnes had been missing for three days. Her father, a wealthy banker, had hired me to track her down. He’d been vague about the details, but the worry in his eyes was real. I’d hit the streets, talked to her friends, her coworkers, even the bums outside her apartment building. But nobody had seen hide nor hair of her since that Friday evening.

I sighed, rubbing my temples. I needed a break from this. Maybe a stiff drink at Finn’s bar. As if on cue, the office door swung open.

“Any luck?” It was Marv, my occasional partner in crime-solving.

I shook my head. “Zilch. The girl’s vanished off the face of the earth.”

Marv flopped down in the client chair, propping his shoed feet on my desk. “Maybe she just ran off. Girl like that, probably had her reasons.”

“Probably,” I agreed. “But her old man’s paying top dollar for peace of mind. Gotta at least make a show of looking.”

Marv shrugged. “Your time, your money.” He leaned forward conspiratorially. “Say, I heard something might be up at the wharf…”

I perked up. “Yeah? What’s the word?”

“Just rumors, you know. But they say there’s a new boat in town. Big, shiny, loaded up with hot merchandise. Supposed to be setting sail tomorrow night.”

I pondered this. Jessica’s boyfriend was a known smuggler. If they’d run off together… “Thanks, Marv. I’ll check it out.”

Marv left and I grabbed my hat. Time to see if these rumors had any weight to them. I headed for the wharf, the briny air filling my lungs. The seagulls cried overhead, a melancholy sound.

I flashed my badge to the night watchman, asking after the supposed new boat. He played dumb, but his shifty eyes said otherwise. I slipped him a bill, and he pointed me towards a rusty warehouse on the edge of the docks.

I approached quietly, my footsteps muffled by the damp ground. The door was unlocked. I slipped inside, my hand resting on my trusty revolver.

The warehouse was stacked with crates - imports and exports, no doubt of the illicit variety. I heard a murmur of voices beyond a curtained doorway. I crept closer, straining to listen.

“…gonna have to leave her behind, there’s no other way.” A man’s voice, gruff and impatient.

“But Mick, I can’t just…” A woman’s voice, young and frightened. My blood ran cold. Jessica.

I burst through the curtain, gun drawn. “Freeze! Police!”

The scene before me was like a bad pulp novel. Mick, the smuggler kingpin, had Jessica cornered. Crates of stolen goods surrounded them.

Mick sneered. “Well, well. If it isn’t the world’s worst detective.”

I kept my gun leveled. “I’m taking Jessica home, Mick. Play nice and this doesn’t have to get messy.”

Jessica looked at me pleadingly. “Please, I don’t want to go back. I want to stay with Mick.”

I faltered. “But your father, he’s worried sick…”

“Let me guess, he paid you handsomely to drag me back,” Jessica spat. “He doesn’t care about me. He just cares about his precious reputation.”

Mick chuckled. “Told you, doll. Ain’t nobody coming to save you.”

I hesitated, my gun trembling. My code was to protect the innocent, bring the guilty to justice. But was Jessica innocent? Or was she a willing participant?

Mick seized the opportunity, lunging for his own piece. I dove to the side, bullets whizzing past my ear. I returned fire and Mick crumpled.

I turned to Jessica, gun still smoking. “It’s over. Let’s go.”

She stared at me hatefully. “You killed him! You murdered him!”

“I had no choice,” I said, holstering my weapon. “He was gonna kill me.”

“You bastard,” Jessica sobbed. “I’ll never forgive you. I’ll tell everyone what you did. I’ll…”

I stopped listening. I knew the drill. Cuffs on Jessica, call it in, face the music. Another case closed, another line blurred. The life of a private eye ain’t all it’s cracked up to be.
