Sofia smoothed the wrinkles from her apron and adjusted her modest cap, ensuring that no stray strands of chestnut hair escaped its confines. It wouldn't do for a woman to show so much as an inch of hair in public, even in the hallowed halls of the University of Bologna. As the daughter of a prominent professor, she had been lucky to receive an education far surpassing that of most women. Sofia’s father had recognized her brilliance early on and shared his love of astronomy with her.

They spent countless nights gazing at the stars through their small telescope, charting the movements of the heavens. Sofia’s mind was sharp and inquisitive, grasping concepts that eluded even some of Sofia’s father’s brightest students. In secret, father and daughter pored over the latest astronomical treatises and calculations. By her eighteenth year, Sofia was making her own observations and discoveries. Her father took pride in her work, and had even published some of her findings under his own name, so as not to arouse suspicion.

When he died suddenly in a fever, Sofia was devastated. She tried to carry on his work, but was barred from attending lectures and using the university’s telescopes and observatories. It wasn’t seemly for a woman to engage in scholarly pursuits, according to the university fathers. Undaunted, Sofia continued her observations from the rooftop of her family home, using the telescope her father had bequeathed to her in his will.

Her reputation grew, albeit discreetly, through a handful of papers her father had published before his death. These were noticed by a few open-minded scholars, including the distinguished astronomer, Professor Giovanni Della Torre. He visited Sofia one day, expressing his admiration for the papers and subtly hinting that he knew the true author.

Professor Della Torre, or “Maestro,” as he insisted Sofia call him, was impressed by her intellect and dedication. He proposed a collaboration of sorts – she would continue her observations and calculations, and send them to him for review. If he deemed them worthy, he would publish them under his own name, ensuring that her work would be taken seriously by the scientific community. Sofia was hesitant, but realized it was the only way her voice could be heard. She agreed reluctantly.

Years passed, and their collaboration proved fruitful. Sofia’s observations of Jupiter’s moons and the phases of Venus were published to great acclaim. Maestro grew famous, while Sofia remained in the shadows, her ego bruised but her desire for knowledge unquenched.

One clear night in late autumn, Sofia climbed to her rooftop observatory, as she did every night. She meticulously scanned the sky, charting the movement of planets and stars. The telescope, though small, was powerful enough to make her observations significant. She recorded everything in her trusty notebook, the pages filled with her neat script and precise diagrams.

As she observed Jupiter, something unusual caught her eye. One of the moons seemed to be missing. She checked her notes from previous nights – no, it had definitely been there before. Had it disappeared? That was impossible. Moons didn’t just vanish, unless …

Sofia’s breath caught in her throat. Unless there was another celestial body, invisible to her telescope, exerting its influence. A theoretical planet, perhaps, with enough gravitational pull to disrupt the orbit of Jupiter’s moon. She made a note of her hypothesis, her heart racing with excitement.

The next few weeks were a blur of observation and calculation. Night after night, she scanned the skies, searching for evidence to support her theory. She noticed slight anomalies in the orbits of Jupiter’s other moons, as if they were being tugged by an unseen force. Her calculations grew more complex and time-consuming, but she persevered.

Finally, after weeks of frustration and hard work, Sofia made the calculations that would change everything. There was indeed another planet, hidden in the glare of the sun, affecting the orbits of Jupiter’s moons. It was a monumental discovery, one that would redefine the known laws of astronomy.

With trembling hands, Sofia wrote a detailed letter to Maestro, outlining her observations and calculations. She waited impatiently for his response, pacing her small room and biting her nails to the quick.

When Maestro’s reply arrived, Sofia tore it open, her heart pounding. He was thrilled by her discovery, he wrote, and agreed that it was the most important astronomical finding in decades. He would announce it in his next lecture at the university. Sofia felt a surge of pride and vindication, quickly followed by a wave of bitterness. Her discovery, her hard work, would be presented to the world as Maestro’s own.

As the weeks passed, Maestro’s lectures on the new planet were met with astonishment and acclaim. Scholars from all over Europe wrote to him, requesting more information and details. Sofia watched from afar as her discovery took the scientific world by storm, her name nowhere to be found.

One evening, there was a knock at Sofia’s door. To her surprise, it was Maestro himself, looking uncharacteristically nervous. He handed her a small package, wrapped in brown paper. Inside was a beautifully bound book, its pages filled with the latest astronomical observations and calculations. Sofia’s breath caught in her throat as she read the title page: “New Observations of Jupiter’s Moons and the Discovery of a Hidden Planet, by Sofia Carmina.”

Maestro had published her work under her own name, defying the university fathers and risking his reputation. Tears sprang to Sofia’s eyes as she read her name on the page, a long-held dream finally realized. She hugged the book to her chest, overcome with emotion.

She knew that this was only the beginning. There would be more observations, more calculations, more discoveries to be made. And now, finally, the world would know the name of Sofia Carmina, astronomer.
