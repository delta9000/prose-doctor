The weight of the Tome of Ages lay heavy in Kira’s lap, heavier than its leather-bound, brass-clasped bulk might suggest. She shifted on the hard stool, her back aching from the hours hunched over the scribe’s lectern. The flickering candle at her shoulder cast restless shadows over the parchment, as if the tome itself was reluctant to reveal its secrets.

It had been the usual, tedious work all day, for her and the dozen other junior scribes in the Archives of the Esterian Citadel. Copying passages from the approved histories, verifying dates and genealogies against the Great Ledgers, researching footnotes for the new edition of Royal Protocol. Dry, dusty, precise. Just the way Head Archivist Thorne liked it. Any deviation, any questioning, was met with cold disapproval. "The past is written, Scribe Kira," he’d droned on her first day, "Our task is merely to preserve its wisdom."

Kira sighed and turned another heavy vellum page. And froze.

It was a minor entry, buried near the end of the section on King Marrot’s reign, two centuries past. A footnote, really. Earlier in the text, it had been noted that on the eve of the Battle of Blackened Hills, Marrot had received a delegation from the Northern Clans in his war-tency. Kira had copied the account of that meeting yesterday from a different ledger – a delegation of five clan elders, presenting a carved staff as a token of truce.

But this entry … this entry said seven elders had come.

Kira frowned. She had a good memory. Five elders, not seven. And there had been a line about the staff being black oak, elaborately carved with scenes of hunting and battle. This footnote just said "a carved staff."

Her heart began to beat faster, a flush creeping up her neck. She looked around. The other scribes were all bent silently over their own work by the light of their candles, the air thick with the smell of hot wax and ink. Thorne’s desk was empty; he’d gone to dine. Quickly, before she could reconsider, Kira slid off her stool.

She knew where the original ledger was kept – Ledger Esteria-734, in the Restricted Collection. No scribe below the Seventh Circle was allowed to handle the originals, only Head Archivist and the senior scribes. But Kira had seen Senior Scribe Borin place it back on the shelf herself yesterday afternoon, after an oil lamp had flooded the ledge with soot and the ledger had been taken out to be cleaned.

Her soft leather slippers made no sound on the smooth stone floor as she hurried between the long rows of bookshelves into the Restricted section. When she found the ledger, she didn’t take it back to her lectern, but propped it open on a nearby shelf, shielding the pages with her body as she turned to yesterday’s entry.

And there it was. Five elders. Black oak staff, elaborately carved with hunting and battle scenes. Word for word what she had copied yesterday.

But that same account, in the Tome of Ages she’d been working from today, said seven elders. And only mentioned "a carved staff".

A change. A deliberate change.

Kira’s hands started to shake as she closed the ledger, her mind racing. Could she have misread it yesterday? She didn’t think so. She was a good scribe – meticulous, precise, as Thorne himself had admitted when he admitted her to the post. Her copies were never questioned. And it was such an inconsequential detail. Why would anyone bother to change it?

Unless, of course, they didn’t want anyone to notice the change.

She swallowed hard. Maybe she was just tired. Maybe it was a mistake. But as she walked back unsteadily to her lectern and sank onto the stool, she knew in her bones it wasn’t. Someone had altered the official records.

The implications made her light-headed. Who? When? And why?

Her eyes fell on the heavy bronze Cinch on her belt, stamped with the seal of the Scribes’ Guild. Every scribe wore one, from the lowliest junior up to the Grand Archivist himself. Kira had been so proud when she’d earned hers. Now, it felt like a brand.

A change in the historical record. That changed everything.

She spent the rest of the night in a haze of unease, her copies slow and clumsy, her mind churning. Every time she thought she heard Thorne’s footsteps approaching, her pulse pounded in her throat. He didn’t return, however, and at the evening bell, she filed out with the other scribes, her ledger clutched to her chest, and walked home through the quiet Citadel streets in a daze.

The next morning, instead of going to the dining hall for breakfast, she detoured to the Scribes’ library. A quick search turned up a half-dozen histories that referenced the Battle of Blackened Hills. And they all said the same thing – eight clan elders came to see King Marrot that night, bearing a ceremonial sword with a blade of meteoric iron.

Eight. Not five, not seven. Eight. And a sword, not a staff.

Kira sat back on her heels, cold dread washing over her. The changes went deeper than she’d imagined. But why?

She checked another source – a compendium of Northern Clan customs, written a century before the battle. It said a black oak staff carved with hunting and battle scenes was a clan chief’s token of sovereignty, not to be taken lightly. A sword would be a gift between warriors.

And the footnotes in the original ledger Kira had seen yesterday had listed the names of the five elders – all later found to be unaligned with any chieftain. The Tome of Ages gave no names. The other histories she’d checked this morning said the delegation had included three chieftains.

Kira pressed the heels of her hands against her eyes. Someone was trying to rewrite history. And they were doing it carefully, subtly, changing just enough to support another version of the story.

But whose version? And to what end?

That afternoon, she asked to be assigned to the Royal Histories again. She had to know how deep the changes went. Shaking, she started turning pages, scanning for anomalies. It was slow going, but she found more – a battle that had been fought in autumn was now said to have occurred in summer. A king who had been noted to have three sons in one entry was said to have four in another. A queen whose marriage alliance was said to have secured peace for two generations was now described as being childless.

The alterations were small. Plausible. But they were there.

And then she found a drastic one. The account of King Marrot’s death. A brief, modest tale in the original ledger of the king dying peacefully in his bed of a fever at age sixty. But the Tome of Ages said he had died in battle, at forty-five, slain by an assassin.

And that changed the succession entirely. In the original, Marrot was succeeded by his appointed heir, a nephew, since his sons had all died young. In the new version, his brother had taken the throne in the midst of war, leading to a new dynasty.

Kira’s head throbbed. She recognized that name – Marrot’s brother. He was the great-grandfather of the current king.

The changes were all funneling toward legitimizing the current royal line.

But why go to such trouble? Surely the current king’s claim was well-established?

A cold suspicion was beginning to form in Kira’s mind. What if there was a reason his claim might be challenged? A reason he felt he needed to shore up his family’s history?

She was getting in far too deep. She shouldn’t be poking around like this. It was dangerous. She should just do her job and copy what was put in front of her, like the other scribes. She’d report what she’d found and let Thorne handle it.

But the thought of telling Thorne made her feel ill. He was head archivist. He must know about the changes. He must be part of it.

She closed her eyes. She didn’t have anywhere near the full picture, certainly not enough to accuse the Head Archivist of the Citadel. She was jumping to conclusions.

She looked up, startled, to see Scribe Borin watching her, his lined face kind but curious.

"Are you quite all right, Scribe Kira? You’ve been staring at that same page for ten minutes."

Kira flushed. "Yes, Senior Scribe. I’m sorry. I was just … considering a phrase. Trying to parse the meaning." She gestured to the open tome.

Borin smiled. "Ah. A scholar’s work is never done. Well, when you’re ready to write, just let me know if you have questions."

As he moved off, Kira stared down at the page, her mind reeling. She had to get to the bottom of this. If the historical records of the Citadel were being systematically falsified, if the Archivists were complicit, it struck at the very heart of the Esterian realm. Nothing would be as it seemed. Every law, every treaty, every royal decree could be called into question. It was unthinkable.

She spent every spare moment for the next week poring over every text she could get her hands on – histories, ledgers, genealogies, treatises. And the more she looked, the more changes she found. It was a subtle, pervasive campaign, stretching back nearly four decades, always altering minor details to gradually reinforce a particular narrative.

A narrative that cast the current royal family in a heroic, unbroken line of succession, and everyone else as schemers and pretenders.

It was masterfully done. If Kira hadn’t had cause to compare two slightly different accounts of the same event, she might never have noticed. Most scribes probably hadn’t. Most scribes were probably just copying the texts put in front of them, never questioning, never comparing.

Kira’s Official History was due to be sent to the printers at the end of the month. For the first time, she wondered who decided what went into the Official History. Who had the authority to make changes? She’d always assumed it was the Head Archivist, but now she suspected it went higher – perhaps to the Royal House itself.

She had to know. She had to see the master copies from which the Archivists worked. If the changes originated from the Archivists, she would find the altered masters in the Restricted Collection. If not, if the Archivists were merely copying from texts provided to them from elsewhere, the masters would still be pure.

Her chance came when she was assigned to help inventory the Restricted Collection. Heart pounding, she offered to start with the Aisle of Kings, where the master copies of the Royal Histories were kept.

It didn’t take her long to find what she was looking for. The master copy of the Official History was in its accustomed place on the highest shelf. It was a gorgeous thing, bound in purple-dyed leather, tooled with gold, much finer than the copy she’d been working from. With trembling hands, she slid it off the shelf and opened it to the section on King Marrot.

She scanned the page quickly. Five elders. Black oak staff. Died in bed at sixty.

Her fingers shaking, she turned to the colophon at the back of the book, hoping to find a date of printing or a list of sources. The page was there, but it had been torn out.

Her breath caught. The colophon was gone.

She looked up, startled, to see Thorne standing at the end of the aisle, watching her.

"An impressive work, is it not?" he said smoothly, taking a step toward her.

Kira’s blood ran cold. She snapped the book shut, clutching it to her chest.

"The master copy," she said stupidly. "I was just –"

"Yes," Thorne said, stopping an arm’s length away. His face was hard in the dim light of the aisle. "I use that one for my monthly reports to the Chancellor."

Kira swallowed. "I was checking for consistency with my own copy."

Thorne’s eyes narrowed. "Were you. And did you find any … inconsistencies?"

Kira’s heart hammered against her ribs. She thought of the colophon, torn out. She thought of the other scribes, oblivious in their work. She thought of the weight of the Tome of Ages, of the hundreds of texts that held the story of Esteria.

She thought of the Northern Clans, and the significance of a carved staff.

"No," she said, her voice surprisingly steady. "None at all."

A flicker of doubt Passed through Thorne’s eyes, then was gone. He held out his hand for the book. After a hesitation, Kira surrendered it. His fingers brushed hers as he took it, and she resisted the urge to pull away.

"Good," he said softly. "The past is written, Scribe Kira. It’s not our place to question it. Only to preserve its wisdom."

Kira nodded, her mouth dry. "Yes, Head Archivist."

He ran a finger along the spine of the master copy, then turned away, the precious book cradled in his arms. Over his shoulder, he said, "I think it’s time you were assigned to a new project. Something less … sensitive."

Then he was gone, leaving Kira alone in the shadowed aisle, the smell of old parchment and ink cloying in her throat. She had to get out of there. She had to think.

She stumbled back to her lectern, her legs shaking. What was she going to do? Go to the Chancellor? He was the king’s right hand. He was probably part of it. Go to the king himself? She wouldn’t even be admitted to the palace.

She thought of the other scribes. Borin. Maybe he suspected something. But she didn’t dare approach him, put him in danger. She was in this alone.
