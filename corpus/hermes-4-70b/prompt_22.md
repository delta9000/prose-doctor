Dr. Thorne pressed the dark helmet onto Mrs. Gable’s head, careful not to catch too much of her brittle white hair in the cold metal clamps. The helmet’s interior flickered to life, bathing the woman’s lined face in a pale blue glow. With practiced ease, he connected the coiled tubes snaking from the helmet to the humming archway behind her, feeding their termini into a socket near the machine’s base. Mrs. Gable shuddered as the tubes began to pulse with a faint light, matching the rhythm of her heartbeat. She tried to scream, but only a choked whimper escaped her lips.

“It’ll be alright, Mrs. Gable,” Dr. Thorne said, straining to sound reassuring. “This’ll only take a moment.”

He didn’t wait for a response, turning instead to the microphone that arced down from his own smaller headset. “Initiate synaptic mapping.” His voice echoed through the sterile chamber, and at the far end, a bank of monitors sprang to life. Jagged green lines leapt across their black surfaces, tracing a spiking topography of Mrs. Gable’s thoughts. Dr. Thorne watched the monitors intently, waiting for the memory to coalesce.

It didn’t take long. In moments, the monitors were a dizzying swirl of color and motion. Mrs. Gable, or rather the specter of her that now inhabited the machine, found herself amongst a crowd. A little girl, no older than six, was tugging at her hand, whining about the heat. The woman shushed her, not unkindly, as she gazed up at the jumbo screens that loomed over the throng. She was waiting for the New Year’s countdown.

But the screens didn’t show the familiar glowing orb. Instead, they displayed the stern visage of Chancellor Kane. His face was gaunt, aged before its time, but his eyes burned with a chilling intensity. He spoke, and his voice was magnified by the microphones that lined the podium before him, blaring over the silent crowd.

“Citizens,” Kane intoned, his voice a gravelly baritone, “tonight marks the end of an era. Tonight, we usher in a new age of prosperity, of order, of control. Tonight, we embrace the future.”

A roar of applause went up from parts of the crowd, though it was hardly universal. Here and there, dotted amongst the throng, people wept openly. Mrs. Gable’s memories offered no context for this display. Dr. Thorne, for his part, was too busy to notice. His fingers danced across the banks of keyboards before him, tracing the contours of the memory he was meant to excise. He had seen its like before, time and time again. It began, as it always did, with a sound.

Drones. Their whining engines cut through the air like knives, drowning out even the Chancellor’s speech. The crowd fled in panic, but there was nowhere to go. Drones ringed the perimeter, hovering with an insectoid patience, waiting for the order to descend. It came in the form of a single gunshot. Then they were among the crowd, laying about themselves with their mechanized arms, bludgeoning and slicing and tearing. When they retreated, no one was left standing. Mrs. Gable’s daughter was sprawled on the ground, her chest a ruined mess of blood and bone.

Dr. Thorne reeled back from his console, a wave of nausea washing over him. The monitors were a blur of red, the memory having shifted to focus on the corpse of the little girl. He’d seen death before, of course, but there was something uniquely terrible about this. The casual brutality of it, the senseless loss of life—it hit him harder than he’d expected.

He forced himself to focus. He was a professional, after all, and he had a job to do. His fingers danced across a new keyboard, this one connected to a device known colloquially as the Memory Guillotine. In theory, it was a simple machine. It located traumatic memories in a person’s subconscious, isolated them, and removed them, leaving the person with no recollection of the trauma. Most of the time, it worked quite well.

This time, it didn’t work at all.

The Memory Guillotine whirred to life, its screens flashing as it went to work on Mrs. Gable’s mind. Dr. Thorne’s fingers danced across his keyboard, guiding the machine to the heart of the memory. He was so engrossed in his work that he almost didn’t notice it when Mrs. Gable began to scream.

It wasn’t a scream of fear or pain, though fear and pain were certainly there. It was a scream of fury, of horror, of loss. It was a scream that shook Dr. Thorne to his very core, forcing him to tear his eyes away from his monitors and look directly at the woman.

Her eyes were wide, unseeing, lost in the throes of the memory. Tears streamed down her face, dripping from her chin to join the puddle already forming on the floor. And still she screamed, a wordless howl of anguish that seemed to go on and on and on. It was, Dr. Thorne thought wildly, the most terrible sound he had ever heard.

He knew, with a sickening certainty, that something had gone wrong. The Memory Guillotine was meant to erase the memory of the massacre, of Mrs. Gable’s daughter’s death, but it seemed instead to be forcing her to relive it. He reached for the controls, meaning to shut off the machine, but then he paused. He’d never seen a reaction like this before. He wasn’t sure what it meant.

He remained frozen, his fingers hovering over the keyboard, for a long moment. Then, with a sigh, he leaned back in his chair. He had a duty to perform, after all, and he couldn’t let his own squeamishness get in the way of that. He took a deep breath, steeling himself, and turned back to the task at hand.

The Memory Guillotine was a marvel of modern technology, but it wasn’t infallible. There were some memories, Dr. Thorne knew, that were too deeply ingrained in a person’s psyche to be removed completely. In cases like these, the best the machine could do was muddle the memory, blur the details until the patient could no longer discern the truth of what had happened. They might have a vague recollection of the event, but it wouldn’t hold any power over them. They wouldn’t wake up in the middle of the night, screaming and crying, as Mrs. Gable no doubt did.

As the machine went to work, Dr. Thorne found himself unable to look away from the monitors. He watched, transfixed, as the memory played out before him. He saw the drones descend upon the crowd, saw the people scatter in terror, saw the little girl fall. He saw the look of horror on Mrs. Gable’s face as she realized her daughter was dead.

And then, quite suddenly, he saw something else. Something he hadn’t noticed before.

As the drones retreated, leaving the square littered with corpses, a man emerged from a side street. He was tall and well-built, with a thick beard and a head of shaggy black hair. He wore a long, dark coat that billowed behind him in the wind. And pinned to his lapel was the unmistakable insignia of the Regime.

Dr. Thorne frowned. He’d never seen anyone quite like this man before. What, he wondered, was someone like him doing at a place like this?

He got his answer a moment later. The man knelt beside one of the bodies, a young woman with fiery red hair. He brushed the hair out of her face, then bent low to press his lips to hers. It would have been a tender moment, if not for the circumstances. If not for the fact that the woman was quite clearly dead.

Dr. Thorne felt a wave of revulsion wash over him. He wanted to look away, but he found himself unable to tear his eyes from the screen. There was something about the man, about the way he carried himself, that seemed oddly familiar. Almost as if—

Then it hit him. He’d seen this man before. He was one of the Regime’s highest-ranking officials. His name was Malachi Thorne.

Dr. Thorne’s heart soared. Malachi Thorne, he knew, was one of the Regime’s most celebrated heroes. He’d been instrumental in the war that had brought the Regime to power, and he’d been awarded the Medal of Valor for his bravery in the field. Dr. Thorne had always admired him, had always wanted to be like him. And now, it seemed, he was.

A wave of excitement washed over Dr. Thorne. He couldn’t believe his luck. To think that he, a humble memory editor, could be related to someone like Malachi Thorne—it was almost too good to be true. He felt a grin spreading across his face, felt his heart pounding in his chest. He could hardly wait to tell his friends, his family, the whole world. He was, he realized, going to be famous.

But even as these thoughts raced through his mind, another part of him was screaming in protest. This couldn’t be right. Malachi Thorne was a hero, a paragon of the Regime. He would never do something like this. He would never desecrate the dead like this. It was impossible.

And yet, the evidence was right there before him. He had seen it with his own eyes. Malachi Thorne, the Regime’s golden boy, kneeling beside a corpse, kissing its cold, dead lips. It was unthinkable. And yet, it had happened. It had to have happened.

Dr. Thorne felt sick. He wanted to vomit. He wanted to scream. He wanted to tear his eyes out, to erase this memory from his mind forever. But he knew he couldn’t. He was a memory editor, but he was also a loyal citizen of the Regime. He knew what he had to do.

He took a deep breath, steeling himself. Then he reached for the controls of the Memory Guillotine. He hesitated for a moment, his finger hovering over the button that would erase this memory from Mrs. Gable’s mind forever. He knew it was the right thing to do. He knew it was his duty.

But even as he pressed the button, he couldn’t shake the feeling that he was making a mistake. That he was, in some way, betraying himself. He tried to push these thoughts aside, to focus on the task at hand, but they kept intruding. He kept seeing Malachi Thorne’s face, kept hearing his own excited voice echoing in his mind. He knew he had to keep this a secret, but he also knew that it wouldn’t be easy. It would eat away at him, day and night, until there was nothing left of him but this one, terrible secret.

The Memory Guillotine whirred to life, erasing the memory from Mrs. Gable’s mind. Dr. Thorne watched as the color drained from her face, watched as her screams died in her throat. He watched as she slumped forward in her chair, unconscious, free from the pain that had haunted her for so long.

He should have felt happy. He should have felt proud. He was, after all, doing his job. He was helping people, healing them, making their lives better. But he didn’t feel happy. He didn’t feel proud. He felt sick, and scared, and alone.

He knew what he had seen. He knew what it meant. And he knew that nothing would ever be the same again.
