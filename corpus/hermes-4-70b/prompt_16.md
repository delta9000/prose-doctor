The roar of the airplane engines had faded to a distant hum, leaving Sarah Carter in a deafening silence as she stood on her doorstep. The once-familiar sight of her blue-painted front door seemed foreign, like a piece of art in a museum—something to be observed from a distance, never touched. She clutched the handle of her suitcase, her knuckles turning white under the strain.

*Click.*

The sound of the key turning in the lock was deafening in the silence. The door creaked open, revealing a dimly lit hallway. A wave of nostalgia washed over her as the scent of home wafted out to greet her—a mixture of vanilla candles, aged wood, and the faintest hint of cinnamon from the rug her partner, Rachel, had woven by hand.

"Hello?" Sarah called out, her voice trembling slightly. "I'm home."

The silence that followed was suffocating. She stepped inside, shutting the door behind her with a soft thud that echoed through the stillness.

*Thump. Thump. Thump.*

The sound of hurried footsteps grew louder, and then she appeared at the end of the hallway—Rachel, her hair longer than Sarah remembered, her eyes wide with surprise.

"Sarah? I... I didn't expect you back so soon."

There was a hint of something in her voice—regret? Resentment? Sarah couldn't place it.

"I finished my assignment earlier than expected," Sarah replied, forcing a smile onto her face. "I wanted to come home."

Rachel nodded, her gaze drifting to the suitcase by Sarah's side.

"You must be tired," she said softly. "Let me take your bag."

She reached forward, but Sarah tightened her grip on the handle.

"I've got it," she said, her voice sharper than she intended. "Where should I put it?"

A flicker of pain crossed Rachel's face, and Sarah felt a pang of guilt in her chest.

"The bedroom," Rachel said finally. "I'll... I'll make us some tea."

As Rachel disappeared into the kitchen, Sarah carried her suitcase down the hallway, each step feeling heavier than the last. When she reached the bedroom, she paused in the doorway, her breath catching in her throat.

The room had been transformed. Gone were the maps and photographs that once adorned the walls, replaced by abstract paintings in muted colours. The bedspread was new—soft grey instead of the vibrant red they'd chosen together. Even the furniture had been rearranged, the armchair where Sarah used to read now facing the window instead of the bed.

*She's erased me from this room,* Sarah realized with a start. *From this house. From her life.*

She set her suitcase down on the floor, her hands trembling as she unzipped it. Inside, nestled among her clothes, was a small wooden box. She opened it slowly, revealing the dog tags she'd worn during her assignment—the only remnants of the person she'd been.

*War Correspondent.*

The words were engraved on the metal, a title she'd once worn with pride. Now, they felt like a burden—a reminder of the life she'd left behind.

"Sarah?"

She jumped at the sound of Rachel's voice, quickly snapping the box shut.

"I've made tea," Rachel said, holding out a steaming mug. "It's chamomile. I thought you might need something to help you sleep."

Sarah took the mug gratefully, inhaling the fragrant steam.

"Thank you," she murmured. "It smells wonderful."

Rachel leaned against the doorframe, her arms crossed over her chest.

"How was your flight?" she asked, her voice casual. "Did you have any trouble at the airport?"

Sarah shook her head, taking a sip of tea.

"It was uneventful," she said. "Just the way I like it."

A silence fell between them, thick and heavy with unspoken words. Rachel fidgeted with the hem of her shirt, her eyes darting around the room.

"Sarah, I..." she began, but her voice trailed off. "I'm glad you're home. Really, I am. It's just..."

"Just what?" Sarah prompted, setting her mug down on the nightstand.

Rachel sighed, running a hand through her hair.

"It's been a long time," she said softly. "Things have changed. I've changed. I'm not the same person you left behind."

Sarah felt a lump form in her throat.

"I know," she whispered. "I'm not the same person either."

Rachel nodded, her eyes glistening with unshed tears.

"I'm sorry," she said. "I wanted to be here for you. I wanted to be the partner you needed. But I couldn't do it anymore. I couldn't keep living my life on hold, waiting for you to come back from some war zone. I had to move on. I had to build a life of my own."

Sarah's heart ached at her words.

"I understand," she said, even though she didn't. Not really. "I don't blame you."

Rachel smiled sadly.

"I'm glad," she said. "Because the truth is, I don't know if there's a place for you in this life anymore. I don't know if we can go back to the way things were."

Sarah felt a chill run down her spine.

"What are you saying?" she asked, her voice barely above a whisper.

Rachel took a deep breath, her shoulders squaring as if preparing for a fight.

"I'm saying that I love you," she said. "But I don't know if that's enough anymore. I don't know if we can make this work."

The words hung in the air between them, heavy and suffocating. Sarah felt as though the ground had been ripped out from beneath her, leaving her suspended in a void of uncertainty.

"I see," she said, her voice trembling slightly. "So what now?"

Rachel shrugged, a helpless gesture that spoke volumes.

"I don't know," she admitted. "I guess we take it one day at a time. We see if we can find a way to make this work. But I need you to understand something, Sarah. I need you to know that if it doesn't... if we can't find a way back to each other... I'll be okay. I've learned to be okay without you."

Sarah nodded, swallowing the lump in her throat.

"I understand," she said. "I'll try not to make it harder for you."

Rachel's eyes filled with tears, and she quickly turned away.

"I should let you get some rest," she said, her voice thick with emotion. "We can talk more in the morning."

Sarah watched as she walked away, her heart heavy with the weight of all the things left unsaid. When the door clicked shut behind her, Sarah sank onto the bed, burying her face in her hands.

*What have I done?* she thought, her mind racing with regrets. *What have I sacrificed?*

She'd left for another war zone, another conflict, another story to tell. She'd told herself it was her duty, her calling. She'd told herself that the world needed to know the truth, and she was the one to tell it.

But now, as she sat in the silence of the bedroom that no longer felt like home, she wondered if it had all been for nothing. She wondered if the stories she'd told, the lives she'd chronicled, were worth the price she'd paid.

*Was it worth losing her?* she wondered, a single tear sliding down her cheek. *Was it worth losing myself?*

She didn't know the answer. All she knew was that the life she'd left behind was gone, and the life she'd returned to was unfamiliar and strange. She was a ghost in her own home, a stranger in her own bed.

And as she lay down on the cool, unfamiliar sheets, Sarah closed her eyes and prayed for sleep to take her away from the pain of the present, if only for a little while.
