The air hung thick and heavy over the city of Everspring, laden with the stench of death. From the crack in her door, Sister Althea could see the pile of bodies in the street, waiting to be collected. The sound of the death cart rattled through the streets, and a spasm of fear gripped Sister Althea’s heart. She had been nursing the sick for weeks now, clutching her few bundles of herbs, praying to the gods for mercy. But mercy seemed a distant hope.
The muffled sound of weeping came from the next room, a sound that Sister Althea had grown all too accustomed to. Sister Althea rose quietly, smoothing her rough woolen habit, and went to see her patient.
Elara was no more than sixteen, a young woman with bright blue eyes and a mass of golden hair that now lay damp and lank against her sweat-soaked pillow. Her face was pale and drawn, her breathing shallow and ragged. The telltale black marks of plague had appeared on her skin, and her eyes burned with fever.
“Sister,” she whispered, reaching out for Sister Althea’s hand. “I’m so afraid.”
Sister Althea sat beside her, taking her hand and holding it tightly. “Don’t be afraid, child. The gods will watch over you.” She pulled out a bundle of dried herbs from the pocket of her habit and began to grind them with a small stone mortar and pestle. She added a few drops of water to the powder and mixed it together until it formed a thick paste.
“This will help you sleep,” Sister Althea said softly, dabbing the paste onto Elara’s forehead. “You need to rest.”
Elara’s eyes fluttered closed, and her breathing grew steadier. Sister Althea stood and tip-toed out of the room, making her way to the small chapel at the end of the hall. She fell to her knees before the altar, praying to the gods for the health of her patients.
The chapel door slammed open, and Sister Althea looked up to see the priest of Everspring, Father Tyrus, standing in the doorway. His face was red with anger, and his eyes were narrowed in suspicion.
“Sister Althea,” he said, his voice dripping with disdain. “I have heard whispers of your actions. They say you are practicing witchcraft.”
Sister Althea rose to her feet, her heart pounding in her chest. “Father,” she said, “I am only trying to heal the sick. The herbs I use are gifts from the gods, to be used for their glory.”
Father Tyrus stepped into the chapel, his eyes scanning the room. “Herbs?” he said, his voice dripping with suspicion. “Or are they something more sinister?” He reached out and grabbed Sister Althea’s arm, his grip tight and painful. “I have seen the way you minister to the sick, the way you spread your ‘medicine’ among them. I have heard the way you pray to the gods, begging for their mercy. But I also hear the whispers of the people, that you are in league with the devil himself.”
Sister Althea wrenched her arm free, her eyes blazing with anger. “How dare you,” she hissed. “I am a servant of the gods, nothing more. I use the gifts they have given me to ease the suffering of the sick, to bring comfort to the dying. If you were truly a man of the gods, you would do the same.”
Father Tyrus’s face twisted into a sneer. “Blasphemy,” he spat. “You dare to question my faith? I have dedicated my life to the service of the gods, and I will not stand by while you lead their flock astray.” He reached out and grabbed Sister Althea’s wrist, his fingers digging into her skin. “I will be watching you, Sister,” he said, his voice low and threatening. “If I find any evidence of witchcraft, any hint of evil, I will not hesitate to denounce you to the authorities.”
Sister Althea tore her wrist free, her heart pounding with fear and anger. “I have done nothing wrong,” she said, her voice shaking. “I will continue to care for the sick, to pray for the dying. And if the gods see fit to punish me for my actions, then so be it.”
Father Tyrus turned on his heel and stalked out of the chapel, slamming the door behind him. Sister Althea sank to her knees, her body shaking with emotion. She buried her face in her hands and wept, her shoulders shaking with sobs.

She thought of the people she had helped, the lives she had touched with her herbs and her prayers. But she also thought of the whispers that followed her through the streets, the suspicious glances and the muttered accusations. She thought of the way the people of Everspring had once welcomed her, had once embraced her as a sister and a healer. Now, it seemed, they saw her as something else entirely.
She rose from her bed and walked to the small window that overlooked the city. The streets were empty, the shops and houses dark and silent. The only sound was the distant tolling of the bell that marked the death of another victim of the plague.

Sister Althea set up a small table and began to grind her herbs, mixing them together in careful proportions. She added a few drops of water to the powder and mixed it together until it formed a thick paste, then began to apply it carefully to the foreheads and wrists of the sick and dying.
