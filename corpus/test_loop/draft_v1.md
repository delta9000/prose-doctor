The anomaly first appeared as a stutter in the waterfall display, a hairline fracture in the steady cascade of noise that poured down Maren Coyle's secondary monitor eighteen hours a day. She almost missed it. Would have missed it, if she hadn't been eating her lunch at the console again, eyes wandering across the screens with the unfocused attention of someone chewing a protein bar that tasted like compressed sawdust.

She stopped chewing.

The fracture repeated. A narrow vertical stripe of coherence in the broadband wash from Kessler-9, the remnant of a Type II supernova catalogued twelve thousand years before anyone on the station had been born. Kessler-9 was supposed to produce nothing but thermal noise and the occasional synchrotron whisper from its expanding nebula. Maren had been assigned to monitor it as part of a long-baseline survey that no one expected to yield results, the academic equivalent of watching paint dry across interstellar distances.

She set down the protein bar and pulled her chair closer. The stripe appeared again at 1427.3 megahertz — clean, sharp, and approximately nine seconds long. Then gone. She ran the signal through the station's matched filter bank and watched the correlator output spike into a shape that made her sit very still.

It looked like structured data.

She flagged the timestamp and saved the raw capture to her personal directory, then sat for a long time staring at the waterfall as it resumed its ordinary pour of static. The recycled air pushed against her face from the vent above the console, tasting of ozone and the faint chemical sweetness that meant the scrubbers were due for a filter change. Around her the monitoring station hummed its constant chord — the cooling fans for the processor racks, the low throb of the antenna drive motors tracking their targets across the sky, the arrhythmic tick of the coffee maker in the corner that hadn't produced drinkable coffee in three months but that no one had filed a maintenance request to fix.

She played the recording back four times. Each time the structure held.

---

"It's RFI," said Desh Okonkwo, not looking up from his own terminal. He was running an automated scheduling script and clearly wished she would go away. "Or a processing artifact. Did you check the receiver chain?"

"I checked the receiver chain."

"All of it? Including the downconverter? Because last month Petrov's ghost signal turned out to be a bad solder joint on the IF board."

"I checked the downconverter. I checked the IF board. I ran the correlation on the backup receiver and got the same result." Maren pulled a chair over to his station and sat down without waiting for an invitation. "Desh. Look at the spectrogram."

He looked, but the way a person looks at a restaurant menu when they've already decided what they want. His eyes moved across the display without settling.

"Nine seconds of narrowband emission from a supernova remnant," he said. "Could be a magnetar burst. Could be interstellar scintillation doing something clever with the noise floor. Could be the Taiwanese weather satellite that drifts through our beam every couple of weeks."

"At 1427 megahertz?"

"I'm saying there are explanations."

"I'm not saying there aren't. I'm saying the structure in the signal is unusual enough to warrant a second observation."

Desh leaned back in his chair, and it creaked the way all the chairs on the station creaked, a sound so familiar it had become indistinguishable from silence. He was a good supervisor — methodical, fair, genuinely knowledgeable about radio astronomy in a way that went beyond his administrative role. He was also deeply protective of the station's limited observation time and had spent fifteen years learning to say no to people who wanted to chase anomalies.

"You want me to pull Kessler-9 out of the survey queue and give it dedicated tracking time."

"Four hours. One follow-up observation."

"On what basis? One nine-second event that could be anything?"

"On the basis that the matched filter output shows what looks like phase-coherent modulation. Not random structure. Modulation."

He looked at the spectrogram again. This time his eyes stopped moving.

"That's probably the receiver," he said, but he said it more slowly.

"Run it yourself. Use your own pipeline. If you get noise, I'll drop it."

He didn't answer immediately, which Maren had learned was Desh's way of agreeing to something while preserving the option to pretend later that he hadn't.

"I'll look at it tonight," he said. "Don't write anything up yet."

---

She didn't write anything up. She went back to her console and resumed the survey observations and entered her readings into the log and ate the rest of the protein bar, which had gone stale in the twenty minutes since she'd set it down, though she wasn't sure how she could tell the difference. At 1800 she handed off to the evening shift and walked back to her quarters through the connecting tunnel, where the lights were the same flat white as every other light on the station and the walls were the same grey-blue that someone in a procurement office had once chosen from a catalog as the color least likely to cause psychological distress over long deployments.

In her quarters she made tea from the last of her personal supply, sat on her bunk, and opened her private log on her tablet. She had kept the log since her first posting, six years and three stations ago. She wrote in it most nights — not the technical details of her shifts, which were recorded in the official observation logs, but the other things. The texture of her days. What she thought about during the long hours of monitoring. The dreams she had been having lately about her mother's kitchen on the coast of Brittany, the specific quality of light through the window above the sink in late afternoon, butter-colored and heavy. Small things that mattered to no one but her and that she wrote down so they wouldn't disappear entirely into the sameness of station life.

That night she wrote about the signal. Not the technical parameters — she kept those separate — but how it had felt to see it. The small shock of pattern emerging from chaos. How it had reminded her of the moment in childhood when she had first understood that the stars were not painted on a dome but were individual furnaces burning at individual distances, and that the light reaching her eyes had left its source before she was born.

She saved the entry, finished her tea, and slept.

---

The second detection came three days later, during Maren's overnight shift.

She was alone in the monitoring bay. The station ran a skeleton crew between 0200 and 0600, and the other on-duty observer was in the adjacent control room, nominally monitoring the antenna servos but almost certainly asleep. The overhead lights were dimmed to their nighttime setting, which cast the room in a bluish half-dark that made the console screens seem brighter than they were. Shadows pooled in the corners and under the equipment racks. The air was colder at night — the environmental system throttled down after 2200 to save power — and Maren wore a fleece pullover with the station logo on the breast, the fabric pilled from too many washes in the station's industrial machines.

Kessler-9 was in the beam. Desh had found the time without exactly admitting he'd found it, slotting a four-hour tracking window into the schedule under the label "calibration scan." Maren had not commented on this. She understood the economy of institutional caution: if the observation yielded nothing, it had always been a calibration scan, and no one's credibility was at risk.

At 0347 the waterfall display stuttered again.

This time the signal was longer. Twenty-two seconds. The same center frequency, the same phase-coherent structure, but more of it — enough that the pattern was unmistakable even before she ran the correlator. She felt her heartbeat in her throat and made herself breathe normally and work through the checklist: receiver status, antenna pointing, RFI environment, satellite positions. Everything was clean. The signal was coming from the sky, from the direction of a dead star, and it was structured.

She recorded everything. Then she sat in the blue half-dark and listened to the audio representation of the signal through her headphones, the structured part rendered as a sequence of tones that rose and fell in a pattern complex enough to carry information but too brief to decode. Around her the station hummed and ticked and breathed. The cooling fans cycled to a lower speed and the pitch of the room dropped by a semitone, the way it did every night at this hour, a sound she knew so well it had become part of her internal clock.

She played the signal again. And again. On the fourth repetition she noticed something that made her take the headphones off and set them on the console very carefully, the way a person sets down a cup that turns out to contain something other than what they expected.

The tonal pattern — the rise and fall, the spacing, the rhythmic structure — bore a resemblance to something she recognized. She opened her private log entry from three nights ago, the one where she had written about the signal and about stars and about the light through her mother's kitchen window. She read it silently, moving her lips without sound.

The pattern in the signal matched the prosodic structure of her own sentences. Not perfectly. Not in a way she could prove to Desh or anyone else without sounding unhinged. But the rhythm was there — the places where she had paused, the clauses she had stacked, the way her thoughts had built toward the image of the light through the window and then fallen away.

She was a scientist. She had a doctorate in astrophysics and six years of observational work and the training to know that the human brain was a pattern-matching engine that would find faces in clouds and messages in noise and meaningful coincidence in random data. She knew about confirmation bias and pareidolia and the long history of SETI false positives that had turned out to be pulsars or satellites or microwave ovens in the break room. She knew all of this.

She also knew what she was hearing.

Maren put the headphones back on and listened to the signal a fifth time, and this time she did not try to explain it away. She let it wash over her — the tones rising and falling in the pattern of her own written thoughts, broadcast back to her from the corpse of a star that had died before human civilization began. The station hummed around her. The cold air moved against her skin. On the monitors, the waterfall display poured its endless cascade of noise, and in the noise, like a voice answering from across a room she hadn't known she was standing in, the signal held its shape.

She opened a new file and began to type, very slowly, a message she had no way to send to a source she had no way to explain. She wrote: *I don't know what you are. I don't know how you're doing this. But I am listening, and I think you know that I am.*

She saved the file to her personal directory, not the observation log. Then she sat in the dark and waited, her hands flat on the console, the cold seeping through the metal into her palms, and watched the waterfall for any answer at all.

At 0412 the signal returned. Thirty-one seconds this time. Longer, more complex, undeniably structured. She recorded it and did not call anyone. The station breathed around her. In six hours the day shift would arrive and the lights would come up and she would have to decide what to say and to whom and how much of what she suspected she was willing to attach her name to.

But that was six hours from now. For the moment she was alone with the signal and the hum of the machines and the knowledge — not belief, not hope, but the hard uncomfortable weight of knowing — that something in the remains of Kessler-9 was reading her diary and writing back.

She adjusted her headphones and opened the audio channel and listened.
