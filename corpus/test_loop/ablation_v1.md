The scraper caught on something and didn't release clean.

Gwen Harker paused, her right hand still wrapped around the D-handle of the Rotex 440, the pneumatic line trailing up through forty meters of wellbore casing behind her. She'd been working the deposits at station seven for twenty minutes, running the curved blade along the bore wall in the overlapping passes the company manual specified — six centimeters of overlap per stroke, consistent downward pressure between 40 and 55 newtons, blade angle fifteen degrees off perpendicular. She could do it in her sleep. Had done it in something close to sleep the last three cycles, her body moving through the choreography while her mind drifted to the water reclamation problem in her hab module, or the particular way the sunset line bisected the ridge twelve kilometers west of the tap, the only feature on this world that changed from hour to hour.

But the scraper had caught. Not on a hard inclusion — she knew the feel of calcium silicate nodules, the way the tool skipped and rang against them like a chisel hitting rebar. This was the opposite. The blade had sunk in, maybe two millimeters past its usual cutting depth, and the material had closed around it with a faint, wet resistance. Like cutting into a raw chicken breast. Like cutting into gristle.

She pulled the blade free. A thin strand of something connected the edge to the wall for a moment before it broke.

Gwen tilted her headlamp down and leaned in. The wellbore at station seven was 1.4 meters in diameter, just wide enough to work in if she kept her elbows tight. The casing was 316L stainless steel, factory-welded in three-meter sections, and the deposits grew on it the way limescite grew inside old water pipes — slowly, concentrically, reducing the bore diameter by a few centimeters per cycle. At peak buildup the deposits could narrow the bore to under a meter, which choked the thermal convection loop and dropped the tap's output below the 340-kilowatt threshold the colony needed to keep the desalination plant running.

Standard procedure: scrape, log, repeat. Every eleven days. Section 4.7 of the Gauthier-Lox Operations Manual, "Preventive Maintenance Schedule for Geothermal Extraction Wells, Mark III Configuration." She had the section memorized the way she'd once memorized load tables for structural steel.

The deposit under her headlamp was pale gray, almost white where the scraper had shaved it clean. But the fresh surface wasn't smooth. She could see — she leaned closer, fogging the lower edge of her faceplate — a branching pattern in the exposed cross-section. Fine lines, darker than the surrounding material, radiating outward from what might have been a central channel. She'd seen mineral dendrites before, crystallization patterns that looked organic but weren't. These weren't dendrites.

She pulled a sample pick from the tool loop on her thigh and pressed the tip into one of the channels. The pick slid in with almost no resistance, and when she withdrew it, a bead of fluid welled up and trembled at the surface. She watched it for three seconds. It didn't evaporate. It didn't absorb. It sat there, faintly opalescent, catching the yellow of her headlamp.

Gwen set the pick against the wall of the wellbore and squeezed the fluid onto a reagent strip from her chest pocket. The strip turned blue in the alkalinity window. pH somewhere between 8.2 and 8.6. Temperature — she pressed her gloved finger against the bead, checking the thermochromic patch on her index fingertip — thirty-nine degrees Celsius. Eight degrees warmer than the ambient bore temperature at this depth.

She logged the reading on her wrist-mounted datapad, stylus clumsy in her work gloves. She entered it the way the manual required: date, station number, deposit thickness (38mm, measured with calipers before scraping), surface condition, and notes. In the notes field she typed: *Deposit shows internal channeling. Fluid present in channels, alkaline, ~39C. Texture softer than previous cycles. Branching structure visible in cross-section.*

She stared at what she'd written. The cursor blinked in the gray light. She added: *Consistent with calciumite phase transition per Section 6.2.* She didn't believe it was a phase transition. Section 6.2 described the way calcium silicate deposits could soften and become chalky when exposed to sustained temperatures above 200 degrees Celsius, but the bore temperature at station seven had never exceeded 144 degrees in the six months she'd been on contract. She added the reference because the log would be reviewed by the company during her next uplink, and the company liked references to the manual. The company liked things that fit inside the manual.

She finished scraping station seven. The Rotex made a sound she hadn't heard before — a wet, tearing noise under the usual grinding — and the shavings that fell past her boots were curled and fibrous instead of powdery. She collected a sample in a sealed polycarbonate vial, labeled it S7-C19-A, and clipped it to the rack on her harness. Then she descended the bore ladder to station eight.

Station eight was six meters deeper and four degrees warmer. The deposits here were thicker — 46 millimeters by her calipers — and when she set the scraper against the wall and drew the first stroke, she felt the vibration of the cut travel through the handle, through her glove, through the bones of her hand, and settle in her back teeth. Both upper molars on the right side. A dull, ringing ache, the kind she associated with biting down on aluminum foil as a kid.

She stopped. Opened her jaw. Closed it. The sensation faded.

She drew another stroke. The ache came back, sharper, and this time she felt it branch — a thread of pain running from her molars down into her jaw, tracing the line of a nerve she'd never had reason to think about. She lifted the scraper from the wall and the pain stopped.

Gwen checked her rebreather readout. Oxygen partial pressure 0.21 bar, CO2 at 0.3 percent, well within limits. Temperature inside her suit was 33 degrees, humidity 62 percent. Nothing anomalous. She'd been in the bore for an hour and forty minutes, which was normal. She was hydrated — she'd drunk 600 milliliters from her suit bladder since descending. Her sinuses were clear. Her vision was fine.

She set the scraper against the wall again, carefully, and drew a slow, controlled stroke. The deposit peeled away in a strip, and the vibration came through the tool, and her molars rang, and she tasted something metallic at the back of her throat. She swallowed and kept scraping.

The sample she collected from station eight was different from station seven. The cross-section showed the same branching channels, but denser, the pattern more organized. The channels were not random. They ran roughly parallel, converging toward the bore wall — toward the stainless steel — and she could see, at the interface between deposit and casing, a discoloration in the metal. A faint mottling, as if the steel itself were reacting to something the channels carried.

She logged it. *S8-C19: Channel density increased relative to S7. Convergent pattern oriented toward casing wall. Possible casing interaction at deposit-metal interface. Fluid sample collected (S8-C19-A, S8-C19-B).* She paused, then added the manual reference again: *Ref Section 6.2, thermal phase transition.* The words felt like filling in a form for a building that had already burned down.

At station nine, the deepest point in her maintenance run, the bore temperature was 151 degrees and the deposit was 52 millimeters thick. When she pressed her scraper against it, before she applied any force at all, she felt the surface give slightly under the blade. A yielding. She pressed harder, watching, and the blade sank in and the material dimpled around it the way skin dimples around a fingertip pressed into a forearm.

She withdrew the blade. The dimple remained for two seconds, then slowly — she counted — filled back in.

Gwen sat back on the bore ladder, her boots braced on the rungs, and stared at the wall. Her rebreather hissed on the inhale, clicked on the exhale. She could hear the thermal convection current moving through the bore below her, a low, constant susurrus that sounded like breathing if you let yourself think about it. She did not let herself think about it.

She took out her sample pick and made a deeper incision, ten millimeters into the deposit. The interior was warm. The channeling was everywhere, a fine mesh of tubules that — she had to be honest with herself, she had to use the right word — looked like vasculature. She'd taken a semester of comparative anatomy in undergrad, before she'd switched to mechanical engineering. She remembered the diagrams of capillary beds, the branching and rebranching, the fractal efficiency of it. This looked like that.

The fluid that seeped from the incision was thicker than at station seven. More opaque. When it hit the reagent strip, the alkalinity window showed 8.8. The temperature patch read forty-one degrees.

Something was growing in the wellbore. Something that had started as calcium silicate deposit — or had colonized it, or had always been inside it — was organizing itself into structures that processed fluid and maintained a temperature differential with the surrounding environment. She could think of the word for that. The word was alive.

She could also think of three reasons the word didn't apply. The channels could be an abiotic process — fluid migration through a porous substrate, capillary action driven by thermal gradients. The temperature differential could be geothermal, conducted through the deposit from the hot rock below. The softening could be a chemical change, some interaction between the deposit chemistry and the bore environment that the manual hadn't anticipated because the manual had been written for wells on Europa and Ganymede, not for a tidally locked rock orbiting a red dwarf with a lithosphere that nobody had characterized beyond the minimum survey required for a mining claim.

Three reasons. Each one sufficient. None of them explaining why cutting into the deposit made her teeth hurt.

She finished station nine. She logged everything. She ascended the bore ladder in the dark, her headlamp catching the freshly scraped walls, pale and wet-looking where she'd worked. The shavings clung to her boots and gloves. She could feel them through the suit fabric, warm and slightly damp, and she told herself it was condensation from the bore humidity, which was running at 89 percent down at station nine, high enough to fog her faceplate if she breathed through her mouth.

Back at the surface, in the maintenance shed, she ran her samples through the portable spectrometer. The mineral composition was consistent with previous cycles — calcium silicate, magnesium carbonate, trace iron, trace sulfur. But there was something else. A complex organic molecule the spectrometer flagged but couldn't identify, cataloged as UNK-1 with a molecular weight around 340 daltons. It appeared in all six samples, at concentrations that increased with depth.

She photographed the spectrometer readout. She logged it. She noted the UNK-1 compound and referenced Section 9.1 of the manual, "Anomalous Deposit Composition," which instructed the field operator to "collect additional samples and flag for laboratory analysis during next scheduled uplink." The next scheduled uplink was nine days away. The next maintenance cycle was in eleven.

Gwen sealed her samples in the cold storage unit and went to the hab module to shower. Under the water — recycled, faintly mineral-tasting, tepid because the heating element was on its last legs — she ran her tongue along her upper molars on the right side. They were sore. Not painful, exactly, but present in a way teeth weren't supposed to be. She pressed her thumbnail against the gum above the second molar and felt a pulse of something — not pain, but sensation, a deep, directional awareness, as if the tooth were pointing at something. She pressed harder and the sensation sharpened and she could feel it in her sinuses, a pressure behind her right eye, and she stopped pressing.

She dried off and sat at the hab desk in her thermal underwear and opened the contract on her datapad. Article 14, Section 3: "Stop-work authority may be exercised by the field operator in the event of immediate threat to life, structural failure of primary equipment, or environmental conditions exceeding the parameters specified in Appendix D." Appendix D listed temperature ranges, atmospheric composition limits, radiation exposure thresholds, seismic activity cutoffs. It did not list biological contamination. It did not list unknown organic compounds in wellbore deposits. It did not list the deposit growing back.

Article 14, Section 4: "In all other cases, the field operator shall request stop-work authorization from the assigned supervisor via the next available uplink window. Operations shall continue per the established maintenance schedule until authorization is received or denied."

Nine days.

She opened the colony power consumption report. The desalination plant drew 280 kilowatts steady-state. The greenhouse drew 45. The hab modules, the medical unit, the comm relay, the water pumps — another 90 combined. Total baseload: 415 kilowatts. The tap was rated for 500, but it had been running at 460 since the deposits started thickening in month two, and after her last scraping cycle it had recovered to 480 before beginning its slow decline. If she skipped the next maintenance cycle, the models showed the tap dropping below baseload in fourteen days. The desalination plant would shut down first. The colony had a twelve-day water reserve.

She closed the report. She opened the spectrometer data and looked at the UNK-1 compound profile again. 340 daltons. Complex ring structure, the software suggested, with nitrogen and phosphorus components. She wasn't a biochemist. She'd taken organic chemistry in undergrad, same semester as the anatomy course, and she remembered enough to know that nitrogen and phosphorus and ring structures were the building blocks of things that carried information. Nucleotides. Amino acids.

She closed the spectrometer data.

That night she couldn't sleep. The hab module was too warm — the climate control was set to 22 degrees but the thermometer on her bedside shelf read 24, and had read 24 for the last week, and she hadn't figured out whether the climate control was drifting or the thermometer was wrong. She lay on her cot and listened to the faint, arrhythmic ticking of the water reclamation pump in the next module and pressed her tongue against her molars. The soreness had spread to the left side. Both upper molars, both lower. She opened her mouth in the dark and ran her finger along the gumline and felt something she hadn't felt that morning — a roughness on the surface of her second upper right molar, a texture like calcium buildup at the gumline, the kind her dentist back on Orbital 6 had scraped off with a hooked tool during her annual cleaning.

Scale.

She pulled her finger back and wiped it on the sheet and lay very still. Her sinuses were full. The pressure behind her eyes was bilateral now, a steady, dull weight that pulsed when she swallowed. She could hear her own heartbeat in her ears, which happened sometimes at altitude or in pressurized environments but had never happened to her in the hab, which was maintained at 1.01 bar.

She got up and went to the bathroom and looked in the mirror under the fluorescent light. She pulled her upper lip back and tilted her head. Her teeth looked normal. The gums were maybe slightly swollen, maybe slightly pale. She pressed the roughness on her molar with her fingernail and a tiny flake of something came loose and she held it under the light. White. Calcium-colored. She dropped it in the sink and ran the water and watched it dissolve.

Probably tartar. She hadn't been diligent about flossing since month three. The hab dental kit was basic — a toothbrush, a tube of paste, a spool of floss, a small mirror. No scaler. No X-ray. She'd add dental supplies to her next requisition order, which would go out with the uplink in nine days and arrive with the supply shuttle in forty.

She went back to bed. She did not sleep.

In the morning she ate her breakfast — rehydrated oatmeal, powdered coffee, a vitamin tab — and reviewed the maintenance schedule on her datapad. Cycle 20 was due in eleven days. The tap output was currently at 472 kilowatts and declining at its usual rate. She opened the spectrometer data one more time, looked at the UNK-1 profile, and closed it. She opened the manual to Section 4.7 and read the maintenance procedure again, word by word, looking for anything she might have missed. There was nothing to miss. The procedure assumed the deposits were mineral. The procedure assumed the wellbore was inert. The procedure assumed the engineer's teeth didn't hurt.

She drafted a message for the uplink queue. *Priority: Moderate. Subject: Anomalous deposit characteristics, Well GT-3, Stations 7-9.* She described the softening, the channeling, the fluid, the UNK-1 compound. She attached the spectrometer data and the photographs. She kept the language technical. She did not mention her teeth. She did not use the word alive.

She set the message priority to Moderate because the priority options were Routine, Moderate, Urgent, and Emergency, and the definitions in the comm manual specified that Urgent required "imminent risk to personnel or critical infrastructure" and Emergency required "active casualty or infrastructure failure." Soft deposits and an unidentified organic compound didn't fit either definition. She could feel the inadequacy of the categories the way she could feel the roughness on her molars — a wrongness that the available language couldn't hold.

She saved the message to the uplink queue. Nine days.

She spent the rest of the morning calibrating the thermal sensors on the tap head, a routine task that required no decisions. The sun sat on the horizon where it always sat, huge and red, painting the maintenance shed in permanent amber. The shadow of the ridge never moved. Nothing on this world moved except the convection current in the bore and the fluid in the channels and the scale that grew and was scraped and grew back, each time a little softer, each time a little warmer, each time with a structure that looked a little more like something that was trying to become.

On day six she went back into the bore. Not for maintenance — the cycle wasn't due for five more days — but to take additional samples, which Section 9.1 authorized, which gave her a reason to log the descent that would survive review.

Station seven: the deposit had regrown. Not to its pre-scraping thickness, but measurably — eleven millimeters of new material over the scraped surface in six days. She'd recorded regrowth rates before. Normal was four to six millimeters per cycle, eleven days. This was nearly double the rate, and the new growth was different. Smoother. More uniform. The channeling was already present, visible as a faint tracery under the surface, like veins under pale skin.

She scraped a small section and the vibration came through the tool and her teeth rang and she felt it this time in her sternum, a buzzing behind the bone, as if something inside her chest were answering. She dropped the scraper. It swung on its pneumatic line, clanking against the bore wall, and where it struck she saw the surface ripple.

Gwen climbed out of the bore. She sat in the maintenance shed with her helmet off and her gloves off and pressed both hands flat on the workbench and breathed. The air in the shed was dry and cold after the bore humidity, and it tasted clean, and her teeth still ached, all of them now, a generalized hum that was almost subsonic, that she could feel more than hear.

She opened the contract on her datapad. Article 14, Section 4. *Operations shall continue per the established maintenance schedule until authorization is received or denied.*

She opened the power consumption report. 458 kilowatts. Declining.

She opened the spectrometer software and ran the new samples. UNK-1 concentrations had increased by forty percent in the regrowth material. The software flagged a second unknown compound, UNK-2, molecular weight 12,400 daltons. A protein. She was sure enough of that to feel the knowledge settle in her stomach like something swallowed cold. Twelve thousand daltons was a small protein, but it was unambiguously a protein, and proteins didn't form in calcium silicate deposits through any process she'd ever read about or could imagine.

She added the data to her uplink message and changed the priority from Moderate to Urgent. She wrote: *Recommend stop-work pending laboratory analysis of deposit samples. Regrowth rate anomalous. Organic compound profile inconsistent with mineral deposition.* She read it back and it sounded measured, reasonable, like something a competent engineer would write, and it did not convey the thing she'd felt in the bore when the surface rippled under the scraper's impact — the recognition, animal and total, that she was inside something, that the wellbore walls were closing around her not through mechanical failure but through growth, through intent, through a patience that operated on a timescale she couldn't outrun.

She saved the message. Three days to uplink. Fourteen days minimum for a response. The next maintenance cycle was in five days.

Gwen sat at the workbench and looked at her hands. Under the fluorescent lights, the skin on her palms was normal — calloused, dry, a small cut on her left index finger from a cable splice last week, already healing. She turned her hands over and looked at the backs. Normal. She pressed her tongue against her molars and felt the roughness there, more pronounced than yesterday, a texture likeite deposit on enamel, and she thought about the scraper and the way the blade had sunk into the deposit at station seven, two millimeters past its usual depth, and she thought about the scale on her teeth, and she thought about the word that connected them, and she closed her hands into fists and opened them again and picked up her datapad and opened the maintenance schedule for cycle 20 and began reviewing the checklist.

Tool inventory. Blade inspection. Pneumatic line pressure test. Reagent strip supply. Sample vials. Harness check. Rebreather cartridge replacement.

Five days.
