The study smelled of dust and ink, a scent Elara had come to associate with the weight of history. She sat at her desk, the dim glow of a single lamp casting long shadows over the brittle pages of the codex. The script was unlike anything she had encountered before—serpentine curves that spiraled into impossible knots, symbols that seemed to defy the rules of syntax. She traced a finger over the faded vellum, her breath shallow. The language was dead, or so she had believed. But the words, when she finally deciphered them, whispered of a future she could not yet name.  

The project had begun as an academic curiosity. The codex, discovered in a sealed chamber beneath a temple in the Andes, was cataloged as a relic of the Xel’kai, a civilization that had vanished centuries ago. Its existence was known only through fragmented accounts and the occasional artifact, but the codex was different. It was complete, its pages bound in a material that resisted decay. Elara had been assigned to translate it, a task she accepted with the reverence of a pilgrim. She had spent months isolating phonemes, mapping the structure of the language, and cross-referencing it with known linguistic roots. The process was slow, deliberate, and exhausting. Yet she felt a strange certainty that the text held something vital, something that had been waiting to be unmade.  

Now, as she pored over the latest section, her hands trembled. The passage before her described a city of glass and steel, its towers “shattered by the sky’s rage.” The words were stark, almost clinical, but the imagery was vivid. She read them again, then a third time, her pulse quickening. The description was too precise, too detailed. She had no records of such a place, no references in the Xel’kai archives. Yet the text seemed to anticipate it, as if the author had not written about a past event but a coming one.  

Elara leaned back, her chair creaking. The lamp’s flame flickered, casting a trembling light over the page. She reached for her notebook, its margins cluttered with notes and sketches. The Xel’kai had no concept of linear time, she reminded herself. Their chronology was cyclical, their myths layered with repetition. Perhaps this was another iteration of a known catastrophe, a myth reimagined. But the phrasing—“the day of the shattered sky”—felt too specific, too immediate.  

She glanced at the clock. 2:17 a.m. The house was silent, save for the hum of the refrigerator in the next room. She closed her eyes, willing herself to think clearly. The codex had been carbon-dated to the 14th century, but that meant nothing. What if the Xel’kai had possessed a different understanding of time? What if their language was not bound by the linear progression of history? The idea was absurd, but so were the words on the page.  

Her phone buzzed on the desk. A message from Dr. Moreau, her colleague and, she suspected, her only true ally in the department. “You still up?” it read. She hesitated, then typed back, “Yes. Need to talk.”  

Moreau arrived within twenty minutes, his usual disheveled appearance magnified by the late hour. He carried a mug of tea, the steam curling into the cold air. “What’s wrong?” he asked, peering over her shoulder.  

Elara hesitated. She had no proof, only a growing unease. “I think I’ve found something… strange,” she said.  

He frowned. “Strange how?”  

She turned the codex toward him. “This passage. It describes a city destroyed by a ‘shattered sky.’ I don’t know what that means, but the details are too precise. It’s not a myth. It’s a record.”  

Moreau scanned the text, his brow furrowing. “You’re assuming the Xel’kai had a concept of the future. They didn’t. Their myths were about cycles, not progression.”  

“But what if they did?” she pressed. “What if this isn’t a myth at all? What if this is a warning?”  

He sat down, the chair creaking. “Elara, you’re overreaching. The codex is old, but that doesn’t mean it’s prophetic. You’re reading meaning into symbols that might not exist.”  

She exhaled sharply. “I’ve cross-referenced the lexicon. The terms are consistent. The structure of the sentence—it’s not poetic, it’s descriptive. It’s like a report.”  

“Reports of what? A disaster that never happened?”  

“Or one that hasn’t yet.”  

Moreau’s gaze sharpened. “You’re suggesting the Xel’kai predicted the future?”  

“It’s not unthinkable. Some ancient cultures had remarkably accurate astronomical calculations. Maybe they had a way of tracking events beyond their time.”  

He shook his head. “You’re grasping at straws. The codex is a relic, not a prophecy. You’re letting your obsession with the Xel’kai cloud your judgment.”  

Elara’s hands curled into fists. “I’m not obsessed. I’m trying to understand what’s in front of me.”  

Moreau leaned forward, his voice lowering. “You know what happens to people who claim ancient texts predict the future? They get dismissed. They lose credibility. You’ve worked too hard to throw it all away on a fantasy.”  

She wanted to argue, to demand that he see the evidence, but the words caught in her throat. Moreau was right, in a way. The implications were staggering. If the codex truly described a future event, what did that mean for the Xel’kai? For history itself? And if the text was accurate, what did it mean for her?  

She looked back at the page, the symbols blurring in the lamplight. The city of glass and steel. The shattered sky. The words were there, waiting. She had spent her life translating dead languages, giving voice to the silent. But this was different. This was a message, not from the past, but from a future that had not yet arrived.  

“Maybe I’m wrong,” she said finally, her voice quiet.  

Moreau studied her, then sighed. “Then let’s find out. Let’s look for other references. Maybe the text mentions a date, or a place. Something we can verify.”  

She nodded, but the doubt had already taken root. She returned to the codex, her fingers tracing the script with renewed urgency. Hours passed, the hours melting into one another. She cross-referenced the symbols with the Xel’kai calendar, which was based on celestial cycles. The codex’s dates were enigmatic, but she found a recurring pattern—a sequence of glyphs that aligned with a specific astronomical event: the alignment of three planets, an occurrence that would not happen for another two centuries.  

Her breath caught. The text described the event in detail, not as a prediction but as a memory. The Xel’kai had not foreseen it; they had lived it. Or rather, they had lived it in a time that was not their own.  

A chill ran down her spine. The implications were too vast, too impossible. She thought of the colleagues who had dismissed her work, the skeptics who had called her theories “medieval nonsense.” What if they were wrong? What if the Xel’kai had access to a timeline that defied their own?  

She flipped through the codex, her eyes scanning for other anomalies. There was a section describing a “great silence,” a time when the world fell mute. Another depicted a “shadow that swallowed the sun.” The descriptions were too precise, too vivid. She began to see a pattern—not of events, but of warnings. The Xel’kai had not merely recorded history; they had documented a future that had already occurred.  

A sudden noise shattered the silence. The front door creaked. Elara froze, her heart hammering. She listened, but the house was still. It was only the wind, she told herself. Or her imagination.  

She returned to the codex, her mind racing. If the Xel’kai had knowledge of the future, how had they acquired it? Had they discovered a way to transcend time, or had they been visited by something beyond their understanding? The questions spiraled, each one more unsettling than the last.  

Her phone buzzed again. Another message from Moreau: “Did you find anything?”  

She hesitated, then typed, “I think I need to take a break.”  

The words felt like a lie. She couldn’t stop now. The codex had become a mirror, reflecting something she could not yet name. She thought of the “shattered sky,” of the city of glass, of the “great silence.” What if the text was not a warning, but a record of a collapse? What if the Xel’kai had not vanished, but had been consumed by the very future they had tried to document?  

She closed her eyes, the weight of the text pressing against her chest. For the first time in her career, she felt the limits of her knowledge, the chasm between what she understood and what she was beginning to suspect. The codex was not just a relic; it was a cipher, a key to a mystery that had no beginning and no end.  

And she was the only one who could unlock it.
