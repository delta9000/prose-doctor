The ink dried too quickly in the Istanbul sun, a fact that made Ali’s hands tremble as he pressed the reed pen to the parchment. The script, a cascade of curves and loops, was nearly complete—*“Allah akbar”*—but the final stroke, the *alif*, had to be perfect. He dipped the pen again, the tip splitting slightly, and sighed. The old man who had taught him, Hafız, had always said the reed was a living thing, its fibers as delicate as a woman’s pulse. Ali had never believed that until now. He traced the *alif* with his thumb, feeling the raised ink, and wondered if the new alphabet would ever hold such weight.  

The workshop smelled of aged paper and myrrh, the same as it had since he’d inherited it from his father. The shelves were lined with scrolls, some from the Ottoman era, others from the caliphates before that. Ali’s fingers brushed one, its edges frayed, the script a tangled web of *nasta‘liq* that only the most patient eyes could parse. He had spent years mastering it, the way the *ba’* curved like a crescent moon, the *ta’* rising like a mountain. Now, the government had declared it obsolete. The Latin alphabet, they said, would bring progress. Ali had heard the speeches in the square, the speeches that made his blood turn to ice.  

A knock at the door. Ali set down his pen and wiped his hands on his apron. The visitor was a young man, his face angular, his suit crisp. Mehmet, his friend since childhood, though they had not spoken in months.  

“You’re still here,” Mehmet said, stepping inside. His eyes swept the room, lingering on the scrolls. “They say the *tahrir* is coming next month. You’ll have to stop this nonsense.”  

Ali stiffened. “It’s not nonsense. It’s *taqaddum*.”  

Mehmet laughed, a low sound. “Tradition is a shackle, Ali. The Latin alphabet will open doors. You’ll see.”  

“I’ve seen enough,” Ali said. “You think the *tashkeel* is complicated? Try reading a *fihrist* in Latin. You’ll be lost.”  

Mehmet’s jaw tightened. “This isn’t about *fihrist*. It’s about *tahsil*. Education. The people need to read, to write, to *think* in a way that’s modern. The old script is a relic.”  

Ali’s hands curled into fists. “And what happens when the *süleymaniye* becomes a museum? When our children forget the *süznâm*? You think the West will respect us if we erase our past?”  

Mehmet shook his head. “You’re clinging to a dead world. The Ottoman Empire is gone. The caliphate is gone. You can’t paint the Bosphorus with the same brush that painted the Crusades.”  

Ali turned away, staring at the window where the water shimmered. The Bosphorus had always been a mirror, reflecting the city’s soul. Now it felt like a wound. He thought of his wife, Zeynep, who had loved the old script as much as he did. She’d once written a poem for him, her *naskh* so precise it looked like a star chart. She’d died two years ago, of typhoid, and the last thing she’d asked for was a poem in the new alphabet. *“Not yet,”* she’d said. *“Wait until the dust settles.”*  

Mehmet’s voice cut through his thoughts. “You’re wasting your time. The government’s not coming to negotiate. They’ll send the police if you don’t comply.”  

Ali faced him, his voice quiet. “Then they’ll have to take this workshop by force.”  

Mehmet exhaled, defeated. “You’re a fool, Ali.”  

“And you’re a coward,” Ali said. “You don’t fear the future. You fear the past.”  

Mehmet left without another word. The door closed with a finality that made Ali’s chest ache. He returned to his work, but the ink had dried completely now, the *alif* unfinished. He stared at it, the curve of the stroke jagged, and realized he couldn’t fix it. The script was broken, like the world outside.  

That night, Ali went to the cemetery. The stones were cool beneath his fingers as he traced the name carved into the headstone: *Zeynep Qamar, 1895–1923*. The date of her death felt like a scar. He had buried her in a shroud of *khaki*—the color of the new army, the color of the future. She’d hated that. *“You’ll forget us,”* she’d whispered as the earth fell on her. *“You’ll write in a language that doesn’t remember us.”*  

He sat on the cold ground, the wind tugging at his coat. The moon was full, casting silver over the graves, and for a moment, he felt her presence. He pulled out a small notebook, the one she’d used for her poems. The pages were yellowed, the ink faded, but the words were still there:  

*“The calligrapher’s hand is a bridge between the divine and the mortal.  
Each stroke is a prayer.  
Each curve, a hymn.  
But what happens when the bridge is torn down?”*  

Ali’s eyes burned. He had written that poem for her, years ago, when the first rumors of the alphabet reform had surfaced. She had read it and wept. *“You’re right,”* she’d said. *“It’s not just ink. It’s us.”*  

He closed the notebook and pressed it to his chest. The wind howled, and he thought of the city beyond the cemetery, of the streets where the new alphabet was being painted on signs, of the children who would grow up never learning the *hijaiye*. He thought of Mehmet, of his certainty, and of the way the world had moved on without them.  

A bird called in the distance, a lone cry that echoed through the tombs. Ali stood, brushing the dust from his knees, and looked toward the city. The minarets of the Süleymaniye stood like sentinels, their shadows stretching across the Bosphorus. He wondered if the new script would ever hold the same weight as the old, if the Latin letters would carry the same sacred geometry. He doubted it.  

He walked back through the cemetery, the stones around him whispering their names. Zeynep’s voice was in the wind, a soft *“No, Ali, not yet.”* He clutched the notebook tighter, feeling the weight of it, the weight of all that had been lost.  

When he reached his workshop, he lit a lamp and unrolled a fresh sheet of parchment. The ink was still in the pot, the reed pen ready. He dipped it, the tip splitting again, and began to write. The *alif* came first, slow and deliberate, each stroke a defiance. The *ba’* followed, its curve a promise. He worked until his hands ached, the room filled with the sound of brush against paper, the scent of ink and memory.  

By dawn, the page was complete. A hymn, a prayer, a rebellion. He folded it carefully, tucking it into the notebook beside Zeynep’s poems. Then he sat by the window, watching the Bosphorus as the first light of day painted the water gold.  

The world was changing. He could feel it in the air, in the way the city’s rhythm had shifted. But here, in this room, the old script still lived. And as long as it did, so would the memory of those who had written it.  

Ali closed his eyes, the weight of the night’s work settling in his bones. He thought of Zeynep, of Mehmet, of the city that had both embraced and abandoned him. And he wondered, not for the first time, if he was fighting a battle that could never be won.  

But then he opened his eyes, and the Bosphorus was still there, reflecting the sky, the city, the past. And he knew, with a certainty that made his heart swell, that some things could never be erased.  

Even if the world tried.
