The house stood at the edge of the town like a wound that had never quite closed. Eleanor had never seen it before, but she knew its reputation. The letter from her aunt’s lawyer had been brief, almost apologetic: *“The property is yours now. Consider it a gift, though I suspect you’ll find it... unwelcome.”* She had packed her suitcase that evening, leaving behind the city’s hum and the scent of coffee and exhaust. The drive south had been long, the highway flanked by pine trees that seemed to lean inward, as though listening.  

The house was smaller than she expected, its brick façade the color of rusted iron. The roof sagged in the middle, and the front porch creaked like a breath held too long. Eleanor unlocked the door with a key that felt heavier than it should, the metal cold against her palm. Inside, the air was thick with dust and the musty tang of old wood. A chandelier hung crookedly, its crystals dull. The floorboards groaned as she stepped over the threshold, the sound echoing into the dark.  

She had only been here once before, as a child, when her mother had taken her to a funeral. The man buried had been a cousin, someone Eleanor had never met, but the house had felt alive then, too—its windows blinking like tired eyes. Now, it seemed to exhale, a slow, rancid breath that carried the weight of unspeakable things.  

The first neighbor arrived an hour later. Mrs. Delmar, a woman with a face like weathered leather, stood on the porch, her hands clasped around a basket of canned peaches. “You’re the new owner,” she said, not as a question but a statement. Eleanor nodded, unsure how to respond. The woman’s gaze flicked to the house, then back to Eleanor. “You sure you want to be here?”  

“I think I’ve already made that decision,” Eleanor said, forcing a smile.  

Mrs. Delmar’s lips pressed into a thin line. “The house has a history. Not all of it’s... comfortable.” She set the basket on the step. “These are for you. Peach preserves. You’ll need something to keep you going.”  

“Thank you,” Eleanor said, though she didn’t reach for the basket.  

The woman hesitated, then turned away, her shoes crunching on the gravel. Eleanor watched her go, the suspicion in Mrs. Delmar’s eyes lingering like a shadow.  

By sunset, the house had begun to whisper. Eleanor moved through the rooms, her fingers trailing over the peeling wallpaper, the floorboards warped by time. The kitchen was empty except for a rusted stove and a shelf of jars whose labels had long since dissolved. The living room held a couch swallowed by dust, its fabric the color of dried blood. In the bedroom, a canopy bed stood at the foot of the wall, its curtains tattered.  

It was in the attic that she found the locked room. The stairs creaked as she climbed, the air growing colder with each step. The attic was a maze of boxes and debris, the ceiling beams sagging like the ribs of a dead animal. At the far end, a door stood out—solid, unyielding, its surface scarred with deep gouges. A brass knob, tarnished and stiff, jutted from the wood.  

Eleanor tried the handle. It didn’t budge. She pressed her ear to the door, but the silence beyond was absolute. Yet, as she stood there, she thought she heard something—a faint scratching, like nails on wood, or perhaps a voice muffled by distance. She pulled away, heart hammering.  

The next day, the town’s whispers followed her. At the general store, the clerk avoided her gaze. At the post office, a woman with a baby in her arms turned her back as Eleanor approached. Even the gas station attendant, a young man with a sunburned face, muttered, “You shouldn’t be there,” when she asked for a bottle of water.  

That night, Eleanor returned to the attic. She brought a flashlight and a crowbar, her hands trembling as she pried the door open. The room beyond was smaller than she’d imagined, its walls lined with shelves stacked with old books and trinkets. A desk sat in the corner, its surface covered in dust. But the most unsettling thing was the window—narrow, barred, and facing the backyard.  

As she stepped inside, a chill swept through her, though the air was still. The flashlight beam caught the edges of a mirror on the far wall, its surface cracked. In the reflection, something moved. Eleanor spun around, but the room was empty.  

She heard it then—a low, rhythmic tapping, like a finger on glass. It came from the window. She approached, her breath shallow. The tapping stopped. A moment later, it began again, slower now, as though someone were counting.  

Eleanor reached for the window, her fingers brushing the cold metal bars. The tapping ceased. She waited, heart pounding, but nothing else came.  

The next morning, she found a note tucked under her car’s windshield wiper. The handwriting was jagged, the words scrawled in red ink: *“Leave it alone.”*  

The neighbors’ visits became more frequent. Mrs. Delmar returned, this time with a man Eleanor didn’t recognize, his face gaunt and shadowed. “You shouldn’t be there,” he said, his voice a rasp. “That room’s not meant for living.”  

“What’s in there?” Eleanor asked.  

The man didn’t answer. He turned and walked away, his steps crunching on the gravel.  

That evening, she returned to the room. She brought a candle, its wax pooling on the desk. The tapping returned, louder this time, accompanied by a faint hum. It was not a sound she could place, but it vibrated in her bones. She stepped closer to the window, and the hum deepened, as though the room itself were breathing.  

Then, a voice.  

It was faint, almost lost in the hum, but unmistakable. A child’s voice, whispering something in a language she didn’t understand. The candle flickered, and for a moment, the room seemed to shift—walls bending, shadows stretching. Eleanor stumbled back, her knees hitting the floor. The voice faded, leaving only the silence of the house.  

The following days were a blur of sleepless nights and fragmented dreams. Eleanor began to see things: a figure in the attic window, a hand brushing her shoulder when no one was there, the scent of lavender and decay in the air. The townspeople’s warnings grew more explicit. At the grocery store, a woman muttered, “She’s not gone,” as Eleanor passed.  

One afternoon, she found a photograph in the attic—a black-and-white image of the house, its windows dark, the yard overgrown. In the center of the photo stood a woman, her face obscured by a veil. Eleanor’s breath caught. The woman’s hand was raised, as though pointing to the locked room.  

That night, the tapping returned, but this time it was accompanied by a sound she had never heard before—a low, keening wail, like a bird in distress. She climbed the attic stairs, her flashlight trembling in her hand. The door to the room was ajar, as if someone had opened it.  

Inside, the room was different. The shelves were gone, the desk replaced by a wooden crate. The mirror on the wall was now clear, its surface reflecting not the room, but a scene outside—Eleanor’s own face, staring back at her.  

The wail grew louder, and the air thickened. She turned, and there, in the corner, stood a child. Small, with hollow eyes and a dress too large for her body. The child raised a hand, pointing at the crate.  

Eleanor backed away, but the door slammed shut. The wail became a scream, and the walls began to crack, splinters of wood flying into the air. The child’s eyes locked onto hers, and in that moment, she understood.  

The room had been a prison.  

The child was not a ghost, but a memory, a fragment of the woman in the photograph, trapped in the house’s bones. The town had known. They had all known.  

The scream died, and the room fell silent. Eleanor’s flashlight dimmed, its light fading as she stumbled down the stairs. The house seemed to exhale, its walls settling into stillness.  

In the days that followed, the town’s whispers faded. The neighbors avoided her, their faces etched with the same fear she now carried. Eleanor sold the house, the money disappearing into accounts she never touched. She left the town behind, the road stretching behind her like a wound.  

But sometimes, in the quiet of the night, she would hear the tapping. A faint, rhythmic sound, coming from somewhere she could not see.  

And she would remember the child, her hollow eyes, and the whispered truth that the house had never let go.
