The air in New Luma tasted like static. Amina inhaled shallowly, fingers curled around the frayed strap of her duffel bag as she stepped off the transit pod onto the platform. The city stretched before her, a jagged skyline of glass and steel, its towers leaning like tired dancers beneath the weight of the sun. Above, a network of elevated walkways hummed with drones, their cargo pods blinking like fireflies. Below, the streets pulsed with a different kind of life—people moving in tight, purposeful clusters, their faces obscured by masks or the perpetual haze of smog. 

She had arrived two days earlier, transferred from the temporary holding center in the delta, where the air had been thick with the brine of flooded fields and the stench of diesel. Now, the air here was different. It was cleaner, but colder, as if the city itself had learned to hold its breath. Amina’s boots clicked against the polished concrete as she followed the flow of bodies toward the registration office, a squat building of gray metal and reinforced glass. Its entrance was flanked by two figures in blue uniforms, their visors reflecting the neon glow of the city. 

The queue was long. Amina joined it, her feet aching in the borrowed shoes her case worker had given her. The shoes were too big, the soles worn thin, and each step felt like a betrayal of the ground beneath her. Around her, others murmured in a dozen languages, their voices blending into a low, metallic drone. A woman in a tattered shawl clutched a child to her chest, the child’s breath fogging the air. A man in a business suit, his tie askew, argued with a clerk whose face was hidden behind a mask. 

When Amina finally reached the counter, the clerk—another faceless figure in blue—scanned her wristband, then frowned. “You’re registered under a different name,” the clerk said, voice flat. 

“It’s the same,” Amina said, her accent thick. “Amina Karim. I was told to use this.” She held up the plastic card issued at the holding center, its edges frayed. 

The clerk’s fingers flew over the keyboard. “No match. You’ll need to reapply.” 

Amina’s stomach clenched. She had been told this would take days, maybe weeks. But the holding center had been overcrowded, its cots inches apart, its showers rationed. She had begged to be moved, and the case worker had said, *“We’ll get you out. Just hold on.”* Now, the clerk’s words felt like a noose. 

“I have a child,” she said, her voice trembling. “My daughter. She’s waiting for me.” 

The clerk didn’t look up. “All applicants are processed in order. You’ll be notified when your file is complete.” 

Amina turned away, her hands shaking. The sun was slipping lower, casting the city in a sickly orange hue. She wandered the streets, her duffel bag dragging behind her, until she found a bench beneath a flickering streetlamp. The seat was cold, and the air smelled of exhaust and something sweet, like overripe fruit. She pulled out a ration bar from her bag, its wrapper crinkling as she unwrapped it. It tasted like cardboard and regret. 

The next day, she returned to the registration office, only to be told her file was still “in review.” The clerk’s eyes lingered on the scars on her wrists—remnants of the levee collapse that had drowned her village. Amina kept her head down, her fingers brushing the scar on her thumb, the one she’d gotten when she’d tried to pry open a roof to save her daughter’s doll. 

By the third day, she had learned the rules. The city’s social hierarchy was etched into every interaction. The privileged lived in the upper tiers, where the air was filtered and the streets were wide and clean. The displaced—refugees, migrants, those deemed “unqualified”—were funneled into the lower districts, where the buildings leaned precariously and the walls were etched with the names of those who had died in the last flood. Here, jobs were scarce, and survival hinged on a system of points: credits earned through labor, or stolen through desperation. 

Amina’s days became a blur of applications and rejections. She attended a language class in a repurposed warehouse, where the instructor, a woman with a voice like gravel, drilled them on the city’s official dialect. “You must speak clearly,” she said. “The system does not tolerate mistakes.” Amina practiced in the mirror, her tongue stumbling over the clipped syllables. 

One evening, she found herself in the medical clinic, seeking treatment for a fever that had plagued her since arrival. The waiting room was a cavern of plastic chairs, the air thick with the scent of antiseptic and fear. A man in a tattered coat sat beside her, his face gaunt, his hands wrapped in a scarf. He didn’t speak, but when Amina’s eyes fell on his hands, she noticed the same scar on his wrist—a jagged line, pale against his skin. 

“They gave us these,” he said, his voice a whisper. “To track us.” 

Amina nodded, her throat tight. She had been given a wristband too, a sleek strip of plastic that pulsed with a faint blue light. It was supposed to register her movements, her presence, her compliance. 

The man’s eyes flicked to her bag. “You carry your past with you,” he said. 

“I don’t have a past,” she replied. 

He smiled, a thin, tired thing. “Everyone does.” 

When her name was called, she stood, clutching her bag. The nurse, a woman with a face like weathered stone, examined her with a scanner. “High fever,” she said. “You’ll need to stay overnight.” 

Amina’s heart sank. She didn’t have the credits for a hotel, and the shelters were overcrowded. But the nurse didn’t offer a choice. 

That night, she lay on a narrow cot, the sheets scratchy and damp. The room was silent except for the hum of machines and the occasional groan of another patient. She stared at the ceiling, trying not to think of her daughter, Kala, who was now in a foster home in the delta. She had been told Kala was safe, but the words felt hollow. 

The next morning, the nurse handed her a vial of medicine. “Take this,” she said. “It will help.” 

Amina took it, but as she left the clinic, she noticed a small bundle on the counter—a sandwich, a bottle of water, and a folded note. The note read: *“Keep going. The city is not done with you yet.”* 

She stared at it, her eyes burning. Who had left it? The nurse? A fellow patient? She didn’t know, but the words felt like a thread, pulling her forward. 

That afternoon, she returned to the registration office, determined. The line was shorter, but the clerk was the same. “Your file is still pending,” the clerk said. 

“I need a job,” Amina said. “Any job.” 

The clerk blinked. “You must have skills.” 

“I can clean. I can cook. I can work long hours.” 

The clerk hesitated, then tapped the keyboard. “There is a position at the waste processing facility. It pays minimum credits, but it’s available.” 

Amina nodded. “I’ll take it.” 

The clerk looked at her, then at the scar on her wrist. “You’ll need a permit.” 

“I don’t have one.” 

“The permit is part of the application process.” 

Amina’s stomach churned. She had read about the permits—documents that required proof of residency, employment history, and sometimes, a bribe. She didn’t have any of it. 

But as she turned to leave, she saw the man from the waiting room, standing near the entrance. He held a small, silver device in his hand, a scanner. 

“You need this,” he said, handing it to her. “It’s a temporary permit. It will get you through the system.” 

Amina stared at him. “Why?” 

He shrugged. “Because the city is full of people who forget where they came from. But sometimes, someone remembers.” 

She clutched the device, its surface warm from his hand. “Thank you.” 

He nodded, then disappeared into the crowd. 

The next day, Amina reported to the waste processing facility, a sprawling complex of steel and concrete, its air thick with the smell of decay. The workers were a mix of displaced people and locals, their faces etched with exhaustion. She was given a uniform, a mask, and a set of gloves. The work was grueling—sorting through mountains of refuse, separating recyclables from hazardous waste. Her hands blistered, her back ached, but she didn’t stop. 

At the end of the shift, she received her first payment: a credit chip, its surface dull and unremarkable. She clutched it, feeling the weight of it in her palm. It was barely enough to buy a meal, but it was something. 

That evening, she returned to the bench beneath the streetlamp, the chip tucked into her pocket. She opened the sandwich from the nurse, the bread stale but the cheese warm. She ate slowly, savoring each bite. 

As she finished, a figure approached—a woman in a sleek, navy-blue coat, her face hidden beneath a hood. She sat beside Amina, her presence quiet. 

“You’re still here,” the woman said. 

Amina looked up. “I have no other place to go.” 

The woman tilted her head. “You have a choice. You always do.” 

Amina frowned. “What do you mean?” 

The woman reached into her coat and pulled out a small device, similar to the one the stranger had given her. “This is a transfer chip. It can move your credits, your data, your status. It can help you get a better job, a safer place to live.” 

Amina’s heart raced. “Why are you giving this to me?” 

The woman’s voice was soft. “Because I was once like you. I didn’t have a choice. But then I found someone who believed in me. Now, I pass that belief on.” 

Amina’s eyes filled with tears. She had spent so long feeling invisible, like the city itself was pushing her away. But here, in this moment, she felt seen. 

The woman stood, her coat rustling. “Use it wisely. And don’t forget where you came from.” 

Amina watched her disappear into the crowd, the chip still in her hand. She didn’t know what the future held, but for the first time, she felt a flicker of hope. The city was harsh, its systems cold, its people distant. But somewhere in its labyrinth of steel and shadows, there were still threads of kindness, waiting to be found. 

And Amina, for the first time in a long while, was ready to follow them.
