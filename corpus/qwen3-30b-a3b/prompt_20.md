The oven hums like a sleeping beast, its heat radiating through the counter where Mira kneads dough. The air smells of yeast and caramelized onions, of thyme and burnt sugar. Her hands move with the precision of a woman who has done this for thirty years, though she’s only thirty-four. The shop is small, its windows fogged with steam, its walls lined with shelves of jars—dried lavender, cinnamon sticks, saffron threads. A bell jingles as the door opens, and Mira glances up, her brow furrowing. The customer is someone she shouldn’t recognize.  

She doesn’t look up again. Instead, she presses the dough into a round, smooth as a stone, and tucks it into a cloth-lined basket. The customer’s footsteps slow as they approach the counter, their presence a ripple in the quiet. Mira’s fingers curl around the edge of the counter, her pulse a steady thrum beneath her skin. She knows that walk, that hunched posture, the way the customer’s shadow stretches long across the tiled floor.  

“You’re late,” the customer says, their voice thin, like a blade dragged across glass.  

Mira’s breath catches. She forces a smile, the kind that doesn’t reach her eyes. “How can I help you?”  

The customer hesitates, their gaze flicking to the loaves cooling on the rack. “I’d like a sourdough. The one with the rye.”  

Mira nods, though her mind is already elsewhere. The bread is a secret, a thing she’s never spoken of, not even to her mother, who once told her, *“Some things are better left in the oven.”* The dough is fed with a starter she’s nurtured for years, a slurry of flour and water that bubbles with something older than her. When she bakes, the bread remembers. Not in the way a person might, but in a way that makes the eater weep, or laugh, or whisper names they haven’t said in decades.  

She slices a loaf, the knife glinting in the amber light. The crust is crackled, the crumb golden and open. She places it on a wooden board, the scent of it rising like a ghost. The customer’s eyes are on the bread, but their hands are trembling.  

“You’ve been avoiding me,” the customer says.  

Mira’s throat tightens. She steps back, her boots scraping against the floor. “I don’t know what you’re talking about.”  

The customer leans forward, their breath fogging the counter. “You remember. You always did.”  

The bell jingles again, and Mira flinches. A new customer enters, a woman with a child in tow, but Mira doesn’t look up. She focuses on the bread, on the way the steam curls from the cut side, on the faint crackle of the oven behind her. The customer’s presence is a weight, a thing that presses against her ribs.  

“I didn’t think you’d come back,” the customer says.  

Mira’s hands freeze. The bread is still warm, still alive. She wants to ask how they know, but the words lodge in her throat. Instead, she offers the loaf. “Here.”  

The customer takes it, their fingers brushing hers. The touch is electric, a spark that travels up her arm. They slice a piece, their knife steady, and take a bite. Their eyes widen.  

Mira watches as color drains from their face. Their jaw tightens, their breath quickens. The bread is silent, but the customer isn’t. A sound escapes them, a low, broken moan. They clutch the counter, their knuckles white.  

“You,” they whisper, their voice cracking. “You were there.”  

Mira’s heart stutters. The customer’s eyes are open, but they’re not seeing the shop. They’re seeing something else, something buried. The air in the shop grows thick, the scent of bread mingling with the metallic tang of memory.  

The customer stumbles back, their breath ragged. “I thought you were gone. I thought you’d gone.”  

Mira’s fingers twitch. She wants to reach for them, to pull them back from wherever they’ve gone, but she knows better. The bread doesn’t just reveal memories—it unravels them, stitches them back together in ways the eater can’t control.  

The customer drops the loaf, its crumb crumbling onto the floor. They sink to their knees, their hands pressed to their temples. “I didn’t mean to leave you,” they say, their voice barely audible. “I didn’t know what else to do.”  

Mira’s chest aches. She wants to explain, to say *I never stopped waiting*, but the words are trapped behind her teeth. She watches as the customer’s shoulders shake, as tears streak down their face. The shop feels smaller, the air heavier.  

Outside, the sun dips lower, casting long shadows across the counter. The other customers have left, their stories untold. Mira steps forward, her voice quiet. “You’re safe now.”  

The customer doesn’t respond. Their gaze is fixed on the bread, on the memory it has conjured. Mira knows what they see: a kitchen, the same one she’s in now, but younger. A different version of herself, stirring a pot of soup, her hair tied back, her face soft. The customer’s reflection in the window—older, worn, but familiar.  

“You were there,” the customer repeats, their voice a plea. “You always were.”  

Mira’s hands shake. She wants to deny it, to say *I don’t know what you’re talking about*, but the truth is written in the bread, in the way the dough rises, in the way the memories surface like bubbles in a pond. She thinks of the day she first baked the bread, of the woman who taught her, of the secret she carried in her bones.  

The customer stands, their face wet, their hands trembling. They reach for Mira, but stop short, their fingers brushing the air between them. “I’m sorry,” they say. “I never meant to hurt you.”  

Mira opens her mouth, but no words come. She watches as the customer turns, their steps uncertain, their figure dissolving into the afternoon light. The bell jingles again, and the shop is still.  

She sinks into a chair, her hands cradling her head. The bread on the counter is still warm, still alive. She wants to take it apart, to smear the crust with her fingers, to taste the memory it holds. But she can’t. Some things are better left in the oven.  

The oven hums, and Mira closes her eyes. She hears the customer’s voice, faint, like a whisper from another room. *You were there.* She isn’t sure if it’s a question or a statement, but it lingers in her chest, a knot of something she can’t untie.  

Outside, the sun dips below the horizon, painting the sky in hues of amber and violet. The shop is quiet, the only sound the crackle of the oven. Mira rises, her legs unsteady, and begins to clean the counter. The bread is still there, its crumb soft, its scent lingering.  

She doesn’t know if the customer will return. She doesn’t know if they’ll remember what they’ve seen, or if the memory will fade like a dream upon waking. But she knows this: the bread remembers. And so do the people who eat it.  

She kneads the dough again, the familiar rhythm grounding her. The shop is her home, her sanctuary, her secret. The world outside is full of stories, but here, in the glow of the oven, the past is always present.  

And Mira, she is the keeper of it all.
