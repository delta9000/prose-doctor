The airport terminal smells like disinfectant and burnt coffee. The correspondent’s boots echo against the tile, too loud in the hollow space. They haven’t worn shoes this clean in years. The soles are still scuffed from the last time they walked through a battlefield, but here, the floor is pristine, and the air is too still. They adjust their jacket, a worn leather thing that smells like dust and gunpowder, and step into the flow of travelers. Their name is on a screen somewhere, but they don’t look for it. They’ve stopped looking for things.  

A man in a suit bumps into them, apologizes in a language they don’t recognize. The correspondent nods, too tired to respond. Their eyes are raw from sleepless nights, their skin pale beneath the stubble. They’ve been away for two years, but the world has moved on without them. They can feel it in the way the sunlight hits their face—too bright, too warm, like a lie.  

The partner’s name is on the list of arrivals. The correspondent’s fingers curl into their palm. They remember the last time they saw them, standing in the doorway of an apartment that no longer exists. The partner had said something about the weather, about the city changing, about how the correspondent’s absence had left a hole. But they hadn’t meant it. Not really. They’d been too busy building a life without them.  

The taxi ride is silent. The driver hums a song the correspondent doesn’t recognize. They watch the city through the window, the buildings taller, the streets narrower. A billboard flashes a commercial for a product they’ve never heard of. The correspondent’s phone buzzes in their pocket, but they don’t check it. They don’t want to know.  

The apartment is the same, but it isn’t. The door is a different color now, the lock newer. They hesitate, then press the buzzer. The intercom crackles. A voice asks, “Yes?”  

“It’s me,” they say.  

There’s a pause. The correspondent hears the sound of a phone being set down, then the click of the door. They step inside. The hallway is narrower than they remember, the air stale. The partner’s voice comes from the living room.  

“You’re back.”  

The correspondent nods. They stand in the doorway, their bag slung over one shoulder. The partner is sitting on the couch, their legs crossed, a mug in their hands. They look the same—same dark hair, same sharp features—but different. There’s a ring on their finger, a thin band of silver. The correspondent’s throat tightens.  

“You didn’t have to come,” the partner says.  

“I know.”  

The room is quiet. The correspondent’s eyes drift to the walls, the furniture. A new sofa, a different rug. The books they used to read are gone, replaced by objects they don’t recognize. A plant wilts in a corner, forgotten. The partner watches them, their expression unreadable.  

“I got a job,” the partner says. “At the library. It’s… stable.”  

The correspondent nods. They don’t know what to say. They’ve spent so long in places where stability is a luxury, where the ground shifts beneath your feet. Here, everything is fixed, predictable. It feels alien.  

The partner stands, walks toward them. They stop a foot away, their eyes searching. The correspondent doesn’t move. They’re afraid of what might happen if they do.  

“You were gone for a long time,” the partner says.  

“I know.”  

The partner’s hand hovers, then drops. They turn away, heading toward the kitchen. “I’ll make tea.”  

The correspondent follows, but stops at the threshold. The kitchen is smaller, the counters cluttered with things they don’t remember. A coffee maker, a toaster, a bowl of fruit. The partner opens the fridge, pulls out a box of tea bags.  

“You still like chamomile?” they ask.  

The correspondent swallows. “Yes.”  

The partner nods, but their hands tremble slightly as they pour water into the kettle. The sound of it boiling fills the silence. The correspondent watches them, the way their shoulders are tense, the way they avoid eye contact. They’ve built a life here, but it’s not the same. It’s not *their* life.  

The partner sets the mug down in front of them. The steam rises, curling into the air. The correspondent picks it up, the heat seeping into their fingers. They take a sip, the taste familiar, but wrong. It’s not the same tea. Not the same hands that used to make it.  

“You should stay,” the partner says.  

The correspondent looks up. “I can’t.”  

The partner’s expression doesn’t change. “Why not?”  

They want to explain. They want to tell them about the nights in the field, the sound of gunfire, the faces of people they couldn’t save. They want to say that the world they left is still screaming, that they can’t just walk away from it. But the words catch in their throat.  

“I don’t belong here,” they say.  

The partner’s lips press into a thin line. “You did once.”  

The correspondent sets the mug down. The steam has stopped, the tea gone cold. They stand, their chair scraping against the floor. “I have to go.”  

The partner doesn’t stop them. They watch as the correspondent walks toward the door, their steps slow, heavy. The partner says nothing. The door clicks shut behind them.  

The street is quieter than they expected. The city hums, but it’s a different kind of noise—no explosions, no distant alarms. Just the sound of cars, the chatter of people, the soft clatter of a bicycle bell. The correspondent walks, their hands in their pockets, their mind a blank.  

They don’t know where they’re going. They don’t know where they belong. The war has shaped them, carved them into something unrecognizable. They’ve seen too much, too many faces, too many deaths. Here, everything is still. The air is clean, the sky clear. It’s beautiful, but it doesn’t feel like home.  

They pass a park, the trees thick with leaves. A child laughs, chasing a kite. The correspondent stops, watching them. The child’s laughter is loud, bright, full of life. They remember their own laughter, years ago, before the war. Before the partner. Before the world turned into something unrecognizable.  

A man sits on a bench, reading a newspaper. The headline is something about a new policy, something about stability. The correspondent doesn’t read it. They turn away, their steps taking them deeper into the city.  

The sun is low now, casting long shadows across the pavement. The correspondent’s boots click against the ground, each sound a reminder of the life they’ve left behind. They think of the partner, of the tea, of the silence in the apartment. They think of the war, of the places they’ve been, the people they’ve known.  

They don’t know if they’ll ever find their way back.  

They walk until the city fades into the distance, until the buildings blur into a haze. They find themselves at the edge of a river, the water reflecting the sky. It’s calm, peaceful. The correspondent sits on the embankment, their back against a tree. They pull their jacket tighter around them, the cold seeping into their bones.  

They think of the partner’s face, the way they’d looked when they opened the door. Not happy, not sad—just… different. The partner had moved on, built a life without them. The correspondent had been gone for two years, but it wasn’t enough time. Not for the partner. Not for the world.  

They close their eyes, the sound of the river filling their ears. They don’t know what to do next. They don’t know if they’ll ever belong anywhere again. The war has changed them, and the world has changed without them.  

They open their eyes. The sky is darkening, the first stars blinking to life. The correspondent stands, their legs stiff, their body aching. They turn away from the river, toward the city, toward the life they don’t know how to return to.  

They don’t know if they’ll ever find their way back. But they have to try.  

The correspondent walks, their steps steady, their heart heavy. The city stretches before them, a maze of light and shadow. They don’t know where they’re going, but they keep moving.  

The gap between the world they left and the one they returned to is vast, but they’ve crossed worse.  

They’ll find a way.
