The rain had been falling for three days straight, turning the city into a smudge of neon and shadow. I was nursing a bourbon in the back of a diner that smelled like motor oil and regret when the woman walked in. She was dressed for a funeral, all black and stiff, with a face that had seen too many sleepless nights. Her name was Clara Voss, and she said her boyfriend, Ethan Cole, had vanished two weeks ago. She’d come to me because the cops didn’t care, and her lawyer said she was “not a priority.” I didn’t ask why. I never do.  

The first stop was The Rusty Nail, a bar on 12th where Ethan had worked as a bartender before he disappeared. The owner, a man named Dutch, had a face like a weathered map and a habit of cleaning glasses with more vigor than necessity. He didn’t remember Ethan leaving. “He just… stopped coming in,” he said, wiping a glass so hard the condensation smeared. “No note, no goodbye. Just gone.”  

I asked about his habits. Dutch said Ethan was quiet, kept to himself, but he’d seen him arguing with a guy in a leather jacket a week before he vanished. “Felt like a bad vibe,” Dutch admitted. “Like someone was pressuring him.” I jotted that down, though it felt like a whisper in a storm.  

Next, I hit the records. The city’s police database was a labyrinth, but I had a contact in the missing persons unit, a woman named Margo who’d once slept with my brother. She gave me a file on Ethan Cole: 28, no priors, last seen at The Rusty Nail, last known address a one-bedroom in the Projects. No credit cards, no phone. Just a trail of empty spaces.  

I went to the address. The building was a rusted relic, its windows boarded up. The landlord, a toothless man named Ben, said Ethan had paid rent on time but hadn’t been seen in weeks. “He kept to himself,” Ben said, eyeing me like I was a cockroach. “But his neighbor, the one in 3B, might know something. She’s a nosy bitch, but she’s got eyes.”  

The neighbor was a woman named Lila, who’d been watching Ethan’s door like a hawk. She said he’d been acting strange, leaving notes on his fridge in a strange, looping script. “He’d write things like *They’re watching* and *I can’t trust anyone*,” she said, her voice trembling. “Then he started packing. He said he was leaving, but he never did.”  

I asked about the guy in the leather jacket. Lila shrugged. “Didn’t see him. But Ethan’s phone? It’s still in his apartment. Maybe you can check it?”  

The phone was a relic, a flip model from the early 2000s. I didn’t have the code, but I knew a guy who did. His name was Sal, a hacker with a penchant for LSD and conspiracy theories. He cracked the phone in under an hour, revealing a string of calls to a number that pinged from a payphone in a seedy part of town. The number was unlisted, but Sal said the location was a dead end—no records, no surveillance.  

I went to the payphone. It was in a tunnel beneath the old train tracks, a place where the city’s ghosts gathered. The only thing there was a single, bloodstained handprint on the glass. I didn’t know if it was Ethan’s or someone else’s, but it felt like a warning.  

Back at the office, I called Clara. She was waiting, her voice tight. “Did you find him?”  

“No,” I said. “But I think he’s hiding. Someone’s after him.”  

She hung up.  

The next lead came from a record I’d almost missed: a zoning violation at Ethan’s apartment. The building had been sold to a developer, and the new owner had a history of harassment. I dug deeper and found a name—Marcus Hale, a real estate tycoon with a reputation for forcing tenants out. His office was in a high-rise, all glass and arrogance.  

Hale wasn’t helpful. He said he’d never met Ethan and had no idea where he was. But when I mentioned the zoning violation, his smile froze. “That building’s been empty for months,” he said. “You’re wasting your time.”  

I left, but I wasn’t done. I went to the city’s planning department and found the original owner of the building—a man named Thomas Grady, who’d died in a car accident two years prior. His wife, Eleanor, was still living in the same house, a crumbling Victorian on the edge of town.  

She let me in, her eyes heavy with grief. “Thomas would’ve protected that building,” she said. “He didn’t want it torn down.”  

I asked about Ethan. She frowned. “He came by a few times. Said he was working on something. He’d leave late at night, always in a hurry.”  

“Did he mention anyone threatening him?”  

She hesitated. “He said he was being followed. But I thought it was his imagination.”  

I left, feeling like I was chasing a shadow.  

The dead end came when I returned to the apartment. The door was unlocked, and the place was ransacked. A notebook lay on the floor, its pages filled with Ethan’s looping script. One entry stood out: *They know. I can’t run anymore.*  

I didn’t know who “they” were, but I felt the weight of it in my chest.  

Back at the office, I called Clara again. This time, she answered. “You found something, didn’t you?”  

“I found a trail,” I said. “But it’s not leading to Ethan. It’s leading to someone else.”  

“What do you mean?”  

“I think Ethan’s not running from someone. He’s running *with* someone.”  

She laughed, but it was hollow. “That’s not possible. He wouldn’t—”  

“Wouldn’t what? Leave you? Betray you? I don’t know, Clara. But he’s not who you think he is.”  

She hung up.  

The personal code came when I realized the developer, Marcus Hale, had ties to a local mob outfit. I couldn’t go to the cops—too much corruption. But I couldn’t just walk away either. I had a rule: no violence, no breaking and entering. But this felt different.  

I followed Hale to a warehouse on the docks, where I saw a group of men unloading crates labeled *electronics*. I waited until nightfall and slipped inside, using a crowbar to jimmy the lock. The place was a front for something bigger—smuggling, maybe. But what I found was a file labeled *Ethan Cole*.  

Inside were photos of him, his face bruised, his hands tied. A note read: *He talks too much. Dispose of him.*  

I closed the file, my hands shaking. This wasn’t just about Ethan anymore. It was about a system that swallowed people whole.  

I called Clara again, my voice grim. “He’s alive. But he’s in trouble.”  

“Where is he?”  

“I don’t know. But if I go in, I might not come back.”  

She didn’t answer.  

I went to the warehouse the next night, armed with a knife and a gun I’d borrowed from a friend. The place was guarded, but I found a way in through the vents. I heard voices below—Hale, a man I didn’t recognize, and Ethan.  

“He’s not worth the trouble,” the stranger said. “We should end him now.”  

“I need him alive,” Hale said. “He knows too much.”  

I crept closer, my heart pounding. Ethan was chained to a chair, his face a mask of pain. “I didn’t mean to—” he started, but the stranger cut him off with a punch.  

I stepped into the light. “You’re coming with me.”  

Hale turned, his face a mix of shock and anger. “You shouldn’t have come here, Marlowe.”  

“I don’t care. Let him go.”  

Hale laughed. “You think you’re some kind of hero? You don’t understand what you’re messing with.”  

“I understand enough.”  

The fight was brutal. I took down two men, but Hale got a shot off, grazing my shoulder. I managed to free Ethan, but Hale fled.  

We ran, the docks behind us, the city’s lights blurring into streaks. Ethan stumbled, his breath ragged. “Why are you doing this?” he asked.  

“Because I have a code,” I said. “And I won’t let you die for something you didn’t do.”  

He didn’t respond.  

Back at the office, I cleaned the wound and called Clara. She came immediately, her face pale. “Is he okay?”  

“He’s alive,” I said. “But he’s not the man you thought he was.”  

She looked at Ethan, who was sitting in a chair, silent. “What happened?”  

“He was in over his head,” I said. “With people who don’t take kindly to being exposed.”  

Clara’s eyes filled with tears. “I just wanted him back.”  

“I know.”  

Ethan finally spoke. “I didn’t want to leave you. But I had no choice.”  

“You could’ve called,” she said. “You could’ve told me.”  

“I thought it would be easier this way.”  

The room was heavy with silence. I didn’t stay to hear the rest. I had a code, and I’d kept it. But as I walked out into the rain, I wondered if the cost was too high.  

Ethan didn’t want to be found. But sometimes, the truth isn’t a choice. It’s a burden. And I’d carried it again.
