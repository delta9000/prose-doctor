The air in the city stank of death. Elara had long stopped noticing the odor of rotting flesh and sour sweat, but the children of the district still clutched their noses as they passed her door. She stood in the dim light of her workshop, grinding dried willow bark into powder, her hands steady despite the tremor in her chest. The plague had taken the baker’s son two days prior, and the fever had crept into the next neighborhood. A wave of coughing echoed from the alley outside, followed by a woman’s wail. Elara set down her mortar and rubbed her temples. The herbs on her table—chamomile, feverfew, yarrow—had saved dozens, but the whispers had begun again. *Witch. Sorceress. Poisoner.*  

A knock at the door. Elara’s fingers brushed the vial of tincture she kept beneath her apron. The visitors usually came in droves, but the priest’s men had forbidden her from tending to the sick. She opened the door a crack.  

“It’s me, Mother Althea,” said the voice. A woman, hunched and gray, her face lined with worry. “The fever… it’s in my daughter. Please, I’ve brought a silver coin.”  

Elara stepped back, pulling the door open wider. Mother Althea’s daughter lay on a patchwork blanket in the corner, her skin mottled with purple blotches. The child’s breath was shallow, her eyes glassy. Elara knelt, pressing a hand to the girl’s forehead. It was hot, but not as hot as it could be. She had seen this before—fevers that broke, then returned stronger.  

“She’s not beyond help,” Elara said, though the words felt hollow. She reached for her satchel, pulling out a vial of thyme oil. “Sit her up. I’ll mix a poultice.”  

Mother Althea hesitated. “The priest said you’re not to be trusted.”  

Elara’s jaw tightened. “Then you’re a fool. Your daughter’s fever is from the plague, not magic.”  

The mother’s hands trembled as she lifted the child. Elara crushed a sprig of sage and mixed it with honey, then spread the paste on a cloth. She pressed it to the girl’s neck. “This will cool her. Drink this,” she said, offering a cup of infusions. “Willow bark for the pain, fennel to settle her stomach. If the fever breaks by morning, she’ll be fine.”  

Mother Althea stared at her, then nodded. “Thank you,” she whispered.  

Elara watched them go, her chest aching. The priest’s influence had spread like rot. She had treated his own nephew last week, and he had spat at her when she left. *Witch’s brew,* he had called it. *Demons in the leaves.*  

That night, Elara sat by the fire, sorting herbs into jars. The candlelight flickered against the walls, casting shadows that danced like specters. A knock at the door. She froze. The priest’s men never came after dark.  

She opened it to find Brother Roderic, his cassock stained with sweat. His face was pale, his lips thin. “I need your help,” he said.  

Elara’s pulse quickened. “The infirmary is full. The priest’s men—”  

“I don’t come as a priest,” he interrupted. “I come as a man. My wife… she’s sick. The fever took her this morning. The others say it’s the plague, but I’ve seen it before. A woman in the next district—same symptoms. She died within hours.”  

Elara stepped closer, peering into his eyes. He was trembling. “What’s her temperature?”  

“High. She’s delirious. She keeps saying… things. About *you.*”  

Elara’s stomach dropped. “What things?”  

Roderic hesitated. “That you poisoned the baker’s son. That you’ve cursed the city. She’s begging me to send you away.”  

A cold knot formed in her chest. “She’s wrong.”  

“I know,” he said. “But the people are scared. They think you’re behind this.”  

Elara’s hands clenched. “Then they’re fools. The plague doesn’t care who you are.”  

Roderic sighed. “I’ll bring her. But if she dies, they’ll come for you.”  

She didn’t answer. She only closed the door, her breath unsteady.  

By dawn, the priest’s men had gathered in the square, their faces shadowed by the rising sun. Elara stood at her door, watching as they hauled a cart of infected bodies into the ditch. The smell of decay was thick, but she forced herself to breathe. She had buried three neighbors that week.  

A cry echoed from the street. A woman staggered, clutching her head. “The healer!” someone shouted. “The witch!”  

Elara’s heart pounded. She stepped outside, her voice firm. “Stay back. The fever is contagious.”  

The crowd surged forward. A man in a priest’s robe pushed through. Brother Roderic. His face was ashen. “She’s worse,” he said. “The fever’s gone. She’s… cold.”  

Elara’s legs nearly gave way. “Where is she?”  

“In the cart.”  

She ran to the ditch, ignoring the jeers. The body was wrapped in a tarp, but she could see the faintest rise of breath. “Help me,” she said, kneeling.  

Roderic hesitated, then grabbed the tarp. Elara pulled it back. His wife’s face was pale, her lips blue. She was still alive.  

“Get her inside,” Elara said. “Now.”  

The crowd murmured. “She’s a witch!” someone shouted. “She’ll bring the plague to you all!”  

Elara turned, her eyes blazing. “If you don’t help, she’ll die. And then you’ll have more than a plague to fear.”  

Roderic’s men hesitated, then lifted the body. Elara led them to her workshop, her hands shaking. She laid the woman on the floor, then began her work.  

The fever had broken, but the woman’s body was weak. Elara mixed a broth of thyme and ginger, then pressed a cloth soaked in lavender oil to her forehead. “She’ll need rest,” she said. “And warmth.”  

The crowd gathered at the door, their faces a mix of fear and hope. “She’s going to live,” Elara said, though she wasn’t sure.  

Brother Roderic stepped forward. “Why did you help her?”  

“Because she’s a woman, not a witch,” Elara said. “And because I’ve seen this before. The fever breaks, then the body… it gives up. But if we act, if we give her what she needs, she might pull through.”  

The priest’s men muttered. “You’re defying the church,” one said.  

“I’m saving a life,” Elara shot back.  

The crowd dispersed, but the tension lingered. That night, Elara sat alone in her workshop, the fire dim. The woman in the next room stirred, her breathing steady.  

A knock. Elara opened the door to find the priest himself, his face drawn with exhaustion. “You saved her,” he said.  

“I did what I could.”  

He stepped inside, his eyes scanning the room. “The people fear you.”  

“I’m a healer, not a sorceress.”  

He sighed. “They think you’re behind the plague. That you’ve cursed us.”  

Elara’s hands clenched. “I’ve done nothing but try to help.”  

The priest’s gaze hardened. “And yet, the sick grow more. The dead pile higher. You are a force of chaos, Elara.”  

“I am a woman who knows herbs,” she said. “Who has saved lives.”  

He looked at her, then at the sleeping woman. “You must leave the city.”  

Elara’s breath caught. “You can’t order me out.”  

“I can,” he said. “The church has the power to declare you a heretic. And if you stay, they’ll burn you.”  

She stared at him, her heart hammering. “You’d let the city die for fear of a woman?”  

He didn’t answer.  

That night, Elara sat by the fire, her hands wrapped around a mug of tea. The woman in the next room stirred, and Elara rose to check on her. She was warmer, her breathing easier.  

But the priest’s words haunted her. *Leave the city.* She had no family, no home beyond these walls. The city was her life.  

A knock at the door. She froze.  

It was Roderic. “She’s awake,” he said. “She said your name.”  

Elara followed him to the room. The woman sat up, her eyes wide. “You saved me,” she whispered.  

“I did what I could,” Elara said.  

The woman reached for her hand. “They’ll come for you, you know. The priest’s men. They’ll say you’re a witch.”  

Elara’s throat tightened. “I know.”  

The woman’s eyes filled with tears. “But you’re not. You’re a miracle.”  

Elara looked away. “I’m just a woman with herbs.”  

The woman squeezed her hand. “Then let them call you a witch. You’ve saved my life.”  

Elara left the room, her chest aching. She returned to the workshop, where the fire had died. She sat on the floor, her back against the wall, and stared into the dark.  

What if the priest was right? What if the plague was a punishment, and she was a part of it? She had seen the signs—new cases rising, the dead piling higher. Had she done something to invite it?  

She thought of her mother, who had taught her the herbs, who had died of the same fever when Elara was a child. *“The world is cruel, but the roots are kind,”* she had said. *“You must choose who you are.”*  

Elara pressed her palms to her face. The weight of the city’s fear, the priest’s threat, the woman’s gratitude—everything pressed against her ribs.  

She had spent her life fighting against the darkness, but what if she was the darkness?  

A sound in the room. She looked up to see the woman standing in the doorway, wrapped in a blanket. “You should rest,” she said.  

Elara shook her head. “I can’t. Not yet.”  

The woman sat beside her. “You’re not a witch,” she said. “You’re a healer.”  

Elara looked at her, the tears blurring her vision. “What if I’m wrong? What if I’m not strong enough?”  

The woman hesitated, then reached out and took her hand. “Then we’ll be wrong together.”  

Elara didn’t know how long they sat there, but the fire had died by the time she finally lay down. The city outside was silent, save for the distant sound of a cart being pulled through the streets.  

In the morning, the priest’s men came. They stood at her door, their faces grim. “You are no longer welcome in the city,” one said.  

Elara stood, her hands steady. “I’ll go.”  

The crowd gathered as she left, their faces a mix of fear and pity. She carried only a satchel of herbs, a few clothes, and the memory of the woman’s words.  

As she stepped into the road, the sun rose over the city, casting long shadows across the cobblestones. The plague still raged, but so did she.  

And somewhere, in the heart of the city, a woman who had once been a healer was alive.  

Elara turned, her jaw set. The road ahead was uncertain, but she would walk it.  

For the living.
