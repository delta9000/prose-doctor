The first light of dawn seeped through the cracks in the abandoned schoolhouse, casting jagged shadows across the bloodstained floor. Elias tightened the tourniquet on a soldier’s leg, his gloved hands steady despite the tremor in his ribs. The ceasefire had been hours old, but the air still crackled with the unspoken threat of resumption. The clinic—a converted classroom with desks moved aside and a chalkboard now covered in emergency triage tags—was a labyrinth of makeshift beds and half-empty supply boxes. A young man from the 7th Infantry lay on a stretcher, his face pale, his breathing shallow. Elias checked the tag: *KIA, 30 minutes ago.* The man was already dead, but the medics had refused to let their own go without a fight.  

The line of the wounded had started at sunrise. First, the wounded from their side—two from the 7th, one from the 21st, their uniforms frayed and stained with mud and blood. Then, the enemy. A boy, no older than seventeen, dragged in by two older soldiers with a ruptured spleen. Elias had pulled the shrapnel from his abdomen with a gloved hand, the boy’s moans sharp and broken. The medics had a rule: no distinctions, no questions. But as Elias wiped his hands on a rag, the boy’s eyes met his, and something in that gaze—unfamiliar, desperate—felt like a mirror.  

“Help me,” the boy whispered, his voice thin, a mix of English and broken dialect. Elias hesitated. The boy’s chest was rising in shallow gasps, his fingers curling into the blanket. He had a concussion, a shattered femur, and a wound that might have been caused by their own artillery. Elias had seen it before: the shredded flesh, the way the body fought to survive even when the mind had given up. He glanced at the other injured, at the face of the dead man on the stretcher. The 7th’s captain had been a friend, a man who had taught Elias to stitch a wound without flinching. Now, Elias was standing over an enemy, the boy’s breath labored, his lips blue.  

The radio crackled. “This is Lieutenant Varga. We need a report on the wounded.” Elias’s fingers curled around the radio, his jaw tight. “We have five injured, two dead. One enemy down, critical.” He paused. “I don’t know how long the ceasefire will hold.” The message was relayed, but the silence that followed left his stomach knotted. The boy’s eyes were still on him, no longer pleading but watching, as if measuring the man who might decide his fate.  

Elias turned back to the wounded. A Corporal from the 21st had a collapsed lung, the chest tube sputtering as it drained. Another had a shrapnel wound to the abdomen, his intestines spilling out like a twisted rope. Elias pressed a gauze pad to the man’s side, his mind calculating the odds. The boy’s wound was a priority, but the 21st Corporal’s was more urgent. He had to choose.  

“Let me help,” the boy said, his voice barely audible. Elias met his gaze again. The boy’s face was streaked with dirt and blood, his uniform torn at the shoulder. He was holding a small, crumpled photo—Elias could see it, even in the dim light. A girl, no older than ten, her face tilted up. The boy’s hand shook as he pressed it to his chest.  

“Can’t talk,” Elias said, his voice flat. “You’ll be okay.” The boy’s lips curled into a faint, painful smile. “I know.”  

Elias turned away. He couldn’t afford to think about the photo. He couldn’t afford to think about the boy’s name, or the girl’s, or what he might have left behind. The radio hissed again. “We’re being asked to surrender our wounded for medical exchange.” Elias’s breath caught. He had heard of such things—exchanges in the dead of night, bodies traded for bodies. But this was a ceasefire. This was supposed to be a moment of respite.  

He checked the Corporal’s pulse. Weak. The man was fading. Elias grabbed a scalpel, his hands moving by instinct. The boy’s eyes followed him, and for a moment, Elias felt the weight of the choice he was about to make. If he saved the Corporal, the boy might die. If he saved the boy, the Corporal might die. The calculus was simple: save the one with the greatest chance of survival. But the heart didn’t follow the math.  

“Elias,” called a voice from the doorway. It was Sergeant Rivera, her face drawn. “The enemy’s asking for a medic.”  

“I’m busy,” he said, not looking up.  

“They’re saying they’ll take the boy if we don’t.”  

Elias froze. The boy’s eyes widened, his hand clutching the photo tighter. Rivera’s voice was low, but the implications were sharp. The exchange would be a trap.  

“Tell them no,” Elias said. “I’m not giving him up.”  

Rivera hesitated, then stepped back. “They’ll come for him anyway.”  

Elias didn’t answer. He worked on the Corporal, his mind racing. The boy’s injuries were severe, but not immediately fatal. The Corporal’s were. If Elias didn’t act now, the man would die. He tied off the incision, his pulse steady, his voice calm. “I need a chest tube.”  

A nurse handed him the kit. Elias inserted the tube, the sound of the man’s breath easing. The Corporal’s eyes fluttered open, then closed again. Elias exhaled, his hands damp with sweat. The boy’s breath was still shallow, his skin clammy.  

“Tourniquet,” Elias said, and a medic tightened the one on his leg. The boy’s eyelids drooped.  

“Why are you helping me?” he asked, his voice barely a whisper.  

Elias didn’t answer. He was already moving to the next patient, a sniper from the enemy side with a bullet wound to the thigh. The man was conscious, his expression defiant. Elias crouched, his hand hovering over the wound. “Hold still,” he said, his tone clipped. The sniper’s eyes narrowed. “You’re a medic. You don’t shoot.”  

Elias met his gaze. “I don’t shoot, but I don’t hesitate either.” He wiped the wound, his hands moving with practiced efficiency. The sniper flinched, but Elias didn’t look up. The boy was still watching him, his expression unreadable.  

The radio buzzed again. “This is Lieutenant Varga. The enemy is requesting a ceasefire for medical exchange.”  

Elias’s fingers tightened on the gauze. He had heard of this—medics negotiating with the other side, their hands stained with each other’s blood. It was a fragile truce, one that could shatter with a single gunshot. He glanced at the boy, still breathing, and the Corporal, still alive.  

“We need to decide,” Rivera said, her voice low. “Do we let the enemy take him, or do we hold him?”  

Elias’s mind turned over the question. The boy had a chance. He was young, resilient. The Corporal was older, already weakened. But the Corporal had been with them for years. Elias had known him since basic training.  

“Get me a stretcher,” he said.  

Rivera hesitated. “You’re not thinking of letting them take him?”  

“I’m thinking of saving both,” Elias said. “But the odds are against it.”  

The boy’s hand trembled, his fingers still clutching the photo. Elias reached out, gently prying it from his grasp. The image was worn, the edges frayed. A girl, her hair in braid, her eyes wide. Elias turned it over. No name. No date. Just a hand-scrawled message in a language he didn’t recognize.  

“Where’s your unit?” he asked.  

The boy didn’t answer. His breath was shallow, his body slipping. Elias’s heart thudded. He had to move.  

“Get the stretcher ready,” he said. “We’re taking him to the rear.”  

Rivera’s eyes narrowed. “You know there’s no guarantee they won’t shoot us when they get him back.”  

“They won’t,” Elias said, though he wasn’t sure.  

The boy’s eyes fluttered open. “No—”  

“I’m not giving you up,” Elias said, cutting him off. “You’re not a prisoner. You’re a patient.”  

The boy’s lips curled again, but this time, it was a grimace of pain. Elias didn’t look at him. He moved to the next wounded, a soldier from the 7th with a fractured pelvis. The man’s face was contorted, his gasps ragged. Elias pressed a hand to his chest, checking for a heartbeat. It was weak, but it was there.  

“Bag him,” Elias said. The medic grabbed a oxygen mask, and Elias turned back to the boy.  

There was a knock at the door. A platoon leader, his face pale. “They’re coming for him.”  

Elias’s hands froze. “How long?”  

“Five minutes. They said they’ll take him in peace.”  

“Peace,” Elias muttered. He had heard of that word in the mouths of the dead. He looked at the boy, then at the Corporal. The Corporal’s breath was steady now, but his lips were still blue.  

“Get him on the stretcher,” Elias said. “We’ll move him to the rear.”  

The boy’s eyes were glassy, his body trembling. Elias helped lift him, his muscles aching. “You’re not going to die,” he said, though the words felt hollow.  

The enemy soldiers arrived in a line, their weapons lowered, their faces grim. Elias didn’t look up, didn’t meet their eyes. He passed the boy over, his hands steady. The enemy medic—a woman with a scar across her cheek—grasped the stretcher, her expression unreadable.  

“Thank you,” she said, not in English, but in the dialect of the boy. Elias didn’t respond. He turned back to the Corporal, his hands already moving to check the chest tube. The woman’s eyes met his, and for a moment, he saw something in them—relief, maybe, or the same exhaustion he felt.  

The exchange was swift. The boy was gone, and Elias was left with the Corporal, whose breathing had steadied. But the weight of the decision lingered. He had chosen the Corporal, but the boy’s face haunted him.  

“Elias,” Rivera said. “We’re not going to get a chance to save the others.”  

He looked up. The line of wounded stretched beyond the doorway, their faces etched with pain. A fighter from the 7th, his arm dangling by a thread of flesh. A nurse from the enemy, her leg mangled. Elias’s hands clenched. The calculus was clear: save the ones who could survive, let the others go. But the heart wasn’t clear.  

The radio crackled. “Ceasefire violated. Hostilities resumed.”  

Elias’s head snapped up. The woman from the enemy side was still there, her eyes wide. The Corporal’s breath was still steady, but the boy’s—Elias didn’t know. He had to act.  

“Get the rest in here,” he ordered. “We’re not waiting.”  

The nurse from the enemy side was the first to be moved, her leg wrapped in makeshift bandages. Elias worked on her, his hands moving with precision. She was alive, but barely. The 7th soldier’s arm was a mess, the bone jutting out. Elias had to amputate.  

“Hold still,” he said, and the soldier nodded, his eyes wild. Elias cut through the flesh, the sound of the scalpel sharp in the silence. The soldier didn’t scream, just stared at him, his face a mask of pain and fear.  

“Why are you doing this?” the soldier asked.  

Elias didn’t answer. He tied off the stump, his hands moving as if through a dream. The nurse’s leg was too damaged, her chances slim. The 7th soldier’s arm was salvageable. Elias’s mind was a storm of numbers and names, of lives he had saved and ones he had lost.
