The ink on the parchment was still damp when Elara first noticed the discrepancy. She had been copying the *Chronicles of the Sunken Kingdom* for the Grand Archivist, a task she had performed countless times before, each entry a meticulous dance of quill and patience. The script was familiar—serifed, deliberate, the same as the original codex she had studied for years. But as she traced her finger over the passage detailing the fall of the city of Varyndor, a shiver crawled up her spine. The dates didn’t align. The siege, which she remembered as occurring in the 37th year of the Dragon’s Reign, was now recorded as the 35th. The number of casualties, once a staggering sixteen thousand, had been reduced to six thousand. Her breath hitched. She glanced at the codex, then at her own transcription, then back again. The words were identical, but the truth had been rewritten.  

Elara’s hands trembled. She had always believed the archives to be sacred, a repository of unimpeachable facts. The Grand Archivist, a gaunt man with a voice like grinding stone, had once told her, *“History is the bedrock of our civilization. To alter it is to breach the covenant of the first scribes.”* But if the records themselves were compromised, what did that mean? She leaned closer, squinting at the faded script. The original text, preserved in the codex’s margins, bore the same inconsistencies. Someone had tampered with the source material.  

She rose from her desk, her chair scraping against the stone floor of the Vault of Endless Quills, a chamber beneath the Citadel of the Silver Flame. The air here was thick with the scent of aged vellum and the faint metallic tang of iron ink. Rows of shelves stretched into shadow, their contents guarded by glyphs that shimmered faintly with dormant enchantments. Elara had spent years in this place, her life bound to the careful preservation of the past. Yet now, the past felt like a stranger.  

“Is something the matter?”  

The voice startled her. She turned to find Master Idris, the Head Archivist, watching her from the doorway. His robes, once a proud indigo, had faded to a somber gray, and his eyes, sharp as a falcon’s, narrowed at the parchment in her hands. “I’m just double-checking the dates,” she said, forcing a calm she did not feel. “The siege of Varyndor… I thought it was in the 37th year.”  

Idris stepped forward, his presence as heavy as the silence between them. “The 35th,” he corrected, his tone measured. “As recorded in the codex. Why the question?”  

Elara hesitated. She could not explain the unease coiling in her gut, the way her pulse thrummed against her temples. “I’ve… I’ve seen conflicting accounts before,” she said, choosing her words carefully. “In the *Tales of the Sapphire Crown*, for instance. The same event is dated differently there.”  

Idris’s gaze flicked to the codex. “The *Sapphire Crown* is a collection of folk stories. You know better than to confuse them with the archives.” His voice was clipped, but there was a flicker of something else—annoyance, perhaps, or irritation. “The codex is the official record. Stick to it.”  

“I was only—”  

“Do not waste my time with doubts, Elara. You are a scribe, not a historian. Your duty is to copy, not to question.” He turned on his heel, his robes swishing like a whisper of thunder. “Return to your work.”  

The words left her throat raw, but she nodded, retreating to her desk. Idris’s dismissal felt like a blade to the ribs. She had always trusted the archives, the meticulous care with which they were maintained. Yet now, the thought gnawed at her: *What if the official record is the lie?*  

That night, she pored over her notes in the dim glow of a single oil lamp. The *Chronicles* were stored in a restricted section of the vault, accessible only to senior scribes. She had never questioned their integrity, but now, the weight of her own pen felt heavier. She cross-referenced the siege of Varyndor with other texts—the *Records of the High King*, the *Annals of the Iron Pact*, even the *Ballads of the Shattered Isles*. Each contained variations. Dates shifted by a year, names omitted, battles exaggerated or erased. The same event, rewritten in different hands.  

Her studies had taught her that the archives were compiled by the first scribes, who had sworn an oath to preserve truth without bias. But if that oath had been broken, if the records were malleable, then what else had been altered? She thought of the *Scroll of the First Dawn*, a document she had transcribed three years prior. The original, she recalled, had described the founding of the Citadel as a moment of unity, a pact between the five kingdoms to end the Age of Ashes. Yet in her recent copy, the passage read: *“The Citadel was built to contain the spread of the Ashen Plague, a curse born of the Fireborn’s ambition.”* The word *curse* had not been in the original.  

Elara’s fingers tightened around the parchment. The Ashen Plague was a sacred subject, a tale of divine retribution that underpinned the Citadel’s legitimacy. To call it a “curse born of the Fireborn’s ambition” was to implicate the ruling house, the House of Emberfall, in the very calamity they had supposedly saved the world from. The implications were dangerous. If the records were being rewritten to shift blame, then someone—*someone*—had the power to manipulate history itself.  

She needed proof. But how? The archives were protected by enchantments, their contents sealed against tampering. Only the Grand Archivist and the Head Archivist had access to the original codices. Yet Elara had noticed something peculiar: the most recent alterations were subtle, almost imperceptible. A single word shifted, a line of text omitted. The changes required a practiced hand, someone who knew the original texts intimately.  

The next morning, she asked for access to the *Annals of the Iron Pact*, a volume she had not worked on in years. The assistant archivist, a wiry man named Teral, frowned. “That’s in the restricted section. You’d need Idris’s permission.”  

“I know,” she said, meeting his gaze. “But I’m certain the Grand Archivist would approve. I’m compiling a comparative study of siege chronologies.”  

Teral hesitated, then shrugged. “I’ll fetch it for you. But don’t expect me to hold your hand through the process.”  

He returned with the annals, their cover embossed with the sigil of the Iron Pact, a hammer crushing a serpent. Elara opened it, her breath shallow. The passage on Varyndor was unchanged—37th year, sixteen thousand dead. But when she flipped to the section on the Ashen Plague, the text had been altered. The original description of the Fireborn’s rebellion had been replaced with a tale of divine wrath, the plague framed as a punishment for their hubris.  

Her heart pounded. This was not a simple error. The changes were deliberate, systematic. She traced the ink with her fingertip, feeling the faint ridges where the text had been scraped away and re-written. The scribe who had done this had used a fine-pointed blade, a tool reserved for the most delicate of edits. Only a senior archivist would have access to such implements.  

Elara’s mind raced. Who had the authority to alter the annals? The Grand Archivist, of course, but Idris had denied her request to see the codex. Yet if the records were being revised, it was likely a broader effort. A faction within the Citadel, perhaps? The High King’s court? She thought of the rumors she had heard—whispers of a secret council that met behind closed doors, of scribes who had vanished after questioning the archives.  

She needed to find another source. The *Tales of the Sapphire Crown* were in the public section, but they were considered unreliable. If she could find a version that predated the alterations, it might confirm her suspicions. She slipped into the lower halls of the archive, where the older texts were stored in a climate-controlled chamber. The air here was cooler, the books stacked in tiers that reached nearly to the ceiling.  

A rustle behind her made her freeze. She turned, but the chamber was empty. The silence felt oppressive. Elara exhaled, forcing herself to focus. She scanned the shelves, her fingers brushing against spines etched with the names of long-dead scribes. Finally, she found a copy of the *Sapphire Crown* from the 42nd year of the Dragon’s Reign. The passage on Varyndor read as she remembered: 37th year, sixteen thousand dead.  

But when she compared it to the newer edition, the date had been changed to 35th. The casualty numbers had been adjusted. The same edits. The same hand.  

A cold dread settled in her chest. This was not an isolated error. It was a pattern. The records were being rewritten, and the changes were spreading like a rot through the annals of history.  

She returned to her desk, her mind a storm of questions. Who had the power to do this? Why? And more importantly, what would happen if she spoke of it? The Grand Archivist’s words echoed in her ears: *“Stick to it.”* But how could she, when the very foundations of her work were crumbling?  

That evening, she sought out her fellow scribe, Lirian, a woman with a sharp tongue and a sharper mind. They had shared a quiet camaraderie in the vault, their conversations often reduced to the occasional exchange of ink-stained fingers and half-remembered jokes. Lirian was the only one she trusted.  

“You look like you’ve seen a ghost,” Lirian said, her eyes narrowing as Elara slumped into the bench beside her.  

“Have you ever noticed… changes in the records?” Elara asked, keeping her voice low.  

Lirian paused, her quill hovering over a blank page. “Changes?”  

“Small ones. Dates, numbers, names. Like someone’s been… adjusting them.”  

Lirian’s brow furrowed. “You’re not the first to think that. But if you’re asking me, I’d say you’re imagining things. The archives are sacred. No one would dare—”  

“Idris did.”  

Lirian’s quill snapped. “What?”  

Elara lowered her voice. “I looked at the codex. The siege of Varyndor—35th year instead of 37th. Casualties trimmed from sixteen thousand to six. And the *Sapphire Crown*… the same edits. It’s not just one text. It’s all of them. Someone’s rewriting history.”  

Lirian’s face paled. “You’re not supposed to question the archives, Elara. You know that.”  

“I know,” she whispered. “But what if they’re lying? What if the Citadel… what if the High King…”  

Lirian pressed a hand to her lips. “Don’t say it. Not here. Not now.” She glanced around, her voice a thread of steel. “You think Idris is behind this?”  

“I think he knows. And he’s not stopping it.”  

A long silence stretched between them. Then Lirian leaned in. “If you’re right, then you’re in danger. The archives are protected by more than just enchantments. There are… consequences for those who challenge the official record.”  

Elara’s throat tightened. “What kind of consequences?”  

Lirian’s gaze flicked to the vault’s entrance. “The last scribe who questioned the records was assigned to the lower halls. No one’s seen her since.”  

The lower halls. A place where the oldest and most fragile texts were stored, the air so thick with dust and decay that even the enchantments struggled to preserve them. It was a fate worse than death—a slow erasure, a life spent in silence and rot.  

“I need to find more,” Elara said. “If I can prove it, if I can show the truth…”  

“You’ll be silenced before you can speak.” Lirian’s voice was urgent now. “Think, Elara. The High King’s rule is built on this. If the archives are lies, then the Citadel is a lie. And the Citadel is the last thing standing between us and the warlords.”  

Elara’s fingers curled into her palms. The warlords. The nomadic tribes who had once ruled the eastern plains, their raids a constant shadow over the kingdoms. The Citadel’s founding had been framed as a triumph, a united front against the chaos of the warlords. If that story was a fabrication, then the entire political order was a fragile house of cards.  

But why would the archives be altered? To control the narrative, to ensure compliance. To prevent dissent.  

She thought of the Ashen Plague again. If the Fireborn’s rebellion had been recast as divine punishment, then the High King’s ancestors were the heroes, not the villains. It was a lie that justified their rule. A lie that kept the warlords in check.  

And if the lie was exposed…  

Elara’s mind reeled. The implications were vast, but the risks were greater. She had spent her life in the archives, her world defined by the neat lines of ink and the weight of truth. Now, that truth felt like a noose around her neck.  

That night, she dreamt of the Citadel’s foundation. She saw it in the firelight of her mind: a gathering of five kings, their faces shadowed, their hands clasped in a symbol of unity. But in the dream, the pact was not a triumph. It was a betrayal. The kings had signed not to end the Age of Ashes, but to claim its ashes for themselves. The fire had not been a curse—it had been a tool, a weapon forged by the Fireborn to break the old order.  

She woke drenched in sweat, the dream’s details etched into her mind. If the archives were lies, then the dream was a fragment of the truth. But how could she verify it?  

The next day, she petitioned for access to the *Scroll of the First Dawn*. Idris denied her, citing “unrelated duties.” But she pressed, and he relented, though his eyes lingered on her with the suspicion of a man who had seen too many scribes wander too close to forbidden knowledge.  

The scroll was stored in the Sanctum of Echoes, a chamber where the oldest texts were kept under a protective ward. Elara entered alone, the air humming with latent magic. The scroll was unguarded, its surface etched with the sigil of the first scribes. She unrolled it carefully, her breath shallow.  

The text was as she remembered: *“In the 37th year of the Dragon’s Reign, the five kingdoms convened in the shadow of the Flame Spire. The Fireborn, once a bitter foe, agreed to lay down their arms. Thus, the Citadel was born, a beacon of unity in a world of ruin.”*  

But beneath the surface, the ink was different. Faint, almost imperceptible, a second layer of script had been added. Elara’s heart lurched as she traced the hidden words: *“The Fireborn’s rebellion was not quelled, but co-opted. Their flames were harnessed, their weapons forged into the Citadel’s foundations. The pact was not a peace, but a conquest.”*  

It was a secret history, one that had been buried beneath the official record. The scribe who had added it had used a different ink, one that reacted to the light of the Sanctum’s enchanted lamps. The text was hidden, but not erased. A message left for those who knew where to look.  

Elara’s hands shook. This was proof. But it was also a trap. Who had added this? And why?
