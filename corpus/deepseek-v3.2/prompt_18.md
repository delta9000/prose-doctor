The night was a vast, silent cathedral, and Maria stood alone in its nave. The stone floor of the observatory tower seeped a damp chill through the soles of her thin slippers, but she did not feel it. Her world had contracted to the circle of darkness at the end of the telescope, a tube of cold brass she had painstakingly polished that afternoon. Beyond it lay infinity.

She adjusted the focus with practiced, gentle fingers, her right eye pressed to the eyepiece. The familiar face of Jupiter swam into view, a striped pearl hanging in the black. The four attendant stars—the Mediccan stars, as Signor Galilei had called them decades before—glittered in their eternal dance around their master. She tracked them nightly, noting their positions in her private ledger, the one bound in plain brown leather that lived beneath a loose floorboard in her small bedchamber. The official logs, bound in fine vellum, were for the hand of Professor Carlo Vascelli.

A soft scrape of a boot on stone made her start. She straightened, her back protesting after hours bent over the instrument.

“Maria? You are still here.”

It was not a question. Professor Vascelli’s bulk filled the narrow doorway, his silhouette blotting out the candlelight from the stairwell. He carried his own lantern, which painted the domed room in jumping shadows.

“I was completing the measurements of the Jovian satellites, Professore,” she said, her voice carefully neutral. “The seeing is exceptionally clear tonight.”

He moved to the telescope, nudging her aside without malice, as one might move a piece of furniture. He peered through the eyepiece. “Hmm. Yes. Good. You have recorded the transit times?”

“In the draft ledger, yes.” She gestured to the small desk where her notes lay, a chaotic but precise sprawl of numbers, diagrams, and annotations in her tight, sloping script.

Vascelli grunted, scanning her pages. “Translate this into the fair log tomorrow. And ensure your handwriting resembles mine more closely this time. The last entry looked… feminine.”

The familiar, dull ache settled in her chest. “Of course.”

He did not look at her. His gaze was on her work, absorbing its clarity. “The Council of the Accademia dei Svegliati meets next week. They wish to hear of our progress on the lunar theory. I will need a comprehensive summary. Your analysis of the libration data was… passably insightful. Expand upon it.”

*Our* progress. *Your* analysis. The alchemy of their arrangement: her eyes, her mind, her endless hours in the cold, transmuted into his prestige. Maria had been Vascelli’s ‘assistant’ for seven years, since her father, a minor noble with a passion for mathematics, had died leaving her penniless and unmarried. A chance remark at a dinner—a correction she’d whispered to him regarding a flawed calculation in a public lecture—had sparked his interest. He saw not a rival, but a remarkable tool. In return for her silence and her labour, he provided her with access to the observatory, a meagre stipend, and the fragile protection of his name. It was a gilded cage, its bars made of printed pages bearing his name and her ideas.

“I will have it ready,” she said.

He lingered, a rare occurrence. Usually, he collected her findings and left. Tonight, he seemed restless. “Turn the glass to the southern quadrant. There is a new nebulosity reported by Hevelius near Scorpius. Verify it.”

Maria obeyed, swinging the great telescope with a quiet grunt of effort. She found the starfield, a shimmering dust of diamond chips against velvet. She knew this region. She had been sketching it for months.

“I see it,” she said. “A faint smudge of milk. It is as he describes.”

Vascelli nodded, satisfied. “Note it. We shall include a confirmation in my next letter to the Royal Society.” He turned to go, then paused. “Do not stay too late. The damp is bad for the instruments.”

When his footsteps had faded, Maria released a breath she hadn’t realized she’d been holding. The tower was hers again. She did not move the telescope from Scorpius. Instead, she returned to her eyepiece, to *her* patch of sky. Just a few degrees west of Hevelius’s nebula was a smaller, denser blur she had first noticed three months prior. It had not been in any catalogue. At first, she thought it a flaw in the lens, a smudge of moisture. But night after night, it persisted. It was not a nebula. It was too compact, too regular. A comet, perhaps, but it showed no tail, no movement against the fixed stars.

Tonight, she aimed for it with a new lens, one she had ground herself in secret, a risky extravagance that had consumed half a year’s stipend. The image snapped into a startling clarity.

It was not a blur. It was a disc. A tiny, perfect, pearl-grey disc.

Her heart hammered against her ribs. She checked her charts, her own private, meticulous charts. The object was not listed. She observed for an hour, then two, making minute adjustments. It did not drift. It was fixed. A new fixed star? But it had a visible diameter, a *face*, unlike the pinpoints around it.

A wild, terrifying thought began to form. She remembered Galileo’s drawings of the planets, how they showed as discs, while the stars remained points of light. This was a disc. But it was far beyond Saturn, the known frontier.

She worked until her eye burned and the first hint of indigo bled into the eastern sky. She filled pages of her private ledger with sketches, coordinates, and frantic, trembling notes. *”A new wanderer? But motionless? A seventh planet? Impossible. The heavens are perfected at six. Yet the eye does not lie. The glass does not lie.”*

The next week was a torment. By day, she transcribed her lunar libration analysis into Vascelli’s fair hand, her mind screaming with the secret in the southern sky. By night, she returned to her disc, monitoring it. No movement. Yet its very presence was a seismic shift in the cosmos.

Vascelli collected the lunar report. “Adequate,” he pronounced, rolling the scroll. “The Accademia will be pleased. Your stipend will be increased by ten scudi this quarter.”

It was a king’s ransom to her. It felt like ashes.

“Professore,” she began, her throat tight. “In my observations of Scorpius… I… I may have found something. An anomaly.”

He was already halfway out the door, late for a salon. “An anomaly? Probably an atmospheric distortion. Note it and we shall review it next month.”

“It is not a distortion,” she said, the force in her voice stopping him. He turned, eyebrows raised. She pressed on, the words tumbling out. “It is a disc. A small, resolved disc. In a place where no star should show a disc. I have observed it for twelve nights. It is stationary.”

A flicker of real interest crossed his face, cutting through his habitual patina of bored entitlement. “Show me.”

That night, under a less-clear sky, she guided him to the spot. It took longer to find, the seeing was poorer. Finally, he saw it. He was silent for a long time, his breathing the only sound in the tower.

“A curious thing,” he murmured, more to himself than to her. “A new fixed star of unusual character. Perhaps a dense nebula.” But she heard the doubt in his voice. He was a mediocre astronomer, but he was not a fool. He understood the implications.

“It has the characteristics of a planet,” Maria whispered, the heresy hanging in the cold air.

“Do not be rash,” he snapped, stepping back from the telescope. “Such a claim… it would be laughed at. A seventh planet? It upends everything. It must be verified. Extensively. You will observe it nightly. Record every detail. Speak of this to no one. *No one*, Maria. This is of the utmost sensitivity.”

The dynamic had shifted. Before, he had exploited her diligence. Now, he commanded her silence. The discovery was too large, too dangerous, and too precious. She saw the calculation in his eyes: the risk of ridicule versus the immortal glory of expanding the solar system.

Weeks bled into months. Maria became a ghost in the tower, living for the clear nights, her ledger growing fat with data. The object—*her* object—remained steadfast. She began to call it *Terra Nova* in her private thoughts, a new world. Vascelli visited less frequently, but his instructions were precise: compile, correlate, prove.

One evening, he arrived with a stranger, a thin, hawk-nosed Frenchman in travel-stained clothes. “Maria, this is Monsieur Leclerc, a natural philosopher from Paris. He is interested in our observational methods.”

Leclerc’s eyes swept over her, not with the usual dismissiveness, but with sharp curiosity. “The famous assistant,” he said in accented Italian. “The Professor speaks highly of your… meticulousness.”

Vascelli clapped a heavy hand on her shoulder, a possessive gesture. “Maria is a wonder with the glass. She has the patience I lack.” He then proceeded to explain *her* telescope adjustments, *her* recording system, as if they were his own.

Leclerc listened, then turned to Maria directly. “And you, Signorina. What celestial mystery currently holds your attention?”

She froze, her eyes darting to Vascelli. He gave an almost imperceptible shake of his head, his smile stiff.

“The… the variations in the lunar albedo, Monsieur,” she stammered, repeating the cover story Vascelli had concocted.

Later, after Leclerc had departed with promises to share Vascelli’s ‘brilliant’ lunar work with the Paris Academy, Vascelli turned on her. “You see? The eyes of Europe are upon us. We must be circumspect. The time is not yet right for your… nebula.”

“It is not a nebula,” she said, a spark of defiance igniting for the first time.

“It is whatever I say it is!” he hissed, his face close to hers. “Remember your place, Maria. Without my name, your work is nothing. Without my protection, you are a woman dabbling in natural philosophy—a curiosity at best, a heretic at worst. This discovery, if it is what you think, is too great for you. It would destroy you. Let it bear my name, and it will enter the world. It will *change* the world. Is that not what you want?”

The truth of it was a physical blow. He was right. A paper authored by ‘Maria Fiore’ would be used to light a fire, not to be read. Her choice was not between credit and obscurity, but between obscurity and oblivion.

The spark died. “Yes, Professore.”

The final night of confirmation was the coldest of the year. A hard, crystalline clarity settled over Bologna. The disc of Terra Nova was sharper than ever before. Maria, cross-referencing her own charts with the most recent star catalogues from Berlin, made a final, definitive check. There was no star recorded there. None. And using a new method of parallax she had been developing in secret—comparing its position from two different observatory apertures—she gathered the first, shaky evidence of its immense distance.

It was a new world.

She sat back, her body numb. The work was complete. The proof, as solid as she could make it, was in her ledger. She heard Vascelli’s heavy tread on the stairs. He had come, as promised, for the final data.

He entered, his face flushed with anticipation and good wine. “Well?”

“It is confirmed,” she said, her voice flat. “It is a planetary body. Its distance, from my calculations, is approximately twice that of Saturn.” She handed him the summary sheet, the fruit of a year’s solitary labour.

He scanned it, his lips moving silently. A slow, triumphant smile spread across his face. “Excellent. *Excellent!* This is monumental.” He looked at her, and for a fleeting second, something like gratitude or guilt touched his eyes. It was gone in an instant, replaced by avaricious glee. “I will draft the announcement for the Accademia immediately. The ‘Vascelli Object’. It has a ring to it, no?”

Maria said nothing. She watched as he gathered her notes, her charts, her life’s work.

“You have done well, Maria,” he said, almost kindly. “Your stipend will be doubled. You may take a few days’ rest.”
