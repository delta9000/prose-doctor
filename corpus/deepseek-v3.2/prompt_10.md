The fluorescent lights of Mercy General’s seventh floor hummed a flat, eternal note, the only constant in the shifting tides of the night. Evelyn Reed moved through it with a practiced quiet, her sneakers whispering against the linoleum. The chart in her hand was for Mr. Henderson in 712, but her eyes kept drifting to the closed door of 714. Empty now. It had been Walter Brennan’s room until three nights ago.

She finished Mr. Henderson’s vitals—BP a little elevated, but his sleep was peaceful—and retreated to the nurses’ station. The desk was an island of light in the dark, quiet ward. The other night nurse, Chloe, was on her break. Evelyn pulled up the electronic records, her fingers hovering over the keyboard. She didn’t need to look; the details were etched behind her eyes, a grim pattern she couldn’t unsee.

Walter Brennan, 74, CHF exacerbation. Admitted 9 days ago. Stable improvement until night 7, post-midnight. Sudden onset of profound bradycardia, unresponsive to atropine. Code called at 01:47. Pronounced 02:23.

She clicked back. Marjorie Finch, 68, pneumonia. Admitted 11 days ago. Night 8, rapid decline in O2 saturation, unexplained respiratory depression. Code called at 02:15. Pronounced 03:01.

Arthur Lumley, 81, post-hip replacement. Admitted 14 days ago. Night 9, acute hypotension, asystole. Code called at 01:10. Pronounced 01:58.

Three patients. All on the General Medicine ward, seventh floor. All showing marked recovery. All dying suddenly, unexpectedly, in the small hours of the morning. And all between seven and fourteen days into their admission. It was the timeline that snagged in her mind, a loose thread she couldn’t stop pulling. Coincidence was a currency she’d stopped believing in after fifteen years of night shifts. The hospital had its own rhythms, its own tides of chaos and calm, but this felt different. This felt like a pattern hidden in the noise.

“You’re doing it again.”

Evelyn jumped. Chloe slumped into the chair beside her, unwrapping a granola bar. “You’ve got that look. The one that says you’re mentally rearranging all the supply closets by molecular weight.”

“Just reviewing charts,” Evelyn said, minimizing the screens.

“Morbid hobby.” Chloe took a bite. “It’s quiet tonight. Enjoy it.”

Quiet. Evelyn’s gaze travelled down the hall. Room 706. Simon Gable, 79, recovering from a nasty GI bleed. He’d been admitted eight days ago. He was turning a corner, they’d said in rounds. Might go home by the weekend. A cold filament of dread unspooled in her stomach.

She stood up. “I’m going to do a round.”

“You just did a round,” Chloe mumbled through her granola bar.

“I’m doing another one.”

The details of the ward sharpened as she walked: the smell of antiseptic and stale air, the faint gurgle of an IV pump, the snore from 708. She pushed open the door to 706 without knocking, the beam of her penlight cutting a gentle path across the floor to the bed.

Simon Gable was asleep, his mouth slightly open. The room was dark save for the dim glow of the cardiac monitor, painting jagged green peaks and valleys across the screen. His rhythm was sinus, a little slow at 58, but steady. His oxygen sensor glowed red on his fingertip, reporting a solid 96%. Evelyn moved closer, her nurse’s eye cataloging everything. The IV in his left hand was patent, no swelling. The saline bag was almost full, dripping at a keep-vein-open rate. His urine bag, hanging from the bed rail, showed a normal, pale straw output for the shift.

She laid a hand on his wrist, feeling for his radial pulse. It matched the monitor, strong and regular. His skin was warm, dry. Nothing was amiss. And yet, the dread sat heavy in her gut. *Night eight*. According to her pattern, if it was real, he was in the window.

“Mr. Gable?” she said softly.

He stirred, blinking blearily. “Hmm? What’s… time is it?”

“Just after one,” she said, smiling. “Everything’s fine. Just checking on you. How are you feeling?”

“Tired,” he rasped. “Feel okay, though. Better.” He coughed, a dry, weak sound. “Thirsty.”

“I’ll get you some fresh water.” She filled his pitcher from the sink in the corner, her back to him. The routine was automatic, giving her eyes a moment to sweep the room again. Nothing. The nightstand held his glasses, a get-well card, a plastic cup. The window reflected the dark room and the steady green trace of his heart.

She helped him sip, adjusted his pillows, and made a show of checking the monitor leads. “You get some rest, Mr. Gable. Ring if you need anything.”

Back at the station, she couldn’t settle. She pulled the records for all three deceased patients again, looking for a common thread beyond the timeline. Attending physicians were different. Nurses were different. Medications… all had standard regimens for their conditions: diuretics, antibiotics, anticoagulants. But one notation caught her eye. All three had received scheduled nighttime medications—their 10 PM rounds—from the automated dispensing cabinet. All three had their final doses signed out by the same per-diem night pharmacist, Leo, who covered several floors. A link? Possibly, but it was thin. Everyone got meds from the ADC.

At 02:30, the silence of the ward was broken by a low, choking gasp from the intercom speaker of the room monitor system. It was from 706.

Evelyn was already moving, Chloe close behind. The green glow of Simon Gable’s monitor, visible through the door window, was no longer a steady rhythm. It was a slow, widening crawl. *Bradycardia. 40… 35…*

“Call a code, Chloe! Now!” Evelyn burst into the room.

Simon Gable’s eyes were wide with panic, his mouth working soundlessly. His skin had taken on a dusky hue. Evelyn slammed her palm against the bedside nurse call button, but she didn’t wait. She tilted his head back, checking for obstruction—clear—and reached for the Ambu-bag hooked on the wall, connecting it to the oxygen flowmeter with a sharp twist.

“Simon, look at me! I’m here, we’ve got you.” Her voice was calm, a forced anchor in the sudden storm. She placed the mask over his nose and mouth and began to squeeze the bag in a steady rhythm, watching his chest rise and fall. “Get the crash cart!” she yelled into the hall.

The room exploded into controlled chaos. The code team arrived in a thunder of footsteps—a resident, two more nurses, a respiratory therapist. The resident, a young man with tired eyes, took over compressions as Evelyn rattled off the situation. “Sudden onset bradycardia progressing to PEA. Unresponsive. No prior distress.”

“Epinephrine, 1 mg, push!” the resident ordered.

Evelyn drew up the medication from the crash cart, her hands steady, and administered it through the IV port. “Epinephrine in.”

“Continue CPR. Let’s get a rhythm check.”

They paused compressions. The monitor showed a weak, agonal rhythm. No pulse.

“Resume compressions. Get ready to intubate.”

The respiratory therapist stepped in with the laryngoscope. Evelyn’s world narrowed to the tasks: drawing up another round of epi, recording times, checking the IV line. But a part of her mind was screaming, detached and cold. *This is it. This is exactly it. Profound bradycardia. Rapid decline. Night eight.*

After twenty-seven minutes, the resident called it. Time of death, 02:57. The team melted away, the adrenaline replaced by a thick, weary silence. Evelyn was left with the respiratory therapist to clean up. As she gently removed the EKG leads from Simon Gable’s still-warm chest, her fingers brushed against the IV site on his left hand. It was slightly puffy. Not dramatically so, but more than it had been an hour ago. She frowned, pressing gently. A tiny bit of erythema, a local reaction maybe. Or something else.

She said nothing. She finished her duties, her body moving on autopilot while her mind raced.

At 4 AM, she was in the office of Donna Pierce, the Director of Nursing for the night shift. Donna was a former ICU nurse with a reputation for efficiency and a low tolerance for what she called “night shift fantasties.” She listened, her expression impassive, as Evelyn laid it out: the three previous deaths, the similar timelines, the sudden physiological crashes with no clear cause, and now Simon Gable.

“Evelyn,” Donna said finally, steepling her fingers on her tidy desk. “You’ve had a tough night. A code is stressful. It’s natural to look for reasons, for patterns, especially when we lose a patient we thought was improving.”

“This isn’t grief, Donna. It’s data. Four patients. All recovering. All dying suddenly between days seven and fourteen of their admission. On this floor.”

“Medicine is not a perfect science. Elderly patients with comorbidities can crash without warning. You know that.”

“I do. But this frequency? This similarity?”

“What are you suggesting?” Donna’s voice took on an edge. “A pathogen? We have infection control protocols. A medication error? Pharmacy audits those ADCs nightly.”

“I don’t know what I’m suggesting,” Evelyn admitted, frustration bleeding into her tone. “But I’m suggesting we *look*. Review the charts together. Flag any commonalities. Maybe it’s a contaminant in a saline flush, or a batch of something. Maybe it’s… I don’t know. But something is wrong.”

Donna sighed, a sound of profound weariness. “Evelyn, you’re one of my best. But what you’re describing is a statistical clustering. It happens. If I took every pattern a tired nurse saw at 3 AM and launched a full investigation, we’d have no staff left. The mortality rate on Gen Med is within expected parameters. Each of these cases was reviewed by the attending and the mortality committee. None were flagged as unusual.”

“They’re not looking for *this* pattern. They’re looking at them individually.”

“Because that’s how medicine is practiced. Patient by patient.” Donna stood up, a clear signal the conversation was over. “I appreciate your vigilance. Truly. File an incident report for Mr. Gable, of course. Detail your clinical observations. But as for this… theory… I need you to let it go. For your own sake. This kind of fixation can burn you out.”

The dismissal was polite, firm, and absolute. Evelyn walked back to the seventh floor as the first grey light of dawn touched the windows. The day shift was arriving, the night’s quiet dissolving into the morning bustle. Handoff was a blur. She mentioned Simon Gable’s code in a flat, professional tone. She did not mention her pattern.

Driving home, the lack of sleep buzzed behind her eyes like static, but her mind was terrifyingly clear. Donna was wrong. The committee was wrong. The puffy IV site on Simon Gable’s hand—it wasn’t conclusive, but it was a thread. All the patients had IVs. All would have received flushes, medications. What if it wasn’t the *what*, but the *when*?

At home, in the unnatural quiet of her living room with the blinds drawn against the day, she opened her personal laptop. She couldn’t access patient records, but she could think. She made a timeline on a notepad. *Day 7-14. Nighttime. Rapid cardiovascular or respiratory collapse.* It pointed to something acute, something introduced. An air embolus? Impossible at a slow KVO rate. A toxin? But they shared no common medications except perhaps saline flushes.

Her thoughts snagged on the per-diem pharmacist, Leo. He was a variable. But he signed out meds for hundreds of patients. It proved nothing.

Then she remembered a detail from Walter Brennan’s chart, the first one. A minor note from a day nurse: ‘Patient reports mild burning at IV site overnight, resolved by morning.’ She’d assumed a bit of phlebitis. What if it was more?

Her certainty hardened, cold and heavy as a stone. It was real. She had no proof, no motive, no mechanism. But she knew. Someone, or something, was killing recovering patients on the seventh floor. And she was the only one who had seen the shadow of it moving through the quiet hours. The next step wasn’t convincing Donna. It was finding proof. And the next patient on the timeline was already in the building, ticking toward their seventh night. She just had to find them before the pattern repeated. Sleep was impossible now. The hum of the fluorescent lights had followed her home, and in its monotonous drone, she heard a countdown.
