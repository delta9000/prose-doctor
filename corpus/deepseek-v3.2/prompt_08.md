The scratch of her pen was the only sound in the Scriptorium of the Argent Scroll. For Elara, it was the sound of order, of history preserved, of truth given permanence. Her world was the vellum before her, illuminated by the precise, golden beam of a sunslate lamp. The scent of iron-gall ink and ageing parchment was the air of her kingdom.

She was recopying the *Annals of the Sun-Kings*, Volume IX, detailing the reign of King Valerius the Stern. The original, bound in worn saffron leather, lay open on her oak stand. Her task was simple, sacred: transfer the words, the precise, elegant High Script of her predecessor, Scribe-Captain Alban, onto fresh, cream-coloured vellum for the Royal Archive. It was rote work, comforting in its monotony. *Valerius, in the seventeenth year of his reign, did pacify the eastern province of Marakand, securing the Silvervein Pass and bringing the light of the Sun-Throne to the rebellious clans.*

Elara’s pen moved, a faithful echo. Her mind, however, often wandered into the margins of history. She had always been a noticer. It was why, three years ago, she’d been quietly reassigned from the more prestigious role of chronicling current court events to this silent, solitary duty of recopying old records. She had asked too many questions about minor discrepancies in grain shipment logs.

Now, as she finished the paragraph on the pacification of Marakand, her eye caught on the next entry. It was a routine note on taxation. *And so, a levy of one part in twenty was established upon the wool yield of the Marakand highlands, to be remitted to the Crown’s granary at Everhold.* She dipped her pen, ready to continue.

But something nagged. A faint, almost invisible ghost of a word beneath the crisp black ink of Alban’s hand. A palimpsest. It was not uncommon; vellum was precious, and older, irrelevant texts were sometimes scrubbed and reused. But the *Annals* were not supposed to be on scraped vellum. They were primary records, kept on fresh sheets.

Setting her own pen down, Elara adjusted the sunslate lamp, angling the beam to graze the surface of the old page. She leaned in, her nose almost touching the parchment. There, beneath *‘one part in twenty’*, she saw it: the faint, brownish shadow of older ink, forming a different word. She traced the spectral lines with a careful fingertip. It wasn't a word. It was a number.

A ‘V’.

In the old numeric script, ‘V’ was five.

*One part in five.*

Her breath caught. A levy of twenty percent was harsh but not unusual for a pacified region. A levy of *fifty* percent was ruinous, a sentence of famine. She stared. Was it a simple error? An initial draft hastily corrected? But Scribe-Captain Alban was famed for his flawless, premeditated hand. He never blotched.

Heart beginning a soft, quick drum against her ribs, Elara turned back a few pages. She found an earlier entry on the initial assessment of Marakand’s resources. The text described *‘fields of plenty, herds like clouds upon the hills.’* She angled the light again. Beneath *‘plenty’*, the ghost-word was *‘sufficiency’*. Beneath *‘herds like clouds’*, the shadows whispered *‘diminished flocks’*.

A cold trickle, like ice melt, started down her spine. This was not a correction. It was a transformation.

For the next three hours, Elara worked with a silent, focused intensity that had nothing to do with her official task. She became an archaeologist of the false. She examined entries not for their content, but for their scars. She found them everywhere in Volume IX. A description of a ‘loyal’ baron originally labelled ‘dissenting’. A report of a ‘minor skirmish’ that had once been a ‘costly battle’. A record of ‘generous Crown relief’ following a flood that had initially been logged as ‘inadequate supplies dispatched’.

The alterations were masterful. The new text was always in Alban’s impeccable hand, perfectly matched to the surrounding script. The erasures beneath were so skillfully done that only the barest chemical ghosts remained, visible only under the sharpest, most oblique light. Someone had not just edited history; they had attempted to erase it utterly, to sew new cloth so fine the seams were invisible.

*Why?* The question screamed in the quiet of the Scriptorium. Valerius the Stern had died forty years ago. His son, King Lycomedes, now ruled, an old man himself. What danger lay in the truth of his father’s reign?

The political danger of knowing struck her then, a physical blow. She was alone. The vast, vaulted chamber, lined with shelves holding ten thousand lies, suddenly felt like a tomb. Scribe-Captain Alban was long dead. Who had done this? Who had ordered it? The current Master of Scrolls, Oren? The Lord Chancellor? The King himself?

She had to verify it. She needed another source, something untainted.

Her mind raced through the archive’s taxonomy. The *Annals* were the official narrative. But there were other records: tax ledgers, military dispatches, private correspondence from Crown officials, all stored in the Catacombs—the labyrinthine lower archives where incomplete or non-canonical documents were kept. If the alterations were as systematic as they appeared, those too might have been purged. But they were vast, poorly indexed. A single, overlooked letter might remain.

Elara’s shift was ending. The great bronze bell in the tower tolled the fifth hour. With a calm she did not feel, she closed the original *Annals* and her own fresh copy, blotting her half-finished page. She tidied her desk, her movements automatic. She nodded to the few other night-shift scribes as she left the Scriptorium, her face a polite mask.

Instead of heading to the scribes’ dormitory, she took a narrow, spiralling stairwell down, deep into the bedrock of the palace complex. The air grew cool and damp. Here were the Catacombs. A lone, ancient lamp-warden sat dozing at a wooden desk. Elara flashed her scribe’s badge—it granted access to all archival levels—and murmured something about verifying a fiscal date for her recopying work. The warden waved her in, uninterested.

The Catacombs were a stark contrast to the orderly Scriptorium. Parchment tubes were stacked in rough heaps. Mildewed ledgers spilled from rusted bins. It was a graveyard of paperwork. Her goal was the ‘Marakand Containers’, records from the eastern province filed by year.

She found the section, a grim, dusty alcove. Using her small hand-lamp, she searched. Most of the official ledgers from Valerius’s seventeenth year were gone, their shelf tags empty. Her heart sank. But then, behind a crate of broken seal-impressions, she saw a small, untreated wooden box, labelled in a chaotic scrawl: *‘Marak. Pvt. Lettrs. Unsorted.’*

Her fingers trembled as she lifted the lid. Inside was a jumble of wax-sealed scrolls, their ribbons faded. She sifted through them, looking for dates, names. One, its seal cracked and missing, caught her eye. The address was to a ‘Master Finlay, Wool Guild, Everhold’. The sender was a ‘Captain Jorun, Garrison Commander, Silvervein Keep’. The date placed it in the autumn of Valerius’s seventeenth year.

She unrolled it. The script was hurried, functional.

*“Finlay—The situation here is dire. The Crown’s levy collectors arrived yesterday. They are taking one part in two of all fleece, not the one in five we were promised after the surrender. There is no reasoning with them. They have the Sun-King’s seal. The clans are not ‘pacified’ as the bulletins say; they are starving and desperate. We found three villages abandoned, the people fled into the mountains. I have written to my commander, but receive only orders to enforce the levy. This will spark more violence. I fear we are not keeping peace, but brewing a poison. Burn this.”*

Elara slumped against the cold stone wall, the letter crackling in her grip. Here was the unvarnished truth, a scream from the past that had somehow escaped the fire. The original ghost in the *Annals* had said one in five. Even that had been a lie, softened from the brutal reality of one in two. The official history had then softened it further to one in twenty. A rebellion caused by famine-level extraction had been rewritten as a pacification of unruly clans. A military quagmire was now a glorious victory.

The scope of the deception was staggering. It wasn’t just about making a king look better. It was about creating a foundational myth. Valerius the Stern hadn’t wisely integrated a rich province; he had brutally crushed and bled a struggling one. The current stability of the east, such as it was, was built on this buried history of theft and suppression. King Lycomedes’s entire legitimacy, the peace of his reign, was narrated by these lies.

And she knew.

The weight of the knowledge was a stone in her gut. She carefully re-rolled the letter. What to do with it? Taking it would be theft, and its absence might be noticed if anyone else ever came looking—a dangerous if. Leaving it here was to leave a piece of truth vulnerable to discovery and destruction.

As she agonised, she heard a sound that froze her blood: a soft footfall on stone, and the distinct, metallic slide of a scabbard tip against a wall. Someone else was in the Catacombs.

Elara blew out her hand-lamp, plunging herself into absolute blackness. She held her breath, listening. The footsteps were measured, unhurried. Not the shuffling of the lamp-warden. They stopped not far from her alcove. A beam of light—brighter, whiter than her oil lamp, the light of a modern sun-crystal—swept over the entrance to her hiding place.

She pressed herself into the shadows behind the crate, the letter clutched to her chest. The light lingered on the empty spaces where the ledgers should have been. A low, thoughtful hum echoed in the stillness. Then the light swept away, and the footsteps receded, fading back up the passage.

She waited in the dark for a count of five hundred, her heart hammering against the parchment in her hands. Someone else was looking for the same records. Someone who knew they should be missing.

The political danger was no longer an abstract concept. It had a footstep, and a light.

She made a decision. With frantic care, she felt for the box and tucked the letter back into its random place, not where she found it, but deeper down. She then pried up a loose flagstone at the base of the wall, scooped out a handful of damp earth, placed the letter inside, and replaced the stone. It was not safe, but it was hidden differently.

She crept out of the Catacombs, past the dozing warden, and up into the palace proper. The corridors, now lit by evening glow-orbs, felt alien and watchful. Every passing guardsman, every clerk, seemed a potential agent of the lie.

Back in her tiny cell in the scribes’ quarters, Elara sat on her narrow bed. She was a keeper of words. That was her entire identity. Now, the words she was sworn to preserve were false, and the true words were a death sentence. To speak of it was treason—not against the crown, but against the official reality the crown had constructed. To stay silent was to become complicit, to let her pen continue weaving the shroud that smothered the past.

She thought of the footsteps in the dark. They knew the records were compromised. They would be watching for anomalies, for questions. Her own reassignment for asking questions about grain logs proved they had systems for dealing with curious scribes.

Sleep was impossible. As dawn tinged the high window of her cell, Elara made a plan. She would not confront. She would not speak. She would observe. She would continue her recopying work with perfect fidelity, becoming a model of unquestioning diligence. But now, her true work would happen in the margins. She would look for more ghosts in other volumes, map the pattern of the alterations. And she would need to find others, not to confess to, but to assess. Was the Master of Scrolls, Oren, a knowing architect or a duped guardian? Were any of her fellow scribes awake in the silent prison of the lie?

The bell tolled for morning shift. Elara rose, washed her face, and smoothed her dun-coloured scribe’s robe. She walked to the Scriptorium of the Argent Scroll, took her seat at her desk, and opened Volume IX of the *Annals of the Sun-Kings* to where she had left off. She dipped her pen in black ink.

She began to copy, her hand steady, her face a placid pool. Inside, a storm raged. The scratch of her pen was no longer the sound of order. It was the sound of a key, turning in a lock deep within her mind, opening a door onto a dark and terrifying landscape where history was a weapon, and truth the most dangerous thing in the world to know.
