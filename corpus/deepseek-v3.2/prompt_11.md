The rain fell on St. Bartholomew’s Cemetery with a soft, insistent rhythm, the kind that soaked through wool and seeped into bones. Umbrellas bloomed like black mushrooms around the open grave. Martin stood apart, under the dripping branches of a sycamore, his coat collar turned up. He watched the cluster of mourners—vague, elderly faces from his father’s bridge club, a few distant cousins he hadn’t seen in twenty years—and felt nothing but a hollow, cold distance.

From under her own umbrella, a compact, severe black one, Elara watched Martin. He hadn’t approached the grave. He hadn’t taken a program from the funeral director. He was just *loitering*, like a spectator at a somber play. It was typical. Her hands, clenched around the umbrella’s handle, were white-knuckled. The pearl ring their mother had left her dug into her finger. Of course he’d stand apart. Martin had made a career of standing apart.

The vicar’s voice, a gentle murmur against the rain, carried the familiar, comforting words. *Ashes to ashes, dust to dust.* Elara focused on the polished mahogany casket suspended over the raw earth. It looked absurdly grand, out of place in the wet, green mess of the cemetery. Their father, Arthur, had been a man of simple, quiet habits—pipe tobacco, birdwatching, thick biographies of naval admirals. This ostentation was Elara’s doing. A final, futile act of filial duty, an attempt to bury him with a dignity she felt he’d lacked in his final, confused years. She wondered if Martin knew she’d paid for it all.

Martin was counting the headstones in the row behind his father’s plot. Fourteen. The oldest was from 1898, the name weathered to a ghost. He thought about time, about how it sanded everything down to nothing. His father’s face, in the hospital bed three days ago, had already seemed less real, a waxen imitation. The argument—the last one, seven years ago—felt more immediate. Arthur’s voice, trembling with a disappointment so profound it had curdled into contempt: *You throw your life away like it’s nothing. You have no anchor, Martin. You’re just… adrift.* He’d left the next morning, and they had not spoken again. The silence had become a third entity in the family, heavier than any presence.

A soft, collective sigh moved through the mourners as the casket began its slow, mechanical descent. Elara’s breath hitched. It was the finality of the movement, the quiet whir of the device. This was it. The man who had read her *The Chronicles of Narnia*, who had taught her to distinguish a chaffinch from a goldfinch by its song, was being lowered into the dark, wet English ground. And her brother, her only remaining link to that man, was lurking under a tree like a stranger. A hot spike of anger, familiar and bitter, pushed the grief aside.

The service concluded. The mourners began to drift toward their cars, murmuring condolences, their shoes sinking into the softened turf. Elara turned, her movements sharp, and walked directly toward the sycamore. She had to get through the reception at the old house. She needed to establish the terms.

“You’re here,” she said, stopping a few feet from him. Her voice was tight, polished like the coffin.

Martin looked at her. She was older, of course. The girl who had been all sharp angles and furious intelligence was now a woman of razor-edged precision. Her black dress was impeccably tailored, her hair a dark, controlled sweep. She looked like a lawyer or a senior executive, which, he supposed, she was. He felt scruffy, unmoored beside her.

“I am,” he said. “It was in the email.”

“You got the email. A modern miracle.”

“I came,” he said, a faint edge in his own voice now. “Isn’t that the important thing?”

“Is it?” She held his gaze. The rain pattered on the leaves above them, a private, dismal soundtrack. “Why? For closure? To make sure he was really gone?”

Martin flinched, almost imperceptibly. He looked past her, to the grave where two men in overalls were beginning to shovel the damp earth. The thud of soil on wood was a dull, awful sound. “I don’t know, Elara. Why does anyone go to a funeral?”

“Duty,” she said, the word quick and hard. “Respect.”

“He didn’t respect my choices. Why should I respect his death?”

It was a cruel thing to say, and he knew it the moment it left his mouth. He saw her eyes flash, not with tears, but with a pure, incandescent fury. She took a step closer. The scent of her perfume, something cold and floral, cut through the petrichor.

“You selfish bastard,” she whispered, the words meant for him alone. “You have no idea. No idea what the last five years were like. The calls in the middle of the night because he’d forgotten how to use the kettle. The slow, humiliating erosion of him. Where was your *choice* then? Where was your precious freedom while I was cleaning up his messes?”

Guilt, sour and immediate, flooded Martin’s throat. He had known, in vague terms. The forwarded emails from doctors, the terse updates from Elara that he’d never answered. He’d sent money once, a substantial sum from a photography job in Patagonia. She’d sent it back. *We don’t need your guilt-money, Martin.*

“You could have asked for help,” he muttered, but it sounded weak, drowned by the rain.

“Ask?” She let out a short, brittle laugh. “You were in Thailand. Or Peru. Or God knows where. Your address was a post office box in Berlin. You made yourself unfindable. You *wanted* to be unfindable.”

She was right. He had. The weight of home, of history, of Arthur’s disapproval and Elara’s accusatory competence, had been a suitcase he was desperate to leave on some distant platform. He’d traveled light, collecting moments instead of responsibilities: sunsets over Angkor Wat, the faces of fishermen in Lisbon, the stark silence of the Icelandic tundra. But standing here now, the ghosts of those places felt insubstantial, wisps against the solid, damp reality of his father’s grave and his sister’s wrath.

“We should go,” Elara said, her voice all business again. She glanced at her watch, a slim silver thing. “People will be arriving at the house. There are things to discuss.”

“What things?”

“The house. The estate. The *things*, Martin.” She turned and began walking toward the line of cars, assuming he would follow.

He looked back one last time. The grave was half-filled now, a mound of dark brown against the green. *Ashes to ashes.* With a sigh that came from his very core, he followed her.

***

The house on Cedar Lane was a modest Edwardian semi, now feeling cavernous and echoey with grief and damp coats. It smelled of old books, lemon polish, and the faint, lingering trace of Arthur’s pipe tobacco—a scent that struck them both with the force of a physical blow as they stepped inside. Martin stopped in the doorway, disoriented. Nothing had changed. The same worn Persian runner in the hall, the same watercolor of the Sussex Downs above the radiator, the same clock on the mantelpiece, its tick suddenly audible in the quiet.

For Elara, the familiarity was a checklist. The rug needed cleaning. The watercolor was faded. The clock was five minutes fast, as it always had been. She moved through the rooms with a hostess’s efficiency, directing the caterers (a simple, tasteful spread of sandwiches and cakes), accepting condolences with a stiff nod. She was the commander of this sad, final operation.

Martin drifted into the living room. A group of elderly neighbors were reminiscing about Arthur’s prize-winning roses. He escaped to the bookshelves, running a finger along the familiar spines: the naval histories, the birdwatching guides, the complete works of Dickens. He pulled out a thick volume on Admiral Nelson. A faded bookmark fell out—a train ticket to Brighton, dated fifteen years prior. He remembered that day. A rare family trip. He and Elara, teenagers then, had spent the entire journey bickering over the window seat. Their father had finally shouted, *Enough!*, his voice cracking with a fatigue that Martin now understood. He slipped the ticket back into the book.

From across the room, Elara watched him touch the books. A part of her wanted to scream at him to stop, that his sentimental browsing was a desecration. He didn’t deserve these memories. He’d forfeited them. But another part, a smaller, locked-away part, saw the slight slump of his shoulders, the careful way he handled the book, and felt a pang of something that wasn’t anger.

“Your father spoke of you,” said a voice at Martin’s elbow. It was Mrs. Allardyce from next door, her eyes magnified by thick glasses. “He showed me your photographs. The ones of the mountains. He said, ‘My boy has an eye for it.’ He was very proud, you know.”

Martin felt his throat tighten. He managed a nod, unable to speak. *Proud?* The word clashed violently with the memory of Arthur’s last words to him. Had time, or illness, softened him? Or was Mrs. Allardyce just being kind?

Elara overheard. Her spine stiffened. *Proud?* While she was managing power-of-attorney and arguing with social workers, their father was showing off Martin’s pictures of Nepal? The unfairness of it was a physical pain. She turned sharply and walked into the kitchen, needing a moment away from the stifling goodwill.

The kitchen was warm, steamy from the caterer’s urn. She leaned against the old Formica counter, the one with the chip in the corner from when Martin had dropped a jar of marmalade, aged ten. She stared out at the small, rain-sodden garden. The bird feeder was empty. Arthur had always kept it full. She’d forgotten.

Martin found her there. The room was silent but for the hum of the refrigerator. He stood in the doorway, holding two cups of tea he’d taken from a passing tray.

“I thought you might…” he offered, holding one out.

She looked at the cup, then at his face. He looked exhausted, older than his thirty-eight years, with new lines around eyes that were the same hazel as her own. “Thank you,” she said, her voice flat. She took it.

They stood in silence, sipping the too-strong, lukewarm tea. The tension was a live wire between them, humming with every unsaid thing: the missed Christmases, the unreturned calls, the vast, lonely territory of their father’s decline.

“Mrs. Allardyce said he was proud of my photos,” Martin said finally, not looking at her.

Elara put her cup down with a sharp click. “Yes. Well. He had a funny way of showing it to you, didn’t he?”

“Did he ever… mention me? Near the end?”

She wanted to wound him. She wanted to say, *No. He forgot you existed.* But the truth was more complicated, and she was so tired. “Sometimes. He’d get confused. Ask when you were coming home from school. Or he’d see a documentary about mountains and say your name.” She paused. “Mostly, he was just… quiet.”

Martin nodded, swallowing hard. He looked around the kitchen. “Nothing’s changed.”

“Everything’s changed,” she corrected, her tone weary. “It just looks the same. That’s the trick.” She folded her arms. “We need to talk about the house. The will is straightforward. Half each. I’ll buy you out. I’ve had it valued.”

Of course she had. Martin felt a surge of resentment. “Just like that? Sell it off?”

“What would you do with it, Martin?” she asked, not unkindly, but with a devastating practicality. “Would you live here? Tend the garden? Join the neighbourhood watch? Or would it just be another thing you walk away from?”

He had no answer. The thought of staying in this quiet, ghost-filled street was impossible. But the thought of it not being here, of this touchstone being erased, was a different kind of pain.

“There are things in here,” he said softly. “Not just the big stuff. Little things.”

“Then take what you want,” she said, her business-like facade back. “I don’t care about the *things*. I just need to settle this.”

He turned to leave, then stopped. His eyes fell on the kitchen door, the one leading to the garden. On the inside, faint but still visible, were a series of pencil marks and dates. *Elara, age 10. Martin, age 7.* A growth chart, kept by their mother.

He walked over and touched the highest mark, labeled *Martin, age 14.* “I’d forgotten this was here,” he murmured.

Elara came to stand beside him. She saw her own name, her own progression from a small child to a teenager who had just surpassed him. The physical evidence of their shared history, in this most mundane of places, was disarming. The anger bled away, leaving only a vast, aching sadness.

“Do you remember,” Martin said, his voice barely above a whisper, “the day he measured us for this? I was jumping up and down, trying to be taller. You were so annoyed. You said it was unscientific.”

A faint, almost imperceptible smile touched Elara’s lips. “I did. And you had a plaster on your knee from falling out of the apple tree.”

“You put iodine on it. It stung like hell.”

“You screamed the house down.”

They stood there, side by side, staring at the faded lines on the doorframe. The rain tapped against the kitchen window. For a moment, the last seven years fell away. They were not the dutiful daughter and the prodigal son. They were just two kids who had grown up in this house, who had shared a life marked in inches and years on a doorframe.

Martin’s hand, resting against the wood, was very close to Elara’s. She didn’t move hers away. The silence between them was no longer charged with hostility, but with a shared, profound loss—not just for their father, but for the people they had been to each other, and the time that had been stolen.

It was Elara who broke the spell, but gently. She let out a long, shaky breath. “The solicitor’s appointment is on Tuesday,” she said, but the edge was gone from her voice. It was just information now.

“Okay,” Martin said. He didn’t move. “I’ll be there.”

She finally looked at him, really looked at him. She saw the boy in the man’s face, the brother who had once smuggled a frog into her bed. She saw his own grief, raw and confused and real. It wasn’t her grief, it wasn’t orderly or dutiful, but it was there.

“There’s a box,” she said, hesitantly. “Up in the attic. With some of your old things. School projects. That ridiculous rock collection. You should take it.”

It was an olive branch, small and brittle.

Martin met her gaze and gave a slow, grateful nod. “Thank you.”

The moment of connection passed, fragile as a soap bubble. From the living room, they heard the vicar’s laughter, a booming sound that felt alien in the house. The demands of the present reasserted themselves. But something had shifted, infinitesimally. The door between them, locked and barred for so long, had not swung open, but a faint light now showed beneath it.

Elara turned and walked back toward the noise of the mourners, her heels clicking on the familiar tiles. Martin stayed for another minute, his fingers tracing the last pencil mark on the doorframe before he, too, turned and followed her back into the wake.
