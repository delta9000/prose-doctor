The smell of rotting lilies clung to the air of the Mossward, the city’s old quarter. It wasn’t the flowers, Elara knew, but the sweet, putrid scent of the lung-fever that had seeped into the very stones. She moved through the narrow, shadowed lanes with the weary familiarity of a ghost, her basket of bundled herbs a small, defiant weight against her hip. Comfrey for knitting bones, yarrow for staunching blood, and the precious, bitter willow bark for fever. And, tucked in waxed cloth at the very bottom, a small pouch of powdered belladonna. A sliver for pain, a pinch for sleep, a little more for a journey from which there was no return. She carried that responsibility like a cold stone in her chest.

Her destination was a crumbling tenement leaning precariously over the Green Canal, which currently flowed a sluggish, suspicious grey. The door was ajar, a silent invitation that was also a warning. Inside, the air was thick with the heat of sickness and the sour smell of fear. A young man, perhaps eighteen, thrashed on a pallet, his skin gleaming with sweat, his breaths a wet, crackling struggle. His mother, a woman with eyes like burnt-out coals, wrung her hands by the hearth where a pathetic flame licked at a pot of water.

“Mistress Elara,” the woman whispered, as if speaking too loud would shatter her son. “They said you wouldn’t come. They said the priests are watching.”

“Let them watch,” Elara said, her voice low and rough from sleepless nights. She set her basket down and knelt, the damp straw of the floor biting through her skirts. She laid a cool hand on the boy’s forehead. Burning. His pulse under her fingers was a frantic, fluttering bird. “His name?”

“Joren.”

“Joren,” Elara repeated, and the boy’s glassy eyes flickered towards the sound. “I am here. You must be still.”

She worked methodically, the ritual of it a bulwark against the despair that pressed in from the corners of the room. From her basket came a clay cup. She crumbled willow bark into it, added a pinch of ground elderflower to ease his breathing, and poured hot water from the pot over it, stirring with a clean hazel twig. The steamy, astringent scent cut through the miasma.

“Lift his head,” she instructed the mother. Together they got the first sips into him, past cracked lips. The fitful thrashing subsided slightly, not cured, but soothed. Next, she took a jar of goose fat infused with rosemary and lavender, and rubbed it in strong, circular motions on his chest. The camphoraceous heat would help break the congestion in his lungs. As she worked, she hummed an old, wordless tune her grandmother had taught her, a melody meant for knitting skin and settling spirit, nothing more.

“Will he…?” the mother began, the question hanging like a noose.

Elara didn’t look up. “The fever must break. The drink will help. The salve will help. You must get as much clean water and thin broth into him as you can. The body fights the battle; we only give it arms.”

She left more willow bark, instructions whispered with fierce clarity, and a small, wrapped honey cake for the woman, who had likely eaten nothing herself in days. Payment was a single copper coin and a handful of dried beans, which Elara accepted with a nod. As she stepped back into the oppressive air of the lane, she felt the weight of eyes. Not the mother’s grateful, hollow gaze, but other, colder ones from behind shuttered windows. The Mossward was a web of suspicion, and she was a fly who moved too freely through its strands.

The walk back to her cottage, a low, timber-framed building nestled where the city wall met a forgotten patch of scrubland, was a gauntlet of silent accusations. A woman yanked a playing child indoors as she passed. Two old men by a tanner’s yard fell silent, their eyes following her basket. The fear of the plague had curdled into something else: a need for a culprit, a source for the divine wrath.

Her sanctuary was her garden, a riot of order behind a wattle fence. Here, monk’s hood stood tall beside valerian, their uses and dangers known intimately to her. Inside, the single room was clean and spare, dominated by a heavy table strewn with drying plants, a mortar and pestle, clay jars, and rolls of linen. The hearth was cold. She was placing the new willow bark strips on a rack when the knock came. Not the timid tap of the sick or desperate, but three firm, authoritative thuds.

She opened the door to the sun-blasted street, squinting. Framed in the light was Brother Aldous, a priest of the Silver Flame. He was a tall man, once robust, now grown gaunt and sharp-edged with zealous hunger. His robes were impeccably clean, a stark rebuke to the filth of the district.

“Mistress Elara.” His voice was a dry parchment rustle.

“Brother Aldous. Do you bring a blessing, or do you require one?” She did not step aside to invite him in. The space was hers.

“I bring a warning.” His eyes, pale as winter sky, swept past her into the dim interior, taking in the hanging herbs, the jars of unguents, the strange shapes of root and flower. “Your… activities are causing unrest.”

“My activities are changing soiled bandages, boiling water, and preparing teas for fever,” she said flatly. “The unrest is caused by the dying.”

“Do not be glib.” He took a half-step forward, invading her threshold. “You move among the sick where others fear to tread. You handle the poisons that grow in your garden. You speak in whispers and give charms.”

“They are not charms. They are medicines. The whispers are instructions so a terrified wife does not forget them.”

“And the results?” he pressed. “How many have you saved?”

The cold stone in her chest grew heavier. “Some. Not enough. The fever is strong.”

“The fever is a scourge sent to test our faith! To purge sin!” His composure cracked, revealing the fervent fire beneath. “And yet you, a woman with no husband, no confessor, with your pagan lore, you presume to thwart the will of the Divine with… weeds and whispers. People are saying you draw your power from a different source. That you bargain for some lives with others.”

The accusation hung in the air, more poisonous than any belladonna. Witchcraft. It was the old, blunt weapon, newly sharpened by panic.

“Who says this?” she asked, her voice dangerously quiet.

“The faithful see what they see. A boy, Joren, in the tenements by the canal. He lives, while the baker’s devout son died just two doors down yesterday. Why does one flourish and the other perish? Where is the justice in that, unless there is interference? An… unnatural selection.”

Elara felt a hot spike of anger. “Joren lives because his mother forces broth down his throat every hour and I gave him medicine to lower his fever. The baker’s son was bled with leeches by the barber-surgeon until he had no strength left to fight. That is the only ‘unnatural’ thing that happened to him.”

Brother Aldous’s face tightened. “You condemn the established practices of the city, then. You set your own wisdom above that of men and the church.”

“I set the evidence of my eyes above foolishness,” she shot back, then bit her tongue. Arguing was oil on his flames.

“Your eyes are clouded, woman. Your soul is in peril. Cease your ministrations. Attend mass. Pray for forgiveness and guidance. Let the city endure its purification under the care of its priests and its God.” His warning was delivered. It was an ultimatum. He turned to leave, then paused. “The guard captain’s daughter has taken ill. The household is in quarantine, under watch of the faithful. You would be… unwise to approach there.”

He strode away, his robe stirring the dust. Elara closed the door slowly, leaning her back against the rough wood. The confrontation left her trembling, not with fear, but with a furious, impotent rage. He spoke of souls, while she held cooling flesh in her hands. He spoke of divine will, while she fought for every single, rasping breath.

As dusk bled into the dark, the rage cooled, hardening into a knot of dread. The priest’s visit was not just a warning; it was a positioning of pieces on a board. The guard captain’s daughter—a powerful man now touched by the plague, his fear a potent tool. If she could help… but it was a trap. A blatant one.

She lit a single tallow candle, its feeble light pushing back the shadows only a few feet. The silence of the cottage, usually a comfort, felt accusatory. She walked to her worktable, her fingers tracing the smooth wood grain, the cool clay of a jar, the rough, dried leaves of a mullein stalk. This was her language, her text. She knew the slowing of a pulse under her fingers, the change in a fevered skin, the subtle shift when pain gave way to sleep. She knew the precise line where remedy became toxin.

But what did she know of the world beyond her garden wall? Of politics and piety and the minds of frightened men? Brother Aldous saw a world of spirits and sin. She saw one of humors and herbs. They were speaking different tongues, and his was the one with the power to have her burned.

A private moment of doubt yawned before her, vast and cold. Was it arrogance? This conviction that she was right? The barber-surgeon, the priests, the wise-women who prescribed prayers and amulets—they were all certain too. Who was she, an unmarried woman with dirty nails and a head full of old wives’ tales, to stand against them all?

She thought of Joren, his breath easing. She thought of the baker’s son, pale and empty. She had done nothing wrong. She had only tried to tip the scales, ever so slightly, towards life. But in the eyes of the city, that effort itself was now the crime. To succeed where others failed was proof of diabolical aid.

Her gaze fell on the small pouch of belladonna. The nightshade. Beautiful, deadly. A few grains could bring peace from unbearable pain. A little more could bring the ultimate peace. The power to grant a merciful end was still a power. Was that, too, witchcraft? Or was it the final, terrible compassion of a healer?

She opened a small, iron-bound chest at the foot of her bed. Inside, wrapped in a piece of undyed wool, was her legacy: a handwritten, illustrated book, its pages stained with use and time. Her grandmother’s hand, her mother’s additions, and now her own notes in the margins. Drawings of plants, charts of the moon’s phases for harvesting, recipes for poultices and tisanes. It was not magic. It was observation, trial, error, and hard-won knowledge passed through generations of women who had tended the sick in secret. It was all she was.

If she stopped now, on the priest’s command, she betrayed that line of women. She betrayed every Joren who might yet live. But if she continued, she would be painting a target on her own back. They would come for her book, for her garden, for her. They would call it a grimoire, her herbs demonic ingredients, her compassion a pact.

The candle guttered, sending a long, dancing shadow of the hanging herbs writhing across the wall like accusing fingers. Elara hugged herself, feeling very small and very alone in the vast, silent darkness of the plague-stricken city. The doubt was not about her craft; it was a cold, practical calculation of survival. Could she do more good by staying silent, by hiding, by letting the storm pass? Or was the act of hiding already a defeat?

A sound pierced the night—not a cry of pain, but the slow, solemn tolling of the death-bell from the septry, marking another soul delivered to the flame. One more. Always one more.

Elara straightened. She closed the chest, securing the latch. She blew out the candle, surrendering the room to the moonless dark. In the blackness, her decision crystallized, not as a blaze of courage, but as a simple, stubborn fact, as undeniable as the properties of her herbs.

She could not un-know what she knew. She could not un-see a sick child. The priest saw a world of souls to be saved or condemned. She saw a world of bodies to be tended. And her hands, whatever anyone called them or the power in them, were made for tending.

Tomorrow, she would go to the market for more honey. It soothed throats and made bitter medicine palatable. She would tend her garden. And she would listen. For the knock of the desperate, not the powerful. The plague would have its way, and the priests would have theirs. But in the spaces between, in the shadowed lanes and fever-ridden rooms, Elara would still have hers. Let them call it what they wanted.
