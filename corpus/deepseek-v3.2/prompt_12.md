The air in the tournament hall tastes of stale coffee and nervous sweat. Anatoly stares at the board, but he does not see the sixty-four squares. He sees a frozen battlefield, a silent scream trapped in lacquered wood. His king, a weighted piece of ebony, feels cold under his thumb, a sensation that travels up the arthritic knuckle of his index finger and settles like a shard of ice in his wrist. He has been holding it for three minutes. Or has it been ten? Time has become a viscous thing, thicker here at the table than anywhere else in the buzzing, cavernous room.

Across from him, the boy—Lucas, twenty-two, a prodigy from Oslo with a shock of white-blond hair and a habit of chewing the inside of his cheek—leans forward, elbows on the table, fingers steepled. He is a collection of sharp angles and impatient energy. He has already made his move, a sly, prophylactic knight retreat to e8, and now he is a predator waiting for the trap to spring. Anatoly can smell the boy’s confidence, a clean, citrus scent cutting through the hall’s staleness. It is the smell of a body that does not yet know pain.

Anatoly’s own move is obvious. *Push the h-pawn. Challenge the dark squares, soften the king’s shelter.* His hand hovers over the carved pawn. But his mind is not here. It is in Reykjavik, 1972, and the piece under his fingers is not cheap tournament plastic but heavy, cold Soviet stone. Fischer’s clock is ticking, a loud, mechanical *clack-clack-clack* that echoed in the hushed theatre. Fischer himself was a coiled spring of tics: adjusting his chair, plucking invisible lint from his sleeve, his breath a visible plume in the over-air-conditioned room. The crowd then was not this diffuse murmur of whispers and clicking camera shutters; it was a held breath, a tangible wall of expectation. Anatoly, at twenty-eight, felt their gaze like a physical pressure, a weight he transformed into fuel. He took Fischer’s bishop with his knight, the *click* of the capture as decisive as a gunshot, and he did not look at Bobby’s face. He looked at the newly powerful knight, its horse-head profile now dominating the center, and he felt not joy, but the pure, cold certainty of geometry.

“Grandmaster?” The arbiter’s voice is soft, a polite intrusion. Lucas’s cheek-chewing has stopped. He is watching Anatoly’s face, not the board, searching for a crack. Anatoly blinks. The frozen battlefield resolves into the present. The pieces are standard Staunton, the black ones slightly worn, their felt bottoms soft from ten thousand prior games. He moves his hand from the king, leaving a faint, damp print on the ebony, and pushes the h-pawn two squares. *H4.* The shove is harder than intended; the pawn wobbles, threatening to topple. Lucas’s eyes flicker with something—not surprise, but a clinical recognition. *He thinks the tremor is fear.* Anatoly tucks his offending hand into his lap, massaging the ache in the joint.

Lucas responds instantly, sliding his bishop to g4, pinning Anatoly’s knight on f3. It is an aggressive, almost insolent move, probing for weakness. The boy’s fingers are long and pale, the nails neatly trimmed. They move the pieces with an economic snap, no hesitation, no lingering touch. Anatoly remembers his own hands at that age. They were instruments of precision, capable of blitzing out thirty moves in a minute, the pieces flying across the board like extensions of his thought. He could feel the grain of the wood, the slight imperfection in the carving of a rook, and it would tell him nothing and everything. Now, the sensory memory is stronger than the sensation itself. His fingers are stiff, the skin like parchment. They remember more than they can perform.

He retreats the knight, a passive, solid move. *Solid. Safe. Old man’s chess.* The thought is a venomous whisper in his own mind. It is met by another memory, this one from his prime, the 1975 Olympiad in Nice. He was playing Black against the legendary Tigran Petrosian, the "Iron Tigran," a master of imperceptible defense. The crowd around their board was five deep, a rustling, coughing organism. Petrosian had a habit of polishing his glasses with a precise, endless circular motion whenever he was in deep thought, the lenses catching the chandelier light. Anatoly, feeling invincible, had sacrificed a rook. Not for a clear attack, but for *initiative*—a concept as tangible to him then as the marble floor under his feet. The gasp from the crowd was a wave of sound that washed over him. He remembered the heft of Petrosian’s captured rook, a beautiful piece of onyx, cool and smooth as it was lifted from the board. He had placed it beside his own clock with a quiet, final tap. He won in thirty-six moves. Afterward, Petrosian had shaken his hand and said nothing, his eyes behind the sparkling glasses unreadable.

Lucas is pressing. He’s castled kingside, his king finding safety while his queen and rooks begin to eye the open files. Anatoly can feel the game’s tectonic plates shifting. The position is becoming dynamic, fluid, the kind of game that favors the young, the quick, the fearless. He needs to create chaos, to make the board a place of fire and shadow, not slow, grinding pressure. He considers a pawn sacrifice, luring the white pawns forward, hoping to exploit the resulting weaknesses.

He reaches for his queen, the most powerful piece, yet so often vulnerable. As his fingers close around her carved crown, a different crown flashes in his mind. Not chess, but real. Moscow, 1980. The World Championship match against Korchnoi. The room was a political arena, thick with cigarette smoke and KGB agents posing as aides. Korchnoi, the defector, was a ghost of a man, pale and sweating, his eyes burning with a hatred that was as much a part of his game as his bishops. He would stare at Anatoly for minutes on end, not at the board, trying to break his spirit. The pieces were exquisite, ivory and rosewood, gifts from the Sports Committee. Anatoly, the Soviet hero, felt the weight of an empire on his shoulders with every move. He remembered the *physicality* of a critical middlegame, the way he’d leaned so far over the board his nose almost touched the squares, the scent of polished wood and Korchnoi’s nervous perspiration filling his senses. He’d found a combination, a breathtaking sequence of five forced moves ending in mate. When he delivered the final move—queen to h7, checkmate—he didn’t look at Korchnoi’s crumpling face. He looked at his own queen, triumphant on the edge of the board, and for a fleeting second, he felt not pride, but a profound, isolating emptiness. The crowd’s roar was muffled, as if heard through water.

The memory leaves him cold. He places his queen on c7, a probing move, offering a trade. It is not the fire he wanted. It is calculation. Prudence.

Lucas does not trade. He sidesteps, queen to d2, connecting his rooks, strengthening his position. Of course. The boy wants complexity, not simplification. He wants to prove himself against the legend in a tangled, messy fight. Anatoly feels a flush of heat rise up his neck. It is not anger, but a deep, humiliating shame. *He is not afraid of me. He is hunting me.*

The murmur of the crowd sharpens. They sense it too. The commentators’ low voices are a susurrus from the giant screens flanking the stage, where the position is displayed for the hundreds of spectators. He can feel their eyes, a thousand tiny points of pressure on his skin. He sees phones held aloft, recording the old lion’s last stand. Once, a gaze like that would have focused him, sharpening his mind to a laser point. Now it scatters his thoughts. He is a specimen under glass.

He must regain control. Not of the crowd, but of the narrative in his head. He forces himself to see only the geometry. The weak pawn on d5. The potential outpost on e5 for a knight. The slight congestion of White’s queenside pieces. He builds a plan, stone by mental stone. He will reroute his knight, bolster his center, wait. It is Petrosian’s chess. Defensive, resilient, waiting for one mistake.

But Lucas makes no mistake. He is a machine, churning out strong, purposeful moves. He advances his central pawns, creating a rolling wave of white that threatens to engulf the black position. Anatoly’s clock is becoming a concern. The digital numbers bleed away faster than the grains of sand in the old analog clocks of his youth. He has thirty minutes left to Lucas’s fifty. Time, his old ally in endgames, is now a silent enemy gnawing at his resources.

A rook exchange happens, the pieces clacking together in the center of the board before being removed. Lucas’s rook is taken off with a flick of his wrist. Anatoly lifts his own captured rook, and its weight feels symbolic. A fortress wall, dismantled.

The endgame approaches. The board is clearer now, pieces reduced. Anatoly has a bishop pair, but they are constrained by his own pawns. Lucas has a knight and a passed pawn on the c-file. A deep, familiar ache begins to pulse behind Anatoly’s eyes. It is the headache of sustained concentration, but deeper, rooted in a fatigue that has settled in his bones. He longs for the silence of his study, for the soft green felt of his personal board, for the absence of eyes.

Another memory surfaces, unbidden, sweet and painful. Not a tournament, but a simul. A small park in Krakow, 1989. He was giving a simultaneous exhibition on fifty boards. The crowd was different—families, children, enthusiasts pressed close, their breath visible in the autumn chill. He remembered a young girl, no more than ten, with serious brown eyes, who had fought him to a dead-drawn rook endgame in a simultaneous exhibition in Prague, 1987. He’d offered her a draw, and she had shaken her head, insisting on playing on. He’d finally won, after eighty moves, and when she stood up, she was not crying, but beaming with a fierce pride. He’d given her his signed scoresheet. The purity of that moment, the chess for its own sake, cuts through him now with a pang of loss. This is not that. This is performance, autopsy, decline.

“Grandmaster?” The arbiter again. Anatoly has drifted, his gaze fixed on a vacant square. He shakes his head minutely, a tremor he cannot control. He has to move. He brings his bishop to e6, blocking the passed pawn. It is a solid move. The only move.

Lucas captures it instantly with his knight. The *click* is loud in Anatoly’s ears. The loss of the bishop is like a door slamming shut. The position is now objectively lost. He knows it. Lucas knows it. The crowd, in a collective, whispered sigh, knows it.

Anatoly does not resign. Not yet. The remaining moves are a formality, a sad processional. He plays on, mechanically, his body moving pieces while his mind finally, fully detaches. He watches his own hands as if they belong to someone else. His king is chased across the board by the White knight and pawn, a tired tension in the air thickening.

He is back in Reykjavik, but at the end. The final handshake. Fischer’s grip was surprisingly weak, damp. The flashbulbs popped like a string of firecrackers, freezing his twenty-eight-year-old face in a mask of triumphant exhaustion. He had felt the world at his feet, an endless expanse of sixty-four squares waiting to be conquered.

The memory dissolves. The world contracts to this table, this lost game, these aching hands. He makes his last, useless move. A pawn push that accomplishes nothing.

Lucas moves his pawn to c2. One square from promotion. One square from queen. There is no defense.

Anatoly looks at the board, at the inevitable future. He sees not the pieces, but the arc of a life: the sharp, aggressive openings of youth, the complex, thrilling middlegames of prime, the long, grinding endgames of age… and finally, the resignation.

He does not look at Lucas’s eager, respectful face. He does not look at the crowd. He looks at his own king, the piece he has shielded, maneuvered, and ultimately failed. He reaches out. His fingers, gnarled and stiff, do not hover this time. They find the cool wood of the king’s crown, and with a slow, deliberate pressure, he tips it over onto its side.

It falls without a sound.

He does not say, “I resign.” The fallen piece says it for him.

For a moment, there is silence. Then, a ripple of applause begins, swelling into a wave. It is not for the winner, not yet. It is for him. For the career. For the end. Lucas stands, reaches across the board, his hand extended. Anatoly looks at it—smooth, young, victorious—and makes himself take it. The grip is firm, confident.

“Thank you for the game, Grandmaster,” Lucas says, his voice sincere.

Anatoly nods. He cannot speak. He releases the hand and begins to set his pieces back on their starting squares, a lifetime of habit. The touch of each piece is a farewell: the clumsy pawn, the angular knight, the solid rook, the diagonal-sliding bishop, the tall, proud queen. Finally, the king. He rights it, places it on its e8 square. *Back to the beginning.*

He stands. The applause washes over him, a warm, blurry noise. He manages a small, stiff bow in the direction of the crowd, a ghost of the dramatic gestures of his youth. Then he turns and walks away from the board, his steps slow on the polished floor. The spotlight follows him for a few paces before swinging back to illuminate Lucas, the new victor, already being interviewed.

Anatoly pushes through the heavy doors at the back of the hall, leaving the noise behind. The corridor is cool and quiet. He leans against the wall, closing his eyes. He does not think of the lost game, or the memories. He thinks of nothing at all. He simply feels the quiet, the solidity of the wall, the slow, steady, ancient beating of his own heart. The last tournament is over. The final move has been made. There is, at last, nothing more to do.
