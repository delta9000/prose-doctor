The rain wasn't water anymore, not really. It was the city weeping a thin, acidic grime that left grey traceries on the polymer-clad towers of the New Metro. Kael watched it streak the transparency of the shelter’s intake booth, distorting the face of the woman behind the screen into a pale, watery blob. His own reflection, superimposed over hers, was a study in worn-out angles: cheekbones sharp beneath skin the colour of old teak, eyes holding a flat, resistant sheen like sea-smoothed stones.

“Placement is conditional on your sector labor allocation,” the woman’s voice crackled through the tinny speaker. She didn’t look up from her panel. “Sector Four-Green has an opening in atmospheric particulate scrubbing. It’s a Tier Three assignment.”

Kael’s stomach tightened. Scrubbing. He’d seen the crews on his way in from the marshaling yards, tethered to the sides of the mega-structures like mites on a beast, shrouded in protective suits as they sand-blasted the accumulated poison of the storms from the building skins. The pay was in credit-chits barely sufficient for the Shelter’s weekly berth fee and synth-rations. Tier Three. Bottom of the visible ladder.

“Is there anything in data-stream sorting? Or civic logistics?” His voice was rougher than he intended, rusted from disuse. He’d been good with systems, once. Before the Salt.

The woman’s eyes flicked up, a flash of impatience. “Tier Three. Scrubbing, waste reclamation, or subterranean conduit maintenance. Your biometrics tag you as physically viable. Take it or the next intake is in seventy-two hours.” Her gaze returned to her panel, a clear dismissal. The choice was no choice at all. The temporary bunk in the overflow warehouse had been a cavern of coughing shadows and the smell of despair. Seventy-two more hours there would break something he couldn’t afford to lose.

“I’ll take the scrubbing.”

A thin plastic chit slid out of a slot. It was warm from the printer. His name, a string of alphanumerics that was now his legal identity, and the designation: *Sector 4-G, Tier 3, Probationary*. Below that, a map to his assigned berth: Stack 17, Pod 78.

Leaving the intake center was like being swallowed by a different kind of storm. The air under the vast bioplastic canopy of the New Metro’s lower sectors was a stew of recycled oxygen, heavy with the ozone tang of electrostatic air-scrubbers and the underlying, unscrubbable scent of too many bodies, too little water. The walkways hummed with a permanent vibration, the lifeblood of the city: mag-lev trams hissed on elevated rails overhead, delivery drones buzzed like metallic insects in designated cargo corridors, and the crowd flowed in relentless, stratified currents.

He saw the hierarchy in the clothing. The low-Tiers, like he would be, wore drab, durable polymers in grey, brown, or faded green. The mid-Tiers sported finer weaves, occasional splashes of colour, personal comms units visibly gleaming on wrists or temples. And then there were the uppers, gliding by in climate-controlled personal pods that detached from the main transit lines, their faces blurred behind tinted screens, their world separate, silent, and elevated in every sense.

Stack 17 was a monolithic residential slab, its surface a patchwork of repair jobs where the self-cleaning nano-coating had failed. The lobby was a cavern of noise—shouting children, the blare of public announcement streams, the thrum of ancient filtration units. The lift to the 78th floor was out of service. A hand-scrawled sign read “Stairs.” Of course.

The climb was a brutal, lung-searing pilgrimage. Each landing was a gallery of makeshift living: curtains strung across doorless entries, the sizzle of cheap protein cookers, the murmur of a hundred private dramas. The air grew thicker, hotter. By the 50th floor, the grime of the climb had mixed with the acidic sweat on his skin.

Pod 78 was a recessed alcove at the end of a dim corridor. The door was a sliding screen that stuck halfway. Inside was a space three paces long and two wide. A wall-bunk folded down, its mattress a thin slab of foam. A small sink dispensed a trickle of lukewarm, mineral-smelling water. A single outlet, a single dim lumen-panel in the ceiling. A viewport, no larger than his head, looked out onto the sheer face of Stack 16 twenty meters away, a cascade of identical pods and flickering lights. This was home. This was what he had traded the vast, dying sky of the coast for. A concrete lung.

The first shift began at 0400. He was issued a suit: a stiff, second-skin ensemble with a sealed helmet and a backpack filtration unit. It smelled of stale sweat and harsh disinfectant. The crew foreman, a man with a voice permanently raspy from filtered air, gave no instructions beyond a grunt and a gesture toward the gantry lift.

Outside the canopy, the world was a roar. The wind, funnelled between the towers, was a physical force. The rain, that greasy drizzle, beaded on his faceplate. The city spread below him like a monstrous circuit board, gleaming and polluted. In the distance, beyond the shimmering energy dome of the core sectors, the deadlands were a brown and grey smudge. Home.

His job was simple, mind-numbing, and terrifying. Tethered to a rail, he was to use a pressurized wand to blast grey-black cake from the building’s ventilation louvers. The particulate was a mix of cosmic dust, industrial fallout, and the pulverized remains of the old world. It came off in choking clouds, sucked immediately into his suit’s filters, which whined in protest. The vibration of the wand travelled up his arms, settling into his teeth. He moved in a slow, precarious dance, one hand on the tether, the other guiding the wand. The world shrank to the next louver, the next meter of rail, the next ten minutes until his mandated hydration alarm would chirp.

On the third day, the bureaucratic encounter came. It was at the Sector Credit Office, a sterile cube where he’d gone to receive his first week’s chits. The clerk, a young man with the pale, soft look of someone who’d never breathed unfiltered air, frowned at his screen.

“There’s a discrepancy. Your berth fee for Stack 17 has been assessed at the standard rate, but your pod is listed as a ‘premium external view’ unit. That’s a twenty percent uplift.”

Kael stared. “Premium view? It’s a hole looking at another wall.”

The clerk shrugged, a gesture of profound bureaucratic indifference. “The designation is in the system. Pod 78, Stack 17. External view premium. The fees are automated. If you don’t pay the uplift, a lien is placed on your account. Accumulated liens lead to suspension of labor assignment and shelter rights.”

A cold, familiar helplessness washed over him. It was the same feeling as watching the saline floodwaters breach the final seawall of his village—a vast, impersonal force, reducing you to nothing. Arguing was pointless. The system was a closed loop. “How do I dispute it?”

“Form 89-D, ‘Habitation Reclassification,’” the clerk recited. “Available at the public infokiosk in the central atrium. Submission backlog is currently… fourteen working days.”

Fourteen days. The uplift would have accrued, the lien would be in place. He’d be working just to pay a fee for a view of concrete he didn’t have. He took the meager stack of credit chits, the deducted amount already subtracted, and left, the taste of metal and defeat in his mouth.

That night, back in Pod 78, he didn’t fold down the bunk. He sat on the floor, his back against the cold wall, and stared at the tiny square of Stack 16. A light was on in a pod directly opposite. He could see a man, not much older than himself, moving about. He watched, numb, as the man heated a meal, scrolled through a data-pad, stretched. A normal life, in a normal cage. The absurdity of his own “premium view” tax felt like a final, mocking straw. He had navigated the drowning world, the brutal refugee queues, the decontamination sprays, the intake assessments, all to be shipwrecked here on this tiny ledge, defeated not by a storm or a wave, but by a line of code in a clerk’s terminal.

The moment of unexpected kindness came two days later, on the gantry. The wind was particularly fierce, a keening monster that shook the rigging. Kael was focused on a stubborn clot of carbonized grime when his hydration alarm chirped. Muting it required tapping a sequence on his suit’s forearm pad. As he shifted his grip, a sudden gust slammed him sideways. His glove fumbled. The pressurized wand, still on, jerked from his grasp.

It didn’t fall. Instead, it whipped on the end of its hose, a wild, metallic serpent, and struck the filtration unit on the back of the suit of the worker next to him—a woman named Lin, he’d heard the foreman call her. There was a sharp *crack*, and a hiss of escaping air. Not a full breach, but the filter housing was compromised. The suit’s integrity alarm would be blaring in her helmet.

She turned, her faceplate reflecting the sickly sky. He fumbled for his comm, switching to the local crew channel. “I’m sorry! The wind, I lost control—”

Her hand came up, not in anger, but in a swift, clear gesture: *Stop*. She pointed to her ear, then made a slashing motion across her throat. Her comm was dead, likely damaged in the impact. Then she pointed at him, and at the building’s access hatch twenty meters down the gantry.

He understood. They couldn’t talk. But a suit malfunction was a reportable incident. A report could mean probation review, fines, credit deductions he couldn’t afford. She was giving him a choice: go, get to the hatch, pretend it didn’t happen. Save himself the trouble.

Shame warred with survival instinct. He’d caused the damage. He began to gesture back, to insist on reporting it, on taking responsibility, but she was already moving. She unclipped a small repair patch from her own belt—a standard issue sealant strip for minor tears—and slapped it over the crack in her filter housing. The hiss dulled to a whisper. Then she pointed again, more urgently, at the access hatch. Her meaning was final. *Go.*

He went. Heart hammering, he made his way to the hatch, cycled through the airlock, and stumbled into the quiet, stale calm of the maintenance corridor. He stood there, helmet in his hands, breathing in the chemical-tanged air, waiting for the foreman’s shout, for the repercussions.

None came. Lin cycled in ten minutes later. She removed her helmet. Her face was sweaty, her dark hair plastered to her forehead. She looked at him, not with anger, but with a weary empathy that cut deeper than any accusation.

“The patch will hold for the shift,” she said, her voice quiet. “The housings on these gen-three suits are garbage. They crack if you look at them wrong. Foreman won’t check unless the system flags it.”

“I should report it,” Kael said, the words hollow. “It was my fault.”

She gave a short, humourless laugh. “And then we both get docked a day’s credits for an ‘unsafe incident.’ You for the lapse, me for the ‘equipment fault.’ The system doesn’t care about wind.” She studied him for a moment. “First week?”

He nodded.

“The trick is,” she said, turning to stow her gear in a battered locker, “to learn what rules are walls, and what rules are smoke. The tax on your ‘view’?” She glanced at him, and he startled. How could she know? “Everyone in Stack 17 with an even-numbered pod on the north face gets that tax. It’s a glitch from the last database migration. A wall. You’ll pay it, or you’ll drown in the paperwork. But this?” She gestured between them, to the invisible incident on the gantry. “This is smoke. You see it, you deal with it, you move on. You don’t feed the system your own flesh.”

She tossed him a small, wrapped bar from her locker—a high-density nutrient block, the kind issued for hazardous duty. “Eat. You look like the wind could use you for a brush.”

He caught it, the smooth wrapper cool in his hand. It wasn’t the food, though his stomach clenched at the sight of it. It was the act. A transaction with no ledger, no chits, no terms and conditions. An offering that acknowledged shared, silent understanding.

“Thank you,” he said, the words inadequate.

Lin just shrugged, a real one this time, not the clerk’s empty gesture. “Next time, watch the wind. And your wand.” She left, her footsteps echoing down the corridor.

Back in Pod 78 that evening, Kael ate half the nutrient bar. It tasted of almonds and chalk. He saved the rest. He finally folded down the bunk and lay on it, looking at his “premium view.” The light was on in the pod across the way. The man was there, reading. For the first time, Kael didn’t see just a stranger in a mirror-image cage. He saw a person, maybe navigating his own invisible glitches, his own bureaucratic storms.

The city outside his small, grimy viewport was still a monstrous, humming machine. The hierarchy was still etched in light and polymer and privilege. He was still Tier Three, a speck on its vast, uncaring skin. But the kindness, small and fierce and pragmatic as a repair patch, had created a tiny pocket of stillness. It was not hope—that was too grand, too fragile a word for this place. It was data. A new point on his internal map. Some rules were walls. Some were smoke. And sometimes, in the space between them, a person could pass another person a bar, and for a moment, the relentless machinery of survival would stutter, and you could remember how to breathe.
