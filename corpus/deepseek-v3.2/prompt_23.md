The silence was the worst part.

It wasn’t a true silence, of course. The ceasefire held, but it was a fragile, wounded thing. The usual background thunder of artillery had ceased, leaving the world eerily hollow. In its place were the sounds it had masked: the low, constant moan of the wind through shattered buildings, the skitter of loose stones, and the human sounds—the weeping, the ragged breathing, the occasional sharp cry of pain that seemed obscenely loud in the new quiet.

Corporal Aris Thorne moved through the rubble-choked street with the weary economy of a man who had been doing this for too long. His med-kit, a bulky olive-drab canvas satchel, slapped against his thigh with each step. The red cross on his armband was faded to a dusty pink. Ahead, the designated casualty collection point—what had once been a small café—was a hive of frantic, hushed activity. They had twelve hours. The clock, invisible and brutal, ticked in the back of every mind.

The CCP was chaos rendered methodical. Stretchers lay in rows on the cracked tile floor, between overturned tables and the skeletal remains of a coffee machine. The air was a thick soup of dust, blood, and the sharp, sweet-sour scent of infection. Aris’s eyes scanned, cataloguing before he’d even reached the triage officer, a harried-looking captain with a blood-stained notepad.

“Thorne. We’re setting up an intermediate staging station two blocks east, in the basement of the pharmacy. It’s more defensible if… well. You’re on initial triage here. Sort the inflow.”

Aris nodded, his throat too dry for words. Triage. The mathematics of misery. He’d done it before, under fire, with the world exploding around him. Doing it in this pressurized quiet felt colder, more deliberate.

He moved to the entryway, where two grunts were carefully lowering a soldier from a makeshift stretcher of a door and jackets. Friendly uniform. The man’s lower leg was a mess of pulped flesh and splintered bone, a tourniquet biting deep into his thigh. Aris dropped to his knees, his fingers going to the carotid pulse. It was rapid and thready. He checked the tourniquet time—marked in black pen on the man’s forehead: *45 min*. He had maybe fifteen minutes before irreversible tissue damage.

“Priority One,” Aris called out, his voice rough. “Immediate surgery, needs evac now.” A stretcher team swooped in, carrying the man toward the rear where the field surgeons worked in a curtained-off area. One saved, or at least given a chance. The calculus began.

The next was a young enemy soldier, his grey-uniformed chest a canvas of shrapnel tears. He was conscious, eyes wide with shock, breathing in quick, shallow pants. Aris ripped open the tunic, revealing a sucking chest wound on the left side. The air hissed in and out of the hole with a wet, gurgling sound. *Tension pneumothorax. Imminently survivable with intervention.* He grabbed an occlusive dressing from his kit, peeled the backing, and sealed it over the wound on the exhale. The hissing stopped. The man’s breathing eased marginally.

“Priority Two,” Aris declared. “Stable for now, needs chest tube.” He marked a ‘2’ on the man’s forehead with his grease pencil. The stretcher-bearers, who a day ago would have been aiming rifles at this boy, lifted him without a word.

And so it went. A stream of broken humanity. A woman with a scalp laceration, bleeding profusely but walking: Priority Three. A man with a distended abdomen, rigid and silent: Priority One, but his eyes were already glazing over. Aris pushed him toward surgery, but the number in his head was grim. An older civilian with a compound fracture of the arm: Three.

He found a rhythm, his hands moving with detached precision. Clean, clamp, bandage, tag, move on. His mind partitioned itself. The part that was Aris Thorne, the boy from a farming town who liked woodworking and his sister’s terrible poetry, was locked in a small, silent room far inside. The part that was Corporal Thorne, 87th Medical Battalion, operated the machinery of the body.

Then they brought in the two together.

They were from opposite sides, caught in the same collapsed building. One, a sergeant from his own company, Henderson, had a massive crush injury to his pelvis. He was ashen, his blood pressure undetectable by Aris’s quick palpation. The other was an enemy officer, his left arm mangled below the elbow, the hand hanging by shreds of tendon and skin. He was bleeding, but the tourniquet on his bicep was holding. He was conscious, watching Aris with dark, calm eyes.

Two operating tables were free. One surgeon was finishing up. In perhaps three minutes, a second table would be available. The clock ticked. Henderson was dying *now*. The enemy officer would lose his arm without prompt surgery, but he wasn’t circling the drain.

Aris looked at Henderson. He knew him. A loud, generous man who shared his care packages. He had a newborn daughter he’d never seen. He looked at the officer. A stranger. The enemy. The man who might have ordered the shell that made this ruin.

The medic’s internal calculus was not of friendship or malice. It was of physiology and time. Henderson’s injury required immediate, massive intervention—vascular repair, stabilization—a huge drain on time, blood, and skill with a survival chance he estimated at less than twenty percent, even if they started right this second. The officer’s was a straightforward traumatic amputation. Clean, quick. A fifteen-minute procedure to save a life with a near-certain outcome.

“Him,” Aris said, pointing to the enemy officer, his voice hollow. “Prep for amputation. Priority One.” He turned to Henderson’s stretcher-bearers. “Take him to the holding area. Make him comfortable.”

*Make him comfortable.* The words were ash in his mouth. He had just consigned a friend to die. The logical part of his brain screamed the rationale: *Maximum lives saved. One certain life versus one probable death. It’s the math. It’s the job.* The locked-away part of him wept.

They moved the officer to the newly cleared table. Aris followed, assisting the tired surgeon, a Major Chen, as they administered a local block and prepped the arm. As Chen worked with swift, brutal efficiency, Aris monitored vitals, held retractors. The officer, whose papers identified him as Lieutenant Valerius, never cried out. His gaze stayed on Aris.

“You speak my language?” Valerius asked suddenly, his accent thick but clear.

Aris, wiping sweat from the man’s brow, nodded curtly.

“You chose me,” Valerius stated. It wasn’t an accusation, nor gratitude. It was an observation.

“Your injury was more survivable,” Aris replied, his eyes on the surgical field, on the flash of the bone saw.

“The big man. Your comrade. He is dead?”

“He will be.”

Valerius was silent for a moment, the only sound the surgical clinks and the distant murmur of the CCP. “A difficult calculus,” he said finally. “To weigh souls on such a scale.”

“It’s not souls,” Aris snapped, the professional detachment cracking. “It’s physiology. Blood volume. Tissue damage. Minutes.”

“Is that what you tell yourself?” Valerius asked softly. “When you close your eyes at night? That it is only hydraulics?”

Chen completed the final suture, the stump now a neat, closed dome of skin. “Done. Move him out.”

As they transferred Valerius to a recovery stretcher, the lieutenant reached up with his good hand and caught Aris’s wrist. His grip was surprisingly strong. “Thank you,” he said. “Not for choosing me. For doing the calculus at all. My men… they would have let me bleed out in the street before the ceasefire.”

Aris pulled his wrist away, but the words stuck in him like shrapnel. He gave a stiff nod and turned back to the inflow.

The hours blurred. The ceasefire’s silence was filled with the sounds of suffering and the soft, relentless voice of triage in his head. *One for immediate, two for delayed, three for minimal, zero for expectant.* He tagged a young soldier with a minor gut wound as a One, knowing it was probably a death sentence but giving him the chance. He downgraded a screaming man with a foot blown off to a Two; he’d live without immediate surgery.

His own canteen was empty. His hands were stained a rusty brown up to the elbows. He was sorting through a pile of discarded webbing for a usable pressure bandage when a cry went up at the entrance.

“Medic! We’ve got a live one, buried in the clock tower rubble! Says there’s a kid with him!”

Aris and another medic, PFC Lara, grabbed kits and followed the grunt out into the twilight. The clock tower was a jagged tooth against the purple sky. In its base, a void of collapsed masonry and twisted girders led down into darkness. A faint tapping could be heard.

They crawled in, headlamps piercing the dust. In a small pocket, they found an enemy soldier, his legs pinned under a massive beam. Beside him, curled and silent, was a local boy of maybe eight, covered in dust but seemingly unhurt. The soldier had his good arm around the child, sheltering him.

“He does not understand me,” the soldier said in broken English, looking at Aris with desperate eyes. “He is in shock. My legs… I cannot feel.”

Aris assessed. The beam was beyond their ability to lift. The soldier’s trauma was catastrophic; his lower body was crushed. Even if they got a jack in here, he’d die from compartment syndrome and renal failure within hours. The boy was physically fine.

The calculus was brutally simple. One salvageable life. One already lost.

Aris turned to Lara. “Get the boy out. Carefully. He may have spinal.”

“What about him?” Lara whispered, nodding at the soldier, who watched them, understanding dawning in his eyes.

Aris met the soldier’s gaze. He saw the plea, then the acceptance. He knelt beside him. From his kit, he took a pre-filled syringe of morphine. He showed it to the soldier.

The man looked at the syringe, then at the boy being gently coaxed out by Lara. He gave a faint, almost imperceptible nod.

“Thank you,” he breathed.

Aris administered the dose, a massive one. Enough to bring peace, not just pain relief. He stayed, one hand on the man’s shoulder, as the labored breathing slowed, then quieted entirely in the dusty dark. He retrieved the soldier’s dog tags, tucked them into his own pocket, and crawled back out into the fading light.

The walk back to the CCP was a numb trudge. The ceasefire’s end was approaching; a tension was tightening the air. Back at the pharmacy basement, the flow had slowed to a trickle. The surgeons were finishing their last cases.

Captain Findlay approached him, looking exhausted. “We’re pulling out in thirty. The evac convoy is here for the Priority Ones and Twos. You’re on the last truck.”

Aris just nodded. He walked among the remaining wounded—the Threes, the walking wounded, the expectant. He stopped by Henderson’s stretcher. A blanket was pulled over his face. Aris stood there for a long moment, the calculus running in an endless, silent loop in his mind: *One for immediate, two for delayed, three for minimal, zero for expectant.*

He had done the math. He had saved those he could. The numbers were clean. The silence, now filling with the distant rumble of engines and the shouted orders to move out, was not clean at all. It was heavy with the weight of the unsaved, a silence that would follow him long after the guns began again. He picked up his kit, the weight familiar and terrible, and went to meet the truck.
